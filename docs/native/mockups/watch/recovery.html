Watch · RECOVERY (v2)

Counts UP through recovery · HR drop is the readiness signal · "Ready" chip appears when HR ≤ readiness threshold
← back to index
Why count UP, not down · documented for future agents:
  • The relevant question during recovery is "how long has this lasted?" not "how much is left?" The runner asks "am I ready to start the next rep?" The HR drop is the answer to that, not the clock.
  • Counting down adds psychological pressure of recovery ending. A countdown says "your rest is shrinking, hurry to enjoy it." That's the opposite of recovery's purpose.
  • Count-up turns recovery into a measurement. "I've taken 0:36 to recover. HR dropped 16 BPM in that time. I'm ready (or not)." That's what the runner needs.
  • The planned recovery duration is still visible · "60s planned" lives in the next-up card so the runner knows the workout's intent without having a countdown in their face.
  • The "Ready" chip appears earlier than 60s if HR has dropped enough. This is the V5 lift · pushed from web · the watch should not enforce a fixed clock recovery if the runner's CV system has already cleared.

State transitions · spec for the SwiftUI state machine:
  • State A → State B trigger: HR ≤ readiness_threshold (default: 130 bpm or 72% of max HR, whichever is lower) FOR sustained 3 seconds.
  • State B does NOT auto-advance. The runner taps the screen (or twists the crown) to start the next rep early. Watch buzzes a soft single-pulse haptic when ready chip appears (cue: "you can go").
  • Recovery max-cap. If the runner ignores the ready chip and lets the clock run, the watch still ends recovery at the planned duration (60s here) and auto-advances to the next rep. The plan is the upper bound; HR-clear is the floor.
  • If HR doesn't clear by the planned end: ready chip never appears, the rep starts on schedule, and the watch flags it later in the summary as "rep 3 started with elevated HR."
What this design assumes about the runner: they trust their body. The countdown-to-zero pattern (common on Garmin / Polar) assumes the workout's clock is the authority. This design says the workout's clock is the upper bound, but the runner's CV recovery is the actual authority on when to start the next rep. That's V5 doctrine · physiology over prescription, prescription over guess.
