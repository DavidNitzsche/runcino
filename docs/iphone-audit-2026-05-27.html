the audit.

Three rounds of mockups put a pattern on the table — race poster spine, sliding sheet, today-first headline, gradient = state. It's close. Before we go all-in, this deck does the homework: how do other apps solve this, what does Faff already know but isn't showing, what does a runner actually think when they pick up the phone, and what real-world states does v2.1 quietly fail at. Four lenses, one refined pattern at the end.
The output isn't more directions. It's conviction — every rule in v2.2 should trace to either a competitor's lesson, a piece of Faff data we have but ignore, a runner's actual workflow, or a known UX pattern that earns its place. No more vibes-only design moves.
02 · the bar

the bar
everything clears.

five answers · under three seconds · every day
From the first deck and locked in by your "core" feedback. Every app — Faff included — gets graded against these five questions. If a competitor's home screen can't answer a question without a tap, that's a miss. Most competitors miss two or three. Faff today misses on ops; v2.1 was projected to clear all five.
01
Run today?
Type, distance, target pace, HR. Visible without tapping.
02
Body today?
Readiness, sleep, HRV, RHR, niggles, ACWR. One readout, honest.
03
Coach says?
Why today is what it is. State-aware. (Optionally muted in v2.1.)
04
Race in N?
Trajectory, gap to goal, what's still wobbling. Confidence.
05
Race-day ops?
Bib, corral, transport, weather, drop bag. The 5am screen.

RUNNA
v · most direct competitor

Workout-first, plan-driven, hard to fault on the daily call — but the body is missing.

The Runna home is a tight execution surface: today's run dominates, START RUN is the only CTA above the fold, weekly progress bar gives the arc, race countdown lives quiet at the bottom. The "what am I doing today" question is answered before your thumb moves.

But health is absent. No HRV. No sleep. No readiness. If you're a low-mileage runner who only wants a plan to follow, that's fine. If you actually want to know whether to do the plan today, you're checking another app.

STRAVA
v · 10.0 · the incumbent

The home is a social feed. Today's workout? Not on this screen. Plan? Not at all.

Strava's home is fundamentally a social broadcasting surface. Open the app expecting to know what to run today and you'll see what your friends did yesterday. Today's plan lives behind a tap (and a paywall). The brand has decided that kudos, segments, and comments are the loop they're optimizing for — not training.

For Faff this is a clarifying negative example. Strava treats running as memory + sharing; Faff treats it as plan + execution. Same data, opposite priors. The Spectator-mode flagship is the only place Faff borrows Strava's pattern — and even there it's directional, not feed-based.

GARMIN
v · connect · data-density king

Body is the headline. Workout is the footnote. Backwards for runners, brilliant for athletes.

Garmin Connect treats the home screen as a physiological dashboard. Body Battery first. Training Status. HRV with a real label ("Unbalanced" — not just a number). Sleep score. Steps. Today's planned workout? A one-line tile at the bottom of a scrolling list.

The data is great. The hierarchy is upside-down for anyone actually following a plan. Garmin assumes you train by feel and use the data to verify; Faff assumes you train by plan and use data to adjust. Same numbers, opposite read.

WHOOP
v · the recovery monolith

One number. One sentence. One screen. Disciplined to the bone.

Whoop's home is the most opinionated app in fitness. The recovery percentage gets 40% of the screen. A single advisory sentence translates it into a recommendation. Sleep + strain + HRV + RHR live below as four small tiles. Nothing else competes.

What this proves: one big number with a verdict-sentence is enough. Whoop users don't open the app for a plan — they open it for permission. Faff's design challenge is harder (we DO have a plan) but the lesson holds: the hero verb should always read as permission or instruction, not just a label.

FITNESS
v · apple · the platform default

Beautiful, generic, plan-blind. Optimized for "did you move today," not "is the plan working."

Apple Fitness sets the design bar — the rings system has shaped a decade of fitness UX. The home is scannable, clean, animated. But it treats every user identically: a casual walker and a marathoner see the same screen, with the same three rings, the same nudges, the same summary.

For Faff: Apple owns the casual user. We can't compete on "did you move." We compete on "did the plan work, what does the body say, what's the next move." Rings have nothing to say about a 6×800.

NRC

The most branded fitness app. The least useful for an actual runner.

Nike Run Club leans hard into brand voice — bold black, orange accents, "Good morning, David" headline, all-caps CTAs. The marketing is excellent. The training plan exists but reads as a side-effect of selling guided runs with celebrity coaches.

"Today: Easy 5–6 mi" is the call — but it's a range, not a number. The coach-led runs are positioned as the hero, not your plan. NRC is what Faff would be if we let the brand layer eat the product.

COROS
v · the clean garmin

Same data as Garmin, half the chrome. Same hierarchy mistake — body first, workout last.

Coros is what Garmin Connect would be if it had been designed in the 2020s. Readiness with a verdict ("↑ Recover well"). Training load with a band ("Optimal · 120–220"). Vitals as a three-tile row. Today's workout as a single tile at the bottom.

Same architectural error as Garmin — the runner has to scroll to find the call. But the typography is more disciplined, the colors are calmer, and the "Above baseline · Recover well" verdict pairing is the strongest verbal frame in the competitive set.

competitive summary

six lessons.

  1. Runna wins on workout-first. The START RUN button is one decision per day. Faff should make today's action equally unambiguous — the hero verb is doing this job already, but we could surface a thumb-anchored CTA at the bottom-sheet collapsed state.
  2. Strava is the negative example. Social belongs in Spectator mode, not on the home. Memory + sharing is a different product than plan + execution.
  3. Garmin and Coros are body-first. Both invert the hierarchy: body battery / readiness as the hero, today's workout as a footer tile. Wrong for plan-following runners. Faff's today-first inversion is correct — but we should keep the body chip honest (Coros's verdict pairing is the model).
  4. Whoop proves single-number + verdict-sentence is enough. Add a single prescriptive sentence under the verb. "Hold under 148." · "Cap at Z2." · "Race pace."
  5. Apple Fitness owns casual. Don't fight it. Faff is for runners with a plan — the empty-state is "rest day," not "go for a walk."
  6. NRC's branding > NRC's product. Steal the typographic confidence. Avoid the brand-eating-the-utility trap.

05 · how runners think

the actual
sequences.

six moments · what runs through the head · what the app should answer
Reasoning-led, not primary-research. But the sequences below are the ones any plan-following runner runs through every week — distilled from common workflows, training-app forum complaints, and patterns visible in our own usage. The point isn't novelty — it's that the home screen should respect this order.
01 · the morning sequence
Wake · 6:00–7:00 am · still in bed · phone within reach
  1. What day is it? Mental model: "is this an easy day or a hard day?" — sets the emotional tone for getting out of bed.
  2. How does my body feel? Before reading any number, the runner does a quick self-scan. Then they want the data to confirm or contradict that gut read.
  3. What did I sleep? First number checked. If awful, the whole day reshuffles.
  4. HRV? Confirms or denies the sleep read. Second number checked.
  5. What's the call? Now they want today's workout. By this point they've already decided whether they want to do it.
  6. How does the weather look? Cold means an extra layer. Rain means an indoor backup. Often the rate-limiting decision.
Implication for the home: open with the workout (already done in v2.1), but the chip strip should include sleep + HRV + weather by default, not just on rest days. The runner is asking these questions whether or not the app cooperates.
02 · the pre-run sequence
Kitted up · 10 min before going out · standing in the hallway
  1. What pace am I holding? The number they say out loud while tying shoes. They want to lock it in.
  2. What's the HR cap? The discipline number. The one to glance at on the watch when they think they're going too hard.
  3. How long is this? Set a mental clock. "Back in 50 minutes."
  4. Anything weird I should know? Niggle? Different route? Wind direction? Coach note?
  5. Watch ready? Glance at the watch face. Should already show today's workout if paired correctly.
Implication for the home: the three stat numbers (distance · pace · cap) are the bedrock. Don't add a fourth. The v2.1 stat strip is right. But the "anything weird" beat is missing — a single one-line consequence sentence under the verb (Whoop / Coros pattern) closes this gap.
03 · the post-run sequence
Back home · sweaty · 5 min after finishing · phone in hand
  1. Did I hit it? First question, always. Pace + distance vs target.
  2. HR — was I in zone? The honest read on whether the run was the run that was planned. Easy days drift up, hard days drift down.
  3. What did the splits look like? Curiosity bait. Were they even, was the back half stronger, where did it fall apart.
  4. How am I supposed to feel about this? The verdict moment. Faff's NAILED IT / GOOD ENOUGH / STEADY verb table is exactly this signal.
  5. What's tomorrow? Pre-load. The runner is already thinking about recovery + next.
Implication for the home: the v2.1 post-run state is the strongest of all six day-states. Verb-as-verdict + stat strip + rep grid + tomorrow chip already nails this sequence. The screenshot opportunity is real — runners want to share this moment.
04 · the race-day sequence
Race morning · 4:30–5:30 am · half-asleep · adrenaline starting
  1. What time is it? First panic check. Did I oversleep?
  2. What's the weather? Decides the kit. Most urgent decision before anything else.
  3. Where am I going? Transportation + corral + start time. Logistics dominate.
  4. Is my bib pinned? Final-check items. Drop bag packed, gels in pocket, watch charged.
  5. Who's tracking me? Emotional anchor. Family at mile 13? Friends at finish?
  6. Goal time? Mental re-anchor. They already know but they want to see it written down.
  7. Coach pep talk? Optional. Some runners want it, some want silence.
Implication for the home: race week takeover (T ≤ 3d) is correct. The order of chips matters — weather first, transport second, logistics third, spectator fourth. v2.1 had them in roughly this order; lock it in by data, not guess.
05 · the rest day sequence
Day off · morning · no rush · sometimes ambivalent
  1. Wait, am I supposed to run today? The "did I forget" gut-check. Doubt-driven.
  2. Show me I'm not slacking. Streaks, weekly mileage, last 3 workouts hit — emotional reassurance.
  3. What's the body doing? Sleep, HRV, RHR — confirm the rest is needed and working.
  4. What's coming? Tomorrow + the long run + the next quality. Anticipation builds.
  5. Am I racing soon? Race countdown for context — "the rest day is part of the taper."
Implication for the home: the rest day panel is the most emotionally loaded screen in the app. Runners need permission, not just a label. v2.1's REST. + vitals + tomorrow chip is right, but missing the streak + weekly mileage reassurance from the data audit. Add those to the rest-day expanded view.
06 · the off-plan sequence (injury, illness, life)
Something is wrong · the plan no longer applies · the app feels accusatory
  1. I can't run. Maybe sick. Maybe injured. Maybe traveling. Maybe work blew up.
  2. Will the app yell at me? Fear: streak broken, plan derailed, "you missed" guilt.
  3. Can I tell the app why? Niggle, illness, travel — the runner wants to be honest with the system.
  4. What now? Modified plan? Just rest? Cross-train? The runner wants permission to adjust without shame.
  5. When can I come back? The hope question. "Three days of rest and I'm back on Thursday's tempo?"
Implication for the home: v2.1 quietly fails here. The promotion resolver doesn't have an "off-plan" state. Niggle / sick / traveling / missed-yesterday all collapse to the planned day's verb. This is the single biggest gap — covered in detail in the edge-cases section below.
06 · what earned its place

patterns we
can lean on.

six apps from outside running · each solves a piece v2.1 needs
v2.1's sliding sheet, tap-to-takeover, gradient-as-state aren't new ideas. They're patterns proven elsewhere. The point of this section: each move v2.1 makes should map to an established UX pattern that already works at scale. Skeptics get reassured; we don't have to invent anything; the implementation cost drops because there's prior art.

Apple Maps · the three-detent sheet

iOS native · 2018-present · billions of users

Maps shows the map (the hero — your context) with a bottom sheet that has three snap points: collapsed (search bar peeking, map breathes), medium (search + recents), expanded (full search results). Drag to change focus, never lose the map.

The detent set [.height(60), .medium, .large] is exactly what v2.1's bottom panel needs — only the content differs.

→ v2.1 inherits this directly. SwiftUI's presentationDetents ships it for free.

Apple Music · mini player → now playing

iOS native · ubiquitous tap-to-expand pattern

The mini player lives at the bottom of every tab — a thin sliver showing the current track. Tap it once and it animates into a full-screen now-playing surface. Album art fills, controls expand, lyrics appear below. Swipe down to return to mini.

The cognitive trick: the user always knows what's playing without having to navigate to find it, and the "expand to focus" move feels native because the entire iOS muscle memory expects it.

→ v2.1's tap-hero-for-takeover should match this animation exactly. Springs from current rect, no fade.

Robinhood · same screen, different lens

2013-present · finance · context-mode switching

The portfolio screen has one number — your total. Tap a time-range chip (1D · 1W · 1M · 3M · YTD · 1Y · ALL) and the same screen rerenders with that period's data. Same hierarchy. Different lens. The chart, the % change, the "Today's gainers" — all keyed off the active mode.

For Faff this validates the resolver model: the home doesn't change shape based on day-state — it changes content. Quality day, easy day, race week — same three bands, different gradient + verb + chips.

→ The resolver isn't picking a layout. It's picking the content for one layout.

NYT · the morning briefing

2014-present · daily-news editorial pattern

Open the app and the first card is "Your Wednesday Briefing" — date, weather, a hero illustration, and three or four bullet items. Below it, the rest of the feed. The briefing is opinionated — it tells you what matters today.

v2.1's hero panel is doing the same job — it's the daily briefing for the runner. The verb is the headline. The chips are the bullets. The bottom panel is "more if you want it."

→ Faff is a fitness app written like a daily newspaper. Lean into the editorial framing — section labels in caps, "Wednesday" not "Today."

Citymapper · purpose per surface

2011-present · transit · context-aware single-screens

Open Citymapper and the entire UI is bent toward one purpose: getting from A to B. There's no "feed." There's no "explore." A search bar, your saved places, time-aware home/work tiles. When you're on a journey, the entire app becomes a turn-by-turn surface.

The lesson is restraint: a successful single-purpose app refuses to be a Swiss army knife. Faff's home should refuse to be a feed. No "discover plans." No "trending workouts." Just today's call, the body, the race horizon, and the chip ladder of context.

→ When tempted to add a feed module, ask: would Citymapper add a feed of routes? No.

Things 3 · today vs upcoming

2017-present · macOS/iOS · cult-followed task manager

Things 3 splits the world into temporal lenses: Today (what's actually happening), Upcoming (the next few days), Anytime (the backlog), Someday (vague). Each lens is its own list — same data, different framing.

The unintuitive design move: the same task can appear in Today and Upcoming, viewed through different lenses. v2.1's tab structure is similar — Today is the lens for now, Plan is the lens for the cycle, Races is the lens for the horizon. Same plan data, three time-frames.

→ The 5-tab IA is right. Each tab is a temporal lens, not a separate feature.

07 · what v2.1 quietly fails at

the honest
edge cases.

twelve states the resolver doesn't cleanly handle today
v2.1's promotion resolver works beautifully for "normal training week, plan being followed." Below: twelve states that exist in real-world running where the resolver either picks the wrong panel, picks none, or silently lies. Each one is either PARTIAL (handled badly) or BROKEN (not handled at all). Fixes proposed for each. This is the most useful section in the audit.
01
BROKEN · MISSED RUN
I skipped yesterday's run.

Tuesday was a quality day. You skipped it. Wednesday morning the resolver shows "RUN EASY" as if nothing happened.

The plan is now lying to you — the easy day was contingent on the quality day having been done.

→ Add MISSED state. Hero: "CATCH UP." or "MOVE ON." (user picks once). Yellow-amber gradient.
02
BROKEN · NIGGLE
Active niggle, doing the run anyway.

You logged a tight L calf last night. This morning the resolver still says "RUN EASY" — same workout, no acknowledgment.

The niggle data is in the system, just not in the resolver's input set.

→ Verb modifier: "RUN EASY. — Watch L calf." Adds a one-line subhead under the verb when niggles.active > 0.
03
BROKEN · SICK
I'm sick — can't run.

No explicit "sick" state exists. Runner has to manually skip every day and the home keeps showing the planned workout like a guilt-trip.

HRV crashed, RHR elevated — the data already knows.

→ Detect (HRV ↓25%+ for 2 days · RHR ↑8+) → propose SICK state. Verb: "REST. — Body's fighting something." Auto-pauses streak.
04
PARTIAL · TRAVEL
I'm in a different time zone.

Wednesday in London = 4am Wednesday morning back home. The plan thinks "today" by phone-local time, but jet-lagged HRV is suppressed and pace targets need adjustment.

→ Detect tz shift via HealthKit location. Camp-mode panel triggers (already in v1 deck). Vitals get a "ADAPTING · D2/5" subhead.
05
PARTIAL · MULTI-RACE
I have two races on the calendar.

A-race London June 11. B-race Hackney Half June 28. C-race parkrun every Saturday. Which one does the chip show?

Resolver currently picks the soonest. That's right for race-week but wrong for a planning lens — you want to see both.

→ Race chip becomes "London 14d · Hackney 31d". Two values, comma-separated, both linkable.
06
BROKEN · NEW USER
I just signed up. No plan yet.

No race. No phase. No vitals history. The resolver has nothing to render — the home is half-empty for the first week of usage.

The first-run experience needs its own gradient/state.

→ ONBOARDING state. Hero: "WELCOME." with a 4-step setup (race? threshold? sync watch? first run). Same pattern, different content.
07
BROKEN · DOUBLES
I have two runs scheduled today.

AM easy 4mi + PM hills 6mi. One verb? One stat strip? The resolver picks one workout and the second one disappears.

→ Multi-workout state. Hero: "AM RUN." / "PM RUN." with a toggle, each with its own stat strip. Or stacked card-on-card.
08
PARTIAL · FAILED SESSION
I cut the workout short.

Started 6×800, did 3, called it. Resolver post-run state currently flips to NAILED IT or GOOD ENOUGH based on the data — but cut-short is its own verb that v2.1 named ("SOMETHING NEXT TIME") and then never built logic for.

→ Result schema needs cut_short boolean. Verb maps to "CUT SHORT. — Body talked. Listen." with amber-red gradient.
09
PARTIAL · NO HR
Watch dead, ran by feel.

No HR data means no zone analysis. Post-run verdict can't be computed honestly. Resolver currently fills in NAILED IT/STEADY off pace + distance only.

→ Verb table needs an "incomplete data" branch. "PACED IT." (no HR) or "BY FEEL." — honest about what we can't say.
10
BROKEN · DEVIATION
I ran something different.

Plan said easy 5.8 mi. You ran a tempo 4mi instead. Resolver flips to post-run with a green NAILED IT because pace + distance close enough — but you literally did the wrong workout.

→ Detect workout-type mismatch (plan.type vs actual.derived_type). Verb: "DIFFERENT DAY." Color: cyan. No guilt, no fake congrats.
11
PARTIAL · OFF PATTERN
I've rested 3 days in a row.

Plan had 1 rest, runner extended to 3. Day 4 morning the resolver still shows the planned workout for that day, but the user needs a re-entry plan, not the originally-scheduled tempo.

→ Detect rest_streak > planned_rest + 1. State: RE-ENTRY. Verb: "BACK IN." Suggest dropping intensity 1 tier for 1-2 days.
12
PARTIAL · OFF-SEASON
I just finished my goal race.

London was last weekend. Now what? Resolver knows there's no upcoming race but doesn't know whether to celebrate, recover, or pivot to base.

→ POST-RACE state for 7-14d. Hero: "RECOVER." with finisher chip — your race time, your PR delta. After 14d, transition to phase panel for the off-season.
summary · what this means for v2.1

we need ~12 day-states.
not 6.

v2.1's six gradients (easy / quality / long / done / rest / race) cover the happy path. The edges above need at least six more states: MISSED · NIGGLE · SICK · NEW USER · DOUBLES · POST-RACE. Plus modifiers (cut-short, no-HR, plan-deviation) that change the verdict logic without needing their own gradient.

The good news: the resolver is a pure function. Adding a state is a new clause in the decision tree, not a new screen. Adding a verb modifier is a new row in the lookup. The slide-sheet substrate doesn't change. v2.1 scales — we just have to build out the table honestly.

09 · the verdict

where this
lands.

what we learned · what to build · what to leave alone
competitive (§03)

v2.1's hierarchy was right. Today-first beats body-first (Garmin/Coros are wrong for plan-runners). The Whoop / Coros verdict-pairing pattern earns a place — add a prescription line. Treadmill mode without elevation (Runna) is malpractice. Strava is the negative example.

data audit (§04)

~15 dark signals. ACWR, streak, year-to-date, race trajectory arc, shoe status, weather chip, phase intent — all produced by code that's already running. Zero new data work. The expanded sheet is where they belong.

mental models (§05)

The runner is asking in this order: what kind of day · body state · workout call · weather / niggles · next moves. Default chip order should match. Off-plan moments need their own states — biggest gap in v2.1.

design patterns (§06)

Nothing in v2.1 is unprecedented. The sheet is Apple Maps. The takeover is Apple Music. The content-by-mode is Robinhood. The voice is NYT. The single-purpose restraint is Citymapper. Six patterns, all proven, stacked on top of running.

the verdict

v2.1 was right.
now make it complete.

The spine of v2.1 — three bands, gradient = state, draggable sheet, today-first headline, tap-to-takeover — survived all four lenses. Competitive validates the hierarchy. Data audit shows the spine has headroom. Mental models match the layout. Design patterns confirm the gestures.

v2.2 is depth, not redesign. Double the state count. Triple the verb table. Add the prescription line. Surface the 15 dark signals into the chip strip and expanded view. Lock the chip order to mental-model order. Sweat the edge cases. Keep everything else.

what this audit deliberately didn't answer
  • Whole-app IA. The 5-tab structure (Today · Plan · Races · Health · Me) is assumed correct. Things 3 / Citymapper patterns suggest it is — but if you want to interrogate that, it's a separate audit.
  • Coach voice tab. v2.1 deferred the LLM coach to its own tab (Confidant chat). This audit doesn't redesign that tab. Worth a dedicated pass once the home is locked.
  • Spectator mode rendering. Flagship feature from v1 — exists as a concept, hasn't been re-audited in this pass. The pattern carries (hero panel + chip strip), but the spectator-side surface needs its own pass.
  • Treadmill mode rendering. The takeover screens from v1 still hold — just need elevation surfaced, which the data audit confirms is one missed signal away.
  • Watch face refinements. Out of scope. The watch has its own design language (NumberFace per memory). The audit only intersects where the watch surfaces during a run.
monday's start line

start sprint 01.

The audit converges. The spine survives, the deltas are scoped, the build order is sequential. Sprint 01 ships v2.1-equivalent functionality on the resolver rails — the architectural investment pays off in sprints 02 and 03 where every refinement above becomes a clause in the resolver or a row in the verb table.

What you actually have to decide: do we ship sprint 01 first and review before committing to the deeper work? Or commit to the full v2.2 spec and ship all three sprints as one push? I'd vote for review-after-sprint-01 — the home is the front door, getting it 80% right and shipping beats getting it 100% right behind a feature flag for two months.
