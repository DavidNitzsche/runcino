# Marathon and Running Training: A Deep Research Report

*Compiled May 2026. A reference synthesis of current research and best-practice coaching for distance running.*

---

## 1. Scope

This report synthesizes current research and contemporary coaching practice on how distance runners build, peak, race, recover, and maintain across an annual cycle. It covers periodization and training intensity distribution, the workout types that drive marathon performance, strength training, fueling, recovery, footwear, cadence, heat training, masters considerations, injury risk, and the principles that integrate them.

The shape of the argument: marathon performance is overwhelmingly an aerobic problem, but the aerobic engine is built through a layered, time-sequenced stack of stresses. Get the sequence wrong and the ceiling stays low. Get it right and adaptation compounds across years.

This document is intended as a reference for building coaching plans, not as a coaching plan itself.

---

## 2. Periodization: the macro structure

### 2.1 The four phases

Modern marathon coaching divides a training cycle into four phases: base, build, peak (or specific), and taper. A reasonable cycle is 16 to 24 weeks, with the longer end appropriate for runners returning from layoff or stepping up in mileage. Most experienced runners do well with 16 to 20 weeks of focused work on top of a maintained base.

**Base phase (4 to 8 weeks):** Build aerobic volume with mostly easy running, strides, and hill sprints. Speed and threshold work is minimal or absent. The goal is to raise the floor of weekly mileage the body can absorb without breaking down.

**Build phase (4 to 8 weeks):** Introduce structured workouts. Threshold runs, longer tempos, mile and 1k repeats, progression long runs. Mileage continues climbing or stabilizes near peak.

**Peak / specific phase (4 to 6 weeks):** Workouts shift toward marathon-specific stress. Long runs include large blocks at marathon pace. Combo workouts mix marathon pace with faster intervals. This is where the race actually gets built.

**Taper (2 to 3 weeks):** Volume drops 40 to 60 percent while intensity is mostly preserved. The literature consistently points to a 2 to 3 week taper for the marathon, with the largest reduction in the final 7 to 10 days.

### 2.2 Linear vs reverse periodization

Classical linear periodization moves from high volume / low intensity to lower volume / higher intensity. This works well for shorter races (5K, 10K), where the peak energy system is closer to anaerobic.

For the marathon, a 2022 *Sports Medicine* review found that elite marathoners often run their highest training volume in the weeks immediately before the taper, not at the start of the cycle. This is the logic of reverse periodization for marathon work: aerobic capacity and marathon-pace specificity are what need to peak last, not VO2max. Practically, the specific phase should still feel high-volume and marathon-paced, not a track block.

The Italian school under Renato Canova goes further. Canova argues that the highest-quality marathon-specific work (large blocks at 95 to 105 percent of marathon pace) belongs in the final 4 to 8 weeks, with the long, easy runs of base phase serving as a means to an end rather than the work itself. His famous line: "What does a 2-hour easy run have to do with the marathon? Nothing." The point is rhetorical, not literal. Easy volume builds the substrate. Marathon-specific work converts it into race fitness.

### 2.3 Mesocycles and microcycles

Inside each phase, organize 3 to 4 week mesocycles with progressive overload, then a deload week with mileage cut by roughly 20 to 30 percent. The deload is when adaptation actually occurs. Skipping it accumulates fatigue and flattens performance.

Within each week (microcycle), the structure should look something like: long run + 1 to 2 quality sessions + easy days + 1 rest or true recovery day. Running 6 days a week is the sweet spot for most experienced 40 to 70 mile runners. Adding a 7th day produces marginal gains and meaningfully more injury risk.

## 3. Training intensity distribution

This is the most studied question in endurance training, and the answer matters substantially.

### 3.1 The three models

**Polarized (POL):** roughly 80 percent easy (Zone 1, below LT1), 5 percent threshold (Zone 2, between LT1 and LT2), 15 to 20 percent hard (Zone 3, above LT2). Almost no time in the "moderate" middle.

**Pyramidal (PYR):** mostly easy, with decreasing proportions of threshold and high-intensity work. More time at threshold than in polarized, less at VO2max.

**Threshold (THR):** higher proportion of threshold work, less easy and less VO2max. The Norwegian Bakken / Ingebrigtsen system is a sophisticated form of this.

A Stöggl and Sperlich randomized controlled trial established that polarized produced the best response across well-trained athletes. But subsequent work, including a 2021 study by Filipas and colleagues, showed that the optimal sequence for distance runners may be pyramidal during base/build, then polarized during the peak phase. The shift makes physiological sense. Pyramidal builds threshold capacity through volume; polarized then sharpens VO2max while preserving threshold.

For the marathon specifically, pyramidal is probably the better default in the build phase. Marathon pace for most trained runners sits at or just below LT1, which means more time in the threshold zone has direct race specificity. The polarized model is more obviously suited to track and shorter-distance racing.

Training volume is also a significant factor. Seiler's research suggests athletes training around 350 hours per year frequently display a threshold-model distribution, while the distribution typically transitions toward polarized or pyramidal patterns when overall training volume surpasses 750 hours annually.

### 3.2 The Norwegian double threshold method

The system Marius Bakken built and the Ingebrigtsen brothers made famous works as follows: instead of one hard threshold session per day, split it into two shorter sessions, both kept *deliberately sub-threshold* (around 2.5 to 3.5 mmol/L blood lactate). The total threshold volume per week is dramatically higher than a traditional model could support, but each individual session is recoverable.

A typical Bakken-era week: two double-threshold days (Tuesday and Thursday, AM and PM), one VO2 / hill session, one long aerobic, plus easy running, totaling 100+ miles per week.

The key error mode for amateurs adopting this is treating it as "two tempo runs in a day." It is not. Each session is intentionally below LT2. That distinction is what makes it sustainable. The Talsnes 2024 crossover trial confirmed that splitting threshold work into two shorter sessions of equal total volume produced superior adaptation versus one long session.

For most working amateurs without time for doubles, a "Norwegian singles" approach is more practical: run threshold sessions slightly more conservatively than usual (sub-LT2, longer total time at intensity) but as a single daily session. The principle of restraint is what carries over.

## 4. Base / maintenance training: the aerobic engine

### 4.1 What easy running actually does

Easy aerobic running, at roughly 65 to 75 percent of max heart rate or about 60 to 90 seconds per mile slower than marathon pace, drives the adaptations that determine marathon performance more than any other workout type:

- **Mitochondrial biogenesis and density.** More mitochondria means more ATP production through aerobic pathways at any given workload.
- **Capillary density.** More capillaries per muscle fiber means better oxygen delivery and waste removal.
- **Fat oxidation capacity.** At easy paces the body relies primarily on fat as fuel, training the metabolic machinery that spares glycogen during the marathon.
- **Type I (slow-twitch) fiber recruitment and adaptation.** These are the marathon fibers.
- **Cardiac output, stroke volume, plasma volume.**

There is some recent debate over whether very-low-intensity Zone 2 specifically is necessary or whether higher intensities also drive these adaptations. The honest answer is that both produce mitochondrial gains, but easy volume is uniquely sustainable. An athlete can run an hour easy six days a week. The same athlete cannot do that at threshold without breaking.

### 4.2 How much volume

The Boston Marathon training study published in *Sports Medicine* in 2025 confirms what coaching has long held: weekly volume in the 12 weeks before a marathon correlates strongly with race performance. The world-class distance runners reviewed by Tjelta and colleagues run 100 to 160 miles per week at marathon training peak.

For competitive amateurs, peak weeks of 50 to 70 miles are realistic and effective. Pushing toward 70 to 85 will likely yield further gains if the body can absorb it (this is a significant qualifier). Above that, the diminishing returns and injury risk are real for runners without years of base.

A useful framework: the peak week of any given cycle should be a number arrived at gradually over multiple cycles, not a number forced this cycle. The targeted block should sit at or just above the previous comfortable peak.

### 4.3 Maintenance running between cycles

Between marathon cycles, the goal is not to do nothing, and not to keep marathon-cycle volume. Maintenance running keeps the engine warm without grinding it down.

Practical structure for maintenance phases of 4 to 12 weeks between marathon cycles:

- 50 to 70 percent of peak marathon volume
- 5 to 6 days per week
- One quality session per week (tempo, fartlek, or short repeats), no marathon-specific work
- One long run of 90 to 120 minutes, easy effort
- Strides 2 to 3 times per week to preserve neuromuscular sharpness
- Strength training emphasis can shift up during these periods

This is also the right time to chase shorter race goals. A 5K or 10K block run during an off period from marathon training builds faster top-end, which directly raises the marathon ceiling when the runner returns to specific work.

## 5. The primary workout types

A complete marathon training repertoire pulls from seven workout categories. Different coaching schools weight them differently, but all of them appear in any well-designed program.

### 5.1 Recovery runs

Truly easy, often well below conversational pace. 30 to 50 minutes. Their job is circulation and active recovery, not adaptation. Pfitzinger draws a distinction here that matters: recovery runs are different from "general aerobic" or "endurance" runs, which are run at a more brisk easy pace and do drive adaptation.

### 5.2 General aerobic / endurance runs

The bread and butter. 60 to 90 minutes at an easy but not slow pace, roughly 15 to 25 percent slower than marathon pace. These are the workouts that build the aerobic engine. For most runners these account for 50 to 70 percent of total weekly mileage.

### 5.3 Medium-long runs

Pfitzinger's signature contribution. A second weekly run of 11 to 15 miles, distinct from the long run. The Lydiard-derived logic: meaningful aerobic adaptation requires runs over about 90 minutes, where even fast-twitch fibers begin to deplete and recruit aerobic pathways. One such run per week is good. Two per week separates serious marathoners from the field. The medium-long run is run at endurance pace, not marathon pace, to keep recovery cost manageable.

### 5.4 Long runs

The headline workout, 16 to 22 miles or 2 to 3 hours. Most should be at endurance pace, with selected long runs incorporating marathon-pace segments or progression structures. A mature marathon plan includes 4 to 7 long runs over 18 miles in the build and peak phases.

Long run variations worth using:

- **Steady aerobic long run:** entirely at endurance pace. Always the foundational version.
- **Progression long run:** start at easy pace, finish the last 4 to 8 miles at marathon pace or slightly faster. Pfitzinger popularized 30 seconds per mile slower than MP for the final segment, then ramping into MP for the very end.
- **Marathon-pace long run:** 14 to 22 miles total with 8 to 14 miles at goal MP in the middle or back half. The single most race-specific workout in marathon training.
- **Hanson "fast finish":** 16 to 18 miles with the final 2 to 4 miles well below MP (closer to half marathon pace). High training stress, high specificity to closing strong on tired legs.
- **Dress rehearsal long run:** 20 to 22 miles, simulating race-day fueling, hydration, kit, and shoes. Usually 3 to 4 weeks before race day.

The 90-minute threshold matters. Below it the work is largely aerobic; above it, the body forces fast-twitch recruitment into aerobic metabolism, which is the adaptation that makes the marathon work.

### 5.5 Threshold and tempo work

Lactate threshold has the highest correlation with marathon performance of any single physiological marker. The University of New Hampshire data on 2,400 recreational marathoners showed lactate threshold correlated with finish time at 0.91 versus 0.63 for VO2max. This is why Pfitzinger, Daniels, Hansons, Canova, and Bakken all build training around it.

Workout structures:

- **Continuous tempo:** 4 to 8 miles at threshold pace (approximately 15K to half marathon effort). Classic and effective, but high stress.
- **Threshold intervals (cruise intervals):** 4 to 6 x 1 mile at threshold pace with 60 to 90 seconds jog recovery, or 3 x 2 miles with 2 to 3 minutes recovery. Allows higher total time at intensity than continuous tempo because of the brief recoveries. This is Daniels' staple.
- **Sub-threshold work:** longer total volume (5 to 8 x 1 mile) at slightly easier pace (just below LT2). The Norwegian-singles adaptation. Best for high-mileage runners who want threshold volume without recovery cost.
- **Long tempos:** 8 to 12 miles continuous at slightly below threshold (closer to marathon pace plus 10 seconds). A specific marathon weapon, not a general workout.

Threshold pace should be calibrated by feel and by recent racing. The classic anchor is "the pace one could race for one hour." For most trained recreational runners that's somewhere around half marathon pace minus 5 to 15 seconds per mile.

### 5.6 VO2max intervals

Workouts at 95 to 100 percent of VO2max. Repetitions of 800m to 1600m at 3K to 5K pace, total work volume around 3 to 6 miles, with recoveries that are 50 to 100 percent of the work interval duration.

For marathon training specifically, VO2max work has secondary importance. It raises the ceiling, which makes everything below it (including marathon pace) feel relatively easier, but it doesn't directly train the marathon energy system. The Pfitzinger plans hold most VO2 work for the late build phase and use it sparingly thereafter. Canova largely abandons it during the specific phase, replacing it with "special speed" work at 90 to 110 percent of marathon pace.

A practical marathon-cycle dose: one VO2 session per week during the build phase, transitioning out as the runner enters the specific phase. Sample workouts: 5 x 1000m at 5K pace, 6 x 800m at 3K pace, 3 x 1 mile at 5K pace.

### 5.7 Marathon-specific workouts

The defining sessions of the peak phase. These exist on a spectrum from threshold intervals at slightly faster than MP to progression long runs to combo workouts.

**Combo / alternation workouts:** alternate marathon pace with faster intervals. Example: 15 minutes MP, 3 min jog, 4 x (90 sec at 10K pace, 90 sec easy), 15 minutes MP, cooldown. Trains the marathon energy system and the ability to recover at MP after surging.

**Canova special blocks:** the most demanding marathon-specific work in modern coaching. Two sessions in one day. Morning: 3K warmup, 10K progressive starting at MP and finishing at 95% of MP, 3K cooldown. Afternoon: 2K warmup, 8K at MP, 6 x 1K at 10K pace with 1K float at 95% MP between, 2K cooldown. These belong only to advanced runners with deep aerobic bases. They will wreck a recreational runner.

**Long marathon-pace runs:** the more accessible version. 18 to 22 miles total with 12 to 16 miles in the middle at goal MP. Done 3 to 5 weeks before race day. This is probably the single most predictive workout of marathon outcome.

### 5.8 Strides and hill sprints

Short bursts of fast running, 80 to 100m at near-sprint pace, 6 to 10 reps with full recovery. They preserve neuromuscular sharpness and running economy without taxing the aerobic or metabolic systems. Add them after easy runs 2 to 3 times per week throughout a marathon block. Hill sprints (8 to 12 seconds, all-out, on a steep hill) provide a similar stimulus with lower injury risk because of reduced impact.

This category is consistently underused by recreational marathoners and consistently emphasized by elite coaches.

## 6. Strength training for runners

The research here has matured significantly in the last decade. The systematic reviews are now consistent on the main points.

### 6.1 What works

Two systematic reviews and meta-analyses (Eihara et al. 2022, Llanos-Lagos et al. 2024) confirm:

- **Heavy resistance training** with loads above 80 percent of 1RM (often 90 percent) improves running economy and time-trial performance more than plyometric training alone.
- The benefit appears at training durations of 10+ weeks, not shorter.
- **Combined methods** (heavy resistance plus plyometric) appear to produce the largest running economy improvements.
- VO2max, peak heart rate, and lactate values are largely unchanged. The benefit is specifically running economy (oxygen cost at a given pace).

Plyometrics improve running economy at slower paces (below about 12 km/h, or roughly 8:00 per mile). Heavy resistance training is more effective at faster paces. Most marathoners benefit from both.

### 6.2 What "heavy" means

Squats (back, front, goblet), deadlifts (conventional, Romanian, single-leg), step-ups, split squats, hip thrusts, calf raises. Loaded with weight that allows 3 to 6 reps for 3 to 5 sets, focused on intent and bar speed rather than failure. This is not bodybuilding hypertrophy work and not high-rep "muscle endurance." It's neural and tendon-stiffness focused.

Plyometrics: pogo hops, jump squats, box jumps, A-skips, B-skips, depth jumps. 3 to 5 sets of 5 to 10 contacts, fully recovered between sets.

### 6.3 Periodization of strength

Strength work needs its own periodization that opposes the running cycle:

**Base phase:** 2 sessions per week, heavy. This is when maximum force capacity is built. Soreness is acceptable because run intensity is low.

**Build phase:** 2 sessions per week, one heavy and one focused on power / plyometrics. Volume trims as run intensity rises.

**Peak / specific phase:** 1 to 2 sessions per week, lower volume, maintaining intensity. The goal is preservation, not gain.

**Taper:** 1 short session in the first taper week. Drop strength work entirely in the final 7 to 10 days.

The most common error is doing the same strength routine year-round at moderate intensity. That's enough to fatigue without driving the adaptations that improve running economy.

## 7. Marathon nutrition and fueling

### 7.1 During-race carbohydrate intake

The recommendations have changed substantially in the last 5 years. The historical 30 to 60 g/h advice is now considered the floor, not the target.

Current evidence:

- **60 to 90 g/h** remains the baseline recommendation for marathon-distance events using multiple transportable carbohydrates (glucose plus fructose).
- A 2025 study of marathon finishers found that runners hitting 60 to 90 g/h were significantly more likely to finish under 3 hours than those under 60 g/h.
- Trail and ultra research now supports **120 g/h** as superior to 90 g/h for muscle damage markers, fatigue resistance, and recovery, when athletes have done gut training.
- The optimal glucose-to-fructose ratio depends on intake. At 90 g/h or below, 2:1 (glucose:fructose) is ideal. At 90 to 120 g/h, switch to 1:0.8, the ratio Kipchoge and many elites now use.

Planning around 80 to 100 g/h is reasonable for most marathoners, with 60 g/h as a floor and 120 g/h as a stretch goal that requires significant gut training in the build phase.

### 7.2 Pre-race fueling

Carbohydrate loading remains supported, with 8 to 10 g/kg/day in the 24 to 48 hours before the race. The protocol works because muscle glycogen storage can be increased 20 to 40 percent above baseline.

Pre-race meal: 1 to 4 g/kg of carbohydrate 1 to 4 hours before the start, low fiber, low fat, low protein. Familiar foods only.

### 7.3 Hydration

Drink to thirst is the consensus replacement for the older "drink ahead of thirst" advice, which led to widespread overhydration and exercise-associated hyponatremia. Aim for roughly 400 to 800 ml/h depending on conditions. Sodium intake of 300 to 700 mg/h matters more for cramping prevention than fluid volume alone.

### 7.4 Daily training nutrition

For training periods: 5 to 8 g/kg/day of carbohydrate for general training, up to 10 g/kg/day for high-volume weeks. Protein 1.6 to 2.0 g/kg/day to support recovery and maintain lean mass during high-volume training. Fat fills the remaining calorie need.

Underfueling daily training (often disguised as "cutting weight for race day") is the most common nutrition mistake among recreational marathoners and a primary contributor to injury, illness, and stalled progress. The literature on relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S) is unambiguous about this.

## 8. Recovery: sleep, modalities, and what actually works

### 8.1 Sleep

Sleep is the single highest-leverage recovery variable. The athletic literature is unanimous:

- 7 to 9 hours per night is the general recommendation, with **8 to 10 hours often suggested for high-load training periods** in elite athletes.
- The Stanford basketball sleep extension study showed measurable performance improvements in athletes who extended sleep to 10 hours per night for 5 to 7 weeks.
- Sleep extension before key races (3 to 7 nights of "sleep banking") buffers performance against pre-race nights of poor sleep.
- 50 to 80 percent of elite athletes report disturbed sleep. Prioritization beats accident.

Naps of 20 to 90 minutes can complement night sleep and have measurable performance benefits, particularly during high training loads or when night sleep is compromised.

### 8.2 Recovery modalities

The evidence ranking, from strongest to weakest:

**High-evidence:** Sleep, nutrition timing (carbs and protein within 30 to 60 minutes post-workout), active recovery (easy spinning or jogging at very low intensity), compression garments during travel.

**Moderate evidence:** Compression boots (Normatec-style), massage, foam rolling. These probably help with subjective recovery and reducing muscle soreness, with modest objective performance effects. The benefit is real but smaller than marketing claims.

**Mixed evidence:** Cold water immersion. Effective for acute recovery between same-day efforts, but post-workout cold immersion may blunt long-term adaptive responses (mitochondrial biogenesis, hypertrophy). Best used tactically near races, not chronically during base/build.

**Low evidence:** Saunas have separate benefits for heat acclimation (covered below). Contrast therapy and IV therapy have weak evidence for performance recovery beyond placebo.

### 8.3 The recovery ratio that matters

Most adaptation happens during recovery, not during training. A useful frame: every hard training stress requires a recovery period of 24 to 72 hours depending on the stress. Stack two hard efforts back to back only if the second is at substantially different intensity (e.g., a long run the day after a track session, not two threshold sessions back to back unless using the Norwegian double).

## 9. Footwear strategy: super shoes and the rotation

### 9.1 What super shoes actually do

Carbon-plated shoes with high-rebound foam (Vaporfly, Alphafly, Adios Pro, Endorphin Pro, Metaspeed, etc.) reduce the metabolic cost of running by approximately 4 percent at race paces. The mechanism is partly the energy return of the foam, partly the lever effect of the curved carbon plate reducing ankle work.

The performance benefit is largest at faster paces. At slow easy paces, the benefit drops substantially.

### 9.2 The training tradeoff

Several considerations argue against doing all running in super shoes:

- The carbon plate reduces foot and lower-leg muscle activation. Over time this may weaken the intrinsic foot muscles and calves.
- A 2023 *Sports Medicine* article documented five cases of navicular stress fractures linked to carbon-plated shoes, particularly during high-mileage usage.
- Foam in super shoes degrades faster than conventional foam. Most super shoes are spent at 200 to 300 miles, sometimes less.
- Anecdotal and limited research evidence suggests reduced muscle soreness from training in super shoes, which may allow higher training loads (Kenyan physiotherapist David Kirui estimates a 25 percent reduction in overuse injuries in his elite group, who do roughly 60 percent of mileage in super shoes).

The mainstream coaching consensus: rotate. Race-day shoes for races and 1 to 2 key marathon-pace or threshold sessions per cycle. Daily training in conventional trainers. Long runs split between marathon-day shoes (for specificity) and other trainers (to spread load).

A practical rotation framework:

- **Race day shoe (super shoe):** marathon, half marathon, key MP long runs near the race
- **Training "super" shoe (e.g., SC Trainer, Endorphin Speed):** tempos, threshold intervals, faster long runs
- **Daily trainer (max cushion):** general aerobic runs, medium-long runs
- **Recovery shoe:** very easy days, recovery runs

### 9.3 The cadence-shoe interaction

There is a real consideration for runners with low cadence (under 165 to 170 spm) about super shoes. The longer stride and softer landing they enable can amplify overstride and increase loading at heel-strike. Building cadence first, then adopting super shoes, is the safer order.

## 10. Cadence and running form

### 10.1 The 180 spm myth

The "180 steps per minute" number originated with Jack Daniels' observation of elite runners at the 1984 Olympics. He counted 46 distance runners and found all but one ran at 180 spm or higher *at race pace*. He never claimed this was a universal target for all runners at all paces. The coaching world distorted this into a one-size-fits-all rule.

Reality:

- Speed = cadence × stride length. Both increase as running speed increases.
- Easy runs naturally produce 160 to 175 spm.
- Tempo runs naturally produce 170 to 185 spm.
- 5K race pace produces 180 to 195 spm.
- Sprint speeds exceed 200 spm.
- Optimal cadence varies by height, leg length, and pace. Taller runners with longer legs naturally have lower cadence at any given speed.

### 10.2 When cadence work helps

Heiderscheit's 2011 study established the practical principle: **a 5 to 10 percent increase from natural cadence reduces impact loading by 10 to 20 percent without a meaningful metabolic penalty.** Pushing further than 10 percent increases metabolic cost and is rarely necessary.

The runners who genuinely benefit from cadence intervention are those whose natural cadence sits below 165 spm at any meaningful pace. Below 160 spm, overstriding is almost certain. Increasing cadence shortens the stride, moves the foot landing closer to under the center of mass, and reduces braking forces. This is a high-leverage intervention for these runners.

For a runner already at 170 to 180 spm at marathon pace, attempting to push higher offers diminishing returns. The work is better spent on hip mobility, glute strength, and posture.

### 10.3 Form work that consistently helps

- **Hip extension and glute activation:** drives stride length without overstriding. Most desk-bound runners have weak glutes and tight hip flexors.
- **Core stability:** prevents pelvic drop and energy leaks during late-race fatigue.
- **Calf and Achilles strength:** essential for absorbing and returning ground contact force.
- **Strides and hill sprints:** drill efficient mechanics under low-fatigue, high-coordination conditions. The most form-improving work most runners can do is more strides, not more form drills in isolation.

## 11. Heat and altitude as training tools

### 11.1 Heat acclimation

A growing body of research, primarily from Scandinavian cycling and skiing programs (Rønnestad and colleagues), shows that heat training can produce hematological adaptations comparable to altitude training:

- 5 weeks of heat training (5 x 50 min sessions per week in a heat chamber or with a heat suit) produced approximately 2.4 to 2.6 percent increases in hemoglobin mass.
- Heat-induced hemoglobin gains can be maintained with as few as 3 sessions per week.
- Unlike altitude adaptations (which reverse within 7 to 14 days of returning to sea level), heat-induced gains are more easily preserved.
- Plasma volume expansion happens within the first week of heat exposure. Red blood cell mass and hemoglobin take 3 to 5 weeks.

For marathoners targeting cool-weather races, a 3 to 5 week heat acclimation block in the late build phase, then maintaining with 2 to 3 sessions per week through the taper, may provide a measurable performance edge. Practical methods: sauna sessions of 20 to 40 minutes post-run, heat suit runs, or training in the warmest part of the day during summer months.

### 11.2 Altitude

Live-high / train-low remains the gold standard for altitude exposure (sleep at 2,000 to 2,500m, train at lower altitude or at lower intensities at altitude). The hematological gains reverse within 1 to 2 weeks of returning to sea level, so timing is critical.

For most amateur marathoners without access to sustained altitude, heat acclimation is now considered the more practical, comparable-magnitude alternative.

## 12. Masters considerations (35+)

The literature on age-related performance shows:

- VO2max declines roughly 5 to 10 percent per decade after age 30.
- Lactate threshold declines more slowly than VO2max.
- Running economy is largely preserved into the 50s for trained runners.
- Performance is well maintained until approximately age 35, with moderate decreases until 50 to 60 years, then more abrupt decline.
- The single 6-year follow-up study of a world-record-holding masters marathoner showed his decline was driven primarily by declining VO2max, not threshold or economy.

What this means in practice:

- VO2max-targeted work (intervals at 3K to 5K pace) may need *more* emphasis, not less, as a runner ages. The decline is the limiter.
- Strength training becomes more important, not less. The propulsive power loss is partly muscular.
- Recovery time between hard sessions extends. Two hard sessions per week often beats three for masters runners, especially after 45.
- Running economy preservation through year-round consistency matters more.
- Sleep needs may increase, not decrease.
- Easy volume tolerance may decline. Total weekly mileage may need to drop 10 to 20 percent compared to a younger version of the same runner, but quality of work can be preserved.

For runners in their late 30s and early 40s, the "masters" considerations are not yet aggressive. Performance can plateau or even improve through better training organization, smarter recovery, and stronger ancillary work. The peak marathon ages for most runners sit between 30 and 40.

## 13. Training load and injury risk

### 13.1 What predicts injury

The 10 percent rule (don't increase weekly mileage by more than 10 percent week to week) is the most-repeated piece of running advice in existence. The research now suggests it's not the most important variable.

A 5,200-runner cohort study published in 2025 found:

- **Single-session spikes** were the strongest predictor of injury. Running a single session 10 percent or more longer than the longest run in the past 30 days raised injury risk substantially:
  - 10-30% spike: 64% higher injury risk
  - 30-100% spike: 52% higher
  - >100% spike: more than doubled
- Week-to-week mileage changes and the acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) showed weaker or no association.
- The largest study of marathon training to date (HSS, 2024) supported ACWR as a marathon-relevant guide, with the sweet spot of 0.8 to 1.3 minimizing injury risk.

The reconciliation: the dangerous thing isn't a 15 percent weekly mileage increase. The dangerous thing is putting all of that increase into one run.

### 13.2 Practical principles

- **Don't make any single long run substantially longer than the longest recent run.** A maximum 10 percent jump on any single session is a useful ceiling.
- Maintain chronic training load. Big mileage drops followed by big jumps back are riskier than steady volume.
- Cumulative load matters more than week-to-week change for non-novice runners. The Comrades research found *lower* training loads were associated with higher injury risk during the race itself, because runners weren't prepared for the demands.
- Strength training reduces injury risk by approximately half across multiple meta-analyses. This is one of the highest-leverage injury prevention interventions available.

### 13.3 Recovery between cycles

A runner who peaks at 70 mpw and runs a marathon needs 2 to 4 weeks of substantially reduced training before returning to focused work. A runner peaking at 100+ mpw may need 3 to 6 weeks. The recovery period doesn't have to mean no running; it means easy mileage at 30 to 50 percent of peak, no quality work, and active monitoring of resting heart rate and subjective fatigue.

The 1 day per mile guideline (26 days of "recovery" after a marathon) is overly conservative for experienced runners doing easy running, but a useful frame for the time before resuming structured workouts.

## 14. Tapering

The taper has been studied extensively. The current evidence converges on:

- **Duration:** 2 to 3 weeks for marathon distance. Shorter for half marathon and below; longer tapers offer minimal additional benefit and may produce detraining.
- **Volume reduction:** 40 to 60 percent reduction from peak. The largest cuts go to easy mileage, not to quality work.
- **Intensity preservation:** keep some short, sharp work at race pace through the taper. Eliminating intensity entirely is detrimental.
- **Frequency:** maintain run frequency at approximately 80 percent of normal. Don't suddenly add rest days; keep the body in its rhythm.
- **Predicted benefit:** a well-executed taper has been measured at approximately 5 minutes 32 seconds of marathon improvement on average across one large recreational-runner data set.

Common errors:

- Cutting intensity along with volume (loses sharpness).
- Adding novel workouts in the final 10 days (no time to absorb them, only time to fatigue from them).
- Overcompensating with rest, eating, or napping changes that disrupt normal rhythm.
- Using the freed-up time for extra strength work, foam rolling, or other ancillary stress.

The mantra: in the final two weeks, the fitness is built. The job is to arrive at the start line rested without losing edge.

## 15. Coaching schools: a brief comparative survey

The major schools of marathon training all work for the right athlete. The differences are in emphasis.

**Lydiard / Pfitzinger:** High aerobic volume, medium-long runs, threshold-focused workouts, conservative VO2max work. Emphasizes endurance throughout. Best for high-mileage runners who can absorb 60+ mpw.

**Daniels:** Pace-zone-driven training (E, M, T, I, R paces). Threshold (T) work as the centerpiece. Cruise intervals. Generally lower mileage, higher specificity per workout. Highly systematized.

**Hansons:** Cumulative fatigue model. Long run capped at 16 miles, but high overall weekly volume so the 16 is run on tired legs. Strong emphasis on race-pace training. Six days per week of running.

**Canova / Italian school:** Funnel periodization. Long buildup of general conditioning, then aggressive marathon-specific work in the final 4 to 8 weeks (large MP blocks, special blocks, alternation workouts). Highest specificity of any system. Best for advanced runners with deep aerobic bases.

**Norwegian (Bakken / Ingebrigtsen):** Lactate-controlled threshold work, often double sessions per day. Sub-threshold emphasis. Track-focused for the Ingebrigtsens, but the principles transfer to the marathon (Sondre Nordstad Moen ran 2:05:48 using these methods). Requires lactate testing or calibrated effort awareness.

**Hudson:** Adaptive training. Plans evolve based on response. Mixes Lydiard volume with Daniels structure. More artistic than systematic.

For programming purposes, no single school should be applied dogmatically. The most successful marathon plans for amateur runners borrow Lydiard volume principles, Pfitzinger structure (medium-long runs, progression long runs), Daniels intensity zones, and Canova-style late-cycle specificity.

## 16. The integration

A few things are clear from a careful read of the literature and the practice of elite coaches:

The marathon is overwhelmingly an aerobic endurance event. Runners who improve year over year are the ones who patiently build aerobic capacity (volume, easy running, long runs) while progressively layering specificity (threshold work, marathon pace, then marathon-specific blocks).

There is no magic workout. Yasso 800s don't reliably predict the marathon. Single tempo runs don't make a marathoner. The cumulative effect of dozens of well-designed sessions over months is what produces the result. Coaches who promote a single workout type as the secret are selling something.

Strength training is genuinely high-leverage and consistently underdone by recreational marathoners. Two heavy sessions per week, year-round, will likely improve marathon time more than any other ancillary intervention, while also dramatically reducing injury risk.

Sleep extension and proper fueling are the highest-leverage recovery interventions and the ones most often compromised. Recovery technology (boots, contrast, massage) helps, but at the margins.

The marathon is one of the few endurance events where consistency over years dwarfs cleverness within a single cycle. The runner who runs 50 mpw consistently for five years will outperform the runner who jumps to 80 mpw for one cycle and gets injured. Build the base. Protect the base. Layer specificity onto the base. Race. Recover. Repeat.
