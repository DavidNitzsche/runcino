# The Amp: A Deep Research Report

*Compiled May 2026 from the official product documentation, the Amp app description, and independent reviews from Healthline, Fast Company, TechRadar, InsideHook, Yahoo, Good Gear, Rolling Stone, and The Manual.*

---

## 1. What the Amp is

The Amp is a wall-mounted, AI-powered, motorized cable strength machine. It launched at CES 2025 from Palo Alto-based AMP FIT Inc., founded by Shalom Meckenzie (formerly of SBTech, which merged with DraftKings in 2020). It belongs to the same product category as Tonal and the discontinued Mirror but is more compact, single-arm, no-screen, and substantially cheaper.

The pitch is straightforward: replace the cable crossover machine in a commercial gym (a heavy, ugly, plate-stacked steel cage) with something that fits in a living room without looking like fitness equipment, and use software and motorized resistance to do everything the steel version can plus modes that physical weights can't replicate.

Price: $1,795 for the device. $23/month subscription with a 12-month minimum commitment for the app, which controls the device. White-glove installation and 5 starter accessories are included. 90-day return window. 1-year limited warranty. HSA/FSA eligible. Affirm financing available at $75/month for 24 months.

## 2. The hardware

### 2.1 Physical dimensions

The device itself is 6 feet tall, 2 feet wide, 1 foot deep, weighing 70 lbs. Wall-mounted, with installation requiring 8 drilled holes (typically into a stud). Setup takes about 30 minutes when done by the white-glove team.

Required floor space for use is 7 feet tall by 3 feet wide with 4 feet of clearance from the wall. So while the unit itself is small, you need a roughly 7x7 ft cleared area to actually train.

Construction is aluminum alloy body with high-strength steel for the arm. The dial ring, knob, and main touchpoints are solid aluminum. Reviews consistently note that the build quality feels premium relative to the price point, closer to Apple than to typical fitness gear.

### 2.2 The arm

A single adjustable arm extends from the vertical column. It has 38 lock points across 10 height positions and 4 angle settings, which together cover essentially every exercise plane: floor-level (rows, deadlifts, kickbacks, ankle-strap work), waist-height (curls, presses, rotations), chest-height (chest press, flyes), shoulder-height (face pulls, shoulder press), and overhead (lat pulldowns, tricep extensions).

The arm slides along the column with a press of a release button (some users report it sticks slightly when new but loosens with use). The angle adjustment lets you change the cable's pull direction without moving the arm height.

### 2.3 The cable and motor

Instead of a stack of weight plates, the Amp uses an electromagnetic motor that generates resistance through the cable. This is the same mechanism Tonal popularized. There is no physical weight to load or unload. The cable feels like a real cable but the resistance is digital.

Resistance range is 5 to 100 lbs. This is the most common point of skepticism for serious lifters accustomed to barbell loads. The Amp's response is that for cable-style training, where you can't recruit the same total-body leverage you get from a barbell squat or deadlift, 100 lbs of cable resistance is meaningfully harder than 100 lbs of plates. It's also enough for the vast majority of single-arm and isolation work, which is what cable machines are best at. The strength ceiling is real for advanced lifters doing bilateral lower-body work, but for the device's intended use case it's appropriate.

### 2.4 The dial

A physical dial on the front of the device controls resistance manually. Turn it to set weight in 5 lb increments. Click the dial to switch between resistance modes. The dial works in concert with the app: the AI can also adjust resistance automatically between sets based on your performance, but you can override at any time via the dial.

### 2.5 The shelf

A small phone shelf is built into the device for follow-along workouts (the app runs on your phone, not on the device, since there is no screen). The shelf can charge your phone while in use. An optional accessory rack ($110, normally $200) holds the attachments off the floor.

### 2.6 Connectivity

WiFi for internet (802.11 a/b/g/n/ac). Bluetooth 5.1 (BLE) for phone-to-device communication. The app currently supports iPhone and Android (Android was rolled out after launch). Apple Health integration syncs workouts to your broader health data.

## 3. How it works: the resistance modes

This is where the Amp differentiates from a traditional cable machine. The motor can shape the resistance curve across a rep in ways physical weights cannot. There are three core modes plus two advanced "game" modes.

### 3.1 Fixed mode

Constant resistance throughout the entire rep, both the concentric (lifting) and eccentric (lowering) phases. This simulates a traditional cable machine or weight stack. If you set 50 lbs, you feel 50 lbs the whole way through. This is the baseline mode and the most familiar.

Use it for: standard strength training, when you want to be able to compare loads across sessions, learning new movements.

### 3.2 Band mode

Resistance starts light at the beginning of the movement and increases as you pull the cable further from its starting position. This simulates a resistance band, which is mechanically the opposite of a free weight. Free weights are hardest at the bottom of a movement (where leverage is worst) and easiest at the top. Bands are easiest at the bottom and hardest at the top, exactly where the muscle is in its strongest position.

Use it for: building peak contractile strength at the top of a movement (peak bicep curl, lockout of a press, top of a hip thrust). Also useful for power and explosive work.

### 3.3 Eccentric mode (also called Amplify)

Resistance is lower on the concentric (lifting) phase and higher on the eccentric (lowering) phase. This is the most physiologically interesting mode because muscle can produce 20 to 40 percent more force eccentrically than concentrically, but traditional weight training uses the same load for both phases (capped by what you can lift concentrically).

Eccentric overload is the gold-standard stimulus for hypertrophy and tendon health. The research on Achilles, patellar, and other tendinopathies consistently points to heavy slow eccentrics (Alfredson protocol and successors) as the most effective rehab and prevention strategy. For runners specifically, eccentric loading of the calf, quad, and hamstring complex has direct injury-prevention value.

Use it for: muscle building, tendon health and rehab, breaking through strength plateaus, training the lowering phase of a movement (which is normally undertrained).

### 3.4 Tempo mode

A "game" mode that enforces a specific cadence on each rep. The app shows visual cues and you keep pace by collecting droplets or matching markers. The point is to build control, time-under-tension, and muscle-mind connection. It's effectively an automated way to enforce 4-second eccentrics or 3-1-3 tempos that are hard to maintain manually.

### 3.5 Drop Set mode

Automated weight reduction for high-intensity finishing work. You start at a heavy resistance, hit failure or the rep target, and the machine instantly drops the weight (no plates to pull) so you can continue without rest. The AI can decide when to trigger drops, or you can manually invoke it. This is one of the things motorized cables do better than physical weights, since drop sets with a stack require manual pin-pulling.

### 3.6 Mode switching

You can change modes mid-workout by clicking the dial, and the AI can also switch automatically based on the workout structure. This means a single workout can mix fixed-mode strength sets with band-mode finishers and eccentric tendon work without changing equipment.

## 4. Included accessories

The 5 included attachments cover the standard cable machine vocabulary:

- **Single Handle:** D-handle for unilateral exercises (single-arm rows, single-arm presses, single-arm curls, lateral raises, woodchops).
- **Dual Handle:** Two-handle wide grip for bilateral movements (chest press, flyes, rows, pulldowns).
- **Rope:** Two-tail rope for triceps pushdowns, face pulls, hammer curls, skull crushers, pullovers.
- **T-Bar:** Straight bar for compound movements requiring two hands on a fixed grip (chest press, shoulder press, deadlifts, squats, rows, biceps curls).
- **Ankle Straps (2):** Padded cuffs for lower-body isolation and Pilates-style work (kickbacks, leg curls, hip abduction/adduction, fire hydrants, hamstring curls, leg circles).

All attachments use a quick-release click mechanism (described as belt-buckle-like). Swapping takes seconds.

The optional accessory rack ($110) mounts to the wall and holds the attachments. Without it, the attachments live on the floor next to the device.

## 5. The app and AI

### 5.1 What the app does

The app is the brain of the system. The device itself has no screen. Everything (workout selection, form videos, weight prescription, mode switching, progress tracking) runs through your phone, which sits in the device's shelf during workouts and connects to the Amp via Bluetooth.

Core capabilities:

- **Auto-adjusting resistance.** The AI sets a starting weight based on your strength assessment and adjusts between sets and across sessions based on your performance. You can override manually with the dial.
- **Rep counting.** Sensors in the device count reps automatically.
- **Tempo and form tracking.** The system tracks movement velocity, range of motion, and rest periods.
- **Mode auto-selection.** The AI can switch between Fixed, Band, and Eccentric within a workout based on what the workout is trying to accomplish.
- **Form coaching.** Video demonstrations and audio cues during each movement.
- **Progress logging.** Every rep, set, weight, and movement is logged automatically. Weekly reports summarize volume, output, and trends.

### 5.2 The strength assessment

When you first set up the Amp, you go through a roughly 10-minute strength assessment that walks you through key movements and uses the data to set baseline resistance recommendations. From that point, the system adjusts based on your actual performance: rep counts, tempo, perceived exertion, and how you respond to challenges.

### 5.3 The AI coach

The marketing terminology around "AI" can be eye-rolling, but functionally what the Amp's system actually does:

- Tracks your performance across sessions (not just within them) to predict appropriate weights
- Notices patterns (e.g., morning vs. evening performance differences, recovery from prior sessions) and adjusts
- Adjusts intensity based on inputs about sleep, stress, and schedule constraints
- Generates custom workouts on demand from prompts (target muscle groups, time available, mood/energy)
- Suggests substitutions if you indicate an exercise isn't working (e.g., shoulder pain → swap shoulder press for a different shoulder-targeted move)

### 5.4 Trainer-led content

The app launched with content from Chris Heria (calisthenics, 8M+ YouTube subscribers), Kinga Strogoff (competitive fitness), Terry Crews, Rachel Joy, and Larry Wheels. The library now features 20+ trainers and is updated weekly. Workouts come in follow-along format with the trainer's voice over the AI's resistance management.

### 5.5 Generative AI workouts

You can describe what you want in natural language ("30-minute upper body push focus, moderate intensity, no shoulder strain") and the system will generate a personalized session with movements, instructional videos, and weight recommendations.

### 5.6 Gamification

The app includes:

- **Personal records and achievements**
- **Leaderboards** for community comparison
- **Challenges**
- **Tempo and Drop Set games** that turn pacing and intensity into score-based mini-games (some reviewers describe it as Guitar Hero-style)

This is meant to drive consistency. It's optional and can be ignored if you find it juvenile.

### 5.7 Subscription

$23/month, billed separately from the device, with a 12-month minimum commitment. One subscription supports up to 15 users on the same membership (a generous family/household allowance).

The app is genuinely required for full device functionality. Without the subscription, the device is mostly inert, since the AI-driven resistance, workout library, mode switching, and tracking all live in the app.

## 6. Customization options

The Amp can be customized at multiple layers:

### 6.1 Workout-level customization

- Choose a pre-built workout from the library, filtered by duration, muscle group, trainer, or difficulty
- Use the AI generator to create a workout from a prompt
- Build a custom workout from scratch by selecting individual movements
- Modify any workout in real time (skip, replace, or add movements)

### 6.2 Movement-level customization

- Adjust resistance manually mid-set with the dial
- Override the AI's weight recommendation for any exercise
- Change resistance mode (Fixed/Band/Eccentric) for any individual movement
- Add Tempo or Drop Set game modes to any compatible movement

### 6.3 Program-level customization

- Follow a guided multi-week program from the trainer library
- Set fitness goals that the AI uses to bias programming (strength, hypertrophy, endurance, recovery, sport-specific)
- Indicate body areas to emphasize or avoid
- Sync with Apple Health to integrate with broader health data

### 6.4 User profile

Up to 15 user profiles per subscription, each with their own assessment baseline, history, preferences, and progress tracking. Useful for households where multiple people use the device.

## 8. The subscription decision

The $23/month is genuinely required. Without it, the AI doesn't run, the workout library is gone, the resistance modes don't auto-adjust, and you lose tracking. The device becomes a manual cable machine.

Annualized: $276/year. Over 5 years that's $1,380, comparable to the device cost. So the total cost of ownership for 5 years is roughly $3,200, which is still substantially less than Tonal ($3,995 device plus $59/month) and competitive with a midrange gym membership.

For comparison: a year of LA boutique strength classes typically runs $2,000 to $4,000. A solid commercial gym membership at $80/month is $960/year.

## 9. Strengths

- **Footprint.** Genuinely sleek. Wall-mounted, doesn't dominate a room, looks intentional rather than utilitarian.
- **Resistance modes.** The Eccentric mode in particular is hard to replicate with traditional weights and is genuinely valuable for tendon health and hypertrophy.
- **Auto-adjusting weight.** Removes the friction of "what should I lift today" and lets you focus on execution.
- **Movement variety.** 600+ movements across strength, mobility, Pilates, and HIIT covers the actual range of work most people need.
- **Family pricing.** 15 users on one subscription is generous.
- **Build quality.** Aluminum and steel construction holds up well; reviews are consistent on this.
- **The "low friction" effect.** Multiple reviewers note that the convenience of having it in the home, plus the AI removing decision fatigue, leads to higher consistency than a gym membership. Consistency is the lever that actually matters.

## 10. Limitations and tradeoffs

- **100 lb resistance ceiling.** For advanced lifters doing heavy squats, deadlifts, or rows, this is a real cap. The marketing claim that "100 lbs feels harder on a cable than 100 lbs on a stack" is partially true but doesn't fully close the gap. If your training requires sustained loads above 100 lbs in compound bilateral movements, the Amp won't replace a barbell.
- **Single arm only.** Unlike Tonal (two arms) or a traditional cable crossover (two columns), the Amp has one arm. Bilateral movements that require two simultaneous independent cables (cable flyes, cable crossovers, dual-arm rows with independent paths) are workarounds rather than native.
- **No screen.** Forces phone use. A non-issue for most people, but worth knowing.
- **Wall mount only.** Renters and frequent movers may not be able to use it. Cannot be moved without re-installing.
- **Subscription dependency.** Without the $23/month, the device is meaningfully diminished.
- **Floor space requirement.** 7x7 ft of clearance is real.
- **App platform maturity.** App is more mature on iOS than Android.
- **AI weight recommendations can be slightly off** in either direction, particularly early in your usage. Manual override is easy but worth knowing.
- **Arm slide can stick when new.** Loosens with use, per reviews.

## 11. Practical operating principles

For getting genuine value out of the device:

- **Complete the strength assessment seriously.** The starting calibration matters; rushing it leads to weeks of misweighted workouts.
- **Use Eccentric mode at least once a week** for any joint or tendon you want to protect. The tendon health research supports this beyond marketing claims.
- **Don't ignore the dial.** The AI's auto-adjustment is good but not perfect. Override when something feels wrong.
- **Use Band mode for movements where you want peak contraction strength** (top of a curl, lockout of a press). It's a different stimulus than Fixed mode.
- **Use Drop Sets sparingly.** They're a high-stress finishing technique, not a default mode. Once or twice a workout is the appropriate dose.
- **Schedule mobility sessions.** The mobility library is underused by most owners. Treat it as a legitimate workout category, not filler.
- **For runners and endurance athletes:** the eccentric mode plus single-leg ankle strap work (single-leg hamstring curls, single-leg hip thrusts, single-leg abduction) is particularly aligned with the running-specific strength research showing single-leg unilateral work and eccentric loading produce the largest carryover to running performance and injury prevention.
- **Schedule lifting at consistent times.** The AI gets smarter when you give it consistent data. Random scheduling produces noisier weight recommendations.

## 12. Where the Amp fits in the home gym landscape

Among connected wall-mounted strength systems:

- **Tonal:** Two arms, screen, $3,995 + $59/month. More serious lifter ceiling. Larger footprint and cost.
- **Tempo:** Uses real weights stored under a screen. $1,995 to $2,495. Larger footprint.
- **Speediance:** Multiple competitor wall-mounted cable systems. Generally similar concept, fewer software features.
- **Mirror (discontinued):** Lululemon shut it down in fall 2023.
- **Amp:** Single arm, no screen, $1,795 + $23/month. Smallest, cheapest in this category.

Among non-connected options:

- **Functional trainer (Rep Fitness Ares, Rogue, etc.):** $1,500 to $4,000. Two arms, real weight stacks, no software. Bigger footprint, no AI features, but unlimited weight ceiling.
- **Cable column with stack:** $500 to $1,500. Manual, limited.

The Amp's positioning is the entry point into smart strength training: cheaper than Tonal, more compact than functional trainers, and the resistance modes (especially Eccentric) provide a genuine training stimulus that physical weights cannot replicate.
