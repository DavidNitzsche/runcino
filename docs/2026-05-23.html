01 · Design thesis 01 / 11

Two faces. One athlete.

Every screen is built for one of two reasons. Coach's brief — short, voiced, opinionated, says what and why. Runner's dashboard — numbers, bands, charts, says how and where you are. We were mixing the two together and the result was a wall of cards that read like a research paper. This deck pulls them apart.

The brief

The coach speaking. Sentences, not stats. Always cites a real number but never makes you read a table to find it. Tells you the call before it tells you the data.

  • Voice paragraph (the read)
  • Focus — the one thing today is about
  • Back off if — the failure condition
  • Signals — 1–3 plain-English lines
The dashboard

The instrument panel. Glanceable. No prose. Every number is a number you might check during the run or while planning the week, not while deciding whether to run.

  • Today card — title · stats · buttons
  • Week strip — 7 days at a glance
  • Trajectory — projected race time vs goal
  • Runs feed — last 7, one line each
The rule we keep breaking

Never explain in the dashboard what the brief already said. The today card shouldn't ship with a "Pace guidance" paragraph; the brief already told you to keep it conversational. The week strip shouldn't ship with phase descriptions; the brief named the phase. Display redundancy is the noise we're cutting.

02 · The Coach brief 02 / 11

Lift the iPhone Coach tab to the web.

Today the web's coach voice is fragmented — a narrative line, a watching strip, a coach-read card, an adapted island. Same content, four containers. The iPhone Coach screen already combined them into a single tight stack: brief · focus · back-off · signals. That's the unit. Promote it to a first-class web slot.

Coach · Sat May 23 · Build week 3 of 18

Good morning, Alex. Mileage is up 14% over the last month and you've kept 82% of it easy — that's exactly how fitness compounds without breaking you. Today is the long one: 14 miles, conversational. If you can't talk in full sentences, ease off — the point of this run is the time on feet, not the pace. 127 days to Berlin.

Focus

Last hour of the long run is where the adaptation happens. Don't get it from negative-splitting — get it from finishing the same pace you started.

Back off if

Heart rate drifts past 168 in the back half — that's threshold creep on an easy day. Walk a minute and reset, or cut the run short at 10 miles. Saturday's long run isn't worth a Tuesday spent recovering.

Signals
On track

34 miles banked last week at 84% easy. That's the highest easy share you've held three weeks running. Don't touch the formula.

Watching

Sleep is 6h 12m average over five nights — about 50 minutes short. Not a stop sign, but it's why today's brief skews conservative on the back half.

On track

Resting HR holding at 48 — same as last month even through the load ramp. The aerobic base is taking the work.

Note · the brief never names ACWR, HRR, or VDOT. Numbers stay; jargon goes. "Mileage up 14%" beats "ACWR 1.14". "Heart rate past 168" beats "Z4". This is the doctrine from the May 22 polish pass — applied to every voice surface.

What the iPhone already does well

One screen end-to-end. Coach speaks first, today follows, week strip anchors. The brief, focus, back-off, signals pattern reads in 20 seconds. No tabs, no scroll discovery problem.

Where the web is behind

iPhone today CoachView · single stack · brief + focus + back-off + signals
Web today CoachNarrativeLine + CoachWatchingStrip + CoachRead + CoachAdaptedIsland — four containers, same content
iPhone today Today hero · title + stats + 2 buttons
Web today Hero card + greet band + check-in card + coach narrative + watching strip — six surfaces above the fold
iPhone today One race trajectory pill ("On track · Berlin · 127d")
Web today Trajectory lives on a different tab; the home tab has no race awareness

The unlock

Both clients render from the same /api/overview payload. Shipping the brief block on the web is a UI-only change — no engine work, no new endpoint, no new copy. The text already exists.

10 · Nav redesign 10 / 11

Six tabs → four.

The web tab bar is Overview · Training · Races · Health · Log · Profile. Overview and Training are 80% the same thing rendered twice. Health is a research dashboard, not a daily destination. Log + Profile collapse.

If you do one thing from this deck

Ship the Brief block on the web home tab. Same payload, same words, same order as the iPhone. It's a single React component over the existing /api/overview data. Three other surfaces shrink the moment it lands.

If you do two things

Add the trajectory pill above the fold on the home tab — one chart, one verdict, one sentence. Berlin is winnable shouldn't live three taps away. It's the line that makes the whole engine matter.
