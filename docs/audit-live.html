The event-driven stress tests found four more engine bugs — and they're the kind that matter most.

These are exactly the moments where the coach is supposed to act like a coach, and right now in each one it doesn't. The agent that ran the ten scenarios caught all four cleanly, root-caused each one, and flagged them for the next engine-fix pass (the engine files are locked by another agent's commits in flight right now, so the fixes get queued, not jammed):

1. After a bad week of runs, the coach pushes you harder, not softer.
The engine plans next week's volume from your four-week average and ignores the fact that this past week cratered. So a runner who skipped three runs gets a "catch-up" week at 1.15–1.35× their normal volume. That's the opposite of what doctrine says. Recalibrate from where the runner actually is, not where the plan thought they'd be.

2. The "rebuild after a break" softening only fires for low-volume runners.
A 50-mile-per-week runner who takes 5 days off carries the rebuild flag — but the actual softening path is gated on weekly volume being under 8 miles. Net effect: high-fitness runners come back from a break and get full build-block quality immediately. The softening logic should fire regardless of base volume; it should just ramp from the actual last-7-days volume.

3. B-races inside an A-race window get threshold work scheduled on top of them.
The engine only branches on the next A-race; it doesn't look at the other races on the calendar. So if you've got a tune-up race 10 days before your goal race, the coach might schedule a VO2 session for that day. The B-race day itself should be a race, and the surrounding ±1–2 days should be easy.

4. The daily plan doesn't read your check-ins. Only the after-the-fact adjustment does.
This is the big one. The function that picks today's workout (pickRun) doesn't read your check-in signal. Only the adjustment layer downstream does. So an ill or exhausted runner gets a threshold session in the underlying plan, and only gets softened by the adjustment on top. The check-in signal needs to flow into the prescription path itself — that way "I slept four hours and I'm sore everywhere" actually changes what the coach prescribes, not just modifies it after the fact.

Also flagged: four signals the engine should have but doesn't.

  • A real illness flag on state — today the only proxy is the qualitative check-in count. A boolean would let the engine cut quality cleanly.
  • An env-disruption flag for heat or travel — currently approximated through poor check-ins + volume crater. A first-class field would be cleaner.
  • A first-distance caveat on race predictions — when you're racing a distance you've never raced, the prediction should widen its confidence interval and say so out loud.
  • A VDOT-upgrade dampener — one fast B-race shouldn't slingshot your VDOT from 49 to 54. Daniels caps trained-runner upgrades at ~1.0 per cycle.

All four bugs are queued as the next wave. They'll ship right after the agent currently fixing the earlier set of engine bugs lands and unlocks the file.
