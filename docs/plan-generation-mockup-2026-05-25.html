2 · THE THREE DECISIONS I NEED YOU FOR

DECISION A · VOLUME CEILING

v1 ramps 7% per non-deload week and tops out at ~40 mpw. For a sub-3 marathon target that's nowhere near the canonical 65-90 mpw peak. Two options:

A1 · RESPECT THE BASE (current)

Start where you are (34 mpw), ramp gently. Never push past what your body has earned in the last 4 weeks. Honest, conservative. Possibly leaves performance on the table for an experienced runner.

Result: Peak ~40 mpw. Risk of running the race undertrained.

A2 · TARGET-DRIVEN RAMP (recommended)

Set a peak target based on race distance + experience tier (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced / Advanced+). Ramp aggressively if base is far below — but cap individual week jumps at 10% per the 10%-rule.

Result: Sub-3 marathoner peaks ~70-85 mpw. Higher injury risk if base is far below. Need to honestly assess: "if I'm at 34 now and need 70 in 18 weeks, that's a 5% sustained ramp + every 4th week cutback — doable but demanding."

DECISION B · ADAPTATION TRIGGERS

When the plan diverges from reality (missed run, killer hard effort, RHR spike, sleep crater), what should happen?

B1 · FLAG ONLY (manual rewrite)

Coach surfaces the divergence in voice ("you missed Tuesday — let's swap workouts manually"). You hit the regenerate button when you want to.

Pro: You stay in control. Con: A missed mid-week + a Sunday long that's already locked in can pile.

B2 · AUTO-REWRITE NEXT 7 DAYS (recommended)

When a key workout is missed or executed off-target, the next 7 days re-shape automatically — adding back the missed work where the load allows, OR scaling down the upcoming long if it's now too aggressive. Coach narrates what changed and why, cited to research. You can override.

Pro: Plan stays a real plan, not a wish list. Con: If the adapter is wrong, you fight it.

DECISION C · QUALITY PRESCRIPTIONS

How specific should "Threshold day" instructions be?

C1 · COACH PROSE

"Today is threshold — controlled hard, just below the line where you'd start to gasp. 3-4 miles of work at a pace you could hold for an hour. Trust the warmup, build into the reps, hold form."

Pro: Adapts to feel. Con: Vague paces for runners who want specifics.

C2 · DANIELS-STYLE STRUCTURED (recommended)

"Today: WU 1.5mi → 4×1mi @ 6:38/mi · 90s float → CD 1mi. T-pace from your VDOT-50 estimate, dropping each rep ±2s from target = the read on your engine today."

Pro: Targetable, watch-loadable. Con: Requires accurate VDOT or LTHR (now we have both via the new zone work).

C3 · BOTH (best of both)

Display structured workout for the watch. Lead with one sentence of coach prose explaining the WHY ("we're at threshold today because last week's intervals showed your engine is ready for that next pace step — see how you respond").

This is what I'd actually build if you say yes to all three.

3 · VOICE INTEGRATION ON WEEKLY RATIONALE

Structural plan is doctrine (deterministic, cited). But the WHY of any given week needs voice. Mockup of what a weekly briefing on /training would look like:

COACH · WEEK 14 OF 28 · QUALITY BLOCK

You're 14 weeks out from CIM and the engine is starting to show. Two weeks ago you held threshold pace through the third rep — that's the cue we needed to step the intervals up. This week is the first time we're touching 42 miles, which is 10% over last week. Bigger than I'd usually push, but the recent run-checkins suggest you're absorbing the work without piling fatigue.

Tuesday is 6×800m at I-pace · 3:25 each. Don't drift faster on rep 1 — even splits or get told. Thursday's tempo is the marker rep: a clean 5 miles at marathon pace is what you'll do on race day, just for two more hours. Saturday's long stays at 14 miles — we're letting your legs catch up before next week's bump.
