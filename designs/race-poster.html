One of the most scenic road marathons on the continent — a point-to-point course carved into the Pacific edge, from redwoods at Big Sur Station to the coastal village of Carmel-by-the-Sea.

The course is five sectors. It opens under the redwood canopy, unfolds into rolling coastal approach, steepens into the Hurricane Point climb at mile 10.6 — a two-mile, 560-foot ascent into a prevailing headwind — then releases into a long descent past Bixby Bridge and the cruise miles to Carmel Highlands.

First run in 1986. Historic records: 2:16:39 men (2019) · 2:38:36 women (1993). Typical field: 4,500 runners. Cutoff: 6 hours. Weather window this year: 52°F · clear · 3 mph SSE.

Race Strategy
Five phases, named.
Phase 01
Ease in
mi 0 – 4
8:35/mi
Redwood corridor, gently rolling. Relaxed shoulders, long stride, no heroics. The crowd will lure you under 8:20 — don't go. Buy the climb ahead.
Phase 02
Coast climb
mi 4 – 10
9:05/mi
Ocean opens up on the left. Rolling approach to Hurricane. Effort over pace; the gradient builds. Gel 2 at mile 8, caffeine for what comes next.
Phase 03
Hurricane
mi 10 – 12
10:17/mi
Two miles of real climb into a headwind. Zone 4 ceiling — walk thirty seconds if HR spikes past 172. Drums at the top. Recover on the descent to Bixby.
Phase 04
Cruise miles
mi 12 – 24
8:15/mi
Rolling with net descent. This is the race. Lock cadence at 178; run by the ocean on your left. Do not hammer — every hammered mile here costs two at 23.
Phase 05
Finish strong
mi 24 – 26.2
8:00/mi
One bump at 22, take it. From the crest, turn the engine up. Final mile flat, Carmel from here. Sarah at Rio & Carmelo. Empty the tank.

What this mockup is arguing

The poster, not the dashboard
Treat the hero like a travel print. Beaded route (your Northing ref), red landmark pins with italic serif labels, plate-mark frame, grain, brand marks at the corners. It's a keepsake, not an infographic — the thing you'd want framed after the race. The data still lives on the page, but the hero earns its size.
Beads over a line
A single stroke says "route." A string of dots says "steps taken" — one bead per half-mile. You can feel the distance accumulate. Size the beads slightly unevenly and the route reads handmade, not machined.
Typography does the work
Fraunces for display (the Sur italic is the move), Cormorant Garamond italic for landmark labels and quieter voice, JetBrains Mono for data, Inter for body. No decorative UI chrome — the type is the design language. Paper color is warm cream on near-black, not pure white.
Phases and gels return
The sections you already liked — Five phases, named and the gel timeline — are kept. They extend the poster voice into the working sections of the page. Phase names get italic accents ("Coast climb", "Ease in"). Feels like a unified object.
What's next if this lands
The Adidas-style clean basemap version becomes the other view — one toggle: Poster ↔ Live map. Race morning on your phone = live map. Planning at your desk / sharing with a coach = poster. Same data, two registers.
