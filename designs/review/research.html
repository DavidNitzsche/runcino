§ 02 · The job

Three users, three moments.

The same runner opens this page at three very different times. What they need is not the same. Design for the worst of the three, not the average.
T−4 weeks · desk

The planner.

"Do I have the fitness for 3:45? If not, what's the realistic goal?"
  • Compare goal pace to projected pace across the course
  • Identify which miles will hurt disproportionately
  • Commit a fueling / pacing strategy to memory
T−1 week · rehearsal

The visualizer.

"What does mile 11 look like? What am I doing when I get to Bixby?"
  • Build a mental map of named landmarks in order
  • Internalize where the gels go and why
  • Rehearse the narrative: "climb → crest → cruise → wall → finish"
Race morning · phone

The reciter.

"What am I supposed to do in the first five miles so I don't blow up?"
  • Re-read the core rule in 15 seconds at the start line
  • Confirm the fuel plan one more time
  • Screenshot the phase strategy for the lock screen
§ 03 · Why the current design fails

Specifically, what's broken.

Not vague — enumerated. Each failure below ties to a specific design pattern we'll either fix or borrow from a reference.
fail 01
Flat tables don't pace Big Sur.

A 26-row table showing 8:34/mi for every mile is a lie. Mile 11 is not 8:34. On the actual terrain, the same effort produces 9:45 at the peak and 8:05 on the Bixby descent. Fix: grade-adjusted pace, visible per-mile, colored by effort.

fail 02
Unnamed peaks mean nothing.

A squiggly line from 0 to 568 ft with "mile 10.6" as the label is a data point. "Hurricane Point · 520 ft over 2 mi · Cat 2 climb" is a narrative. Fix: categorize and name every climb, FT-style.

fail 03
Generic basemaps bury the story.

Mapbox default grey makes a 26-mile coastal marathon look like a suburban 10K. Big Sur's identity is the Pacific on one side and a ridgeline on the other. Fix: either no basemap (poster) or a warm hillshade that foregrounds terrain.

fail 04
No hierarchy between map and elevation.

Current treatment gives equal weight to the route and the profile. But they answer different questions. Fix: one is the hero (map-as-portrait), the other is the functional overlay (profile-with-annotations).

§ 04 · Reference library

Eight sources, one pattern extracted from each.

Each card below: the source (click through to verify), what they do, the specific pattern I'm borrowing, a sketch of that pattern applied to Big Sur, and the failure mode if we copy it badly.
R01 · poster discipline

Muir Way relief maps.

muir-way.com ↗
Muir Way sells archival-grade topographic posters — two-color palette per piece, off-white or dark canvas, the geography centered as the hero, a single stat strip at the foot. A poster reads from across a room because it commits to one shape and one contrast pair. No basemap clutter; the terrain is the subject.
Pattern extracted
  • Two-color palette discipline. One canvas color, one ink, one accent.
  • Subject-as-silhouette. The route/coastline IS the artwork. No basemap noise behind it.
  • Stat strip at the foot with tabular numerals and tiny caps labels — "distance / gain / goal" as typographic rhythm, not a UI.
Failure mode if copied badlyPoster aesthetics are for looking, not operating. If we go full poster, the runner can't actually use it on race morning. Reserve this for the hero block; keep functional data elsewhere.
Sketch · what it becomes for Big Sur
Ink + terracotta, route as silhouette, one peak labeled, stat strip at foot. Two colors carry the whole composition.
R02 · climb categorization

FT Tour de France stage profiles.

ig.ft.com/tour-de-france ↗
The Financial Times' cycling graphics team treats each mountain as a character with a name and a rank. Every climb gets a category badge (HC, Cat 1–4), length, average gradient, and summit elevation inline with the profile. The elevation curve is filled in the FT's signature salmon pink; climbs rise out of it as labeled triangles with attached data.
Pattern extracted
  • Every climb gets a badge category — HC / Cat 1–4 — treating a marathon like a cycling race.
  • Each badge carries length · gradient · summit elevation right at the peak.
  • The profile is the hero chart, not an afterthought chart below the map.
Failure mode if copied badlyMarathons don't use HC/Cat 1 terminology. We have to invent plausible categories ("HC = race-defining", "Cat 3 = recoverable bump"). If we're lazy with the taxonomy, it reads as affectation. Calibrate categories to marathon intuition, not cycling grades.
Sketch · climb-as-character on Big Sur
Salmon canvas (FT signature), one HC climb with summit stats inline, two lower cats annotated. Elevation is the hero chart.
R03 · cartographic restraint

Swisstopo hillshade.

map.geo.admin.ch ↗
The Swiss federal mapping agency has the most refined cartographic style in the world. Hand-shaded relief in warm sepia and ochre, brown contour lines at 20m intervals, italic serif labels curved along ridgelines and valleys, water in pale cyan. Their maps feel analog — painted, not rendered. Labels respect hierarchy: major peaks in larger italic, minor features in smaller upright.
Pattern extracted
  • Warm sepia hillshade (not grey Mapbox) as the basemap. Terrain reads warm.
  • Curved italic serif place labels along natural features — "Pacific Ocean" arcs along the coast, not a pin.
  • Muted, restrained palette: 4 hues total (paper / ink / water / accent).
Failure mode if copied badlyWe can't match Swisstopo's hand-drawn craft at runtime. A procedural hillshade from radial gradients will always feel fake. Either commit to a real DEM-based hillshade tile service (Stamen Terrain, Mapbox Outdoors styled warm) or embrace a stylized interpretation that doesn't pretend to be the real thing.
Sketch · warm-paper Big Sur, curved labels
Warm paper, ochre hillshade, italic serif "Pacific Ocean" arcing along the coast, dotted red trail overlay — Nat Geo/Swisstopo vocabulary.
R04 · annotated editorial

NYT marathon interactives.

nytimes.com · NYC marathon graphics ↗
The NYT Graphics team treats a race course as a story with mile-by-mile annotations. Off-white canvas (#F7F6F2 — not pure white), Cheltenham serif annotations with thin leader lines pointing to specific points ("Mile 16: Queensboro Bridge, +80 ft"). Each annotation earns its place by naming something specific that happens there — not "mile marker" but "the 5th Ave hill, where most runners bonk."
Pattern extracted
  • Warm off-white canvas. Pure white is clinical; #F7F6F2 is editorial.
  • Inline annotations with leader lines — each one names a specific event at that mile, not just the mile number.
  • Serif body copy for prose; tabular mono only for stats.
Failure mode if copied badlyNYT's annotations feel earned because each sentence is newsworthy. If we pad annotations with generic copy ("mile 8: keep running"), the device collapses into template. Every annotation must reveal something the reader didn't know.
Sketch · every climb earns an annotation
Off-white, salmon profile fill, four hand-placed annotations — each one revealing something specific about what happens there.
R05 · big-number tiles

Strava stat cards.

strava.com ↗
Strava's entire UI runs on oversized tabular numerals paired with tiny caps labels, on a near-black or pure-white card. Orange (#FC4C02) is the single accent — never more than one color in the decoration. The route is rendered as a 2px orange line on a desaturated grey Mapbox base, which makes it the only bright thing on the screen. Year-in-Sport extends this with editorial scale jumps: 48pt headline sitting directly next to 10pt caption.
Pattern extracted
  • Big number / tiny label stat tile — the core Strava idiom.
  • Desaturate the basemap so the route is the only bright thing.
  • One warm accent color; everything else is monochrome.
Failure mode if copied badlyThe orange accent only works because Strava is relentlessly disciplined about using nothing else. The moment we add a second accent (say, green for descents), the whole system degrades to generic SaaS. Pick one warm, hold the line.
Sketch · stat tile + dark map
Four-up stat bar with mono caps + display serif numerals. Dark desaturated map with orange-only route. Ruthless color discipline.
R06 · linked map + elevation

Komoot tour detail.

komoot.com ↗
Komoot's tour page is the most functional route-detail UI on the web. Three columns: stat stack / map / highlights list. The elevation profile below the map is linked to it — hover a mile on the profile, a pin drops on the map. Surface composition shown as a thin horizontal stacked bar ("62% paved, 28% gravel, 10% trail"). Warm greige background, forest green as the accent, orange route line.
Pattern extracted
  • Map ↔ elevation are linked. Scrubbing the profile moves a pin on the map.
  • Surface/grade composition as a thin horizontal stacked bar — a single glanceable summary.
  • Highlight list to the side, keyed to miles, with short notes. Not a table — a narrative timeline.
Failure mode if copied badlyThe scrub interaction is lovely on desktop and irrelevant on a phone. If the linking is the primary affordance, mobile degrades. Design the static composition to work without the interaction, then layer hover as enhancement.
Sketch · map + linked profile + timeline
Map + profile vertically linked (dashed sync line). Surface composition bar. Highlight timeline to the right, keyed to miles.
R07 · editorial hero

Outside Magazine features.

outsideonline.com/running ↗
Outside's long-form features open with a full-bleed photograph of landscape + figure, with a dramatic serif headline overlaid bottom-left. No logo clutter, no nav competing with the hero. A four-cell stat bar runs directly below — distance / vert / record / date — with hairline dividers as the transition into body copy. Pull quotes are set in oversized italic serif, treated like graphic elements rather than text.
Pattern extracted
  • Photo-first hero with overlaid serif headline — the image does the emotional work.
  • Four-cell stat bar as the structural transition between hero and body.
  • Oversized italic pull quotes treated as visual beats, not text.
Failure mode if copied badlyThe hero requires a real photograph. A procedural SVG gradient "sky" doesn't land the same way. Either we source a licensed Big Sur photo, or we commit to illustration (not photorealism) as the hero idiom.
Sketch · photo hero + stat bar transition
Dark cinematic hero with overlaid display serif, amber italic accent. Clean four-cell stat bar as transition. Pull quote sets the opening.
R08 · 3D flyover (use sparingly)

Relive flyovers.

relive.cc ↗
Relive renders post-run 3D flyovers — satellite terrain tilted obliquely, the route drawn as a bright magenta-to-red polyline with soft bloom, photos popping up along the track as rounded thumbnails. The camera banks into turns and pulls up for summits. Pacing is staccato — 3–5 seconds between "moments." The chrome is minimal: distance and elevation readout in a bottom HUD.
Pattern extracted
  • Oblique 3D terrain (not top-down) — preserves the physicality of a climb.
  • Glowing route as the only bright element on a dark terrain — pure luminance.
  • Moments, not continuous scroll — animate between 3–5 narrative beats (start, climb, peak, descent, finish).
Failure mode if copied badlyRelive works because it's an animation with audio. Static screenshots of Relive-style 3D terrain look like a cheap mobile game. If we want flyover energy, it must animate. Otherwise we're better off with a 2D poster.
Sketch · oblique 3D terrain moment
Oblique terrain (not top-down), stars, glowing pink-to-cream route ribbon, runner dot at peak. Works as a 3-beat animation, not a static image.
