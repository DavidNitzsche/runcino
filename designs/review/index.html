The profile.

Three climbs worth naming, one of them enormous. The first ten miles lie to you; the second six break you; the last ten give it back.
HC · mile 10.6
Hurricane Point
+520 ft · 2.0 mi · 4.9% avg
The only sustained climb. Sea wind direct on the left shoulder. Back off to 9:40 pace; recover on the descent to Bixby.
Cat 3 · mile 16
Carmel Highlands
+180 ft · 1.4 mi · 2.5% avg
The quiet killer. Legs already taxed; drive with cadence, not effort. Short and recoverable if you stay patient.
Cat 4 · mile 22
The Wall
+90 ft · 0.6 mi · 2.9% avg
The last real bump. Hurts because of where it sits. Count to 200; crest it; the finish is downhill from here.
Strategy

Run the arc.

The Big Sur course is not a pace problem. It is a patience problem. The first ten miles drop gently to the mouth of the canyon; run them slow. You are not banking time. You are buying the next hour.

Hurricane Point is two miles of work at marathon effort — which, on a climb, means dialing back ten to twenty seconds per mile and holding form. Crest it, breathe, and descend cleanly into Bixby. The middle of the race belongs to the Pacific and the cadence.

Miles twenty through twenty-four are where Big Sur decides its story. If you ran the opening right, this is cruise. If not, this is survival. Either way, there is one more bump at twenty-two. Take it.

Profile in cross-section.

The coast road climbs once, dramatically, between miles nine and eleven, then unwinds in long diminishing ridges toward Carmel. Vertical exaggeration 8×.
§ 01 · mile 10.6
Hurricane Point
568 ft · climb +520 ft over 2 mi
Named for the wind, which arrives on the northwest face without warning and does not soften until the road pitches down toward Bixby. The course's only sustained climb, and its defining event.
§ 02 · mile 11.8
Bixby Creek Bridge
260 ft · arch span 320 ft · 1932
The descent from Hurricane Point delivers runners onto the open deck of Bixby Bridge, where a grand piano is played at the course's halfway point — a Big Sur tradition since 1987.

The Wall
90 ft · climb +90 ft over 0.6 mi
A minor headland on any other day. At mile twenty-two it becomes the pivot of the race. Runners who paced the opening will cross it; those who did not will walk it.

Big Sur is a race about one climb. Everything that happens before Hurricane Point is preparation; everything after is consequence. The course rewards runners who treat the first ten miles as warm-up rather than opportunity — and it punishes, slowly and elegantly, anyone who mistakes the first descent for the race.

§ Race strategy

Five phases, named.

Phase 01
Ease in
mi 0 — 5.8

Canyon drop and the first two bridges. Relaxed shoulders, long stride, no heroics. Buy the climb ahead.
Phase 02
Climb effort
mi 5.8 — 10.6
9:20 /mi
Rolling approach to Hurricane. Gradient builds. Keep cadence; accept the slower split.
Phase 03
Hurricane
mi 10.6 — 12.0
9:45 /mi
Two miles of real climb into wind. Zone 4 ceiling. Summit, recover on the descent to Bixby.
Phase 04
Cruise
mi 12.0 — 21.0
8:30 /mi
Rolling with net descent. This is the race. Lock cadence at 178; run by the ocean on your left.
Phase 05
Finish strong
mi 21.0 — 26.2
8:20 /mi
One bump at 22. Take it. From the crest, turn the engine up. Final mile flat. Carmel from here.
