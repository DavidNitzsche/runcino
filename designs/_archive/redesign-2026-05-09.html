Redesign · May 9 2026 · ground up

Less data console.
More knowing coach.

The previous deck took the same dashboard and put three skins on it. That's the wrong move. The first question isn't "how should this look" — it's "what is each tab actually for, when do I open it, what do I need in three seconds."

A runner doesn't open the app to see twelve KPI tiles. They open it to know what to run today, whether their body is ready, where they are in the build, and when the race is. The redesign treats every tab as a question the runner showed up to ask — and answers it in plain language first, charts second.

01
Each tab answers one question.

Not "shows you a domain" — answers. Today: what do I run? Training: where is the plan going? Health: how is my body? Races: what's coming up? Log: what have I done? Profile: who am I to the engine? If a tab needs a tutorial, the question is wrong.

02
Plain language headlines. Numbers as evidence.

"Your body is rebuilding" before "RECOVERY 88." A coach speaks in sentences and reaches for the chart when it sharpens the point. Numbers serve the headline, not replace it. JetBrains Mono is for the stuff that actually wants precision (paces, distances, splits) — not labels.

03
Time-aware. Restraint over density.

Morning shows today's session. Afternoon shifts to "how was today." Evening surfaces tomorrow. The dashboard isn't a billboard of every metric we have — it's a coach who knows what matters right now. A blank space is information: it means nothing else needs your attention.

Six tabs. Six questions. Six answers.

The engine and the doctrine don't change. The validator stays. The audit-trail stays. What changes is the surface the runner reads — from "every number we have" to "the right number, in the right voice, at the right moment." The data console becomes the coaching voice. Everything else is editing.
