What this mockup is arguing

Core premise
A race isn't a map and it isn't a stats card. It's a sequence of decisions compressed into three-and-a-half hours. Every element on this page is pinned to a mile marker, because that's how you'll actually experience the race.
What the timeline shows
Six horizontal layers, all sharing one mile axis. Top-down: phase bands (what character of the race), action pins (gel, aid, walk zone, kick), target pace (what your watch should read), elevation ribbon (the tattoo — what the terrain looks like), mile axis (anchor), grade + HR cap (what your body will feel). Scrub left to right and you've rehearsed the race.
What it kills
The geographic map is gone. A point-to-point marathon doesn't need lat/lon — it needs narrative. The stats card is folded into the identity strip. The mile splits table is collapsed (it can live below, on demand). The elevation no longer sits in its own section — it's the spine of the timeline.
The three-things dock
Below the timeline, three cards summarize the whole race into decisions you'll actually remember on the start line. Everything else is research. These three are the race. If the timeline is the rehearsal, these are the mantras.
What's missing
Weather hourly, logistics, gear, full mile splits, fueling detail — all still needed, but below the fold. Their job is reference, not headline. Hover interactions (a vertical scrub-line that snaps to a mile, showing all six layers at that exact point) are the obvious next step — but the static layout has to work first.
