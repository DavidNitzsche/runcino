# 00B — Recovery and Rest Protocols (Generic)

A reference document on recovery for runners across all distances, training loads, ages, and experience levels. Recovery is the period during which training adaptations occur — not the absence of training. Insufficient recovery converts training into damage; sufficient recovery converts training into fitness.

## Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Recovery** | The physiological return to or beyond pre-stress state across muscular, neural, hormonal, and immune systems. |
| **Rest** | Complete absence of training stress (zero running, no cross-training). |
| **Active recovery** | Very low-intensity movement (walking, easy spinning, recovery-pace jogging) intended to maintain blood flow without adding training stress. |
| **DOMS** | Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness — peaks 24–72 h post-effort, driven by eccentric muscle damage. |
| **EIMD** | Exercise-Induced Muscle Damage — the underlying microtrauma that produces DOMS, elevated CK/LDH, and impaired force production. |
| **CK** | Creatine kinase — muscle-damage biomarker; peaks ~24 h post-marathon, normalizes ~144 h. |
| **HRV** | Heart Rate Variability — variation in beat-to-beat intervals; reflects autonomic balance; common metric is RMSSD. |
| **RHR** | Resting Heart Rate — beats/min measured at full rest, ideally on waking. |
| **Cutback week** | A planned week of reduced volume (typically every 3–4 weeks) inserted into a training block. |
| **Reverse taper** | Post-race progressive return to volume and intensity, mirroring a pre-race taper in reverse. |
| **A race / B race / C race** | Priority labels: A = peak goal race, full taper and recovery; B = secondary race, partial taper; C = training race or hard workout substitute. |
| **mpw** | Miles per week. |
| **Carbon-plated shoe / "super shoe"** | Racing shoe with embedded carbon-fiber plate and high-rebound foam (e.g., PEBA midsole). |

## The Three Categories of Recovery

Recovery operates on three timescales. Each requires different tactics. Confusing them produces either chronic underrecovery or excessive detraining.

| Category | Scale | Trigger | Goal | Primary tools |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **In-week recovery** | Hours to days | Each hard session | Restore for the next hard session | Sleep, nutrition, easy runs, rest days |
| **Cutback weeks** | Every 3–4 weeks | Cumulative training stress | Consolidate adaptation, reduce injury/illness risk | 20–40% volume reduction, intensity preserved or reduced |
| **Post-race recovery** | Weeks | Race-day damage | Repair tissue, restore CNS, reverse-taper to next block | Rest days, reverse periodization, no structured workouts |

```
Time scale of recovery:
[hours]----[days]----[weeks]----[months]
   ^         ^         ^           ^
in-week    cutback   post-race   season-end
                                  break
```

A complete training program nests all three. Skipping any tier compounds debt.

## In-Week Recovery

### Hard/Easy Alternation

The single most important in-week principle: never stack two hard days back-to-back unless the plan explicitly calls for a "stress block" followed by extended recovery. Default rule:

```
hard day → 1–2 easy/recovery/rest days → next hard day
```

| Stimulus on day N | Minimum gap before next hard day |
|---|---|
| Threshold/tempo (≤30 min at LT) | 1 day easy |
| VO2max intervals | 2 days easy |
| Long run with marathon-pace work | 2 days easy |
| Long run, easy pace (≥150% normal long run) | 1–2 days easy |
| Race or race-effort time trial | See post-race table |

### Recovery Run vs. Easy Run

These are distinct sessions. Mislabeling them produces fatigue accumulation.

| Attribute | Recovery run | Easy run |
|---|---|---|
| Effort (RPE 1–10) | 2–3 | 3–4 |
| Heart rate | ≤60% HRmax | 60–70% HRmax |
| Pace vs. marathon pace | 60–90 s/mi slower than easy pace; 10–20% slower than MP | 5–10% slower than MP |
| Duration | 20–45 min (cap to avoid glycogen depletion) | 45–90 min |
| Training stimulus | Minimal — purpose is circulation, not adaptation | Aerobic base, capillarization, mitochondrial density |
| When to use | Day after hard session, double days | Most non-hard days |

A recovery run does not make recovery faster than rest; it maintains coordination, blood flow, and routine without adding stress. If true recovery is in doubt, choose rest.

### Sleep — The Highest-ROI Recovery Tool

Sleep is the single most evidence-supported recovery modality. Effects are larger than any supplement or device.

| Sleep duration / night | Effect on running performance |
|---|---|
| <6 h | Significant decrement: increased perceived exertion, reduced time to exhaustion, elevated cortisol, suppressed glycogen synthesis, increased injury risk |
| 6–7 h | Below athlete optimum; cumulative deficit over weeks |
| 7–8 h | General-population minimum; adequate for low-load training |
| 8–9 h | Recommended baseline for training athletes |
| 9–10 h | Optimal for high-load or competitive training; supported by sleep-extension trials |

Mechanism summary: insufficient sleep elevates cortisol, suppresses testosterone and growth hormone, blunts muscle protein synthesis, impairs glycogen storage, and reduces immune function. Reaction time and decision-making degrade before subjective fatigue appears.

### Sleep Extension and Sleep Banking

| Concept | Protocol | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep extension | Add 60–120 min/night for 5–7+ nights | Stanford basketball cohort: +9% free-throw accuracy, faster sprint times after extending to 10 h. Tennis: improved serve accuracy after +2 h. Reaction time consistently improves. |
| Sleep banking | Add 60–90 min/night for several nights *before* an anticipated short-sleep period (e.g., race travel, redeye flights, race morning) | Originated in military/shift-worker research. Reduces performance decrement after one night of restricted sleep. |
| Naps | 20–90 min, ideally after lunch, completed ≥6 h before bedtime | Restore performance after partial sleep restriction. Short naps (≤30 min) avoid grogginess; longer naps include slow-wave sleep and aid muscle repair. |

Practical sleep hygiene rules:

- Fixed wake time, even on rest days.
- Cool, dark, quiet bedroom; ~18°C optimum.
- No alcohol within 3 h of sleep — alcohol fragments REM and reduces growth-hormone release.
- Caffeine half-life ~5 h — last dose ≥8 h before bedtime for most.
- Late-evening high-intensity training and bright light exposure delay sleep onset.

### Nutrition for In-Week Recovery

The 0–4 h post-session window contains the largest glycogen-resynthesis driver. The full recovery window extends to 24 h.

| Window | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0–30 min post-session | 1.0–1.2 g/kg carbohydrate + 20–30 g protein | Highest glycogen synthesis rate; most relevant if next hard session is <24 h away |
| 0–4 h post-session | 0.8–1.2 g/kg/h carbohydrate continued | Drops in priority if next session is >24 h |
| 24-h post-session | 5–10 g/kg/day carbohydrate (training load–scaled); 1.4–2.0 g/kg/day protein | Standard endurance daily intake |
| Hydration | Replace 1.25–1.5× sweat losses with sodium-containing fluids | Weighing pre/post is the simplest measure |

When the next session is >24 h away and total daily intake is adequate, exact post-run timing matters less. When sessions are <24 h apart (doubles, race weekends, multi-stage events), early intake is critical.

### Recovery Modalities — Ranked by Evidence

Effect-size ordering from systematic reviews and meta-analyses on muscle damage, soreness, and performance return:

| Tier | Modality | Evidence summary |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Strong** | Sleep ≥8 h/night | Largest effect on recovery and performance of any modality. |
| **A — Strong** | Adequate carbohydrate + protein | Causal for glycogen and muscle protein synthesis. |
| **A — Strong** | Easy/rest days between hard sessions | Foundational; supported by all training literature. |
| **B — Moderate** | Massage (manual or percussive) | Most effective single modality for DOMS in meta-analysis; effect peaks ~48 h. Limited effect on performance metrics. |
| **B — Moderate** | Compression garments | Small-to-moderate effect on perceived soreness and strength recovery 2–96 h post-exercise. No performance benefit *during* running. |
| **B — Moderate** | Cold water immersion (10–15°C, 11–15 min) | Reduces soreness and inflammation post-endurance. Caveat: blunts strength/hypertrophy adaptation if used after strength work. |
| **B — Moderate** | Sauna post-session (15–30 min, 3–4×/wk) | Builds plasma volume, improves heat tolerance, ~2% endurance performance gain over 3 weeks. Recovery effect itself is modest; performance adaptation is the main benefit. |
| **C — Weak/mixed** | Contrast water therapy | Small benefit for soreness/strength; not clearly superior to single-modality approaches. |
| **C — Weak/mixed** | Pneumatic compression boots | Subjective improvement; small or null on objective biomarkers. |
| **C — Weak/mixed** | Foam rolling | Short-term ROM and perceived soreness benefits; small effect size. |
| **D — Insufficient/negative** | IV vitamin/saline therapy | No clear evidence of benefit over oral hydration. WADA prohibits IV >100 mL/12 h for in-competition athletes regardless of contents. Bypasses GI filtration — risk without proven benefit. |
| **D — Insufficient/negative** | Cryotherapy chambers | Limited evidence beyond what cold-water immersion provides. |
| **D — Insufficient/negative** | NSAIDs as routine recovery aid | Blunt adaptation; impair tendon and bone healing; renal risk during dehydration. Use only for acute medical indications. |

## Cutback Weeks (Down Weeks, Recovery Weeks)

A cutback week is a planned reduction in training volume that allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate while preserving fitness. Cutback weeks exist because the training-adaptation curve is non-linear: 3 weeks of progressive load + 1 week of reduction produces more adaptation than 4 weeks of monotonic load.

### Frequency

| Profile | Cycle |
|---|---|
| Default for most runners | 3 weeks load → 1 week cutback |
| Higher-mileage / experienced | 4 weeks load → 1 week cutback |
| Injury-prone / older / returning from injury | 2 weeks load → 1 week cutback |
| Late-block (peak weeks of marathon prep) | 2 weeks load → 1 week cutback |

### Depth of Cutback by Mileage Tier

The percentage cut is from the highest week in the preceding load block (not the average). Intensity sessions are kept but typically reduced by one rep or one set; volume is the primary lever.

| Peak-load mpw | Cutback mpw | % reduction | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 20–40 | 16–28 | 20–30% | Drop the long run by 20–30%; remove 1 of 2 quality sessions or shorten it. |
| 40–60 | 30–48 | 20–30% | Long run –25%; keep one quality session, simplify the second. |
| 60–80 | 42–60 | 25–30% | Long run –25–30%; one true quality session only. |
| 80+ | 56–68 | 25–35% | Long run –30%; quality reduced or replaced with strides + tempo segments. |

### What to Cut First

In priority order (cut from the top until target volume is hit):

1. Total volume (drop a weekday run, shorten others).
2. Long run length (–20–30%).
3. Second quality session (replace with easy + strides).
4. Supplemental drills/strides.
5. Strength volume (rarely cut intensity; reduce sets).
6. Last to be cut: the one remaining quality session that defines the block (e.g., threshold tempo in a marathon block).

### What Cutback Weeks Are Not

- They are not rest weeks. Mileage is reduced, not zeroed.
- They are not taper weeks. The goal is fatigue dissipation, not peak performance freshness.
- They are not optional during high-load blocks. The first cutback week skipped is when injury risk climbs.

## Post-Race Recovery

### Recovery by Distance

Two complementary frameworks, used together:

- **Daniels rule:** 1 easy day per 3 km raced before the next quality session. Conservative for shorter races, light for marathon/ultra.
- **Galloway/coaching consensus:** 1 easy day per mile raced before resuming hard training. Heavier; better fit for marathon and longer.

Use the larger of the two for marathon and beyond.

| Distance | Total recovery days (no quality) | Days of zero/very-light running | Return to long runs | Return to quality workouts | Earliest reasonable next race-effort |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5K | 3–5 | 1–2 | Day 4–5 | Day 6–8 | 7–10 days |
| 10K | 5–7 | 2–3 | Day 5–7 | Day 7–10 | 10–14 days |
| Half marathon | 10–14 | 3–5 | Day 7–10 | Day 10–14 | 21–28 days |
| Marathon | 21–28 | 5–10 | Week 2–3 (short) | Week 3–4 | 8–12 weeks |
| 50K | 14–21 | 3–7 | Week 2 | Week 3 | 6–10 weeks |
| 50-mile | 21–28 | 7–10 | Week 3 | Week 4 | 8–12 weeks |
| 100K | 28–35 | 10–14 | Week 4 | Week 5 | 10–16 weeks |
| 100-mile | 28–42 | 14–21 | Week 4–5 | Week 6+ | 12–24 weeks |

CNS recovery in ultras lags musculoskeletal recovery; tendons and ligaments take longer than muscles. Returning to structured workouts before week 3 in a 50+ miler significantly raises tendon-injury risk.

### Recovery by Effort (A vs. B vs. C Race)

| Race priority | Effort given | Taper before | Recovery scale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A race** | Maximum, full taper, peak day | 2–3 weeks | Full table above |
| **B race** | Hard but not depleted; 1-week taper | 7–10 days | 60–70% of A-race recovery duration |
| **C race / hard workout substitute** | Strong effort, no taper | 0–3 days easy | 25–50% of A-race recovery duration; treat like a hard workout |

For a B-race half marathon, expect 7–10 days of recovery rather than 14. For a C-effort 5K, expect 2–3 easy days instead of 5.

### Muscle-Damage Biomarker Timeline (Marathon)

| Marker | Peak | Return to baseline |
|---|---|---|
| Creatine kinase (CK) | 24 h post | ~144 h (6 days) |
| Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) | 24–48 h | ~192 h (8 days) |
| Cardiac troponin (hs-TNT) | 24–48 h | ~96 h (4 days) — transient elevation, not pathological in healthy runners |
| Hamstring T2 MRI signal | Day 1–3 | ~Day 8 |
| Subjective DOMS | 24–72 h | 5–7 days |

Returning to easy running at 48 h post-marathon does not impair muscle damage recovery in studies up to 8 days post; it may speed neuromuscular recovery. Returning to *hard* running before day 7 demonstrably impairs recovery.

## Week-by-Week Protocols

### Half Marathon Recovery (14-day)

| Day | Session | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0 (race) | Race | Cooldown walk 10 min; 30–60 g carb + 20 g protein within 30 min |
| 1 | Rest or 20–30 min walk | Hydration + protein focus |
| 2 | Rest or 20–30 min easy spin / swim | No running |
| 3 | 20–30 min very easy jog or rest | RPE ≤3 |
| 4 | 30–40 min easy | RPE 3–4 |
| 5 | Rest or 30 min easy | |
| 6 | 40–50 min easy + 4× 20 s strides | Strides only if legs feel clean |
| 7 | 45–60 min easy (medium-long) | First "real" run |
| 8 | 30–40 min easy or rest | |
| 9 | 45 min easy + 6× 20 s strides | |
| 10 | 40 min easy or short fartlek (4× 1 min @ 10K effort) | First intensity, optional |
| 11 | 30–40 min easy | |
| 12 | 50–70 min easy long run | |
| 13 | 30 min easy or rest | |
| 14 | Resume normal training week | Full quality workouts allowed |

### Marathon Recovery (4-week reverse taper)

| Week | Volume vs. peak | Long run | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Week 1 (race week) | 10–20% (race itself + walks) | None | None | Days 0–3: walks only or rest. Days 4–7: 20–30 min very easy jogs every other day. No strides. |
| Week 2 | 30–40% | 45–60 min easy | None | All easy, RPE 3–4. Strides only if legs are clean by day 11–13. |
| Week 3 | 50–60% | 60–75 min easy | Strides + light fartlek (4–6× 1 min @ 10K effort) | First structured surges. No threshold or VO2max. |
| Week 4 | 70–80% | 75–90 min easy with optional last 15–20 min @ MP | One light tempo (15–20 min @ HMP) | First true workout. Re-evaluate before adding a second quality session in week 5. |

Full return to peak training load typically week 5–6. A second hard race-effort within 6 weeks of a marathon is achievable for short distances (5K–10K) but with increased breakdown risk.

### Marathon Recovery, Conservative (6-week)

For older runners, runners with prior injuries, runners coming off a difficult marathon, or marathons run in heat / with significant carbon-plated-shoe exposure:

| Week | Volume vs. peak | Long run | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 10% | None | None | Walks; one or two 20-min jogs late in week. |
| 2 | 25% | 40–50 min easy | None | All easy. |
| 3 | 40% | 60 min easy | Strides only | |
| 4 | 55% | 70–80 min easy | Light fartlek | |
| 5 | 70% | 80–90 min easy | Tempo 15–20 min @ HMP | |
| 6 | 85% | 90+ min, optional MP segments | Two quality sessions | Resume normal block |

## Recovery Scaled to Weekly Mileage

Recovery requirements scale with absolute training load, but not linearly — higher mileage runners often have superior recovery infrastructure (sleep habits, nutrition, fitness). The tables below are heuristic.

### 20–40 mpw

| Element | Target |
|---|---|
| Sleep | 7.5–9 h |
| Rest days/week | 1–2 |
| Cutback frequency | Every 3 weeks |
| Cutback depth | 25–30% |
| Easy days between hard sessions | 1–2 |
| Post-marathon full return | 4–5 weeks |
| Recovery modalities priority | Sleep, nutrition, easy days. Skip extras. |

### 40–60 mpw

| Element | Target |
|---|---|
| Sleep | 8–9 h |
| Rest days/week | 1 |
| Cutback frequency | Every 3–4 weeks |
| Cutback depth | 20–30% |
| Easy days between hard sessions | 1–2 |
| Post-marathon full return | 4–6 weeks |
| Recovery modalities priority | Sleep, nutrition, easy days, +/- compression, +/- massage |

### 60–80 mpw

| Element | Target |
|---|---|
| Sleep | 8.5–9.5 h, +/- 20–30 min nap |
| Rest days/week | 0–1 (often replaced by 30-min recovery shake-out) |
| Cutback frequency | Every 3–4 weeks |
| Cutback depth | 25–30% |
| Easy days between hard sessions | 1–2; doubles common |
| Post-marathon full return | 5–7 weeks |
| Recovery modalities priority | Sleep, nutrition, easy days, compression, massage 1–2×/month, optional sauna |

### 80+ mpw

| Element | Target |
|---|---|
| Sleep | 9–10 h, regular nap 20–60 min |
| Rest days/week | Often 0; 1 day with single short shake-out |
| Cutback frequency | Every 2–3 weeks |
| Cutback depth | 25–35% |
| Easy days between hard sessions | 1–2; doubles standard |
| Post-marathon full return | 5–8 weeks |
| Recovery modalities priority | Sleep, nutrition, easy days, compression, regular massage, sauna for adaptation, periodic full down weeks (40–50% volume) |

## Reverse Periodization for Marathon Recovery

Standard linear periodization moves from high-volume base → race-specific intensity → taper.

A *reverse taper* (post-race) inverts this: progressively rebuild volume first, then add intensity. The classic ordering, week by week:

```
Week 1: rest / walk / minimal jog
Week 2: rebuild frequency (most days = short easy)
Week 3: rebuild duration (longer easy runs, no quality)
Week 4: reintroduce strides, then short tempo
Week 5: reintroduce one quality session (threshold or fartlek)
Week 6+: return to full structure
```

This ordering matches tissue-repair timelines:

| Tissue | Adaptation/recovery time after marathon |
|---|---|
| Glycogen | 24–72 h with adequate carbohydrate |
| Muscle fibers (microdamage) | 5–10 days |
| Connective tissue (tendon, fascia) | 2–4 weeks |
| Bone remodeling (post-stress) | 3–6 weeks |
| CNS / hormonal balance | 2–4 weeks |
| Immune | 1–3 weeks |

Loading high-intensity work before connective tissue and CNS recover risks tendon injury and stress fractures — the most common post-marathon injury pattern.

## Warning Signs of Incomplete Recovery

No single metric is reliable; multiple converging signals carry far more weight than any one in isolation.

### Quantitative Signals

| Signal | Threshold suggesting incomplete recovery | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| HRV (RMSSD) | Drop >1 SD below 7-day rolling baseline for 3+ consecutive days | Most useful as a personal trend, not absolute number. Confounded by alcohol, illness, menstrual cycle, travel. |
| RHR (morning) | +5 bpm above 14-day baseline, sustained 3+ days | Sleep-tracker overnight RHR more reliable than single-point morning measurement. |
| Sleep efficiency | <85% on tracker, or subjective non-restorative sleep | Cumulative deficit over a week is the warning. |
| Body weight | >2% drop over 1 week without intent | Often indicates underfueling + inadequate recovery. |
| Performance | Can't hit prescribed paces at usual HR/RPE for 2+ workouts | Strongest single performance indicator. |
| Submaximal HR | HR ≥5 bpm higher than usual at fixed easy pace | Aerobic decoupling without training stimulus = fatigue. |

### Qualitative Signals

| Signal | Description |
|---|---|
| Mood | Persistent low mood, irritability, loss of enthusiasm for running |
| Motivation | Skipping or dreading workouts that previously felt routine |
| Soreness | Persistent muscle soreness >5 days without obvious cause |
| Heaviness | "Heavy legs" sensation that does not clear after 2 easy days |
| Sleep | Difficulty falling asleep despite fatigue, or early-morning waking |
| Appetite | Suppressed appetite or carbohydrate cravings disproportionate to training |
| Illness | Recurrent URTI; minor injuries that don't resolve |
| Libido | Suppressed libido; in females, menstrual cycle disruption |

### Decision Matrix

| Signal count (over 5–7 days) | Action |
|---|---|
| 0–1 quantitative + 0–1 qualitative | Continue training |
| 2 quantitative + 0–1 qualitative | Insert easy days; defer next quality session 24–48 h |
| 1–2 quantitative + 2+ qualitative | 3–5 day cutback (50% volume, no quality) |
| 3+ quantitative or 3+ qualitative | Full cutback week (40–50% volume); consider medical review |
| Persistent for >2 weeks | Stop structured training; medical/coach review for non-functional overreaching or overtraining syndrome |

The progression from functional overreaching (fitness builds after recovery) → non-functional overreaching (weeks to recover) → overtraining syndrome (months) is a continuum. Catching it at functional overreaching is the goal.

## Multiple Races Per Year — Cadence Guidelines

### Marathons / Race-Effort Marathons

| Profile | Marathons per year (race-effort, A-priority) |
|---|---|
| New marathoner (first 1–3 marathons) | 1 |
| Recreational, well-established | 2 (spring + fall) |
| Experienced sub-elite | 2 + 1 supported B-race marathon |
| Elite professional | 2 (3 in Olympic/championship years, but considered too many for paid performance contracts) |
| Marathoners >50 yr | 1–2 |

Minimum spacing between race-effort marathons:

| Spacing | Risk profile |
|---|---|
| <8 weeks | Very high — second marathon will be 5–10% slower, injury risk elevated, recovery prolonged |
| 8–12 weeks | High — possible for experienced runners; the second is rarely a PR |
| 12–16 weeks | Standard for spring + fall double-up |
| 16–24 weeks | Conservative; allows full block + taper |

### Half Marathons

| Profile | Race-effort halves per year |
|---|---|
| Recreational | 2–4 |
| Experienced | 4–6 |
| Elite | 6–10 (often as workouts in marathon prep) |

Minimum spacing for race-effort halves: 4–6 weeks.

### 5K / 10K

These can be raced more frequently because total muscle damage is lower.

| Profile | Race-effort 5Ks per year | Minimum spacing |
|---|---|---|
| Recreational | 6–12 | 2–3 weeks |
| Competitive club / sub-elite | 10–20 | 1–2 weeks |
| Track / road racing season | 15–25 | 1 week (with reduced load between) |

### Ultra Distances

| Distance | Maximum per year |
|---|---|
| 50K | 4–6 |
| 50 mile | 2–4 |
| 100K | 2–3 |
| 100 mile | 1–2 (rarely 3 for elites; cumulative connective-tissue load is the limiter) |

### Annual Race Planning Heuristic

```
Total race-effort days per year ≤ 20–25 for most runners
A races: 2 max per year
B races: 4–6 per year
C races / workout efforts: as fits within recovery
```

A complete season also includes a 2–4 week off-season break (no structured training) once per year, typically after the fall A race.

## Carbon-Plated Shoe Effect on Recovery

Carbon-plated "super shoes" (high-compliance PEBA midsole + embedded carbon plate) reduce muscle work — particularly at the calf and ankle — by storing and returning more energy in the midsole.

### Recovery Effects

| Effect | Direction | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Calf and Achilles muscle activation during running | Decreased | More energy stored in foam, less in tendons. |
| Post-race CK and DOMS in lower-leg muscles | Anecdotally decreased | Limited peer-reviewed data; matches biomechanical predictions. |
| Bone stress on metatarsals and navicular | Increased | Force transfer pattern shifts loading into the foot bones. Multiple case series report bone-stress injuries (BSI) of foot in super-shoe users. |
| Recovery time at the level of muscle | Slightly shorter (likely 1–3 days for marathon) | Most relevant for races; less for daily training. |
| Recovery time at the level of bone/connective tissue | Same or longer | The bones still absorbed marathon-distance load; super shoes do not reduce skeletal recovery. |

### Practical Adjustments

| Scenario | Recovery adjustment |
|---|---|
| Marathon raced in super shoes, no foot symptoms | Standard 4–6 week return; muscle recovery may be ~1–2 days faster |
| Marathon in super shoes, foot soreness or "hot spots" | Add 1 week to return; treat as suspected early BSI until cleared |
| First-time super shoe user | Treat as a new stress; expect more soreness in unfamiliar muscle groups (often hip flexors, anterior tibialis) |
| High volume in super shoes during training | Limit to 1–2 sessions/week; rotate with non-plated shoes for daily mileage |

Super shoes shift, but do not eliminate, recovery cost. The window saved on muscle damage may be paid back by skeletal load if mileage in super shoes is unbounded.

## Recovery Technology and Modalities — Detailed Review

### Compression Garments

| Use case | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Post-exercise (2–96 h window) | Moderate effect on perceived soreness and strength recovery (meta-analysis). Mechanism: improved venous return, reduced edema. |
| During running (race or training) | No meaningful performance benefit. |
| Travel (long flights post-race) | Reasonable use; reduces leg swelling. |

**Bottom line:** worth wearing in the 24–48 h after long efforts; not worth racing in for performance.

### Sauna

| Protocol | Effect |
|---|---|
| 15–30 min @ 80–90°C, within 30 min post-run, 3–4×/week, 3+ weeks | ~2% endurance performance improvement; plasma volume expansion; heat acclimation |
| Single session as recovery aid | Minimal direct recovery effect; mostly subjective relaxation |

Hydrate aggressively (1–1.5 L during/after). Avoid sauna immediately after a race or hard session if hydration is uncertain. Sauna is more useful as an adaptation tool than a recovery tool.

### Cold Water Immersion / Ice Bath

| Protocol | Effect |
|---|---|
| 10–15°C for 11–15 min, post-endurance | Reduces inflammation and DOMS |
| After strength work | Blunts hypertrophy and strength gains — avoid in muscle-building phases |
| Pre-race or pre-quality | No benefit; potential negative if it impairs warm-up |

Most useful in racing blocks (multi-day events, race-week recovery). Avoid year-round use if strength gains matter.

### Contrast Water Therapy

Alternating ~1 min hot (38–40°C) / 1 min cold (10–15°C) for 10–15 min total. Modest evidence of benefit for soreness and strength recovery; not clearly superior to single-modality CWI. Reasonable choice if both are easily available.

### Massage (Manual and Percussive)

| Use case | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Post-exercise (within 2 h) | Most effective single modality for DOMS in meta-analysis; effect peaks ~48 h |
| 24–48 h before key session | Caution — deep tissue work can leave legs flat; prefer light flushing massage |
| Performance metrics (strength, endurance) | Small or null direct effect |

Frequency: 1–4×/month is typical for high-mileage runners; recreational runners gain most from a session in the 48–72 h after a long race.

### Pneumatic Compression Boots (NormaTec, etc.)

20–60 min, gradient pressure cycles. Subjective "fresher legs" effect is consistent; objective biomarker effects are small. Useful adjunct, not a substitute for sleep or easy days. Common at high mileage in the 0–48 h post-race window.

### IV Therapy

Not recommended as a routine recovery tool.

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| Evidence | No high-quality evidence of benefit over oral hydration in normally hydrated athletes |
| Risk | Bypasses GI filtration; risk of nutrient toxicity, infection, electrolyte derangement |
| WADA | Infusions >100 mL per 12 h are prohibited in-competition regardless of substance (with narrow exceptions); used to evade biological-passport detection |
| Appropriate use | Medically supervised treatment of clinically significant dehydration or hyponatremia post-ultra |

For oral rehydration after long races, sodium-containing fluids (e.g., 30–60 mmol/L Na) at 1.25–1.5× sweat losses are evidence-based and free of these issues.

### Cryotherapy Chambers (–110°C, 2–3 min)

Convenience advantage; evidence base no stronger than cold-water immersion. Not a meaningful upgrade over an ice bath.

### NSAIDs

| Use | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Pre-race / during race | Avoid — increased AKI risk, particularly with dehydration; potential GI bleed; no performance benefit |
| Post-race for soreness | Avoid as routine; blunts adaptive inflammation |
| Acute injury (per medical advice) | Short-term use at lowest effective dose |
| Chronic pain | Medical supervision required |

### Foam Rolling / Self-Myofascial Release

Small but consistent short-term effects on perceived soreness and ROM. Cheap and harmless. Not a high-priority recovery modality but a reasonable habit.

### Stretching

Static stretching post-run does not measurably reduce DOMS or speed recovery. Mobility work for joint range and movement quality is independently useful.

---

## Recovery Hierarchy — Single-Page Summary

When in doubt, work down this list. Higher items dominate lower ones.

```
1. Sleep 8–9+ h consistently
2. Adequate carbohydrate and protein
3. Hard/easy alternation in the week
4. Rest days as scheduled
5. Cutback weeks every 3–4 weeks
6. Reverse-taper post-race per distance table
7. Compression / massage / sauna / cold for marginal gains
8. Tech (boots, cryo, contrast) as convenience
9. Avoid: routine NSAIDs, IV therapy, ice baths after strength work, training through 3+ warning signs
```

If items 1–6 are not in place, no amount of items 7–9 will compensate.
