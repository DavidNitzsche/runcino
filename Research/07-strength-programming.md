# 07 — Strength Training Programming for Runners

Periodized resistance training for distance runners. Reference doc covering heavy lifting, plyometrics, single-leg work, tendon loading, hip/glute/core, concurrent-training research, and equipment options (bodyweight, cable/functional trainer, full gym).

---

## Definitions

- **1RM (one-rep max):** heaviest load a lifter can move once with full ROM and acceptable form.
- **Heavy resistance training (HRT):** ≥80% 1RM, 3–6 reps/set, with high-force intent.
- **Maximal strength training (MST):** ≥85% 1RM, 1–4 reps, explosive concentric intent.
- **Plyometrics:** SSC exercises exploiting elastic recoil. Categorised by ground-contact intensity: low (skips, pogos), medium (bounds, hops), high (depth/drop jumps).
- **Running economy (RE):** steady-state O2 cost at submaximal pace. Lower = better.
- **Concurrent training:** simultaneous strength/power + aerobic endurance training.
- **Interference effect:** attenuation of strength/hypertrophy/power adaptation when combined with high-volume endurance work.
- **Stretch-shortening cycle (SSC):** rapid eccentric→concentric muscle action storing and releasing elastic energy. Running = repeated SSCs at ankle, knee, hip.
- **Eccentric:** muscle lengthening under load.
- **Isometric:** muscle contracting without length change.
- **Anti-rotation / anti-extension / anti-flexion:** core categories defined by which spinal motion they resist.

## 1. Research summary

### 1.1 Performance literature

The case for resistance training in distance runners rests on a robust series of systematic reviews and meta-analyses. "No strength work" is no longer defensible for runners targeting performance.

| Review | Year | Athletes | Intervention duration | Key finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Balsalobre-Fernández et al. | 2016 | 93 trained M/L distance | 8–12 wk | Strength training improved RE; effect size large |
| Blagrove, Howatson & Hayes | 2018 | 469 trained ≥6 mo | ≥4 wk | 2–3 sessions/wk improved RE, time-trial, sprint; VO2max and body comp unchanged |
| Beattie et al. | 2017 | Collegiate/national distance | 40 wk | Maximal + explosive lifting improved RE, vVO2max, reactive strength |
| Llanos-Lagos et al. | 2024 | M/L distance | ≥8 wk | RT improved RE at all running speeds; max-strength loads (≥80% 1RM) most effective |
| Llanos-Lagos et al. | 2024 (companion) | M/L distance | ≥8 wk | RT improved performance across distances; effect magnitude scales with load |
| Eihara et al. | 2022 | All levels | ≥4 wk | Heavy RT (≥90% 1RM, ≤4RM) ≥10 wk slightly superior to plyometrics for RE |

Bottom-line conclusions:

1. Strength training improves running economy. Moderate-to-large effect, dose-dependent on load.
2. Heavier loads beat lighter loads. 3–6 reps at ≥80% 1RM produces the largest economy gains. Hypertrophy-style sets are inferior for this.
3. Heavy lifting and plyometrics both work; heavy lifting is slightly more reliable. Combining the two beats either alone.
4. VO2max, lactate threshold, body composition typically do not change. The mechanism is neural and elastic.
5. Minimum effective duration ~8 wk; ≥10 wk more reliable; gains continue across years.
6. Two sessions per week is the sweet spot; three yields more but competes with run volume.

### 1.2 Injury-prevention literature

| Study | Year | Population | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lauersen, Bertelsen & Andersen | 2014 | 26,610 athletes | RT cut all sports injuries to <1/3; overuse injuries roughly halved |
| Lauersen, Andersen & Andersen | 2018 | Updated set | RT was the only single-component intervention with strong injury-prevention effect; dose-dependent — every 10% increase in RT volume reduced injury risk ~4 percentage points |
| Multiple trials | various | Distance runners | RT addressing hip abductors, calves, hamstrings reduces tendinopathy and patellofemoral pain incidence |

The 2018 Lauersen meta-analysis is the strongest single piece of evidence in the running-injury-prevention literature. Stretching does not show this effect; balance/proprioception shows a smaller effect; multi-component programs show smaller effects than strength training alone.

### 1.3 Mechanisms

| Mechanism | What changes | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Neural drive | Motor-unit recruitment, rate coding | More force per stride at submax pace |
| Tendon stiffness | Achilles, patellar stiffen | Better elastic-energy return |
| Type I/IIa fibre force | Force per fibre rises | Lower relative effort per stride |
| Intermuscular coordination | Improves | Less wasted work |
| Hypertrophy | Minimal under HRT-for-runners protocols | Not the goal |

Strength training for runners works by making existing tissue produce force more efficiently, not by adding muscle. Hypertrophy is a side-effect.

## 2. Phase-by-phase programming

The strength macrocycle is **inverse to the run macrocycle.** When run intensity is low, strength intensity is high. As run intensity climbs toward the goal race, strength shifts from development to maintenance to off.

### 2.1 Phase × variable matrix

| Variable | Off-season | Base | Build | Peak | Taper | Race week |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Run focus | Low volume, easy | High volume, mostly aerobic | Adding intensity (threshold, VO2) | Race-pace specificity | Volume drop | Sharpening |
| Strength goal | Hypertrophy, weakness correction | Build maximal strength | Maintain max strength + add power | Maintain | Reduce | Stop or minimal |
| Sessions/wk | 2–3 | 2–3 | 2 | 1–2 | 1 | 0 |
| Load (% 1RM) | 60–80% | 80–90% | 80–95% | 80–90% | 70–85% | n/a |
| Reps/set | 8–12 | 4–6 | 3–5 | 3–5 | 3–5 | n/a |
| Sets per primary lift | 3–4 | 3–5 | 3–4 | 2–3 | 1–2 | n/a |
| Plyometrics | Low (intro) | Low–mod | Moderate–high | Maintenance | Low or off | Off |
| Plyo contacts/wk | 40–80 | 60–120 | 100–200 | 80–150 | 30–60 | 0 |
| Olympic-derivative power | Off | Optional | Yes | Maintain | Off | Off |
| RPE cap on lifts | 8 | 8–9 | 8–9 | 7–8 | 6–7 | n/a |
| Last heavy session | n/a | n/a | n/a | n/a | 7–10 d before race | n/a |

### 2.2 Phase durations (marathon/half cycle)

| Phase | Duration | Run anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Off-season | 2–6 wk | Post-race recovery + transition |
| Base | 8–12 wk | Aerobic development |
| Build | 6–10 wk | Threshold, specific endurance |
| Peak | 3–4 wk | Race-specific work |
| Taper | 2–3 wk | Volume reduction |

For 5K–10K targets, compress base/build; peak becomes longer relative to the cycle. For ultra-distance, lengthen base, shorten peak, and keep more strength volume deeper into the cycle since intensity costs are lower.

### 2.3 Phase narratives

**Off-season (2–6 wk).** Deload 1–2 wk, then hypertrophy work to correct asymmetries and address chronic weak points (glute med, posterior chain, calf). The only window where higher-rep fatigue lifting is appropriate.

**Base (8–12 wk).** Maximal-strength block. Heavy compound lifts at 80–90% 1RM, 3–6 reps, 3–4 sets, 2× wk. Plyometrics low–moderate. Largest run-economy gains come from this phase.

**Build (6–10 wk).** Add power expression. Heavy lifts plus Olympic-derivative work (jump squats, trap-bar jumps, hang pulls if competent) or higher-volume plyometrics. Run intensity rises (threshold, VO2). Strength sessions stay at 2× wk and shorten.

**Peak (3–4 wk).** Maintenance — same loads as build, reduced sets. 1–2 short sessions/wk.

**Taper (2–3 wk).** Reduce volume 40–60% in wk one, 60–70% in wk two. Maintain load, cut sets. No new exercises. Last heavy session 7–10 d before race.

**Race week.** Stop entirely 5–10 d out, or a single very short and light "movement" session 4–5 d before race. No new exercises, no novel intensity, no plyometrics within 7 d of race.

## 3. Exercise selection by phase

### 3.1 Base: heavy bilateral compound lifts

Primary:

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Load | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back squat | 3–5 × 3–6 | 80–90% | Or front squat for back-squat issues |
| Trap-bar deadlift | 3–5 × 3–5 | 80–90% | Friendlier than conventional |
| RDL | 3–4 × 5–6 | 75–85% | Posterior chain |
| Hip thrust | 3–4 × 5–8 | 75–85% | Direct glute load |

Secondary (1–2/session):

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Standing calf raise (heavy) | 3–4 × 6–8 | Plantarflexors |
| Walking lunge | 2–3 × 6–8/leg | Single-leg pattern |
| Pull-up / row | 2–3 × 6–10 | Posture |

### 3.2 Build: heavy + power

Carry 1–2 base lifts (typically squat + RDL or DL). Add:

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Trap-bar jump | 3–5 × 3–5 | 20–40% trap-bar 1RM, max-velocity |
| Jump squat | 3–4 × 3–5 | BW or ≤30% 1RM |
| Hang clean / hang pull | 3–5 × 2–3 | Only if technically competent |
| Box jump (low–mod) | 3–4 × 3–5 | Step down, never jump down |

### 3.3 Peak: maintenance

One heavy bilateral lift + one single-leg + one power expression + calves + core. Two short sessions/wk (30–40 min).

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Load |
|---|---|---|
| Trap-bar DL OR front squat | 2–3 × 3–5 | 80–85% |
| Bulgarian split squat | 2 × 5/leg | Moderate |
| Trap-bar jump or pogo hops | 2–3 × 3–5 | Light/explosive |
| Single-leg calf raise (heavy) | 2 × 6–8/leg | Heavy DB |
| Anti-rotation core | 2 × 8/side | Moderate |

### 3.4 Taper: reduced volume

Wk 1 of taper: drop sets ~40%, same loads, fewer reps.
Wk 2 of taper: drop sets ~60–70%, same loads, fewer reps; last heavy lift 7–10 d before race.
Race wk: stop, or one very short and light session ≥4 d before race.

### 3.5 Off-season: hypertrophy + weakness correction

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Load | Targeting |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goblet or back squat | 3 × 8–12 | 65–75% | Bilateral base |
| RDL | 3 × 8–10 | 65–75% | Posterior chain |
| Bulgarian split squat | 3 × 8–10/leg | Moderate DB | Single-leg |
| Single-leg RDL | 3 × 8/leg | Light–mod | Hip stability |
| Hip thrust | 3 × 10–12 | Moderate | Glutes |
| Standing calf raise | 3 × 10–12 | Moderate | Plantarflexors |
| Tibialis raise / heel walk | 2 × 12–15 | BW | Anterior shin |
| Pull-up / row | 3 × 8–10 | n/a | Posture |
| Bird dog, side plank, dead bug | 2 × 30–45 s | n/a | Core endurance |

Off-season is also when imbalances should be diagnosed. Quick battery: single-leg balance ≥30 s eyes closed; single-leg calf raise ≥25 reps to fatigue; single-leg RDL with 10–15 kg DB without trunk twist; side plank ≥45 s each side. Failures dictate emphasis.

## 5. Heavy lifting protocols

### 5.1 Core lifts

```
TIER 1 — primary (pick 1–2 per session):
   Back squat, front squat, trap-bar deadlift,
   conventional deadlift, Romanian deadlift (RDL)
TIER 2 — single-leg primaries:
   Bulgarian split squat, reverse lunge,
   knee-height step-up, single-leg RDL
TIER 3 — accessories:
   Hip thrust (bilateral or single-leg),
   standing or seated calf raise, walking lunge
UPPER (1 push + 1 pull):
   Push-up, DB/BB bench; pull-up, DB row, cable row
```

### 5.2 Form cues

| Lift | Cue |
|---|---|
| Back squat | "Brace, sit between hips, drive the floor away." Knees track toes. Parallel depth minimum. |
| Front squat | "Elbows up, big breath, vertical torso." Easier on lumbar than back squat. |
| Trap-bar deadlift | "Hips back, chest up, push the floor." More vertical torso than conventional; first choice for most runners. |
| Conventional deadlift | "Bar over mid-foot, take slack out, drive hips and chest together." Higher technical demand. |
| RDL | "Soft knees, hinge at hips, bar drags down legs." Stop when hamstrings peak (~mid-shin). |
| Bulgarian split squat | "Front foot far enough that the front shin stays vertical. Drive through front heel." |
| Step-up | "Plant the whole foot, drive through the heel, no trailing-foot push-off." |
| Hip thrust | "Ribs down, chin tucked, full lockout, glutes squeeze at top." |

### 5.3 Loading guidelines

Working sets after warm-up ramp: 80% 1RM × 3, 85% × 3, 90% × 2–3, 92.5% × 1–2. Add load when all prescribed reps are completed at target RPE on the final set; typical progression 2.5–5 lb upper, 5–10 lb lower, smaller as the lifter advances. Never grind a max in-season. If a working set turns into RPE 9.5–10, the load is too heavy for the phase.

### 5.4 Warm-up ramp for heavy lifts

```
Empty bar × 8
40% 1RM × 5
60% 1RM × 3
75% 1RM × 2
85% 1RM × 1 (skip if working sets at 80%)
Working sets at prescribed % 1RM
```

## 6. Plyometric protocols

### 6.1 Categories and contact intensity

| Category | Examples | Primary effect |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Pogos, skipping, ankle hops, line hops | Stiffness, reactive strength |
| Moderate | Broad jumps, lateral bounds, SL hops, alt bounds | Power, force application |
| High | Depth/drop jumps, max bounding, repeat hurdle hops | Reactive strength, peak SSC |

### 6.2 Weekly contact-count progression

| Phase | Contacts/wk | Sessions/wk | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Off-season (intro) | 40–80 | 1–2 | Low intensity only |
| Base | 60–120 | 1–2 | Low–mod. Add unilateral hops |
| Build | 100–200 | 2 | Mod–high. Introduce depth jumps if appropriate |
| Peak | 80–150 | 1–2 | Maintenance; no new exercises |
| Taper | 30–60 | 1 | Low intensity, optional |
| Race week | 0 | 0 | None within 5–7 d of race |

Progress weekly contact volume by ≤10–20% per week.

### 6.3 Exercise list by tier

```
TIER 1 (low-intensity, beginners, all phases):
   Pogo hops, ankle hops, line hops (F/B and side/side),
   A-skip, B-skip, box step-up to balance, low tuck jumps
TIER 2 (moderate-intensity):
   Broad jump w/ stick, single-leg hop for distance,
   lateral bound w/ stick, alternating bounds,
   box jump (low–mod, step down), bodyweight squat jump
TIER 3 (high-intensity, advanced only):
   Depth jump (12–24 in), drop jump (24–30 in),
   repeat single-leg hops, bounding for distance/speed,
   repeat hurdle hops
```

### 6.4 Surface considerations

| Surface | Stiffness | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Grass / field turf | Soft | Default for distance runners |
| Synthetic track | Mid | Good elastic return |
| Wood / sprung floor | Mid | Acceptable |
| Rubber gym mat | Soft | Lower-intensity work, beginners |
| Concrete / asphalt | Hard | Avoid for plyometrics |
| Sand | Very soft | High muscular load, low elastic return; specialty only |

### 6.5 Session structure

```
Warm-up: 5–10 min easy run + dynamic mobility + skipping drills
Low-tier: 2–3 exercises × 2–3 × 6–10 reps  (~50–90 contacts)
Mid/high (build/peak only): 1–2 exercises × 2–3 × 3–6 reps
Rest: 60–90 s low; 90–120 s mid; 2–3 min high
Total: 60–150 contacts depending on phase
```

## 7. Hill sprints as strength/power work

Hill sprints produce neural and structural adaptations close to those of heavy lifting, with very low injury risk and zero equipment cost.

### 7.1 Protocol

| Variable | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Duration per rep | 6–10 s |
| Effort | Maximal (95–100%) |
| Hill grade | 6–10% (turnover slows, knee drive stays high) |
| Recovery | Walk down, ≥2 min between reps |
| Reps/session | Start 4–6, build to 8–12 over 6–8 wk |
| Frequency | 1–2× wk |
| Phase | Late base through build; maintenance in peak; off in taper |
| Position in week | After an easy run, or as the speed element of an easy day |

### 7.2 What hill sprints train

Neural drive, plantarflexor stiffness, Achilles loading, hip-extension power, stride mechanics under high force. Low impact — the grade shortens air phase and softens landings.

### 7.3 Hill sprints vs flat sprints vs heavy lifting

| Quality | Hill sprints | Flat sprints | Heavy lifting |
|---|---|---|---|
| Force production | High | High | Highest |
| Speed of contraction | High | Highest | Low |
| Hamstring strain risk | Low | High | Low |
| Equipment | None | Track | Full gym |
| Skill demand | Low | High | Moderate |
| Run-specificity | Highest | Highest | Lowest |

For runners without gym access, a 1× wk hill-sprint session plus bodyweight strength work covers most of the strength stimulus.

## 8. Single-leg work

Running is unilateral. No stride contains a moment of bilateral support. Single-leg training addresses asymmetries that bilateral work cannot reach and trains hip-knee-ankle stability that bilateral lifts bypass through compensation.

### 8.1 Core single-leg exercises

| Exercise | Pattern | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Bulgarian split squat | Knee-dominant | Quads, glutes; loadable to ~60–70% bilateral squat |
| Reverse lunge | Knee-dominant | Less knee stress than forward lunge |
| Step-up (knee-height box) | Knee-dominant | Highly runner-specific |
| Single-leg RDL | Hip-dominant | Hamstrings, glute max, hip stability |
| Single-leg calf raise | Plantarflexion | Achilles, soleus |
| Single-leg hip thrust | Hip extension | Glutes |
| Walking lunge | Combined | Loadable, gait-specific |

### 8.2 Programming

At least one heavy single-leg lift in every base/build session. Bulgarian split squats benefit from heavy load (rear-foot support provides enough stability to actually load the working leg) — 5–8 reps/leg with DBs or a barbell during base. Step-ups use a knee-height box; only the front leg works (no trailing-foot push-off). Single-leg RDLs start light or bodyweight, progressing to 15–25 lb DBs.

### 8.3 Asymmetry threshold

≥15% bilateral difference in single-leg strength (e.g., 5RM Bulgarian split squat) → program the weaker leg first and add 1–2 extra sets on that side. ≥25% difference is a yellow flag — investigate for unresolved injury, mobility limitation, or movement compensation before heavier loading.

## 9. Calf and Achilles loading

The plantarflexor complex (gastrocnemius, soleus, Achilles) is the single most loaded structure in running, absorbing and returning ~6× bodyweight per stride. Targeted loading is non-negotiable, especially over age 35.

### 9.1 Heavy slow resistance (HSR) — the modern default

Validated for Achilles and patellar tendinopathy (Beyer 2015), now widely used for prevention as well as rehab.

```
- Standing calf raise (gastroc bias):  4 × 6–8, 3 s up / 3 s down
- Seated calf raise (soleus bias):     4 × 6–8, 3 s up / 3 s down
- 3× wk on non-consecutive days; aim for true 6–8RM
- Pain up to 4/10 during loading acceptable; should not increase 24 h post
```

### 9.2 Alfredson eccentric protocol (rehab)

Original 12-wk protocol for chronic midportion Achilles tendinopathy. Used as rehab, not as default loading.

```
- Single-leg eccentric heel drop, knee straight:  3 × 15
- Single-leg eccentric heel drop, knee bent:      3 × 15
- 2× daily, 7 d/wk, 12 wk; bodyweight → backpack as tolerated
- Mild pain up to 5/10 acceptable; pain should not worsen day to day
```

HSR shows comparable or superior outcomes to Alfredson with lower volume and higher adherence; it is the preferred modern default.

### 9.3 Isometric protocol (acute pain, in-season tendon management)

```
- Heavy isometric plantarflexion: 5 × 30–45 s at ~70% MVC
- Rest 1–2 min; 1–2× daily during reactive phase
```

Best-supported for patellar tendinopathy; Achilles evidence mixed for acute analgesia but supportive for stiffness/strength gains over weeks.

### 9.4 Progression matrix

| Tendon state | Default protocol | Sessions/wk |
|---|---|---|
| Healthy, training | HSR calf raises (heavy, slow) | 2–3 |
| Mildly reactive | HSR + isometric pre-run | 3 |
| Reactive / painful | Isometric primary, HSR within pain limits | daily isometric, 2–3 HSR |
| Chronic (>3 mo) | HSR full protocol or Alfredson 12 wk | 3 (HSR) or daily (Alfredson) |
| Post-rupture / surgical | Sports medicine referral; phased return | per provider |

## 10. Hip and glute programming

Hip-abductor and external-rotator weakness is associated with patellofemoral pain and IT-band syndrome. Current evidence supports the *prevention* role of hip strengthening more confidently than the *therapeutic* role for established injury — but the programming takeaway is the same: train them.

### 10.1 Targets

| Muscle group | Role in running | Weakness signs |
|---|---|---|
| Glute max | Hip extension, terminal stance | Underactive; quad-dominant gait |
| Glute medius | Pelvic stability | Trendelenburg drop, knee valgus |
| Glute minimus | Frontal-plane stability | Same as glute med |
| Deep external rotators | Hip control | Internal rotation collapse |
| Hip flexors | Swing-phase drive | Often tight rather than weak |
| Adductors | Frontal-plane stabilizer | Undertrained; groin issues |

### 10.2 Exercise selection

| Exercise | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Barbell hip thrust | Glute max | Loadable, high-tension |
| Single-leg hip thrust | Glute max + stability | Higher demand |
| Standing cable/band hip abduction | Glute med | Loadable |
| Side-lying clamshell, banded | Glute med + ER | Activation |
| Side-lying leg raise | Glute med | Activation |
| Copenhagen plank | Adductors | High-value, often missed |
| Single-leg RDL | Glute max, hip stab | Compound, loadable |
| Lateral band/monster walk | Glute med | Activation, warm-up |

### 10.3 Sample weekly distribution

```
A (heavy day): Hip thrust 3×6; Bulgarian split squat 3×6/leg;
               Cable hip abduction 2×10/leg
B (single-leg): Single-leg RDL 3×8/leg; Step-up 3×6/leg;
                Copenhagen plank 2×20–30 s/side
Pre-run (3–5 min, daily): Banded clamshell 1×10/side;
                Banded lateral walk 1×10/direction; Glute bridge 1×10
```

## 11. Core programming

The core is trained by *what it resists*, not *what it produces*. Running requires resistance to spinal motion (anti-rotation, anti-extension, anti-flexion, anti-lateral-flexion), not crunches.

### 11.1 The four anti-categories

| Category | Resists | Exercises |
|---|---|---|
| Anti-extension | Lumbar extension | Dead bug, hollow hold, plank, ab wheel |
| Anti-rotation | Trunk rotation | Pallof press, kneeling cable chop hold, single-arm carry |
| Anti-lateral flexion | Side-bending | Side plank, suitcase carry, single-arm farmer carry |
| Anti-flexion | Forward bend | Bird dog, modified McGill curl-up, back extension hold |

### 11.2 McGill Big 3 — minimum effective core base

```
- Modified curl-up:  hold 8–10 s, 5–3–1 descending (5, 3, 1 reps)
- Side plank:        hold 8–10 s, 5–3–1 each side
- Bird dog:          hold 8–10 s, 5–3–1 each side
- ~6–8 min total, daily or 4–5× wk
```

5-3-1 descending volume is intentional: short repeated holds train endurance without spinal compression buildup that long holds produce.

### 11.3 Loaded carries

Often missed; high-value. Train trunk under running-like demands.

| Carry | Volume | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| Suitcase (single-arm) | 3 × 30–40 m/side | Anti-lateral flexion, grip |
| Farmer (bilateral) | 3 × 30–50 m | Posture, trunk endurance |
| Front-rack (single-arm) | 3 × 30 m/side | Anti-rotation + anti-lateral flexion |
| Overhead | 2 × 20–30 m | Shoulder stability, core under load |

### 11.4 Sample core block (10 min, 2–3× wk)

```
McGill Big 3 (full 5-3-1 each); Pallof press 2×8/side w/ 3 s holds;
Suitcase carry 2×30 m/side.
```

## 12. Concurrent training research

### 12.1 Timeline

| Era | Marker | Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1980 | Hickson | Concurrent S+E produces less strength gain than strength alone |
| 2012 | Wilson et al. meta-analysis | Endurance attenuates strength, hypertrophy, and (most strongly) power; effects scale with endurance volume and modality |
| 2014–2020 | Various | Largest interference for same-session/back-to-back; minimal with ≥6 h separation; lower for cycling than running |
| 2022 | Schumann et al. meta-analysis | Concurrent training does *not* compromise hypertrophy or maximal strength; explosive strength can be attenuated, especially same-session |
| 2024–2025 | Updated reviews | 3–6 h between sessions reduces molecular interference; sequence matters less than spacing |

### 12.2 Practical interference rules

| Scenario | Risk | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Strength on a different day than hard run | Minimal | Default |
| Strength + easy run, ≥6 h apart | Low | Any phase |
| Strength + easy run, same session | Low–mod | Run first if running goal; lift first if strength |
| Strength + hard run, <6 h apart | Mod–high | Avoid in build/peak |
| Strength + hard run, same session | High | Avoid except compressed schedules |
| Strength immediately after long run | High | Avoid; recovery is compromised |
| Power/explosive lift after long endurance | Highest | Power adaptations are most fatigue-vulnerable |

### 12.3 Session-stacking matrix

| Day type | Allowed strength session |
|---|---|
| Easy run day | Heavy strength (≥6 h apart, or same session if needed) |
| Hard workout day | Light maintenance only on the same day, or none |
| Long-run day | None on same day, or strength ≥6 h before |
| Recovery day | Light strength acceptable; heavy lifting better on hard-run day PM |
| Off day | Possible, but real off days are valuable — don't fill them |

"Hard day hard, easy day easy" applies: pair heavy strength with hard running rather than spreading hard sessions across more days. The total stress on a hard-run day is high either way; clustering hard work preserves more recovery days.

| Threshold workout | Heavy lifting | 24 h, or same day with ≥4 h gap |

| Strength done | Hard run allowed | Min separation |

## 14. Time-of-day for strength relative to running

| Sequence | Effect on running | Effect on lifting | Use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Run AM, lift PM (≥6 h) | Best for run quality | Best for lift quality | Default for serious athletes |
| Lift AM, run PM (≥6 h) | Slight fatigue residue | Best for lift quality | When lifting is the priority |
| Run then lift, same session | Compromises lift (esp. lower) | Reduced loads | Time-constrained schedules |
| Lift then run, same session | Compromises run quality | Best | When the lift is primary |

Best practice: separate by ≥6 h whenever possible. If combining, the goal that matters most that day takes the first slot. Heavy lifting before a workout-run is poor practice. Power and plyometric work should always be done fresh, never after a long run.

---

## 15. Frequency recommendations

| Frequency | When appropriate | Tradeoffs |
|---|---|---|
| **1×/wk** | In-season peak; very high run volume; older runners; minimum effective dose | Maintains adaptations; insufficient for major gains |
| **2×/wk** | Default for most runners year-round (base, build, peak) | Best balance |
| **3×/wk** | Off-season hypertrophy; weakness blocks; low run volume; first 1–2 yr lifting | Interference risk if run volume is also high |
| **4+×/wk** | Rare; only with reduced run volume | Significant run compromise unless volume drops |

Practical rule: in any week with ≥50 mi / ~80 km running, two strength sessions is the ceiling. Three is feasible only if at least one is short and movement-quality focused (not heavy lifting).

## 16. Bodyweight-only programming (travel, no equipment)

Bodyweight strength is sufficient for maintenance during 3–14 day travel windows. Not sufficient for max-strength development in advanced lifters but covers most structural and stability needs.

### 16.1 The travel circuit (20–30 min, 2–3× wk)

```
A: Strength — 3 rounds
   Bulgarian split squat       8/leg
   Single-leg glute bridge     10/leg
   Push-up (slow tempo)        8–15
   Single-leg calf raise       12/leg

B: Stability — 2 rounds
   Side plank                  30–45 s/side
   Bird dog                    8/side, 2-s holds
   Dead bug                    8/side, 2-s holds

C: Power (optional) — 2 rounds
   Broad jump                  3
   Single-leg hop in place     5/leg
   Pogo hops                   20
```

### 16.2 Progressing bodyweight load

Tempo (3–5 s eccentric), bottom pauses, increased ROM (elevated rear foot, deficit lunge), single-limb variations, reduced rest, plyometric overload, cluster sets. Each tactic raises effective load on a movement bodyweight alone has outgrown.

### 16.3 Travel hill-sprint substitute

When bodyweight is insufficient and weights are unavailable, a 1× wk hill-sprint session (6–10 × 8 s) plus the bodyweight circuit covers the strength stimulus for 1–2 wk travel windows.

## 17. Cable machine / functional trainer programming

A motorized cable machine with electromagnetic resistance (typical 5–100 lb range) supports the full range of single-arm/single-leg work plus three resistance modes that physical weights cannot replicate. The 100 lb ceiling is real for bilateral compound lower-body work but rarely a constraint for runner-relevant programming.

### 17.1 Mode selection

| Mode | What it does | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed | Constant resistance through rep | Default strength; comparable to weight stack |
| Band | Light at start, heavy at end | Top-of-rep contraction, explosive concentric, hip thrusts |
| Eccentric | Higher load on lowering phase | Tendon health, hypertrophy, calf/hamstring eccentric work |
| Tempo | Enforced cadence | Time-under-tension, rehab, control work |
| Drop set | Auto-reduces weight at fatigue | Finishing work, off-season hypertrophy |

### 17.2 Runner-specific programming on a cable trainer

```
HEAVY STRENGTH (base):
- Cable goblet squat (T-bar, low pulley)            4 × 6
- Cable RDL (T-bar, low pulley)                     4 × 6
- Cable single-leg RDL (single handle)              3 × 6/leg
- Standing calf raise (T-bar shouldered)            4 × 8

POWER / BUILD (band mode where noted):
- Cable jump squat (band mode, light load)          4 × 4
- Cable resisted broad jump (band mode, light)      3 × 5

SINGLE-LEG / HIP:
- Cable Bulgarian split squat                       3 × 8/leg
- Cable step-up                                     3 × 6/leg
- Ankle-strap kickback (hip extension)              2 × 12/leg
- Ankle-strap standing hip abduction                2 × 12/leg
- Cable hip thrust (band mode top-of-rep load)      4 × 8
- Cable Pallof press                                3 × 10/side

TENDON (eccentric mode):
- Eccentric heel drop (ankle strap or T-bar)        4 × 8/leg, 3 s ecc
- Ankle-strap single-leg hamstring curl, eccentric  3 × 8/leg
```

### 17.3 Why eccentric mode matters for runners

Eccentric overload (loading the lowering phase 20–40% heavier than the lifting phase) is the gold-standard stimulus for tendon adaptation. Achilles, patellar, and hamstring tendinopathy protocols (Alfredson, Silbernagel, HSR) all rely on heavy eccentric loading. An automatic eccentric mode delivers this without the partner-assist or plate-juggling overhead a barbell setup requires. Highest-value applications: single-leg heel drop (Achilles), single-leg hamstring curl with ankle strap, eccentric squat/split squat (patellar tendon), eccentric RDL.

### 17.4 Cable trainer limitations

The 100 lb resistance ceiling caps bilateral lower-body max-strength work — advanced lifters cannot replicate a heavy barbell squat. Single-leg work, eccentric overload, and band-mode lifts narrow this gap but don't close it. Running-specific power expression (jump squats, trap-bar jumps) is approximated, not replicated. For general performance and injury resilience, a cable trainer covers ≥85% of the programming needs in this document. For maximal strength in heavy compound lifts, a barbell remains superior.

## 18. Female-specific considerations

### 18.1 Cycle phase and strength training

The hypothesis that follicular-phase-emphasised resistance training produces superior strength and hypertrophy gains has limited and inconsistent support. Early studies (Reis, Sung) suggested follicular advantage. Subsequent systematic reviews (Colenso-Semple 2023; Elliott-Sale) found low-quality, inconsistent evidence and concluded current data do not justify cycle-phase programming as a default.

Practical guidance:

| Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Do not abandon programming during late luteal / menstrual phase | No reliable evidence strength performance is materially compromised |
| Track symptoms over 2–3 cycles before adjusting load | Individual variation far larger than population averages |
| Allow flexible day-shifting (±1 d) | Manages symptom days without disrupting the program |
| Watch for RED-S signs at any phase | Cycle disruption is a signal, not a programming variable |

### 18.2 RED-S and strength training

Resistance training is protective against bone loss in athletes with low energy availability but does not compensate for the underlying deficit. If menstrual function is disrupted, the priority is restoring energy availability. Strength work should continue (it preserves bone density); heavy run volume should be reduced before reducing strength volume.

### 18.3 Pregnancy and postpartum

Outside this doc's scope; defer to obstetric clinicians. General principle: strength training is generally safe through pregnancy with load adjustment, and is the most evidence-supported intervention for postpartum return to running.

## 19. Masters-specific considerations (40+, especially 50+)

### 19.1 Aging trajectory

| Variable | Aging | Strength-training effect |
|---|---|---|
| Type II fibre area | Declines fastest | Mostly preserved |
| Maximal strength | Declines ~1–2%/yr after 50 | Largely reversible |
| Muscle mass | Declines ~0.5–1%/yr after 50 | Largely reversible |
| Tendon stiffness | Declines | Heavy loading restores |
| Bone density | Declines | HRT and impact both effective |
| Recovery rate | Slows | Longer between heavy sessions |
| VO2max | Declines ~10%/decade | RT does not directly slow this |

### 19.2 Programming adjustments

| Variable | Default | Masters adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Heavy lifting freq | 2× wk | 2× wk; 72–96 h between heavy lower |
| Plyo intensity | Low–high tier | Low–moderate; pogo/line hops over depth jumps |
| Rep range | 3–6 for strength | 4–6; rarely below 4 |
| Volume per session | Standard | 10–20% lower; similar weekly |
| Warm-up | 5–10 min | 10–15 min |
| Eccentric loading | Standard | Higher emphasis |
| Single-leg work | Standard | Higher emphasis |
| Protein intake | 1.2–1.6 g/kg/d | 1.6–2.0 g/kg/d; ≥30 g/meal × 4 meals |

### 19.3 Exercise adjustments

- Trap-bar > conventional deadlift (less spinal flexion).
- Front squat / goblet squat > back squat (less anterior shear; easier shoulder mobility).
- Higher emphasis on step-ups, split squats, unilateral work (falls risk, asymmetries).
- Loaded hip thrust (glute load without spinal compression).
- Heavy slow eccentric calf and hamstring work (tendon stiffness response is exceptional).
- Skip: depth jumps from boxes >18 in, max-effort plyometrics on hard surfaces, conventional deadlift at near-1RM.

Masters runners train at the same %1RM as younger runners. The change is volume and frequency, not intensity. Lifting heavy is *more* important with age, not less.

## 20. Sample weeks

### 20.1 Base, 2× strength, mid-volume runner

```
Mon: Easy run + Strength A (heavy lower) PM
Tue: Workout (tempo)
Wed: Easy run
Thu: Easy run + Strength B (single-leg + power) PM
Fri: Easy or off
Sat: Long run
Sun: Recovery or off

Strength A: Trap-bar DL 4×5; back squat 3×5; standing calf 3×6;
            pull-up 3×6; McGill Big 3 full. ~50 min
Strength B: Bulgarian split squat 3×6/leg; RDL 3×6; hip thrust 3×8;
            box jump 3×4; pogo hops 3×20; Pallof 2×8/side. ~45 min
```

### 20.2 Build, 2× strength, marathoner

Same structure; sessions shorten and shift toward power.

```
A: Trap-bar DL 3×4; trap-bar jump 4×3; walking lunge 2×6/leg;
   single-leg calf 3×6/leg; carry 2×30 m. ~35 min
B: Front squat 3×4; hip thrust 3×5; single-leg RDL 2×6/leg;
   box jump 3×3; side plank 2×30 s/side. ~30 min
```

### 20.3 Peak / taper

```
3 wk out: 2 sessions, drop sets ~25%, hold load
2 wk out: 1–2 sessions, drop sets ~50%, hold load; last heavy lift mid-week
Race wk:  0–1 sessions; if 1, very short, very light, ≥4 d before race
```

### 20.4 Off-season, 3× strength

Run volume at 50–70% of peak. Strength is primary stress. Mon strength A (lower hypertrophy), Wed strength B (upper + core), Fri strength C (single-leg + posterior chain). Easy runs on the off-strength days.

## 21. The eight rules

1. Lift heavy at least once a week, year-round. Dose-dependent injury reduction; running-economy benefit requires ≥80% 1RM.
2. Two strength sessions/wk is the default. One is maintenance only; three+ requires reduced run volume.
3. Inverse periodization to running. Heaviest lifting in off-season/base; maintenance in build/peak; reduced/off in taper.
4. Heavy bilateral compound lifts in base, plus power expression in build. Heavy lifting + plyometrics > either alone.
5. Single-leg, single-side work in every session. Running is unilateral.
6. Calves, hips, and core are non-negotiable. HSR for calves; loaded hip thrusts and unilateral hip work; anti-rotation/anti-extension core including the McGill Big 3.
7. Pair hard with hard. Heavy strength on hard run days, not on recovery days. Preserve real recovery.
8. Stop heavy lifting 7–10 d before A-races. Resume within 1 wk post.
