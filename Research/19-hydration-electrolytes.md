# 19 — Hydration and Electrolyte Management

Hydration science applied to running. Generic reference for prescribing hydration plans across distances, durations, and conditions.

## Definitions

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Euhydration | Normal body water content; baseline state |
| Hypohydration / dehydration | Body water deficit (used interchangeably) |
| Hyperhydration | Body water excess vs. baseline |
| EAH | Exercise-associated hyponatremia: serum [Na+] < 135 mmol/L during or ≤ 24 h post-exercise |
| EAHE | Symptomatic EAH with altered mental status (cerebral edema) |
| Sweat rate | Volume of sweat lost per unit time (L/hr) |
| [Na+]sweat | Sodium concentration in sweat (mmol/L or mg/L) |
| Plasma osmolality | Solute concentration in plasma; ~280–295 mOsm/kg in euhydration |
| BHI | Beverage hydration index — 2-h fluid retention vs. water (water = 1.0) |

---

## Daily Hydration Baseline

Total daily water includes beverages + food (~20% from food). Baseline is pre-training, before exercise losses.

### Daily fluid targets (ml/kg/day)

| Population | ml/kg/day | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary, temperate | 30–35 | Total intake (fluids + food) |
| Recreational runner (3–5 h/wk) | 35–40 | Add training-day sweat losses |
| Endurance runner (6–10 h/wk) | 40–45 | Plus sweat replacement |
| High-volume (10+ h/wk) | 45–55 | Plus sweat replacement |
| Hot/humid | +5–10 | Above baseline |
| Altitude (> 2,500 m) | +0.5–1 L total | Respiratory/renal losses |

National Academies absolute baseline (sedentary): male ~3.7 L/day total (~3.0 L from beverages); female ~2.7 L (~2.2 L beverages).

### Hydration status indicators

| Marker | Euhydrated | Hypohydrated |
|---|---|---|
| First-morning urine specific gravity (USG) | < 1.020 | > 1.020 |
| Urine color (Armstrong scale 1–8) | 1–3 (pale yellow) | 4+ (dark) |
| Body mass (multi-day stable) | Within 1% of baseline | > 1% below baseline |
| Plasma osmolality | 280–295 mOsm/kg | > 295 mOsm/kg |

USG and urine color are practical field tools. WUT check (Weight, Urine, Thirst) — any two abnormal = hypohydration.

## Drink-to-Thirst vs. Structured Hydration: The Equipoise

**Position A — Drink to thirst (Noakes; Hew-Butler 2015).** Thirst is an accurate physiological signal; ad libitum drinking prevents EAH and meaningful performance-impairing dehydration. Forced "stay-ahead-of-thirst" drinking is a primary EAH driver.

**Position B — Structured (ACSM 2007).** Sweat rates vary widely; thirst lags loss in some athletes during prolonged/hot exercise. Plan to limit body-mass loss to < 2% from measured sweat rate. Relevant for high-sweat-rate athletes in heat.

| Scenario | Default approach |
|---|---|
| < 60 min, any | Drink to thirst; pre-hydrate |
| 60–90 min, temperate | Drink to thirst |
| 90 min – 3 h, temperate | Drink to thirst with sodium fluid |
| 90 min – 3 h, hot/humid | Structured + thirst as upper bound |
| > 3 h, any | Sweat-rate plan + thirst as upper bound |
| Slow runners (> 5 h marathon) | Cap; thirst-led; EAH risk high |
| Elite/fast in heat | Structured, body-mass-loss target |

Convergent rule: never drink so much that body mass increases during exercise. Mass gain = overdrinking = EAH risk.

## Pre-Race Hydration

**24 h pre-race.** Daily baseline fluid + 500–1,000 ml; liberal salt with meals; no alcohol; maintain habitual caffeine; verify first-morning USG < 1.020.

**2–4 h pre-race (prehydration).**
```
Volume: 5–10 ml/kg body mass of fluid 2–4 h pre-exercise
Sodium:  1,000–1,500 mg added to pre-race fluid (sodium preloading)
Form:    Sports drink, ORS, or water + electrolyte tab
```
Sodium preloading at ~7.5 g NaCl/L (≈ 3,000 mg Na/L) divided across 2 h pre-exercise increases plasma volume and reduces urinary loss of ingested fluid. Standard sports drinks (~230–690 mg Na/L) are insufficient for pre-loading.

**0–60 min pre-race.** 60–30 min: stop large bolus drinking; allow void. 15–10 min: 150–250 ml top-up if thirsty. 0: final mouthful. Goal: start euhydrated with plasma volume mildly expanded, not over-volumed.

## During-Race Hydration by Distance and Conditions

Default ranges. Adjust to individual sweat rate.

### Fluid intake during running (ml/hr)

| Distance/duration | Cool (< 15 °C) | Temperate (15–22 °C) | Warm (22–28 °C) | Hot (> 28 °C) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5K (< 25 min) | 0 | 0 | 0–100 | 100–200 |
| 10K (35–60 min) | 0–200 | 100–300 | 200–400 | 300–500 |
| Half marathon (75–150 min) | 200–400 | 300–500 | 400–600 | 500–800 |
| Marathon (3–5 h) | 300–500 | 400–600 | 500–700 | 600–900 |
| Ultra (> 5 h) | 400–600 | 400–700 | 500–800 | 600–1,000 |

**EAH ceilings.** General upper limit: ~800 ml/hr. Slow finishers (> 5 h marathon, > 6 h 50K): ~500 ml/hr. Body mass should drop 1–3% during long events; mass gain = overdrinking.

**Per-aid-station targets** (aid every 1.5–3 km / ~10–15 min): 400 ml/hr → 100 ml/station; 600 ml/hr → 150 ml; 800 ml/hr → 200 ml.

## Sweat Rate Calculation

**Protocol.** (1) Void, weigh nude or in dry minimal clothing to 0.1 kg. (2) Run 60–90 min at race-relevant intensity in target conditions. (3) Record fluid consumed (ml). (4) Towel off, reweigh in same condition. (5) Subtract any urine voided.

```
Sweat loss (L) = pre-weight (kg) - post-weight (kg) + fluid in (L) - urine out (L)
Sweat rate (L/hr) = sweat loss (L) / duration (hr)
```

Example: 70.0 kg pre, 68.5 kg post, drank 0.5 L, no urine, 60 min → 2.0 L/hr.

Test separately at cool (~10 °C), temperate (~18 °C), warm (~25 °C), hot (~30 °C+) and at easy + race-pace intensities. A library of 4–6 measurements covers most race scenarios. Limit single-session test to 45 min – 2 h.

### Typical runner sweat rates

| Category | L/hr |
|---|---|
| Low | < 0.8 |
| Average | 0.8–1.2 |
| High | 1.2–1.8 |
| Very high (heat + hard effort) | > 1.8 |

## Sweat Sodium Concentration

Population range: ~10–90 mmol/L (~230–2,070 mg/L) — ~10-fold variation. Largely genetic; relatively stable within an individual at a given training/acclimation status.

### Classifications

| Category | mmol/L | mg/L |
|---|---|---|
| Low | < 30 | < 700 |
| Medium | 30–50 | 700–1,150 |
| High | 50–70 | 1,150–1,600 |
| Very high ("salty sweater") | > 70 | > 1,600 |

### Salty-sweater indicators (without lab test)

White salt crusts on kit/hat/skin; stinging eyes from sweat; salty taste on lips; history of cramping in long/hot events despite adequate fluid; cystic-fibrosis carrier history (elevated sweat NaCl).

### Testing options

| Method | Description | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pilocarpine iontophoresis (PH Advanced) | Resting forearm sweat via electrical stim; lab-analyzed | Single-site; moderate correlation with exercise whole-body |
| Patch-based (during exercise) | Absorbent patch worn during run; lab-analyzed | Better matches exercise; site matters |
| Whole-body washdown | Research gold standard | Reference method; impractical |
| Wearable sensors (hDrop, Gatorade Gx) | Skin-contact ion-selective electrodes | Improving; validation ongoing |

Pilocarpine single-site tests have known limits — sweat [Na+] varies with intensity, heat, body region, and acclimation. Treat results as class (low/medium/high) rather than exact prescription.

**Variability factors.** Exercise intensity, ambient heat, heat acclimation status (acclimation lowers sweat [Na+]), aerobic capacity, body size, sex, dietary sodium, and protective clothing.

## Sodium Intake During Exercise

| Scenario | Sodium (mg/hr) |
|---|---|
| < 60 min, any conditions | 0–200 (not required) |
| 60–90 min, temperate | 200–400 |
| 60–90 min, hot | 300–600 |
| 90 min – 3 h, temperate | 300–600 |
| 90 min – 3 h, hot/humid | 500–900 |
| > 3 h, temperate | 500–800 |
| > 3 h, hot/humid | 700–1,200 |
| Salty sweater + hot + > 3 h | 1,000–2,000+ |

ACSM baseline: 300–600 mg/hr. Individual loss = sweat rate × sweat [Na+]; replace 50–100% for events > 3 h or extreme heat.

```
Hourly sodium loss (mg) = sweat rate (L/hr) × sweat [Na+] (mg/L)
Example: 1.4 L/hr × 1,200 mg/L = 1,680 mg/hr loss
Replace 50–80% → 840–1,344 mg/hr
```

**Sodium concentration in fluid.** Optimal absorption and EAH prevention: 10–30 mmol/L Na in drink (230–690 mg/L). Standard sports drinks fall in this range; high-Na products (LMNT, PH 1500) exceed it for salty sweaters or pre-loading.

## Hyponatremia (Exercise-Associated)

Per Hew-Butler 2015 consensus. Serum [Na+] < 135 mmol/L during or within 24 h of activity.

| [Na+] (mmol/L) | Classification |
|---|---|
| 130–134 | Mild EAH |
| 125–129 | Moderate EAH |
| < 125 | Severe EAH; encephalopathy risk |
| < 120 | Cerebral edema, seizure, death possible |
| < 115 | Often fatal without intervention |

**Mechanism.** Almost always dilutional: excess hypotonic fluid intake exceeds renal excretion capacity, often combined with non-osmotic ADH/AVP secretion (suppressing urine output despite fluid excess). Sodium loss alone rarely causes EAH; over-drinking is dominant.

### Risk factors

| Factor | Notes |
|---|---|
| Slow finish (> 4 h marathon) | More time to over-consume |
| Female sex | Smaller body mass, higher relative intake |
| Low BMI | Lower fluid distribution volume |
| Inexperienced racers | Following "drink as much as possible" advice |
| Many aid stations on course | More drinking |
| Non-acclimated in cool conditions | Low sweat rate, normal drinking |
| NSAID use pre/during | Impairs renal water excretion |
| Body mass gain during race | Direct evidence of overdrinking |
| Sodium-poor fluid > 1.5 L/hr for hours | Dilutional risk |

### Symptoms

| Stage | Signs |
|---|---|
| Mild | Bloating, puffy hands/face, mild headache, nausea, weight gain |
| Moderate | Vomiting, worse headache, confusion, dizziness, lethargy |
| Severe | Altered mental status, seizures, respiratory distress, coma |

Symptoms overlap with dehydration — but weight gain, puffiness, and clear copious urine flag EAH. Never give hypotonic fluid to a downed runner who has gained weight.

**Prevention.** Cap fluid intake by sweat rate. Body mass should drop 1–3% in long events. Sodium-containing fluids > 90 min. No NSAIDs pre/during racing. Slow runners and first-timers: conservative caps. Drink to thirst is the simplest safe default.

**Treatment (acute, suspected EAH).** Stop fluid intake immediately. Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) is first-line for symptomatic EAH; isotonic saline can worsen it. Field: small volume of high-Na fluid (~100 ml strong oral electrolyte) only if conscious; transport for definitive care.

## Dehydration Symptoms and Performance Impact

Cheuvront & Kenefick (2014). Aerobic performance is consistently impaired at ≥ 2% body mass loss from water deficit, especially in warm/hot conditions (41/60 observations across 34 endurance studies).

| Body mass loss (%) | Effect |
|---|---|
| 1–2% | Negligible to small in cool; small impairment in heat |
| 2–3% | ~3–5% slowdown in endurance running; cognitive blunting |
| 3–4% | 5–10% slowdown; clear cardiovascular strain |
| 4–6% | Severe performance loss; thermoregulation compromised |
| > 6% | Heat illness risk high; medical emergency potential |

Casa et al. (2010) trail-running: ~5% slower at ~2% body mass loss vs. euhydrated.

### Symptoms by severity

| % loss | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | Thirst (variable), mildly elevated HR for given pace |
| 2–3 | Persistent thirst, dry mouth, mild dizziness, perceived exertion up |
| 3–4 | Headache, fatigue, irritability, dark urine, reduced sweat |
| 4–6 | Nausea, weakness, cessation of urine, mental cloudiness |
| > 6 | Cramps, hyperthermia signs, syncope risk |

**Mechanisms.** Reduced plasma volume → ↓ stroke volume → cardiovascular drift (↑ HR at given pace) → ↓ VO2max in heat. Augmented core temperature rise. Glycogen utilization rises. Cognition declines (vigilance, decision-making, mood).

## Heat Acclimatization and Hydration Adaptations

Heat acclimation (lab) and acclimatization (field) yield similar adaptations over ~7–14 days of repeated heat-exposed exercise.

### Adaptation timeline

| Day | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | ↓ HR at given workload; ↓ perceived exertion |
| 3–5 | Plasma volume begins expanding; sweating earlier |
| 5–7 | PV expansion peaks (4–15%); core temp rise blunted |
| 7–10 | Sweat rate ↑; sweat [Na+] ↓ |
| 10–14 | Full adaptation; performance gains in cool + hot |

### Magnitude

| Variable | Change | Typical |
|---|---|---|
| Plasma volume | ↑ | +4–15% (range 3–27%) |
| Sweat rate (heat) | ↑ | +10–20% |
| Sweat [Na+] | ↓ | −20 to −60% |
| HR submax | ↓ | −5 to −15 bpm |
| Core temp rise | ↓ | Reduced |
| VO2max heat | ↑ | +5–8% |
| TT heat / cool | ↑ | +6–8% / +3–6% |

**Decay.** Plasma volume expansion decays substantially within ~7 days of removed heat stress; full decay in 2–4 weeks. Maintenance: 1–2 heat sessions/week.

### Protocols

| Method | Sessions | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Live in heat | Daily | 7–14 days |
| Heat training in cool climate | 5–10 over 10–14 days | 60–90 min |
| Sauna post-run | 4+/week | 20–30 min |
| Hot bath post-run | 4+/week | 30–40 min at 40 °C |

Hydration during heat-acclimation sessions: normal to slightly above; do not deliberately dehydrate.

Capsules separate sodium dosing from fluid volume — useful when fluid demand is low but sodium loss is high (cool weather, salty sweaters), or to bolt onto plain water / aid-station Gatorade.

## Cupless Racing

Increasingly common; eliminates paper-cup waste.

| Format | Description |
|---|---|
| BYO flask | Runners carry soft flask / handheld; stations have refill spouts |
| Provided collapsible cup | Race issues flexible cup (e.g., HydraPak SpeedCup) at pickup |
| Bib-attached cup | Reusable cup clipped to belt or bib |

**HydraPak SkyFlask (and similar).** Soft collapsible TPU folds flat empty; sizes 350/500 ml; Speed handheld has thumb loop (no grip required); wide-mouth flip-top fills fast at race pumps; insulated variants (SkyFlask IT) hold cold ~2 h.

**Tips.** Pre-fill before start. Top up at first station before bottlenecks. One flask for sodium drink, second for plain water/cooling. Test fit and bounce.

## Pre-Cooling for Hot Races

Pre-cooling lowers core temperature before exercise, expanding heat-storage capacity. Effective at > 30 min and > ~28 °C / WBGT > ~24.

| Method | Effect | Practical fit |
|---|---|---|
| Ice slushie / slurry ingestion | Internal cooling via heat of fusion | 7–10 ml/kg in 30 min pre-start |
| Ice vest (10–20 min) | External skin cooling; large surface | Crew-supported events |
| Cold water immersion | −0.3 to −0.5 °C core | Rarely practical pre-race |
| Cold towels / ice dousing | Cheap; modest | Good supplement |
| Combined (vest + slushie + towels) | Largest, most consistent | Best practical protocol |

**Benefit.** Pre-cooling improves endurance in heat by ~5–7% (moderate-to-large pooled effect). At the 2019 World Athletics Championships marathon, ~80% used pre-cooling; ~53% used ice vests.

**Sample hot-marathon protocol.** T-60 min: begin slushie sipping (500–700 ml total). T-45: don ice vest. T-25: light dynamic warm-up (no hard intervals). T-10: remove vest, ice towel on neck/wrists. T-2: final cold-drink sip. Avoid hard warm-ups in heat — they offset pre-cooling gains.

## During-Race Cooling

External + internal cooling during the event reduces thermal strain and slows performance decay.

| Method | Notes |
|---|---|
| Crushed ice in cap | ~5–10 min per fill |
| Ice in bra/sleeves | Targets large vessels |
| Cold sponge / dousing head | Evaporative; works in dry heat |
| Cold water on quads/forearms | Skin cooling; moderate effect |
| Cold drink ingestion | Sustained internal cooling |
| Ice slurry ingestion | Best internal cooling (rare on road) |
| Hand cooling (palms in ice water) | Cools via glabrous-skin AVAs |

**Cold drink temp.** Cold (4–10 °C) drinks reduce thermal strain more than ambient. Slushies/slurries (−1 to 0 °C) deliver largest internal cooling per ml but need crushed-ice infrastructure.

**Caveats.** Avoid water on heels/socks if blistering is a risk. Very cold drinks can cause GI distress; train it. In humid heat, evaporative cooling is blunted — ice contact and ingestion matter more.

## Caffeine and Hydration

The dehydration claim is largely a myth in habituated users at moderate doses.

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| Killer et al. 2014 | 4 mg/kg caffeine in coffee × 4/day × 3 days vs. water: no difference in 24-h urine volume, USG, plasma osmolality, body mass |
| Zhang et al. 2015 (meta-analysis) | Caffeine ≤ 400 mg/day causes no meaningful fluid imbalance in habituated users |
| Acute high-dose (> 500 mg) in non-habituated | Mild transient diuresis; small; offset by fluid in beverage |

**Rules.** Habitual coffee/tea counts toward daily fluid intake. Race-morning caffeine (3–6 mg/kg): not a hydration concern. Caffeine pills/gels during race (100–300 mg/dose, total < 6 mg/kg): no concern. Non-habituated users: small diuretic response possible; train it. Caffeine is ergogenic (3–6 mg/kg ≈ 1–3% endurance improvement); hydration is not a reason to avoid it.

## Alcohol and Recovery

Detrimental to recovery and rehydration at significant doses.

| Domain | Effect | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Diuresis | Suppresses ADH → ↑ urine | Dose-dependent above ~0.5 g/kg |
| Muscle protein synthesis | Suppressed via mTOR | 1.5 g/kg post-exercise: −37%; with protein, −24% |
| Glycogen resynthesis | Reduced when alcohol displaces carbs | Significant > 1 g/kg |
| Sleep | Reduced REM, fragmented | Even small doses |
| Hormones (males) | ↓ testosterone, ↑ cortisol | Dose-dependent |
| Rehydration | Beer/wine retain less fluid than water | Higher %ABV = worse |

| Context | Rule |
|---|---|
| Training-day post-run | < 0.5 g/kg minimal impact (≈ 1 drink for 70 kg) |
| Hard / long run | Avoid same-day alcohol; rehydrate first |
| Race recovery | Replace 150% fluid loss + sodium before any alcohol |
| Race week (taper) | Minimize |
| 24 h post-race | Higher tolerance; still moderate |

Rule: replace fluid losses with water + sodium, then optionally a moderate drink with food. Beer is not a recovery drink — its BHI is no better than water.

## Aid Station Strategy

Most road races: aid every 1.5–3 km (~ every 10–15 min). Marathon majors: 17–20 stations.

| Race | Approach |
|---|---|
| 5K | Skip unless very hot |
| 10K | Optional; sip if > 50 min and warm |
| Half | Every other station in cool; every one in heat |
| Marathon | Every station; alternate water / sports drink |
| Ultra w/ crew | Refills + nutrition swap at major stops |
| Ultra no crew | Drop bags pre-stocked |

**Per-station.** Reach 5–10 m early (target 2nd/3rd volunteer for less crowding). Pinch cup top for sipping. Walk 5–10 sec while drinking if needed. Pre-decide water vs. sports drink per station.

**Sample marathon plan (60 kg, temperate).** Miles 3/6/9/12: 100 ml sports drink. Miles 15/18/21/24: 150 ml alt water/sports drink. Gels at miles 6/12/18 with water. Total ~600 ml + 90 g carbs + ~600 mg Na.

## Hydration in Cold Conditions

Cold hydration is routinely underdone.

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Cold-induced diuresis (CID) | Peripheral vasoconstriction → central blood volume ↑ → ADH ↓ → urine ↑ |
| Suppressed thirst | Central fluid shift blunts osmoreceptor response |
| Respiratory water loss | Cold dry air → more evaporative loss per breath |
| Heavy clothing | Increased metabolic cost → sweating under layers |
| Reduced availability | Frozen bottles; cold drinks unappealing |

**Daily intake.** Active person in cold: 4–6 L/day total fluid, similar to summer. Do not down-regulate baseline.

### During cold-weather running

| Run length | Action |
|---|---|
| < 60 min | Pre-hydrate; usually no need to carry |
| 60–120 min | Carry insulated flask; sip every 15–20 min even if not thirsty |
| > 120 min | Insulated bladder/flask; warm electrolyte drink option |
| Sub-zero conditions | Insulated tube/flask; warm fluid in thermos; sodium more important than sugar |

Insulated flasks (HydraPak SkyFlask IT, Salomon, Nathan) prevent freezing for ~2–4 h. Warm liquids (~30–40 °C) are better tolerated than cold; add electrolytes.

**Post-run cold.** Continue drinking 1–2 h post-run; respiratory and urinary losses persist. Warm broth or hot drink with salt is an effective rehydration vehicle (fluid + sodium + comfort).
