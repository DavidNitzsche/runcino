# 16 — Form, Cadence, and Biomechanics

Reference doc on running gait, form analysis, common errors, and corrective interventions. Conceptual overview. For specific drill prescriptions, see Task 21.

## 1. The Running Gait Cycle

The **gait cycle** is the period from one foot strike (initial contact of a foot with the ground) to the next ipsilateral foot strike (same foot). One cycle = two steps = one stride.

### 1.1 Phase Definitions

| Phase | Definition | % of cycle (running) |
|---|---|---|
| Stance | One foot on the ground | ~40% (decreases with speed) |
| Swing | That same foot off the ground | ~60% (increases with speed) |
| Flight (aerial) | Neither foot on the ground | 2 brief windows per cycle |

Running differs from walking by the presence of flight phase. Walking always has at least one foot down (no flight; ~60% double support).

### 1.2 Stance Sub-phases

| Sub-phase | Event boundaries | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Initial contact | Heel/foot touches ground | Begin loading |
| Braking (absorption) | Initial contact to mid-stance | Decelerate body, store elastic energy |
| Mid-stance | Center of mass directly over foot | Transition |
| Propulsion (push-off) | Mid-stance to toe-off | Accelerate body forward |
| Toe-off | Foot leaves ground | End of stance |

### 1.3 Swing Sub-phases

| Sub-phase | Function |
|---|---|
| Initial swing (toe-off → max knee flexion) | Heel rises toward glute (recovery) |
| Mid-swing | Leg passes under body |
| Terminal swing | Leg extends forward, decelerates pre-contact |

### 1.4 Stance Time Behavior with Speed

As pace increases:
- Stance time decreases (down to ~150 ms for elites at race pace).
- Flight time changes less.
- Stance share of total cycle drops from ~40% (jog) toward ~22% (sprint).

## 2. The 180 SPM Myth and the Actual Cadence Research

### 2.1 Origin of the 180 Number

Coach **Jack Daniels** observed runners at the 1984 Los Angeles Olympics. Across distance events from 800 m to marathon, all but one of 46 athletes ran at ≥180 steps per minute (spm). This single observation, made on world-class athletes at race pace (i.e., sub-5:00/mile), became the populist "180 spm rule."

Daniels never prescribed 180 as a universal target. He has stated his intent was to discourage extremely low cadences, not to push every runner to a single number.

### 2.2 What Heiderscheit's Research Actually Shows

Heiderscheit et al. (2011, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*) is the foundational cadence study. Forty-five recreational runners ran at preferred cadence and at ±5% and ±10% manipulations.

Key findings:
- Increasing cadence **5%** above preferred reduced energy absorption at the knee by ~20%.
- Increasing cadence **10%** above preferred reduced energy absorption at the knee by ~34%.
- Vertical center-of-mass excursion and step length both decreased.
- Hip and knee loading dropped substantially.
- Effects on running economy were not the goal; the loading reductions are the headline.

The intervention in the literature is **a relative shift (5–10% above habitual)**, not an absolute target. A runner habituating at 158 spm pushed to 174 spm has gained the documented benefit; pushing them to 180 spm offers no evidence-based additional gain and may degrade economy.

### 2.3 Individual Variation

Cadence at any given pace is influenced by:

| Factor | Direction of influence on cadence |
|---|---|
| Leg length (shorter) | Higher cadence at given pace |
| Pace (faster) | Higher cadence |
| Fatigue (later in run) | Cadence drops |
| Footwear (super shoes) | Cadence often slightly lower |
| Surface (uphill) | Cadence rises; stride length shortens |
| Surface (downhill) | Cadence often drops; stride lengthens |

A 5'4" runner at 9:00/mile and a 6'2" runner at 9:00/mile cannot share the same cadence — geometry forbids it.

## 3. Cadence by Pace

Typical ranges for trained adult distance runners. Use as anchor points, not prescriptions.

| Effort | Pace example (mid-pack) | Typical SPM range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Walking | n/a | 100–120 | For reference |
| Easy / recovery | 9:30–11:00/mi | 160–172 | Often where habituation issues hide |
| Aerobic / steady | 8:30–9:30/mi | 165–176 | |
| Marathon pace | 7:30–8:30/mi | 170–180 | |
| Threshold / tempo | 6:45–7:30/mi | 174–184 | |
| 5K race | 5:45–6:30/mi | 178–188 | |
| Mile / 1500 m race | 4:45–5:30/mi | 184–196 | |
| Sprint (200–400 m) | sub-3:30/mi | 200–230 | |

**Diagnostic rule:** if easy-pace cadence is below 160 spm and the runner is of average height (170–180 cm) or shorter, suspect overstriding. If cadence is below 155 spm at any pace for a non-tall runner, this is a strong red flag.

For tall runners (>185 cm / 6'1"), expect cadence 5–8 spm lower than average at the same pace.

## 4. The Speed Equation: Stride Length × Cadence

### 4.1 Formula

```
Speed (m/s)   = stride_length (m) × stride_frequency (strides/s)
Speed (m/s)   = step_length (m)   × step_frequency (steps/s)
Pace (min/km) = 1000 / (60 × speed_m_s)
```

A **step** is one foot strike to the next opposite foot strike. A **stride** is one foot strike to the next ipsilateral foot strike. **Stride = 2 steps.** **Cadence** in this doc means steps per minute (spm) unless noted.

### 4.2 Worked Example

Runner at 170 spm with average step length 1.20 m:
- 170 / 60 = 2.833 steps/s
- 2.833 × 1.20 = 3.40 m/s
- Pace = 1000 / (60 × 3.40) = 4:54/km (≈ 7:53/mi)

### 4.3 The Two Levers

To run faster, **only** two things can change: cadence, step length, or both. From a coaching standpoint:

| Lever | How it grows | Risk profile |
|---|---|---|
| Cadence | Improves with cueing, neuromuscular drills, music tempo, fitness | Low — small relative shifts are well-tolerated |
| Step length | Grows from hip extension power, ankle stiffness, posterior-chain force | Higher — pushed by reaching forward = overstriding |

Coaching maxim: **earn more step length from behind the body (push), not in front of it (reach).**

## 5. Overstriding

### 5.1 Definition

**Overstriding** = the foot lands significantly in front of the runner's center of mass (COM), typically with a near-extended knee and the shin angled forward (toes pointing up).

It is identified by the **horizontal distance from foot at initial contact to COM (vertical projection of pelvis)**, not by absolute stride length.

### 5.2 Identification (video, sagittal view)

| Cue | Threshold suggestive of overstriding |
|---|---|
| Knee at initial contact | Near-full extension (>170°) |
| Shin angle at initial contact | Tilted forward of vertical (toe well above heel) |
| Foot horizontal offset from COM | More than ~15–20 cm in front |
| Audible foot strike | Loud heel slap |
| Foot strike | Often pronounced rearfoot |

### 5.3 Consequences

- Braking impulse increases 20–50% per step.
- Vertical loading rate rises; impact peak more pronounced.
- Hamstring late-swing peak forces rise (eccentric strain injury risk).
- Tibiofemoral and patellofemoral compressive loads rise.
- Documented associations: tibial stress reactions/fractures, patellofemoral pain, anterior tibialis overuse, exertional compartment syndrome (in some populations).

### 5.4 Correction

Primary intervention: increase cadence 5–10%. Mechanically, a higher step rate at fixed pace forces the foot to land closer to under the COM. This is the highest-leverage single change in gait retraining.

## 6. Foot Strike Pattern

### 6.1 Definitions

| Pattern | Initial contact | Strike index |
|---|---|---|
| Rearfoot strike (RFS) / heel | Heel | 0–33% |
| Midfoot strike (MFS) | Whole foot / just behind ball | 34–66% |
| Forefoot strike (FFS) | Ball of foot; heel may not touch | 67–100% |

Strike index = position of center of pressure at initial contact, normalized to foot length.

### 6.2 Population Prevalence (Larson et al. 2011)

936 runners at the 10 km mark of a road half/full-marathon (recreational and sub-elite):

| Pattern | % |
|---|---|
| Rearfoot | 88.9% |
| Midfoot | 3.4% |
| Forefoot | 1.8% |
| Asymmetric | 5.9% |

Asymmetry rate dropped from 10 km to 32 km — runners converged toward bilateral RFS as fatigue accumulated.

### 6.3 Daoud / Lieberman 2012 — Injury Rates

52 Harvard XC runners: 69% habitual RFS, 31% habitual FFS. Habitual rearfoot strikers had **~2× the rate of repetitive stress injuries** vs habitual forefoot strikers. Traumatic injury rates did not differ. Caveat: small, retrospective, single team.

### 6.4 Hamill et al. 2017 — Systematic Review

Three common pro-FFS claims tested:
1. FFS more economical → **not supported**.
2. FFS reduces impact / loading rate → mixed; impact shifts to ankle/Achilles, doesn't disappear.
3. FFS reduces injury risk → **not supported as a generalization**.

Some individual runners benefit; predicting which ones is not yet possible.

### 6.5 Coach's Synthesis

| Statement | Evidence |
|---|---|
| "Heel striking causes injury" | False as stated. Overstriding causes injury; heel strike often co-occurs but is not the mechanism. |
| "Forefoot striking is faster" | Correlation, not causation. Faster speeds make RFS mechanically awkward. |
| "Switching to FFS reduces injury" | Not supported by RCTs. Increases Achilles/calf/metatarsal injury risk during transition. |
| "Knee load decreases with FFS; ankle load increases" | True. Injuries shift, do not vanish. |

**Default:** do not change foot strike pattern absent specific indication. Address overstriding via cadence first.

## 7. Ground Contact Time (GCT)

**Ground contact time** is the duration the foot is in contact with the ground per step, in milliseconds.

### 7.1 Typical Ranges

| Population | Effort | GCT (ms) |
|---|---|---|
| Recreational | Easy run | 250–300 |
| Recreational | 5K race | 220–260 |
| Trained sub-elite | 5K race | 190–220 |
| Elite distance | Race pace | 155–200 |
| Elite at sub-4:00 mile pace | Race | 130–160 |

GCT is highly pace-dependent. A given runner's GCT at easy pace is naturally 50–80 ms longer than at threshold pace. Comparisons must control for pace.

### 7.2 Relationship to Performance

Folland et al. and Santos-Concejero reported that shorter stance time correlates with better running economy in mixed groups. The relationship is not strictly linear — extremely short GCT requires very high vertical force, which may itself be costly. Optimum exists in a band, not at a minimum.

### 7.3 Reducing GCT

Realistic block-level expectation: 10–20 ms reduction at a given pace over 12–16 weeks. Levers:

| Intervention | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Plyometrics (pogos, bounds, hops) | Ankle and reactive stiffness |
| Heavy strength (calf, posterior chain) | Force application rate |
| Cadence increase 5–10% | Mechanically shortens GCT |
| Strides, short hill sprints | Fast neural drive |

### 7.4 GCT Asymmetry

Sustained side-to-side GCT asymmetry >3–5% is a flag for unilateral weakness or residual injury compensation. Investigate before chasing GCT reduction.

## 8. Vertical Oscillation

**Vertical oscillation (VO)** = vertical displacement of the runner's center of mass (or proxy: torso/sacrum) per step, in cm.

| Population / context | VO (cm) |
|---|---|
| Elite at race pace | 4–7 |
| Trained at marathon pace | 6–9 |
| Recreational at easy pace | 8–11 |
| "Bouncy" runners | >11 |

**Vertical ratio** = VO / stride length × 100. Lower is more efficient.

| Quality | Vertical ratio (%) |
|---|---|
| Excellent | <6 |
| Good | 6–8 |
| Average | 8–10 |
| Poor | >10 |

Excess VO is metabolically expensive: each cm of bounce lifts body mass against gravity with no horizontal payoff. Elite runners have systematically lower VO at the same speed than sub-elites (Folland; pace-controlled studies). VO and GCT trade off — you cannot minimize both simultaneously. Goal: lower-than-current for inefficient runners, not minimum possible.

## 9. Hip Drop (Trendelenburg / Pelvic Drop)

**Pelvic drop / Trendelenburg sign** = during single-leg stance, the swing-side pelvis drops below horizontal, indicating insufficient hip abductor (primarily gluteus medius) torque on the stance side. Described by Friedrich Trendelenburg, 1895.

### 9.1 Identification (video, posterior view)

| Severity | Pelvic drop on swing side |
|---|---|
| Normal | 0–4° |
| Mild | 5–7° |
| Moderate | 8–10° |
| Severe | >10° |

If a line across the iliac crests tilts visibly toward the swing leg at mid-stance, hip drop is present.

### 9.2 Associated Injuries

ITB syndrome, patellofemoral pain syndrome, greater trochanteric pain, tibial stress injuries, ACL risk in cutting sports.

### 9.3 Correction Pathway

1. Strength gluteus medius (single-leg bridges, side-lying abduction, Copenhagen plank, single-leg squat with pelvis-level cue).
2. Motor control: single-leg stance, step-down progressions.
3. Cadence increase reduces single-leg stance loading.
4. Verify post-intervention with repeat video.

See Task 21 for drills.

## 10. Cross-Over Gait (Scissoring / Tightrope Gait)

**Cross-over gait** = the foot at initial contact lands at, on, or across the body's midline, producing a narrow base of support.

### 10.1 Identification (posterior view video)

| Pattern | Description |
|---|---|
| Normal | Feet land ~5–10 cm lateral to midline |
| Mild | Tracks slightly overlap |
| Severe | Feet cross midline ("tightrope") |

Visual cue: imagine a line beneath the runner's spine. Feet should land on either side, not on or across it.

### 10.2 Consequences

Forces increased hip adduction and internal rotation. ITB strain rises. Associated injuries: tibial stress fractures, ITB syndrome, lateral knee pain, medial tibial stress syndrome. Compensations: trunk lean, lateral pelvic shift, arm crossover.

### 10.3 Correction

| Intervention | Effect |
|---|---|
| Hip abductor strengthening | Reduces hip adduction angle |
| Cue "run on either side of a center line" | Kinesthetic reference |
| Cadence increase 5–10% | Reduces peak hip adduction |
| Foot-strike-zone tape on treadmill | Real-time feedback |

## 11. Arm Swing

### 11.1 Principles

| Element | Default |
|---|---|
| Driver | Shoulders (gleno-humeral), not elbows or hands |
| Plane | Sagittal (forward/back), not transverse (across body) |
| Elbow angle | ~90°, opening slightly on backswing |
| Hands | Relaxed, loose fist, no clenching |
| Crossing midline | Avoided — fingers should not pass past the sternum |
| Backswing extent | Hand to hip pocket / iliac crest |
| Forward swing extent | Hand to chest height (slower paces) → chin (faster) |

### 11.2 Why Arm Swing Matters

Arellano and Kram (2014) showed swinging arms reduce metabolic cost ~3–4% vs arms restrained. Mechanism: arms counter-rotate against the legs, controlling angular momentum so the trunk doesn't rotate excessively. Modeling confirms active swing reduces torso angular motion and metabolic energy.

### 11.3 Cross-Body Arm Swing — Diagnostic

| Sign | Likely cause |
|---|---|
| Hands cross midline | Tight pectorals, weak mid-back, compensating for pelvic rotation |
| Shoulders shrugged | Tension; cue "drop the traps" |
| Hands clenched | General tension; cue "potato chip in each hand" |
| Asymmetric arm swing | Trunk asymmetry, prior-injury compensation |

If arms cross midline severely, suspect the **legs are crossing midline first** and arms are compensating. Address the lower-half pattern; arms often follow.

## 12. Forward Lean

**Forward lean** = anterior tilt from the **ankles**, maintaining a straight line ankle → hip → head. *Not* a hip-flexion bow.

### 12.1 Two Common Errors

| Error | Description | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Bending at the waist (hip flexion) | Trunk tilts; pelvis/legs do not | Glutes deactivate; quad-dominance worsens; lumbar load rises |
| Backward lean (extension at hips) | Common in fatigued / core-weak runners | Heel-striking ahead of body; braking; lumbar compression |

Trunk lean of ~5–10° from vertical is common in efficient runners. Larger postural lean (15°+) is associated with **reduced** running economy (Teng & Powers) due to greater extensor reliance and altered hip geometry.

Forward lean is often a **result** of strong core, glute drive, and momentum, not something to consciously force. Cueing "lean from the ankles" works for some; "tall posture, eyes ahead" works for others.

## 13. Head and Neck Position

| Element | Default |
|---|---|
| Chin | Neutral, not jutting |
| Eyes | 10–20 m ahead, not at feet |
| Neck | Long, occiput up and back |
| Jaw | Loose |

Looking down increases cervical/thoracic flexion → trunk flexes at hips → quads engage more, glutes less → cascading form breakdown.

## 14. Posterior Chain Activation

| Muscle group | Primary running role |
|---|---|
| Gluteus maximus | Hip extension during push-off; pelvis stability |
| Gluteus medius | Pelvis level in single-leg stance; hip abduction |
| Hamstrings | Eccentric deceleration of swing leg; hip extension assist |
| Calves (gastroc/soleus) | Plantarflexion power; elastic energy storage |
| Erector spinae | Trunk extension/stability |

These muscles produce horizontal propulsion (push-off) and stabilize the pelvis and trunk. Efficient runners exhibit visible glute contraction at toe-off, hip extension reaching neutral or slightly beyond, heel kick toward glute in early swing, and calf engagement at push-off.

## 15. Anterior Chain Dominance (Quad-Dominant Runners)

**Quad dominance** = quadriceps perform a disproportionate share of propulsion and shock absorption that should be shared with glutes and hamstrings.

### 15.1 Identification

| Sign | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| Heel strike well in front of body | Quad/hip-flexor reaching, not glute push |
| Quads fatigue first; descents painful | Quads loaded eccentrically without glute support |
| Limited hip extension at toe-off | Tight hip flexors, dormant glutes |
| Anterior pelvic tilt at standing | Tight hip flexors, weak glutes/abs |
| Patellofemoral pain | Quad overuse, lateral tracking |
| "Sit-on-quads" posture | Pelvis behind feet, knees doing the work |

### 15.2 Causes

Sedentary posture (tight hip flexors, dormant glutes); knee-dominant strength bias (squats, leg press without hip thrust/RDL); cycling background; chronic overstriding; restricted hip extension.

### 15.3 Corrections

| Intervention | Target |
|---|---|
| Hip thrust, glute bridge | Glute max strength in extension |
| RDL, single-leg RDL | Eccentric hamstring + hip hinge |
| Hip flexor mobility (couch stretch) | Hip extension ROM |
| Pre-run glute activation (band/monster walks) | Wake the glutes |
| Cadence increase | Reduces overstride |
| Cue: "push the ground behind you" | Recruits posterior chain |

Folland and others identify hip-extension torque as a predictor of efficient mechanics; balanced posterior chain correlates with improved economy at race pace.

Drill prescriptions are detailed in **Task 21 — Drills for Problem X**. Categories at a glance:

| Drill family | Targets |
|---|---|
| A-skip, B-skip, A-march | Knee drive, foot strike under COM, posture |
| High-knees | Hip flexor + cadence |
| Butt kicks | Hamstring recovery / heel-to-glute |
| Strides (15–20 s near-max) | Neuromuscular cadence + economy |
| Pogo hops, ankling | Reactive stiffness, ankle GCT reduction |
| Bounding | Stride length / posterior chain power |
| Karaoke / grapevine | Hip mobility, lateral stability |
| Single-leg drills (skip, hop) | Hip stability, glute medius |

## 17. Form Changes with Fatigue

Late-race kinematic breakdown is documented. Chan-Roper et al. (marathon, 2013) compared kilometer 8 to kilometer 40:

| Variable | Direction late-race |
|---|---|
| Contact time (GCT) | Increased ~13% |
| Cadence | Decreased in non-elites; elites preserve |
| Peak knee flexion at support | Decreased ~3% (stiffer, less shock absorption) |
| Peak hip extension during swing | Decreased ~28% (less posterior drive) |
| Peak hip flexion during swing | Increased ~7% |
| Trunk lean | Increased (slumping forward) |

Pattern: fatigued runners shift to shorter, slower, more anterior, more compressed mechanics. Pelvis rotates less; trunk slumps; hip extension fades; GCT lengthens. In non-elite marathoners, late-race slowdown is driven primarily by decreased step length rather than decreased cadence; elites preserve step length better.

Coaching implications:
- Late-race breakdown is fatigue-induced, not a form flaw to drill out preemptively.
- Strength training and long-run time-on-feet are the main interventions.
- Drill form when tired (e.g., 4 × 100 m strides in the last mile of a long run).
- Some kinematic deviations persist 2 days post-marathon — recovery is biomechanical, not just subjective.

## 18. Form Changes with Super Shoes

Carbon-plated, high-stack midsole footwear ("super shoes") alters mechanics:

| Variable | Direction in super shoes |
|---|---|
| Running economy | Improved ~4% (population mean) |
| Peak vertical GRF | Higher |
| Cadence | Slightly lower |
| Flight time | Longer |
| Ankle plantarflexion ROM | Reduced (plate constrains) |
| Calf muscle work | Reduced |
| Hamstring + glute load | Increased (work transferred up chain) |
| Metatarsal bending work | Reduced |

Implications: ~10% of runners are non- or negative responders. Bone stress injury risk shifted to navicular and proximal sites is emerging in case series. Easy-day mileage in super shoes may delay normal calf adaptation.

**Coaching rule:** use super shoes for race-effort and key sessions; rotate footwear; preserve regular shoe time for posterior-chain loading and sensory feedback.

## 19. When to Change Form: Default Position

**Default: do not change form.** Most healthy runners run in a pattern reflecting their anatomy, training history, and movement habits. The body self-organizes toward an efficient solution given the constraints (Cavanagh; motor learning research).

Forced form changes risk: disrupting an efficient pattern, loading novel structures (e.g., calf/Achilles in an FFS transition), reduced economy, and cognitive load that interferes with effort regulation.

**Form is downstream of strength, mobility, and cadence in most cases.** Address those upstream first; form often self-corrects.

## 20. When Form Change Is Warranted

| Indication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Chronic injury unresponsive to conventional management | Last-resort biomechanical intervention |
| Severe overstriding (cadence well below norms for height) | Cadence retraining is high-value, low-risk |
| Severe pelvic drop with associated lateral injury | Strength + cueing + cadence |
| Severe cross-over gait with associated injuries | Direct retraining + strength |
| Habitually clenched or asymmetric upper body | Cueing intervention is cheap, low-risk |
| Adolescent / growth phase (rapid limb-length changes) | Movement is being re-learned anyway; coachable window |
| Competitive runner with large performance ceiling | Targeted, evidence-based intervention with monitoring |
| Post-injury return with documented compensations | Re-pattern before re-loading |

### 20.1 Highest-Yield Single Intervention

Across the gait-retraining literature: **a 5–10% cadence increase**. It is the single intervention with the most consistent biomechanical and injury-rate evidence (Heiderscheit; Willy; subsequent reviews). It addresses overstriding, hip adduction, knee load, and vertical excursion all in one cue.

### 20.2 Faded Feedback

Motor learning research (Willy et al.) shows that **faded feedback** (initially constant, then progressively withdrawn) outperforms continuous cueing for durable retention of the new pattern. Practical structure: 4 weeks of in-session feedback (metronome, visual mirror) → 4 weeks of intermittent → 4 weeks unprompted.

## 21. Self-Analysis: Video Methodology

### 21.1 Setup

| Parameter | Specification |
|---|---|
| Camera | Smartphone at ≥120 fps (60 fps minimum) |
| Camera height | Hip height of runner |
| Distance | 5–7 m for sagittal; 3–5 m for frontal/posterior |
| Background | Plain wall, fixed reference if possible |
| Lighting | Even, no backlighting |

### 21.2 Required Views

| View | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Sagittal (side) — both sides | Foot strike, knee/shin angle, hip extension at toe-off, trunk lean, head |
| Frontal (head-on) | Knee valgus, hip drop, foot midline crossing, arm swing |
| Posterior (behind) | Pelvic drop, cross-over gait, butt kick height |

### 21.3 Capture Protocol

1. Warm up 10 min — analyze warm gait.
2. Capture 30+ s at each pace of interest (easy + tempo + threshold).
3. Capture 30+ s while moderately fatigued (e.g., end of long run) — fatigue exposes flaws.
4. Note pace, surface, shoe for each clip.

### 21.4 Frame-by-Frame Checklist

| Event | Check |
|---|---|
| Initial contact | Foot under or in front of COM? Knee bent? Strike pattern? |
| Mid-stance | Pelvis level? Knee tracking over foot? |
| Toe-off | Hip extension at neutral/beyond? Glute activated? |
| Swing | Heel kicked toward glute? Knee high enough? |
| Throughout | Trunk lean? Arms sagittal? Head still? Bounce? |

## 22. Treadmill vs Overground Gait Analysis

Van Hooren et al. (2020) meta-analysis: treadmill running is largely comparable to overground for most outcomes. Significant differences:

| Variable | Treadmill vs overground |
|---|---|
| Stride length | ~3% longer, less variable |
| Ground contact time | ~12% shorter (some studies) |
| Foot strike | Tendency more posterior (more RFS) |
| Variability | Lower (more uniform) |
| Heel velocity post-toe-off | ~19% lower |
| Wind resistance | Absent (use 1% incline at faster paces) |

| Use | Better tool |
|---|---|
| Pace-controlled standardized analysis | Treadmill |
| Naturalistic mechanics | Overground |
| Pre-race specific training | Overground |
| Injured runner first return | Treadmill (lower variability) |

For diagnostic gait analysis, treadmill gives controlled, repeatable conditions but understates variability and slightly distorts foot strike. **A complete picture uses both** when feasible. Treadmill belts decelerate at foot strike and accelerate at toe-off (not perfectly constant), introducing subtle propulsion differences from overground.

## 23. Quick-Reference Diagnostic Table

| Observation | Likely problem | Primary intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Cadence <160 spm at easy pace, average-height runner | Overstriding | Cadence +5–10% |
| Foot lands well in front of body, knee straight | Overstriding | Cadence +5–10%; cue "land under hips" |
| Heel slap audible | Overstriding + ankle stiffness deficit | Cadence; ankling drills |
| Pelvis drops on swing side (>5°) | Hip abductor weakness | Glute medius strength + cadence |
| Knee collapses inward at stance | Hip abductor + external rotator weakness | Strength + neuromuscular drills |
| Feet land on or across midline | Cross-over gait | Cueing + hip strength + cadence |
| Hands cross midline, shoulders rotate | Tight pectorals + trunk compensation | Mobility + arm-swing cues |
| Excessive vertical bounce | High VO; quad-dominance | Cadence; posterior chain strength |
| Trunk slumps forward (hip flexion) | Core weakness, fatigue | Core work; cue "tall posture" |
| Heel does not rise toward glute in swing | Stiff hip flexors / dormant hamstrings | Mobility + butt kicks + RDL |
| Limited hip extension at toe-off | Quad-dominance / tight hip flexors | Hip thrust, mobility, cadence |
| GCT >280 ms at threshold pace | Reactive stiffness deficit | Plyometrics + strides |
| Asymmetric GCT/cadence/stride | Compensation, possible residual injury | Investigate before retraining |
