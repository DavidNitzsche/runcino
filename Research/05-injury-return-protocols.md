# 05 — Injury-Specific Return-to-Run Protocols

> Reference for an AI running coach. Generic, evidence-backed protocols a coach can suggest — not diagnostic or medical advice. Every injury entry includes red-flag criteria for medical referral. Coaches must default to medical referral when red flags appear.

---

## 1. General Principles

### 1.1 The Walk-Run Protocol Structure

The walk-run protocol is the universal scaffold for return-to-running (RTR) after any injury that has required a layoff longer than ~2 weeks. It allows graded mechanical loading while limiting cumulative impact stress per session.

**Structure**
- A session is a continuous block (e.g., 30 minutes) composed of repeating run-walk intervals.
- Run interval starts short (1 minute) and progresses; walk interval starts long (4-5 minutes) and shrinks.
- Total session time generally stays constant or grows slowly while the run-walk ratio shifts toward more running.
- Frequency: every other day during early stages (alternate-day rule) — the off-day is for tissue adaptation and pain monitoring.

**Generic walk-run progression template (8 stages)**

| Stage | Run (min) | Walk (min) | Repeats | Total run time | Sessions/wk |
|------:|----------:|-----------:|--------:|---------------:|------------:|
| 1 | 1 | 4 | 5 | 5 min | 3 |
| 2 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 10 min | 3 |
| 3 | 3 | 2 | 5 | 15 min | 3 |
| 4 | 4 | 2 | 4 | 16 min | 3-4 |
| 5 | 5 | 1 | 4 | 20 min | 3-4 |
| 6 | 8 | 2 | 3 | 24 min | 3-4 |
| 7 | 12 | 2 | 2 | 24 min | 3-4 |
| 8 | 25-30 (continuous) | — | — | 25-30 min | 4 |

**Rules of thumb**
- Spend at least 2 sessions at each stage before progressing.
- If pain or swelling rises, drop back one stage and re-attempt after 48-72 h.
- Surface: flat, firm, predictable (treadmill, paved path) for stages 1-5; soft surface, rolling terrain only after stage 6.
- Pace: easy/conversational only. No tempo, intervals, or hills until full continuous-running base is restored.

### 1.2 Pain Monitoring Rules

**Three-rule system (modified from Silbernagel 2007 and tendinopathy traffic-light frameworks)**

1. **The 0-10 in-session rule.** Pain during the run, measured on a 0-10 numeric rating scale (NRS, where 0 = no pain, 10 = worst imaginable pain).
   - 0-2: green. Continue, progress next session.
   - 3-5: amber. Tolerable. Hold current load; do not progress.
   - 6+: red. Stop the session. Drop a stage next attempt.

2. **The 24-hour rule.** Pain the morning after the session, and stiffness on the first 5-10 minutes of weight-bearing.
   - Same or better than baseline within 24 h: tolerated. Progress permitted.
   - Worse but resolves by 48 h: hold load; repeat the same stage.
   - Still worse at 48 h: drop a stage; reassess.

3. **The location rule.** Pain must be at the original injury site only. New pain elsewhere — especially if compensatory (other knee, opposite hip) — means form is breaking down; reduce volume or stop.

**Notes**
- The 24-hour rule applies primarily to tendinopathy and overuse soft-tissue injury. For bone stress injury, the threshold is stricter: any pain during running on a healing bone is a red light.
- Day-of-session pain ≤ 3/10 is the broadest evidence-based threshold (Silbernagel pain-monitoring model).

### 1.3 Cross-Training vs. Complete Rest

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Acute injury, first 24-72 h | Relative rest. Avoid aggravating motion. Walking OK if pain-free. |
| Soft-tissue injury beyond 72 h | Cross-train at intensities that don't reproduce symptoms. |
| Tendinopathy (any stage) | Complete rest is contraindicated — tendons require load to remodel. |
| Low-risk BSI | Non-impact only (pool, cycle, elliptical) until pain-free walking ≥7 days. |
| High-risk BSI | Non-weight-bearing only until clinician clears. |
| Post-surgical | Follow surgeon's protocol. |

Pool running (deep-water with flotation belt) preserves VO2max and running-specific neuromuscular patterns; trained runners can maintain aerobic fitness for 4-6 weeks. Elliptical and cycling preserve VO2max but are less running-specific. Match sessions to normal training profile (long aerobic 60-90 min; intervals 4-6 × 3-5 min hard).

### 1.4 Return-to-Volume Guidelines

After clearing the walk-run progression and re-establishing continuous easy running:
- **10% rule** (convention, not strongly evidence-supported but a reasonable safety margin): weekly mileage +≤10%/week.
- **Down weeks**: every 3rd-4th week, reduce volume 20-30% to consolidate.
- **Pre-injury volume** typically takes 4-8 weeks of continuous running to reach. Heuristic: weeks off ≈ weeks to rebuild base.
- **Long run** ≤30% of weekly volume during rebuild.

### 1.5 Return-to-Intensity Guidelines

**Volume before intensity, always.** Sequence:
1. Continuous easy running re-established (≥3 weeks at pre-injury easy volume).
2. Strides (4-6 × 15-20 sec controlled fast, full recovery).
3. Tempo / threshold (begin 2 × 8 min).
4. VO2max intervals.
5. Hills and sprints (last — peak impact load).

Each phase ≥1-2 weeks before adding the next. Tendinopathy and BSI need 4+ weeks of continuous easy before strides; 6+ weeks before threshold.

### 1.6 Red Flags Requiring Medical Evaluation (Consolidated)

Refer to a clinician (sports medicine physician, orthopedic surgeon, or specialist physiotherapist) when any of the following are present.

**Universal**
- Focal, point-tender bone pain worsening with weight-bearing, hopping, or single-leg jump (suspect BSI).
- Night pain or rest pain.
- Visible swelling, deformity, bruising, or palpable muscle/tendon defect.
- Sudden audible "pop" with inability to continue (suspect rupture).
- Strength or ROM deficit >50% vs. uninjured side.
- Numbness, paraesthesia, or weakness in a dermatomal/myotomal pattern.
- Symptoms persisting ≥6 weeks despite appropriate load management.
- Symptoms worsening rather than improving.

**REDs / triad**
- Amenorrhea or menstrual irregularity in female runners.
- Recurrent stress fractures (>1).
- Disordered eating signs, unintended weight loss, low BMI.
- BSI in non-weight-bearing bone (rib, sacrum, pelvis) — suggests low energy availability.

**Joint-specific**
- Knee: locking, giving way, true effusion.
- Hip: C-sign, restricted internal rotation in flexion (intra-articular pathology).
- Ankle/foot: inability to weight-bear immediately after acute injury (Ottawa rules).
- Calf: unilateral swelling, warmth, calf tenderness — rule out DVT.

**Cauda equina / spinal (emergency, ED — not clinic)**
- Bilateral leg weakness or numbness.
- Saddle anaesthesia.
- New bowel/bladder dysfunction.

## 2. Plantar Fasciitis / Plantar Fasciopathy

### 2.1 Pathophysiology
Persistent overload of the plantar aponeurosis at its calcaneal origin. Histology shows a degenerative process (fasciopathy), not acute inflammation — "fasciitis" is mechanistically inaccurate.

### 2.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Sharp pain at medial calcaneal tubercle.
- Worst with first morning steps / post-prolonged-sitting (post-static dyskinesia).
- Reduces during activity, rebounds afterward.
- Tenderness on direct palpation; reproduced by toe dorsiflexion (windlass test).

### 2.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain >4/10 during weight-bearing walking.
- Antalgic gait (limping).
- Acute exacerbation within 72 h of attempted running.

### 2.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Week | Loading work | Running |
|------|--------------|---------|
| 1-2 | High-load calf raises with towel under toes (windlass-loaded), 3 × 12 RM, every other day. Walking only. | None |
| 3-4 | Progress to 4 × 10 RM. Add isometric holds. | Walking 30-45 min daily as tolerated. |
| 5-6 | 5 × 8 RM. Begin pool running 2-3×/wk. | Walk-run intervals stage 1-2 if morning pain ≤2/10. |
| 7-8 | Maintain HSR strength work. | Walk-run stages 3-5. |
| 9-10 | Strength 2-3×/wk (5 × 8 RM). | Walk-run stages 6-8 → continuous easy. |
| 11-12+ | Strength 2×/wk maintenance. | Add volume; intensity later. |

**HSR (high-load strength training, Rathleff 2015)**: heel raises with toes dorsiflexed over a rolled towel, performed slowly (3 sec up, 2 sec hold, 3 sec down), every second day. The towel pre-tensions the plantar fascia via the windlass mechanism.

### 2.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Calf, intrinsic foot, soft-tissue work to gastroc-soleus.
- Supportive shoe with cushioned heel; avoid minimalist during recovery; avoid barefoot on hard floors.
- Night splints or low-Dye taping for symptom modulation only — not curative.

### 2.6 Criteria to Progress
- Morning pain ≤2/10.
- Pain-free unilateral heel raise × 25.
- No symptom rebound 24 h post-loading.

### 2.7 Medical Referral
- Failure to improve over 6-12 weeks of structured loading.
- Suspected calcaneal stress fracture (squeeze test positive, focal bone pain).
- Numbness/burning into arch (suspect Baxter's nerve entrapment, tarsal tunnel).
- Bilateral or atypical presentation in a young athlete (consider seronegative spondyloarthropathy).

## 3. Achilles Tendinopathy — Mid-Portion

### 3.1 Pathophysiology
Loading-related pathology of the Achilles tendon 2-7 cm proximal to insertion. Cook-Purdam continuum: reactive → disrepair → degenerative. Disorganized collagen, increased ground substance, neovascularization — not classic inflammation.

### 3.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Pain/stiffness 2-7 cm above heel, worse with first morning steps.
- Warm-up phenomenon: better during run, worse after.
- Tendon thickening/nodularity; tender on heel raise/hop.
- Royal London test: tender at rest, less tender in max dorsiflexion.

### 3.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain >5/10 during loading.
- Inability to perform a single-leg heel raise.
- Suspicion of partial or complete rupture (palpable gap, positive Thompson test, sudden loss of push-off).

### 3.4 Graded Return Protocol

The protocol uses Silbernagel's pain-monitoring model: pain ≤5/10 during exercise, no rise in morning stiffness or pain over baseline.

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Pain control | 1-2 | Isometric calf holds, 5 × 45 sec, 2-3×/day. Pain ≤3/10. | Off running. Pool running OK. |
| 2 — Heavy slow load | 3-6 | HSR calf raise with knee straight + bent (gastroc + soleus), 3 × 15 → 4 × 12 → 4 × 10 → 4 × 8 (progressing weekly). Eccentric phase 3 sec; heel drops below step. 3×/wk. | Walk-run stages 1-3 if 24-h rule clean. |
| 3 — Energy storage | 7-10 | Continue HSR. Add hops, double-leg → single-leg, low to higher reactive. | Walk-run 4-7 → continuous easy 25-40 min. |
| 4 — Sport-specific | 11-12+ | Plyometrics, bounding, sprint mechanics. | Add strides, then tempo, then intervals. |

### 3.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Posterior chain strength (glutes, hamstrings).
- Calf flexibility (mid-portion tolerates dorsiflexion).
- Temporary heel lift (6-12 mm) reduces tendon strain early.
- Address training error: most cases involve recent volume/intensity spike.

### 3.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain ≤2/10 during loading.
- Single-leg heel raise capacity: 25+ reps to failure (or within 80% of unaffected side).
- Tolerates hopping × 30 sec without next-morning symptom rise.
- 24-h rule: pain and morning stiffness no worse than baseline after running.

### 3.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected rupture (sudden pop, positive Thompson test) — emergency referral.
- No improvement after 12 weeks of structured loading.
- Severe focal swelling or systemic symptoms (rare; consider haglund's, retrocalcaneal bursitis, inflammatory arthropathy).

## 4. Achilles Tendinopathy — Insertional

### 4.1 Pathophysiology
Pathology at the Achilles enthesis on the calcaneus, often with Haglund's deformity, retrocalcaneal bursitis, or calcific change. Dorsiflexion compresses deep fibres against the calcaneus — compressive component matters.

### 4.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Pain at the insertion, not mid-tendon.
- Worse with rigid heel-counter footwear; aggravated by stretching or below-step heel drops.
- Possible visible Haglund's prominence.

### 4.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain on level walking.
- Acute inflammatory flare with retrocalcaneal swelling.

### 4.4 Graded Return Protocol — Floor-Level Calf Raises

Critical difference from mid-portion: avoid dorsiflexion past neutral during the early loading phase. Heel raises performed on flat floor (not on a step), without dropping the heel below floor level. Stretching is generally not used or is restricted to neutral.

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | 1-3 | Isometric calf holds at neutral, 5 × 45 sec, 2-3×/day. | Off. Pool running OK. |
| 2 | 4-7 | HSR floor-level calf raise (no step drop), knee straight + bent, 3-4 × 8-15 RM, 3×/wk. | Walk-run stages 1-3, flat surfaces only. |
| 3 | 8-10 | Add gentle plyometrics, careful with end-range dorsiflexion. | Walk-run 4-7. |
| 4 | 11+ | Progress to step-based work as symptoms allow. | Continuous easy → strides → tempo. |

### 4.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Heel lift (8-12 mm) to offload insertion. Avoid backless shoes (Crocs, slides) and rigid heel counters that aggravate Haglund's.
- Soft-tissue work to gastroc-soleus.

### 4.6 Criteria to Progress
- Same as mid-portion (pain ≤2/10, heel raise capacity, hopping tolerance) but with floor-only standards initially.

### 4.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected enthesopathy in young male with bilateral disease and morning back pain (consider spondyloarthropathy).
- Persistent symptoms ≥3-6 months despite appropriate dorsiflexion-restricted loading.
- Significant Haglund's with retrocalcaneal bursitis unresponsive to footwear modification.

## 5. Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)

### 5.1 Pathophysiology
Lateral knee pain from compression of soft tissue deep to the iliotibial tract over the lateral femoral condyle near 30° flexion. Modern model: compression, not friction. Associated with hip neuromuscular control deficits (glute medius weakness, contralateral pelvic drop, hip adduction in stance).

### 5.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Sharp lateral knee pain, typically at predictable distance/time into a run.
- Tenderness at lateral femoral epicondyle; positive Noble compression test.
- Worse downhill and on cambered surfaces.

### 5.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain in early minutes of running (severe presentation).
- Pain on stairs descending.
- Pain on level walking.

### 5.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Week | Loading | Running |
|------|---------|---------|
| 1-2 | Hip abductor/external rotator strengthening: side-lying clams, side-lying leg raises, banded lateral walks, single-leg sit-to-stands. 3-4 × 10-15 daily → every other day. | Off running. Cycling OK if pain-free; avoid high seat or aggressive cleated pedals. Pool run. |
| 3-4 | Progress to single-leg deadlifts, step-downs, Copenhagen adductors. Eccentric hip work. | Walk-run stage 1-2, flat course only, short distance. Avoid downhills. |
| 5-6 | Add resisted weight-bearing hip work (barbell or single-leg squats). | Walk-run 3-5; alternate-day; flat. |
| 7-8 | Continue strength 2-3×/wk. Add running drills targeting cadence and pelvic stability. | Continuous easy 25-40 min. |
| 9+ | Maintenance strength 2×/wk. | Re-introduce hills (uphill first, then downhill). Add intensity last. |

### 5.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Cadence +5-10% reduces hip adduction at footstrike.
- Avoid cambered roads, repetitive same-direction track loops, aggressive downhills during recovery.
- Foam rolling lateral thigh = symptom relief only; the ITB does not stretch. Target hip mobility and gluteal strength.

### 5.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free single-leg squat to 60° knee flexion.
- Pain-free Noble compression test.
- Tolerates 20 min continuous flat running without symptom onset.

### 5.7 Medical Referral
- Failure to improve after 8-12 weeks of progressive hip-focused rehab.
- Lateral knee pain with effusion, locking, or mechanical symptoms (consider lateral meniscus, lateral compartment chondral lesion).
- Suspected lateral collateral ligament or biceps femoris pathology.

## 6. Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)

### 6.1 Pathophysiology
Anterior/peri-patellar knee pain from overload of the PF joint and surrounding tissue. Multifactorial: quadriceps and hip strength, foot mechanics, training load, central pain processing. Not purely a tracking problem.

### 6.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Diffuse anterior knee pain, around or behind the patella.
- Worse with stairs descending, squatting, prolonged sitting ("theatre sign").
- Often bilateral; no effusion, no instability, full ROM.

### 6.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain >4/10 on level walking or stairs.
- Effusion (consider intra-articular pathology — refer).

### 6.4 Graded Return Protocol — Pain-Guided

The PFPS evidence base supports running through monitored, low-grade symptoms (≤2/10 NRS in-session) with training modification rather than complete cessation, provided pain doesn't rise day-to-day.

| Week | Loading | Running |
|------|---------|---------|
| 1-2 | Hip + quad strength: clamshells, side-lying abduction, single-leg sit-to-stands, wall sits, step-ups (limit to 30° flexion if painful). 4-6×/wk. | Reduce volume to ~50% of pre-flare. Lower intensity. Pain ≤2/10 during run. Avoid hills, fast running. |
| 3-4 | Progress to single-leg squats, Bulgarian split squats, deadlifts. Add isometric quadriceps holds. | Increase volume 10-15% if symptoms stable. Keep flat and easy. |
| 5-6 | Heavy compound work as tolerated (squats, lunges, leg press). 2-3×/wk. | Approach pre-flare volume. Add gentle rolling terrain. |
| 7-8 | Maintain strength 2×/wk. | Re-introduce strides, then tempo. |
| 9+ | Maintenance. | Intervals, hills last. |

### 6.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Cadence +5-10% reduces PF peak load.
- Hip + quadriceps combined strengthening superior to quadriceps alone (NATA; JOSPT CPG).
- Patellar taping (McConnell, kinesiology) for symptom modulation only.
- Address training-load error: mileage spike, new hill or pace work.

### 6.6 Criteria to Progress
- Stairs pain-free.
- Single-leg squat to 60° pain-free.
- 24-h rule clean after each session.

### 6.7 Medical Referral
- True effusion.
- Mechanical symptoms (locking, catching, giving way).
- History of trauma.
- Failure to improve after 6-8 weeks.
- Adolescent runner with anterior knee pain — consider Osgood-Schlatter, Sinding-Larsen-Johansson.

## 7. Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)

### 7.1 Pathophysiology
Loading-related patellar tendon pathology, typically at the proximal attachment to the inferior pole of the patella. More common in jumping athletes; appears in runners doing heavy downhill, hill repeats, plyometrics.

### 7.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Focal pain at inferior pole of patella.
- Worse with jumping, stairs descending, deep squat, prolonged sitting.
- Tender with knee extended; less tender with knee flexed.

### 7.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain on level walking.
- Pain ≥4/10 during squat.
- Inability to perform a single-leg decline squat.

### 7.4 Graded Return Protocol

Heavy slow resistance (HSR) and isometrics have emerged as preferred loading strategies for patellar tendinopathy. Eccentric-only programs (e.g., decline squat) work but show lower patient satisfaction at follow-up than HSR.

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Pain modulation | 1-2 | Isometric quad/knee extension holds 5 × 45 sec @ 70% MVIC, 2×/day. | Off. Cycling if pain-free, avoid steep climbs. |
| 2 — Heavy slow load | 3-8 | HSR squats / leg press / Bulgarian split squats. 3-4 sets × 6-15 RM, 3-second eccentric and concentric. 3×/wk. Progress load weekly. Pain ≤3/10 during, no rise next day. | Walk-run 1-3 starting wk 4-5. Flat. |
| 3 — Energy storage | 9-12 | Add plyometrics: pogos → CMJ → drop jumps. Continue HSR 2×/wk. | Continuous easy. Add strides. |
| 4 — Sport return | 13+ | Plyometric maintenance. Strength 2×/wk. | Tempo, then intervals, then hills. |

### 7.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Address quadriceps eccentric strength deficit.
- Hip strength (glute medius, max).
- Address ankle dorsiflexion limitation if present.
- Cadence increase to reduce eccentric quadriceps demand at landing.

### 7.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free decline squat (25° board).
- Squat 1.5× bodyweight (relative target; deficit vs. uninjured side <10%).
- Tolerates jumping × 20 reps without 24-h symptom rise.

### 7.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected patellar tendon partial or full tear (pop, gap, inability to extend knee).
- Symptoms refractory to 12+ weeks of structured loading.
- Imaging-significant tendon pathology in a high-volume runner — may need shockwave therapy or specialist input.

## 8. Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS / Shin Splints)

### 8.1 Pathophysiology
Diffuse exercise-induced pain along the posteromedial tibial border. A bone-stress continuum: periosteal traction, cortical strain, possibly tibialis posterior/soleus origin overload. Can progress to tibial stress reaction or fracture if loaded inappropriately.

### 8.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Diffuse (not focal) tenderness along medial tibial border, distal-third, ≥5 cm length.
- Worse early in run, may improve mid-run, returns afterward.
- No focal point-tenderness; negative hop test. (Either positive → suspect stress fracture.)

### 8.3 Contraindications for Running
- Focal point-tenderness.
- Positive single-leg hop pain.
- Night pain or rest pain.
- Pain on level walking that persists day-to-day.

### 8.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Running |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Relative rest | 2-6 weeks (pain-dependent) | Calf, tibialis posterior, intrinsic foot strength. Hip strength. Pain-free walking only. | Off running. Cross-train: pool running, cycling, elliptical. |
| 2 — Reintroduction | Weeks 3-7 | Continue loading work. Begin treadmill walking on grade. | Walk-run stage 1-2 on flat, soft surface (treadmill ideal). Alternate-day. |
| 3 — Progression | Weeks 5-10 | Calf raises 4 × 12-15. Single-leg work. | Walk-run stages 3-5. |
| 4 — Volume rebuild | Weeks 8-14 | Maintenance strength. | Continuous easy. Volume +10%/week. |
| 5 — Intensity | Weeks 12+ | Strength 2×/wk. | Strides → tempo → intervals → hills. |

### 8.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Calf, tibialis posterior, intrinsic foot strengthening.
- Hip strength to reduce GRF asymmetry.
- Cadence +5-10% reduces tibial strain.
- Address training error (volume spike, surface change, abrupt shoe transition).

### 8.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free hopping × 10 single-leg.
- No tenderness on tibial palpation.
- Tolerates pre-run loading drills without symptom onset.

### 8.7 Medical Referral
- Focal tenderness, positive hop test, night pain → image to rule out stress fracture.
- Failure to resolve at 6-8 weeks.
- Recurrent MTSS — screen for REDs/female athlete triad, vitamin D, biomechanical contributors.

## 9. Tibial Stress Reaction / Stress Fracture

Bone stress injury (BSI) sits on a continuum: stress reaction (early bone marrow oedema, periosteal change, no fracture line) → stress fracture (visible cortical fracture line) → complete fracture. Risk stratification by location is critical: high-risk sites have poor blood supply, are tension-loaded, and have high rates of delayed union, non-union, or complete fracture if loaded prematurely.

### 9.1 Pathophysiology
Repetitive submaximal loading exceeds bone's remodeling capacity. Contributors: training-load spike, low energy availability (REDs), low BMD, biomechanical factors, vitamin D deficiency, prior BSI.

### 9.2 Risk Stratification (Critical for Coaching Decisions)

| Location | Risk | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Posteromedial tibial shaft | Low | Most common in runners; conservative management; clinical (not radiological) signs guide return. |
| Anterior tibial cortex ("dreaded black line") | **High** | Tension side, poor blood supply; high non-union rate; surgical consultation often needed. |
| Femoral shaft | Low to moderate | Usually heals with offloading. |
| Femoral neck — compression side (inferior) | Moderate | Conservative if confirmed compression-side. |
| Femoral neck — tension side (superior) | **High** | Risk of displacement and avascular necrosis; surgical fixation often indicated. |
| Tarsal navicular | **High** | Avascular central third; non-weight-bearing 6+ weeks; consider CT to monitor. |
| Fifth metatarsal base (Jones zone) | **High** | Notoriously poor healing; often surgical (intramedullary screw) for athletes. |
| Metatarsals 2-4 (shaft) | Low | Usually heal in 6-8 weeks of relative rest. |
| Calcaneus | Moderate | Usually heals; positive squeeze test. |
| Sesamoid | Moderate-high | Slow to heal; orthotics important. |
| Pubic ramus | Low | Heals with rest. |
| Sacrum | Low to moderate | Often associated with REDs — workup required. |
| Medial malleolus | High | Tension side; surgical risk. |

### 9.3 Diagnostic Signs
- Focal point-tenderness on bone.
- Pain progressing over weeks; eventually painful with daily activity.
- Positive single-leg hop; positive fulcrum test (femoral shaft).
- MRI is imaging modality of choice (sensitive to early oedema); X-rays often negative for 2-4 weeks.

### 9.4 Contraindications for Running
**All confirmed BSIs: no running until clinical clearance.**

### 9.5 Graded Return Protocol — Low-Risk BSI (Posteromedial Tibia, Metatarsal 2-4, Femoral Shaft, etc.)

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Notes |
|-------|----------|---------|-------|
| 1 — Offload | 2-4 weeks | Pain-free walking only; cross-train (pool, cycle, elliptical). | Boot or rest depending on severity. |
| 2 — Pain-free walking | 1+ week | Achieve 5+ consecutive days fully pain-free with daily activity. | Gate to phase 3. |
| 3 — Reintroduction | 2-3 weeks | Walk-run stage 1-2 on soft surface, alternate-day. Pain ≤0/10 during run. | Any pain = stop and reassess. |
| 4 — Volume build | 3-6 weeks | Walk-run stages 3-7 → continuous easy. +10%/week. | Volume only. |
| 5 — Intensity | 2-4+ weeks | Strides → tempo → intervals. | Each step ≥1 week. |

**Total return: 8-16 weeks typical.** Lower-risk BSIs may resolve in 6-12 weeks; higher-volume runners and those with REDs typically take longer.

### 9.6 Graded Return Protocol — High-Risk BSI (Anterior Tibia, Femoral Neck Tension, Navicular, Jones, Medial Malleolus, Sesamoid)

**Coach role: do not lead the return-to-run timeline. Defer to the treating clinician. Monitor and support.**

Generic timeline (clinician-led):
- Phase 1: 6-8+ weeks non-weight-bearing or boot, depending on site.
- Phase 2: progressive weight-bearing 4-6 weeks; cross-train as cleared.
- Phase 3: imaging confirmation of healing before any running (this is the key difference from low-risk).
- Phase 4: return-to-run begins as for low-risk, but more conservative.

**Total return commonly 4-9 months.**

### 9.7 Parallel Work
- Adequate energy availability, calcium 1000-1500 mg/day, vitamin D sufficiency.
- DEXA scan if recurrent BSI or risk factors; address REDs/triad if menstrual disturbance.
- Strength training to load skeletal system safely; cadence/biomechanics only after structural healing.

### 9.8 Criteria to Progress (Low-Risk)
- 5+ consecutive days fully pain-free in daily activity.
- Pain-free single-leg hop on the affected side.
- No bone tenderness on palpation.

### 9.9 Medical Referral
**All suspected BSIs warrant medical evaluation.** Coaches should refer immediately on suspicion (focal bone tenderness, positive hop test). Re-imaging and clearance are clinician decisions.

## 10. Hamstring Strain — Acute

### 10.1 Pathophysiology
Acute eccentric overload, typically biceps femoris long head. Askling 2007 functional types:
- **Type I — sprinter**: high-speed mechanism, BFLH musculotendinous junction, faster RTR.
- **Type II — stretching**: slow over-stretch (kicking, dance), proximal semimembranosus near ischial tuberosity, much longer RTR.

### 10.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Sudden pain during sprint (type I) or slow stretch (type II).
- Localized tenderness, bruising 24-72 h later.
- Pain on resisted knee flexion / hip extension; loss of passive SLR.

### 10.3 Contraindications for Running
- Antalgic gait at walking speed.
- Inability to pass low-speed pain-free running test.
- Active range-of-motion deficit >20°.

### 10.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Goals | Activities |
|-------|----------|-------|------------|
| 1 — Protection | Days 0-5 | Reduce pain, swelling. Maintain ROM. | Pain-free isometrics. Walking, stationary cycling at low resistance. |
| 2 — Sub-max strength | Days 4-14 | Restore strength through pain-free range. | Isometric and slow eccentric work (Nordic curl progression with limited range). Low-speed treadmill jogging if pain-free at walk. |
| 3 — Eccentric loading | Wks 2-4 | Progress eccentric capacity through full range. | Askling protocol: extender, diver, glider. Walk-run progression. Stride-out drills. |
| 4 — High-speed return | Wks 3-6+ | Restore sprint capacity. | Progressive sprint exposure: 50% → 75% → 95% → max. Add change-of-direction. |
| 5 — Sport-specific | Wks 4-8+ | Confidence, full intensity. | Full training; structured speed sessions. |

**Type I (sprint mechanism)** typical RTR: 3-6 weeks for recreational runners; longer for elite sprinters.
**Type II (stretch mechanism)** typical RTR: 8-30+ weeks. Coaches should be much more conservative.

### 10.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Eccentric hamstring strength (Nordic curls, RDL) — best-evidence prevention.
- Hip extensor strength, trunk/pelvic control, hip flexor mobility.

### 10.6 Criteria to Progress / RTR Clearance
- Pain-free walking, jogging, sprinting in sequence.
- Strength deficit <10% vs. uninjured side.
- Pain-free Askling H-test (high-speed knee extension test) — promising but not consensus-required.
- Psychological readiness (confidence to sprint).

### 10.7 Medical Referral
- Palpable defect or gap (suspect grade III tear).
- Significant bruising tracking down the leg.
- Proximal injury near ischial tuberosity with bony tenderness — consider avulsion (especially adolescents).
- Re-injury within 6 months.
- Slow stretch-type injury with persistent posterior thigh + buttock pain.

## 11. Proximal Hamstring Tendinopathy (PHT)

### 11.1 Pathophysiology
Loading-related pathology at the hamstring origin on the ischial tuberosity. Compressive component: hip flexion compresses tendon against ischium. Common in distance runners and middle-aged endurance athletes.

### 11.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Deep, localized buttock pain at the ischial tuberosity.
- Worse with sitting (hard surfaces), driving, uphill or fast running.
- Pain on resisted hamstring contraction in lengthened position (long-lever bridge, single-leg deadlift).
- No true neural symptoms (distinguishes from sciatic radiculopathy).

### 11.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain on sitting at rest >5/10.
- Antalgic gait.
- Severe sciatic nerve irritation symptoms (refer).

### 11.4 Graded Return Protocol — Goom 4-Stage Framework

| Stage | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Isometric | 1-2 | Long-lever bridge holds, prone hamstring isometrics. 5 × 45 sec, daily. Avoid stretching. | Off or short flat easy if pain ≤2/10 and no compression. |
| 2 — Isotonic, neutral hip | 3-6 | HSR hamstring work: bridges, hip thrusts, prone leg curl. 3-4 sets × 8-15. 3×/wk. | Walk-run flat, alternate-day. |
| 3 — Isotonic, hip flexion | 7-10 | Add deadlifts, single-leg deadlifts, Romanian deadlifts (controlled range progressing to deeper hip flexion). | Continuous easy. Reintroduce strides cautiously. |
| 4 — Energy storage | 11+ | Plyometrics, sprint mechanics, A-skips, bounding. | Tempo, intervals, hills (uphill last; uphill compresses the tendon). |

### 11.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- **Avoid prolonged sitting and hamstring stretching during early recovery** — both compressive. Use wedge or split cushion.
- Pelvic/hip stability; kinetic chain (foot mechanics, ankle dorsiflexion, hip mobility).

### 11.6 Criteria to Progress
- Sitting tolerance >30 min pain-free.
- Single-leg deadlift to 80% of unaffected side.
- Pain-free uphill running.

### 11.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected high-grade tear or avulsion (sudden mechanism, pop, severe tenderness, imaging).
- Sciatic nerve symptoms.
- Failure to improve over 12 weeks of structured loading.

## 12. Calf Strain (Gastrocnemius / Soleus)

### 12.1 Pathophysiology
Acute musculotendinous injury, most commonly medial gastrocnemius MTJ ("tennis leg"). Soleus strains: subtler, often subacute. Mechanism: forceful eccentric load on contracted calf during accelerations or hills.

### 12.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Sudden sharp calf pain ("kick" sensation).
- Localized tenderness; bruising may track to ankle in 48-72 h.
- Pain on resisted plantarflexion; reduced dorsiflexion ROM.
- Soleus strain presents deeper and more diffuse.

### 12.3 Contraindications for Running
- Antalgic gait.
- Inability to perform a double-leg heel raise.
- Suspected Achilles rupture (Thompson test) or DVT — refer.

### 12.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Running |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Protection | Days 0-5 | Pain-free walking. Heel lift in shoe. Pain-free ankle ROM. | Off. |
| 2 — Sub-max | Days 3-10 | Double-leg heel raises 3 × 15. Progress to single-leg. Cycling, pool running OK. | Off. |
| 3 — Eccentric loading | Wks 2-4 | HSR calf raise (knee straight + bent). Progress weight. Add hops on uninjured then injured side. | Walk-run stage 1-3 if 25 single-leg heel raises pain-free. |
| 4 — Reactive load | Wks 4-6 | Plyometrics: pogos, skipping. Sprint mechanics. | Continuous easy → strides. |
| 5 — Sport return | Wks 5-8+ | Full strength and reactive maintenance. | Full intensity sequence. |

**Typical RTR**: 2-4 weeks for grade I, 4-8 weeks for grade II, longer for grade III.

### 12.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Strengthen both gastroc (knee straight) and soleus (knee bent) — soleus commonly under-loaded.
- Ankle dorsiflexion mobility; hip/core strength.
- Address training error (hill volume spike, fast finishes).

### 12.6 Criteria to Progress
- 25-30 pain-free single-leg heel raises.
- 10-20 pain-free single-leg hops.
- Pain-free fast walking and easy jogging.

### 12.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected Achilles rupture: sudden pop, palpable gap, positive Thompson test, inability to push off — emergency.
- Calf swelling with warmth, persistent pain, recent immobilization or long flight — DVT screening.
- Recurrent calf strains — workup for chronic exertional compartment syndrome, popliteal artery entrapment, lumbar contributors.

## 13. Hip Flexor Strain / Iliopsoas-Related Pain

### 13.1 Pathophysiology
Loading injury to hip flexor complex (iliopsoas, rectus femoris, sartorius, less commonly TFL). Iliopsoas tendinopathy is more common in distance runners than acute strain.

### 13.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Anterior hip / deep groin pain.
- Worse with hill running, fast running, kicking, rising from seated.
- Pain on resisted hip flexion (worse with knee extended for rectus femoris).
- Thomas test for tightness/pain; possible snapping with iliopsoas tendinopathy.

### 13.3 Contraindications for Running
- Antalgic gait.
- Inability to clear stairs without pain.
- Suspected avulsion fracture (adolescent with sudden mechanism).

### 13.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Running |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Protection | 1-2 weeks | Pain-free walking. Isometric hip flexion holds. Avoid kicking, sprinting. | Off or very easy flat short jogs if pain-free. |
| 2 — Strength | 2-4 weeks | Eccentric-biased hip flexor work (controlled lowering of leg from flexed position). Banded hip flexion. Bulgarian split squats, step-ups. 3×/wk. | Walk-run on flat; avoid hills and fast running. |
| 3 — Functional | 4-6 weeks | Add lunge variations, Bulgarian splits with weight, cable-resisted hip flexion. Plyometrics. | Continuous easy. Strides. |
| 4 — RTR | 6-8 weeks+ | Maintenance strength. | Hills, intervals, sprint work in sequence. |

### 13.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Eccentric hip flexor loading (research-supported for tendinopathy).
- Hip extensor (gluteal) strength; address anterior pelvic tilt and lumbar extension overuse.
- Address training error (uphill or speed-work spike).

### 13.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free single-leg sit-to-stand.
- Pain-free 45° and 90° cuts.
- Pain-free single-leg hop on injured side.

### 13.7 Medical Referral
- Adolescent runner with sudden mechanism — consider apophyseal avulsion (anterior superior or inferior iliac spine, lesser trochanter).
- Deep groin pain unresponsive to load management — consider intra-articular hip pathology, FAI, labral tear, sports hernia, athletic pubalgia.
- Snapping hip with persistent pain — internal vs. external snapping; may need imaging.

## 14. Piriformis Syndrome / Deep Gluteal Syndrome

### 14.1 Pathophysiology
Sciatic nerve entrapment/irritation in the deep gluteal space, classically by the piriformis. Modern term "deep gluteal syndrome" is broader (also obturator internus, gemelli, fibrous bands). Diagnosis is clinical and exclusionary — rule out lumbar radiculopathy.

### 14.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Deep, dull buttock pain, may radiate into posterior thigh (rarely past knee).
- Worse with prolonged sitting, hill running.
- Tenderness on deep palpation; pain on FAIR test (flexion-adduction-internal rotation).
- Negative SLR and no dermatomal numbness distinguishes from radiculopathy.

### 14.3 Contraindications for Running
- Significant sciatic nerve symptoms (true paraesthesia, motor weakness).
- Pain with light walking.

### 14.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-2 | Reduce sitting time; sit on wedge or split cushion. Gentle piriformis and hip ER mobility, neural mobility (sciatic glides). | Reduce volume to pain-tolerable level. |
| 2 — Strength | 3-5 | Hip extensor, abductor, ER strengthening. Glute max bridges, banded clams, side-lying abduction, lateral walks. | Continue reduced-load running if pain ≤2/10. Avoid hills. |
| 3 — Progressive load | 6-8 | Single-leg work, deadlifts, step-ups, lunges. | Progress volume +10%/wk. |
| 4 — Sport return | 9+ | Maintenance. | Reintroduce hills, intervals. |

### 14.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Glute max + medius strengthening — most evidence-supported intervention.
- Hip/lumbar mobility; sitting ergonomics (sustained sitting is the most common aggravator).
- Running specifics: cadence, hip stability.

### 14.6 Criteria to Progress
- Sitting >30 min pain-free.
- Pain-free single-leg squat.
- No reproduction with FAIR or piriformis stretch test.

### 14.7 Medical Referral
- Any true neurological deficit (motor weakness, dermatomal numbness, reflex changes).
- Bowel/bladder symptoms — emergency.
- Failure to improve at 8-12 weeks.
- Consider lumbar imaging if symptoms persist or worsen.

## 15. Posterior Tibial Tendinopathy

### 15.1 Pathophysiology
Loading pathology of tibialis posterior tendon, posterior/below the medial malleolus. Tibialis posterior is the primary dynamic supporter of the medial arch; advanced dysfunction can progress to acquired flatfoot.

### 15.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Medial ankle pain behind/below medial malleolus, may extend to navicular.
- Worse with prolonged or cambered-surface running, overpronation.
- Pain on resisted inversion in plantarflexion.
- Single-leg heel raise: difficulty inverting the heel at top.
- "Too many toes" sign (forefoot abduction visible from behind) in advanced cases.

### 15.3 Contraindications for Running
- Inability to perform single-leg heel raise.
- Pain on level walking.
- Suspected stage II or higher posterior tibial tendon dysfunction (rigid flatfoot, severe weakness — refer).

### 15.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Running |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-3 weeks | Isometric inversion holds. Resisted band inversion. Short-foot exercise. | Off; cycling, pool. |
| 2 — Progressive load | 4-8 weeks | Heavy slow inversion work; eccentric heel raise emphasizing inversion (heel raise + tilt heel inward at top). 3 × 15 → progress weight, slow tempo. 3×/wk. | Walk-run flat surface, cushioned shoe; avoid cambered surfaces. |
| 3 — Functional load | 8-12 weeks | Single-leg heel raise → progressed loaded. Plyometrics. | Continuous easy. |
| 4 — Return to sport | 12+ weeks | Maintenance. | Add intensity. |

### 15.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Intrinsic foot (short-foot, toe yoga), calf, and hip strength.
- Supportive shoe; medial post or orthotic if marked overpronation; avoid barefoot on hard floors.

### 15.6 Criteria to Progress
- 25+ pain-free single-leg heel raises with appropriate inversion at top.
- Pain-free single-leg hop.
- No symptom rebound 24 h post-run.

### 15.7 Medical Referral
- Loss of single-leg heel raise capacity — suspect stage II PTT dysfunction.
- Rigid flat foot deformity.
- Failure to improve over 12 weeks.
- Suspected tear or full-thickness tendinopathy on imaging.

## 16. Peroneal Tendinopathy

### 16.1 Pathophysiology
Loading pathology of peroneus longus/brevis around or distal to the lateral malleolus. Linked to prior ankle sprain, lateral instability, hindfoot varus, or trail/cambered running.

### 16.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Lateral ankle pain, posterior/below lateral malleolus.
- Worse with uneven or cambered surfaces.
- Tenderness along peroneal tendon course; pain on resisted eversion; possible sheath swelling.

### 16.3 Contraindications for Running
- Suspected peroneal subluxation (palpable tendon click over fibula on dorsiflexion-eversion).
- Suspected partial or full tear.
- Pain at rest or on level walking.

### 16.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Loading | Running |
|-------|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-2 weeks | Isometric eversion. Banded eversion. Single-leg balance. | Off; cycle, pool. |
| 2 — Progressive load | 3-6 weeks | HSR eversion work; eccentric weight-bearing inversion (controlled lowering). Single-leg balance progression. | Walk-run on flat, predictable surface. Avoid trails, cambered roads. |
| 3 — Reactive | 6-9 weeks | Single-leg hops (linear → lateral → multi-direction). Cutting drills. | Continuous easy. Begin trails cautiously. |
| 4 — RTR | 9-12+ weeks | Maintenance. | Add intensity, then technical surfaces. |

### 16.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Lateral ankle proprioception (single-leg balance, foam pad). Address chronic ankle instability if present.
- Hip strength for lateral chain control.
- Avoid worn lateral-wear shoes and cambered surfaces; consider lateral wedge for hindfoot varus.

### 16.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free single-leg hop in all directions.
- Resisted eversion strength to within 90% of unaffected side.
- 24-h rule clean.

### 16.7 Medical Referral
- Suspected peroneal subluxation/dislocation — surgical consultation.
- Suspected partial tear, longitudinal split tear (often imaging-required).
- Failure to improve over 12 weeks.

## 17. Metatarsalgia

### 17.1 Pathophysiology
Umbrella term for plantar forefoot pain under metatarsal heads (typically 2-4). Mechanism: excessive plantar pressure during forefoot loading. Differential: Morton's neuroma (neural, interspace), sesamoiditis (1st MTP), capsulitis, Freiberg's, stress fracture (focal bone tender).

### 17.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Diffuse plantar forefoot pain under MT heads.
- Worse with forefoot striking, racing flats, hill running.
- "Pebble in shoe" feeling; tender on direct palpation.

### 17.3 Contraindications for Running
- Focal point-tenderness on a single metatarsal shaft (suspect stress fracture — refer).
- Significant interdigital symptoms with neural quality (suspect Morton's neuroma).
- Pain on level walking.

### 17.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-2 | Reduce forefoot loading: cushioned shoe with metatarsal pad. Intrinsic foot strength. | Walk only if walking is pain-free; otherwise cycling/pool. |
| 2 — Strength | 3-4 | Calf raise, intrinsic foot, toe-flexor strengthening. | Walk-run stage 1-3 in cushioned shoe. |
| 3 — Progression | 5-8 | Continue strength. Plyometrics. | Walk-run 4-7 → continuous easy. |
| 4 — RTR | 9+ | Maintenance. | Intensity progression. |

### 17.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Cushioned shoe, appropriate width, stiff forefoot rocker if needed; metatarsal pad proximal to MT heads.
- Avoid abrupt transitions to minimal/zero-drop; address training error.

### 17.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free walking without metatarsal pad.
- No focal bone tenderness.
- 24-h rule clean.

### 17.7 Medical Referral
- Focal metatarsal shaft tenderness or positive tuning-fork test — image to rule out stress fracture.
- Persistent symptoms despite footwear modification.
- Suspected Freiberg's disease (especially adolescent, 2nd metatarsal head).

## 18. Morton's Neuroma

### 18.1 Pathophysiology
Compression/irritation of the common digital nerve, usually between 3rd-4th MT heads. Strictly perineural fibrosis (not a true neuroma). Aggravated by narrow footwear, forefoot loading, high-impact running.

### 18.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Burning, electric pain in affected interspace, often into toes.
- "Lump in shoe" / "wrinkle in sock" sensation.
- Worse with forefoot loading; relieved by shoe removal/massage.
- Mulder's click: mediolateral forefoot squeeze produces palpable click, reproduces symptoms.

### 18.3 Contraindications for Running
- Severe symptoms reproduced by walking.
- Significant numbness in the affected toes (consider referral).

### 18.4 Graded Return Protocol

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-4 | Wide toe-box shoe. Metatarsal pad placed proximal to metatarsal heads. Avoid tight shoes. | Reduce volume to pain-tolerable level; flat surface. |
| 2 — Reload | 4-8 | Intrinsic foot strength. Toe spacers if tolerated. | Walk-run if symptomatic; otherwise continuous easy reduced volume. |
| 3 — Progress | 8-12 | Maintain footwear modifications. | Volume rebuild +10%/wk. |
| 4 — RTR | 12+ | Maintenance. | Intensity progression. |

### 18.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Footwear is cornerstone: wide forefoot, cushioned, rocker if needed.
- Metatarsal pad placement critical (proximal to heads, not directly under them).
- Avoid narrow racing flats; toe-spreading and intrinsic foot strength.

### 18.6 Criteria to Progress
- Symptoms manageable in normal running shoes.
- 24-h rule clean.

### 18.7 Medical Referral
- Severe persistent symptoms despite 8-12 weeks of conservative measures.
- Significant numbness or motor symptoms.
- Failed conservative management — consider corticosteroid or alcohol injection, surgical neurectomy.

## 19. Hip Labral Irritation / Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) Syndrome

### 19.1 Pathophysiology
Anterior hip pain from contact between femoral head-neck and acetabular rim, sometimes with labral pathology. Morphologies: cam (femoral), pincer (acetabular), mixed. Labral tears occur with or without FAI morphology. Imaging findings are often asymptomatic — correlate with clinical picture.

### 19.2 Diagnostic Signs
- Anterior hip / groin pain, often with C-sign.
- Worse with prolonged sitting, deep squat, hip flexion + IR.
- Limited and painful IR in 90° hip flexion; positive FADIR provocation.
- Sometimes mechanical (catching, clicking).

### 19.3 Contraindications for Running
- Pain on level walking.
- True locking or giving way.
- Severe limitation of daily activity.

### 19.4 Graded Return Protocol

Conservative management is recommended for at least 3-6 months before surgical consideration.

| Phase | Weeks | Loading | Running |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 — Settle | 1-4 | Activity modification: avoid deep squat, prolonged sitting. Hip mobility within pain-free range. Glute strengthening. | Reduced volume if tolerable; flat surfaces; cycling or pool if not. |
| 2 — Strength | 4-8 | Progressive hip and core strengthening. Single-leg balance, glute bridges, deadlifts (limited hip flexion if symptomatic), lateral band walks. | Walk-run stages 1-5 if symptoms tolerable. |
| 3 — Functional | 8-12 | Plyometrics, sport-specific drills, controlled hip flexion loading. | Continuous easy → strides. |
| 4 — RTR | 12+ | Maintenance. | Add tempo, intervals, hills cautiously. |

### 19.5 Parallel Rehab Work
- Glute max + medius strengthening; core/lumbopelvic stability.
- Address habitual postures (prolonged sitting, anterior pelvic tilt).
- Cadence increase reduces hip flexion peak during running.

### 19.6 Criteria to Progress
- Pain-free walking and stairs.
- Pain-free single-leg squat.
- 24-h rule clean.

### 19.7 Medical Referral
- True mechanical symptoms (locking, giving way).
- Failed 3-6 months conservative management.
- Substantial functional limitation.
- Suspected intra-articular pathology — imaging (MRI arthrogram) and orthopedic consultation.

## 20. DOMS / Generic Acute Soreness — Run Through or Back Off

### 20.1 What DOMS Is
Delayed onset muscle soreness: diffuse muscle pain peaking 24-72 h after unaccustomed eccentric loading. Mechanism involves muscle fibre disruption and remodeling, not lasting damage. Characterized by:
- Diffuse, bilateral or muscle-group-wide tenderness.
- Reduced strength and ROM transiently.
- No focal point-tenderness or sharp pain.
- Resolves spontaneously in 3-5 days.

### 20.2 Run Through vs. Back Off — Decision Framework

| Symptom | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Mild-moderate diffuse muscle soreness, ≤4/10, normal gait | Easy or recovery run. May feel better partway in. |
| Moderate soreness with stiffness, ≤6/10, mildly antalgic | Active recovery: walk, very easy short run, cross-train. Skip intensity. |
| Severe soreness, >7/10, antalgic gait | Rest 1-2 days. Walking, gentle cycling. Reassess. |
| Sharp, focal pain | NOT DOMS — investigate as injury. |
| Pain that worsens during the run | NOT DOMS — back off. |
| Pain still present >5-7 days | NOT DOMS — investigate. |
| Bilateral leg weakness, dark urine, swelling | Possible rhabdomyolysis — emergency. |

### 20.3 Practical Coach Rules
- DOMS does not require complete rest in most cases. Easy aerobic activity is generally appropriate.
- Hard running on top of significant DOMS rarely yields good adaptation; defer the quality session.
- DOMS that recurs week after week from the same training stimulus suggests training is too aggressive.
- Persistent unilateral soreness in a focal location is not DOMS — treat it as a developing overuse injury and apply pain monitoring rules.
