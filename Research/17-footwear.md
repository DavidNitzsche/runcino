# 17 — Footwear: Shoe Selection, Rotation, and Lifecycle

Reference for running shoe selection, rotation strategy, and lifecycle management. Generic guidance — not personalized.

## Definitions

- **Daily trainer**: cushioned shoe designed for the bulk of weekly mileage at easy/steady pace.
- **Super shoe**: racing shoe combining a curved carbon-fiber plate with high-stack, highly resilient PEBA or similar foam.
- **Drop (heel-toe offset)**: vertical height difference between heel and forefoot, measured in mm.
- **Stack height**: total midsole + outsole material thickness under the foot, measured in mm.
- **Pronation**: inward rotation of the foot during ground contact; a normal shock-absorption mechanism.
- **Overpronation**: pronation past neutral that some clinicians associate with medial-side injuries.
- **Last**: the foot-shaped form a shoe is built around; determines width, volume, and toe-box shape.
- **Rocker**: forward-curved sole geometry that promotes roll-through.
- **Rotation**: parallel use of multiple shoe models across the training week.

## Super Shoes (Carbon Plate + Responsive Foam)

### Performance benefit

Hoogkamer et al. (2018) measured an average **4% reduction in metabolic cost** of running across 18 trained male subjects in the Nike Vaporfly 4% prototype vs. established marathon flats (Adidas Adizero Adios Boost 2 and Nike Zoom Streak 6) at 14, 16, and 18 km/h. Range across subjects: **1.6% to 6.3%** improvement.

Joubert and Jones (2023) compared seven different carbon-plated racing shoes plus one traditional flat (Asics Hyperspeed) in 12 trained male runners. Findings:

- Top-performing super shoes (Alphafly, Vaporfly Next%, Metaspeed Sky, Endorphin Pro, RC Elite) improved running economy ~2.7–3.5% vs. flat control.
- Some carbon-plated shoes showed minimal advantage — **the carbon plate alone is not the mechanism**; the resilient PEBA-class foam paired with stiff plate is what produces the effect.
- Brooks Hyperion Elite 2 (older foam) underperformed the foam-leading shoes despite having a plate.

### Mechanism

Three contributors, in approximate order of magnitude:

1. **Resilient foam** (PEBA, e.g. Nike ZoomX, Adidas Lightstrike Pro, Saucony PWRRUN PB): high energy return per unit compression; lightweight per unit volume permits high stack without weight penalty.
2. **Curved carbon plate**: shifts the metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint mechanics — reduces negative work at the toes ("teeter-totter" effect, per Hoogkamer/Kram).
3. **Geometry / rocker**: the curved profile reduces ankle work and smooths roll-through.

### Individual response variation

Joubert reported that runners who benefited most tended to share:
- Higher vertical oscillation
- Lower stride rate (cadence)
- Heel or rearfoot strike pattern

Forefoot strikers with high cadence and low oscillation often see smaller gains. Some runners are **non-responders** with negligible economy improvement in any super shoe.

### Translation: economy → finish time

A 4% economy improvement translates to roughly a **2% performance improvement** for marathon (since aerobic capacity is the bottleneck and the relationship is nonlinear). Practical estimates:

| Marathon time | Approximate super-shoe time savings |
|---|---|
| 2:30:00 | ~3:00 |
| 3:00:00 | ~3:30 |
| 3:30:00 | ~4:00 |
| 4:00:00 | ~4:30 |
| 4:30:00 | ~5:00 |

Non-responders may see no benefit. Some runners report perceived effort improvement even when measured economy is unchanged — this is real but not guaranteed.

## Super Shoe Injury Research

Tenforde et al. (2023, *Sports Medicine*) — case series of bone stress injuries (BSI) in elite/sub-elite runners using carbon-fiber plate (CFP) shoes. Five cases of navicular BSI, plus additional metatarsal and calcaneal cases. Hypothesized mechanism: stiff plate alters foot mechanics so that less energy is absorbed by ankle/intrinsic foot musculature and ligaments, with load shifted proximally and dorsally into the midfoot bones.

Other reported associations (case-level, not RCT):
- **Achilles tendinopathy** — particularly with low-drop super shoes and rapid transition
- **Posterior tibial / peroneal strain** — from altered foot mechanics
- **Plantar fasciitis** — from rigid forefoot plate
- **Calf strain** — from rocker geometry forcing different push-off mechanics

### Risk-management decision rules

| Rule | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Cap super-shoe usage at 10–15% of weekly mileage | Limits exposure to altered loading pattern |
| Introduce super shoes gradually over 4–6 weeks | Allows tissue adaptation |
| Avoid back-to-back hard sessions in super shoes | Recovery for forefoot/midfoot bones |
| If midfoot pain develops, stop immediately | Navicular BSI has a long return-to-run timeline |
| Don't pair super shoes with new high-volume block | Combined novelty stimulus |

## When to Use Super Shoes

| Use case | Justification |
|---|---|
| **Race day** (10K, half, marathon) | Maximum economy benefit when fitness peaks |
| **1 dress-rehearsal long run**, 2–3 weeks before race | Confirms fit, foot tolerance, blister hot spots |
| **1–2 key marathon-pace sessions** per cycle | Tunes neuromuscular pattern; calibrates pace feel |
| **Major tune-up race** | Standard race-day kit |

| Use case | Avoid because |
|---|---|
| Recovery / easy runs | Foam wear without benefit; injury risk; ruins lifespan |
| Daily intervals / weekly tempos | Excess exposure; cumulative midfoot load |
| First long run of a marathon block | Premature foam wear; injury risk |
| First time on race day | Unknown fit/tolerance is gambling; always pre-test |

## Daily Trainer Requirements

A daily trainer absorbs the majority (60–80%) of weekly mileage. Selection criteria:

| Criterion | Target |
|---|---|
| Stack height | 28–36 mm (heel) |
| Drop | 6–12 mm (match runner's habitual drop ± 2 mm) |
| Cushioning feel | Moderate — enough for protection, not so plush it feels unstable |
| Outsole rubber | Full-coverage carbon rubber for durability (300+ mile target) |
| Upper | Engineered mesh, gusseted tongue, secure heel counter |
| Weight | 250–310 g (men's 9); lighter ≠ better for daily |
| Lifespan target | 400–500 miles |

Avoid choosing a daily trainer based purely on race-shoe brand loyalty — daily trainers and race shoes are engineered for different priorities.

## Stability vs. Neutral — The Pronation Debate

### Historical model

Until ~2010, shoes were prescribed by static arch height: low-arch / overpronator → motion-control or stability shoe; high-arch / supinator → neutral cushioning.

### Current evidence

| Study | Population | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Knapik et al. (military, 2010, 2014) | 7,000+ recruits | Assigning shoes by plantar shape did **not** reduce injury rate vs. neutral assignment |
| Ryan et al. (2011) | Female recreational runners | Motion-control shoes did **not** reduce injury risk; some increased pain |
| Nigg et al. ("preferred movement path") | Lab studies | Runners adapt kinematics regardless of shoe — body chooses its preferred path |
| Malisoux et al. (2016, RCT, 372 runners) | Recreational | Standard vs. motion-control shoes — no overall injury difference, **but** in pronated-foot subgroup, motion-control reduced injury risk |
| Willems et al. (2021, secondary analysis) | Recreational | Motion-control shoes reduced **pronation-related** pathology specifically; no effect on other injuries |

### Practical decision rule

| Indicator | Suggested category |
|---|---|
| No prior injury history | Neutral |
| History of medial tibial stress syndrome, posterior tibialis tendinopathy, knee valgus pain, or plantar fasciitis with marked pronation | Trial stability or guide-rail |
| Comfortable in current shoe, no issues | Stay |
| Static arch height alone | **Not** a sufficient prescription criterion |

Comfort filter (Nigg) — runners who self-select a comfortable shoe have lower injury rates than those prescribed by arch type.

### Modern stability execution

The medial post (firmer foam wedge under the arch) of the 2000s has largely been replaced by:
- **Guide rails** (Brooks): firmer foam buttresses on both sides of the heel/midfoot
- **Wider midsole geometry** (Hoka Arahi, Asics GT-2000)
- **Rocker + broader base**: passive stability via geometry

These approaches are less corrective and more "centering" — they nudge rather than block.

## Drop / Heel-Toe Offset

| Drop range | Loading shift | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| 0 mm (zero drop) | Maximum Achilles/calf load | Altra; experienced minimalist runners |
| 1–4 mm (low) | Higher Achilles/calf load; lower knee load | Forefoot/midfoot strikers; lighter trainers |
| 5–8 mm (mid) | Balanced | Modern trend; most race shoes |
| 9–12 mm (high) | Lower Achilles load; higher knee load | Traditional trainers; rearfoot strikers |
| 12+ mm (very high) | Minimum calf load | Rare in modern lineup |

### Transition rules

- Change drop by no more than **2 mm at a time**.
- Allow **4–8 weeks** of adaptation per drop change ≥ 4 mm.
- Sudden drops to lower offset most commonly produce: Achilles tendinopathy, calf strain, metatarsal stress reactions.
- Sudden moves to higher offset most commonly produce: knee anterior pain, hip-flexor tightness.

Malisoux et al. (2016, RCT on drop): no overall injury difference, but **occasional runners benefited from lower drop** while **regular runners had higher injury rates with lower drop**. Interpretation: low-drop tolerates a lower mileage load before tissue overload; high-drop is more forgiving for high-volume runners.

## Stack Height and the World Athletics 40 mm Rule

Effective 1 November 2024, World Athletics caps **road racing shoe stack height at 40 mm** (measured at 12% and 75% of internal length, larger value taken). Track event limits:

| Event | Max stack |
|---|---|
| Track ≤ 800 m | 20 mm |
| Track > 800 m | 25 mm |
| Cross country | 25 mm |
| Road racing | 40 mm |
| Field events (jumps) | varies; 20–35 mm |

Spikes additionally limited to one rigid plate in midsole.

### Practical implications

- All current World Athletics-approved super shoes are at or below 40 mm.
- Wearing a non-compliant shoe at a sanctioned race risks DQ for elite category — generally unenforced for back-of-pack.
- Recreational runners are not bound by this rule on training runs, only at sanctioned competition.

## Shoe Rotation

### The injury-prevention case

Malisoux et al. (2013/2015), prospective study of 264 recreational runners over 22 weeks. Result: runners who used **multiple shoe models in parallel had a 39% lower running-related injury rate** than single-shoe users. Mechanism (proposed): variation in load distribution across tissues.

Hamill and colleagues (2013–2014): biomechanical work showing shoe parameters (drop, stack, plate stiffness, geometry) measurably alter peak eversion, eversion velocity, joint angle range. Different shoes load tissues differently — rotating distributes that load.

### Recommended rotation size

| Weekly mileage | Suggested rotation |
|---|---|
| < 20 mi (32 km) | 1–2 pairs |
| 20–40 mi (32–64 km) | 2–3 pairs |
| 40–70 mi (64–112 km) | 3 pairs |
| 70+ mi (112+ km) | 3–4 pairs |

Diminishing returns past 4 pairs. Variety of model is more valuable than total count — rotating two identical pairs offers little biomechanical variation.

### Rotation by workout type

| Slot | Purpose | Example shoe roles |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Easy / recovery | High cushioning, durable | Max-cushion daily, older daily |
| 2. Daily / steady | Workhorse | Standard daily trainer |
| 3. Tempo / workout | Lighter, responsive | Tempo trainer or super trainer |
| 4. Race day / key sessions | Maximum economy | Super shoe |

Add stability slot (replaces 1 or 2) if pronation-related history. Add trail slot if any off-road volume.

### Drying rotation

Shoes recover ~24 hours of foam decompression and full upper drying between uses. Two pairs in rotation eliminate consecutive-day wear of one pair. This alone extends lifespan even before considering load variation.

## Mileage Lifespan by Category

| Category | Typical lifespan | Cushioning floor |
|---|---|---|
| Daily trainer | 400–500 mi (640–800 km) | Replace at 500 mi or when midsole feels flat |
| Max cushion | 400–600 mi (640–960 km) | Generally outlasts daily trainers |
| Tempo / speed trainer | 300–400 mi (480–640 km) | Lighter foam wears faster |
| Super shoe (PEBA) | 150–250 mi (240–400 km) | Foam compresses; benefit decays after ~150 mi |
| Racing flat | 200–300 mi (320–480 km) | Less midsole to lose |
| Trail (lugged) | 300–500 mi (480–800 km) | Lugs wear before midsole; outsole-driven |
| Track spike | 100–200 mi or 1–2 seasons | Often retired by upper failure |
| Stability | 400–500 mi | Similar to daily trainer |

### Modifiers

| Factor | Effect on lifespan |
|---|---|
| Body mass +20 kg above reference (75 kg) | −20–30% mileage |
| Surface: pavement vs. crushed limestone | −15–25% on pavement |
| Hot/wet storage between runs | −10–20% |
| Heel-strike runner | Faster heel rubber wear, similar foam life |
| Forefoot strike | Faster forefoot foam wear |
| Treadmill use | +10–15% (smoother, controlled surface) |

### Storage degradation

Unworn shoes degrade slowly. PEBA and EVA foams remain serviceable ~3–5 years in sealed conditions; ozone, heat, and humidity accelerate breakdown. Extended garage / hot car storage is the worst case.

## Signs of Shoe Degradation

| Sign | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Outsole rubber worn through to midsole foam | Outsole done — midsole wearing fast next |
| Compressed heel foam (visible asymmetric crush) | Cushioning gone on that side |
| Midsole feels firm/dead vs. when new | Foam fatigued |
| Creasing fully through midsole | Structural collapse |
| Upper tearing at flex points or heel | Fit compromised |
| New onset of joint/tendon soreness in old shoe | Cushioning loss; rotate out |
| Insole compressed flat | Replace insole; check shoe overall |
| Heel counter collapses inward | Stability gone |

The "press test": place thumbs in midsole and press. Healthy foam compresses then springs back. Dead foam compresses and stays compressed.

## Proper Shoe Fit

### Four dimensions

1. **Length** — thumb-width (~12 mm) of space between longest toe and shoe end at full forefoot loading.
2. **Width** — forefoot held without lateral pressure on 1st and 5th metatarsal heads; heel snug.
3. **Volume** — instep height; lacing should secure midfoot without crushing top of foot.
4. **Toe box shape** — anatomical (foot-shaped) vs. tapered.

### Sizing rules

- Try on later in the day (feet swell with activity).
- Wear running socks of intended thickness.
- Run on the treadmill or hop in store if available.
- Half-size up vs. casual shoes is typical for running shoes.
- Brand cross-fit varies (see brand table) — never assume size.
- Foot length grows ~0.5 size from age 30 to 50 in many runners; remeasure annually.

### Foot type considerations

| Foot feature | Selection guidance |
|---|---|
| High arch | Look for cushioning + flexibility; avoid rigid shanks |
| Low arch / flat | Trial stability or guide-rail; avoid heavily rockered narrow lasts |
| Narrow heel | Brands with narrow heel cup (NB, Asics, Nike) |
| Wide heel | Brooks, Hoka, Saucony |
| Wide forefoot | Hoka, Altra, Brooks (in 2E), New Balance (2E/4E) |
| Narrow forefoot | Asics, Nike, On |
| High volume / tall instep | Avoid low-profile uppers; gap-lace |
| Low volume / shallow foot | Full-sleeve uppers, snug performance lasts |
| Bunion / Morton's neuroma | Wide toe box (Altra, Topo, Hoka wide) |
| Long toes | Anatomical toe box (Altra, Topo, Lone Peak) |

## Sock Interaction

| Sock thickness | Effect on fit |
|---|---|
| Ultra-thin | Acts like increasing shoe volume by ~0.5 size |
| Standard performance | Reference fit |
| Thick / cushioned | Decreases effective shoe volume by ~0.5 size |

### Materials

| Material | Properties |
|---|---|
| Merino wool (e.g. Smartwool, Darn Tough) | Thermoregulating, anti-odor, moderate moisture wicking, slower to dry |
| Synthetic (polyester, nylon, elastane) | Fast wicking, fast drying, durable |
| Synthetic + merino blend | Balanced; good for variable conditions |
| Cotton | Avoid — retains moisture, increases blister risk |
| Double-layer (Wrightsock, Injinji blends) | Reduces friction at sock-skin interface; blister prevention |
| Toe socks | Reduces inter-toe friction; helpful for blackened toenails |

Always test new socks on a short run before a long run or race — sock change is a top blister cause.

## Insoles and Orthotics

| Type | Use case | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Stock insole | Default; replace when compressed flat | Often the limiting durability factor in long-term shoe feel |
| Cushion replacement (e.g. Sof Sole) | Restore feel in older shoe | Doesn't restore midsole life |
| Arch-support OTC (Superfeet, Currex) | Mild plantar fasciitis, arch fatigue, structure | Affects shoe volume — re-test fit |
| Custom orthotic (podiatrist) | Diagnosed pronation, leg-length discrepancy, post-surgical | Effective in shoes designed for removable insole |
| Carbon-fiber footplate orthotic | Post-injury 1st MTP / turf toe protection | Restricts shoe selection |

Orthotics reduce shoe volume substantially. When fitting a shoe to take an orthotic, fit with the orthotic in place. Some shoes (super shoes, racing flats) have non-removable footbeds and won't accept orthotics.

## Lacing Techniques

| Technique | Solves |
|---|---|
| **Standard criss-cross** | Default |
| **Heel lock / runner's loop** | Heel slippage; finish lacing by passing each lace through the same-side top eyelet to form a loop, then cross laces through opposite loops |
| **Window / gap lacing** | Top-of-foot pain; skip the criss-cross over the painful spot, lace parallel through that pair of eyelets |
| **Wide-foot lacing** | Forefoot tightness; skip first criss-cross, lace straight up bottom two eyelets |
| **Narrow-foot lacing** | Loose fit; use two columns of eyelets (where present) to cinch midfoot tighter |
| **Lydiard / parallel lacing** | Reduces top-of-foot pressure across whole foot |
| **High-instep lacing** | Skip middle eyelet on each side to relieve instep pressure |

Tightness rule: tight enough that the shoe doesn't slide on the foot; loose enough that the dorsalis pedis pulse is not occluded. Heel-lock too tight is a leading cause of extensor tendonitis on top of foot.

## Treadmill vs. Road Shoe Choice

Treadmills already provide ~15–30% less impact than asphalt due to belt deck flex. Recommendations:

| Concern | Treadmill choice |
|---|---|
| Default | Same shoe as outdoors; switch only if needed |
| Long treadmill blocks (winter) | Same shoe as outdoors, but consider rotating to extend each pair |
| Belt-slip / forefoot tracking | Avoid heavily rockered max-cushion (rocker + moving belt = imbalance) |
| Perceived heat / sweat | Slightly more breathable upper |
| Sole wear | Treadmills wear sole rubber slower; foam wears similarly |

Dedicated "treadmill shoes" are largely a marketing category. A standard daily trainer works fine.

## Trail Shoe Selection

### Buffed vs. technical

| Terrain | Lug depth | Stack | Plate | Examples (category) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smooth single-track, fire road | 2–4 mm | 28–34 mm | Optional | Hoka Challenger, Saucony Ride TR |
| Mixed, occasional rocks | 3–5 mm | 28–32 mm | Light rock plate helpful | Hoka Speedgoat, Brooks Cascadia |
| Technical / mountain | 4–6 mm | 22–30 mm | Yes | La Sportiva Bushido, Salomon Sense Ride/Wildhorse |
| Mud / soft | 6–8+ mm | 22–28 mm | Optional | Salomon Speedcross, Inov-8 Mudclaw |
| Snow / winter | Lugs + spikes/screws | Variable | — | Microspikes over road shoe; or Salomon Snowcross |

### Other trail features

- **Toe rock cap**: protects against root/rock impact at toe.
- **Gaiter attachment**: for sand/scree.
- **Drainage ports** / non-absorbent uppers: for water crossings.
- **Outsole compound**: Vibram Megagrip is the current benchmark for sticky wet rock; harder compounds last longer on dry hardpack.

### When a road shoe suffices

If the surface is groomed crushed limestone, bike-path, or grassy park trail with no exposed roots/rocks/mud, a road daily trainer works. Trail-specific gear is justified once the surface introduces rotation, rock impact, or sustained traction loss.

## Track Spikes vs. Flats vs. Trainers for Track Sessions

| Workout | Recommended | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Distance intervals (mile +) on rubberized track | Tempo trainer or daily trainer | Cushion needed for repeated turns |
| Short intervals (200–400 m) | Tempo trainer or flat | Spikes optional pre-meet only |
| 1–2 race-prep sessions before track meet | Spikes | Acclimate to footwear and pace feel |
| 5K race on track | Flat or distance spike | Spike preferred for ≤ 5K |
| Cross-country race | Cross-country spike | 6 mm max stack on grass/dirt |

Spikes have negative drop, minimal cushion, and rigid plate — high stress per stride. Limit volume to <10% of weekly mileage. Off-season athletes lose tolerance to spikes within ~6 weeks; ramp 2 sessions before relying on them.

## Width Sizing

| Men | Women | Description |
|---|---|---|
| B | AA | Narrow |
| D | B | Standard ("medium") |
| 2E | D | Wide |
| 4E | 2E | Extra wide |
| 6E | 4E | Extra extra wide (rare) |

US default ships D for men, B for women. Brands with strong wide-width programs: Brooks, New Balance, Hoka, Altra. Sparse wide programs: Nike, On, Adidas, Mizuno. If a runner needs a wide and a brand doesn't offer it, accept that brand isn't a fit — don't compensate with up-sizing length, which creates heel slip.

## Buying Online vs. In-Store Fitting

| Channel | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| **Specialty running store** | Gait analysis, multi-brand try-on, expert staff, in-person fit, treadmill testing | Higher price; smaller inventory of older models |
| **Brand flagship store** | Deep model lineup; brand-specific expertise | Single-brand only |
| **Online (Running Warehouse, Zappos)** | Wide selection, return policies (up to 90 days), often lower price | No try-on without ordering multiples |
| **Discount/closeout (Amazon, eBay)** | Lowest price; older models | Counterfeit risk; size/version uncertainty; older foam may have aged |

### Recommended process

1. First fitting (or after foot change): in-store.
2. Subsequent purchases of known models in known size: online with free returns.
3. Major shoe-type change (drop, category, stability transition): in-store.
4. New brand: in-store or online with return policy.

Counterfeit super shoes are common in resale channels — buy from authorized retailers when paying premium.

## Volumental and 3D Foot Scanning

Volumental's in-store scanner captures 12 measurement points in ~5 seconds: foot length, width, ball width, instep height, heel width, arch height. Reported accuracy ~1 mm in-store, ~5 mm mobile. The system pairs scan data with purchase/return data across millions of shoppers (40M+ scans, 3,000+ stores) to predict fit per model.

Notable insight from Volumental research: of 841 women's running shoe models analyzed, **~40% did not fit true to labeled size**. This means cross-brand sizing assumptions based on Brannock device alone are unreliable.

Practical use:
- Scan once, record values (length, ball width, instep, heel width).
- Use scan data to filter models — width ≥ X, instep ≥ Y.
- Re-scan annually or after pregnancy, weight change, or injury.

Other scanners: Aetrex Albert, FeetID. Nike's older Nike Fit app uses phone-based scanning with somewhat lower accuracy.

## Break-In Period

| Shoe type | Break-in needed? |
|---|---|
| Modern daily trainer | 2–3 short runs (10–25 mi) |
| Max cushion | Minimal; ride softens slightly |
| Stability with medial post | 1–2 weeks |
| Super shoe | Generally none — but **always run at least 5–15 mi before race day** to confirm fit |
| Racing flat | Minimal |
| Track spike | None — but sessions of progressive volume to acclimate body |
| Trail shoe (firm midsole, lugged) | 1–2 short runs to soften upper, settle insole |
| Zero-drop (Altra) | Body adaptation, not shoe — **4–8 weeks of gradual mileage** in zero-drop |

Modern foams and uppers are largely ready out of the box. The bigger consideration is body adaptation to a new geometry — different drop, different rocker, different stack.

## Shoe Care

| Action | Why | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Air-dry, never machine-dry | Heat warps midsole, breaks adhesives, cracks foam | Every wet run |
| Newspaper stuffing for wet shoes | Wicks moisture from inside-out | Each wet event |
| Remove insoles between runs | Allows airflow, faster drying | Daily |
| Untie laces, don't shove on/off | Prevents heel-counter collapse | Always |
| Wash with mild soap and brush; never washing machine | Agitation breaks down materials and adhesives | When needed |
| Store in cool, dry, dark place | UV and heat degrade foam and rubber | Always |
| Avoid car trunk / hot garage | Heat compresses foam permanently | Always |
| Rotate to allow foam recovery | Memory foams compress and re-expand over ~24 hr | Daily |
| Don't wear running shoes for non-running use | Concrete and walking gait wears outsole differently | — |

A pair worn only for runs and rotated with at least one other pair will outlast a pair worn daily — often by 30–50%.
