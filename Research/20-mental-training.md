# 20 — Mental Training and Sport Psychology

Reference for sport psychology applied to distance running. Covers goal-setting frameworks, arousal regulation, attention strategies, pain tolerance, identity, burnout prevention, and referral criteria. Definitions on first use; lookup tables and decision rules where applicable.

**Term primer:** Arousal = activation level. State anxiety = transient situational anxiety. Trait anxiety = stable disposition. Self-efficacy = belief in capacity to execute (Bandura). Cognitive appraisal = mental interpretation of a stimulus as threat or challenge. RPE = perceived exertion.

## Goal Setting

### Goal types (Locke & Latham; sport psych adaptations)

| Goal type | Definition | Example | Use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outcome | Result relative to others | "Place top 10 in age group" | Rare; depends on others' performance |
| Performance | Personal end-state, independent of others | "Run 3:15 marathon" | Race target |
| Process | Specific actions / behaviors | "Hold 7:25 pace through 20 mi; eat gel every 30 min" | Daily training, race execution |

**Rule:** Process goals dominate for moment-to-moment behavior. Performance goals frame the season. Outcome goals are decoration. Locke & Latham's goal-setting theory holds that specific, challenging goals produce higher performance than vague or easy ones, via four mechanisms: directing attention, mobilizing effort, increasing persistence, and prompting strategy discovery.

### SMART criteria

| Letter | Criterion | Running example |
|---|---|---|
| S | Specific | "Sub-1:45 half marathon" not "run faster" |
| M | Measurable | Time, distance, splits — quantified |
| A | Achievable | Within ~5% of current fitness ceiling |
| R | Relevant | Aligned with broader running purpose |
| T | Time-bound | Tied to a specific race date |

### Daniels' A/B/C tiered race goals

A three-tier system providing fallback targets so a race remains "successful" across condition variability.

| Tier | Definition | Probability of achievement on race day | Emotional outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Stretch goal — ideal conditions, perfect day | ~20-30% | Walking-on-clouds result |
| B | Realistic goal — solid execution, minor adversity tolerated | ~50-60% | Smiling, satisfied |
| C | Floor goal — finish respectably despite significant adversity | ~80-90% | Knew the effort was honest |

**Rule:** Set all three before race day. C-goal must be defensible under bad weather, GI distress, or sub-par sleep. Without a C-goal, runners DNF rather than salvage a finish.

### Goal-setting decision rules
- Process goals daily; performance goals quarterly; outcome goals never as primary motivator.
- Goals must be written; re-evaluate at 4-week intervals with objective fitness data.
- Difficulty target: 50-70% confidence of achievement on the B-goal. >90% = too easy; <30% = too hard.

## Mental Rehearsal and Visualization

### PETTLEP framework (Holmes & Collins, 2001)

A seven-element model for designing imagery that achieves **functional equivalence** — engaging the same neural pathways as actual movement, increasing transfer to performance.

| Element | Definition | Running application |
|---|---|---|
| **P**hysical | Body posture / kit matches the real task | Imagery in race shoes, running clothes, standing |
| **E**nvironment | Visualize the actual venue | Use course map, photos, prior course recon |
| **T**ask | Content matches skill level and demand | Image surges, hill climbs, finish kick at race effort |
| **T**iming | Imagery in real-time, not slow-mo or fast-forward | A 5K segment imagined takes ~real 5K duration |
| **L**earning | Adapt content as fitness/skills evolve | Update imagery script as training progresses |
| **E**motion | Generate the affective state the task creates | Include nerves, confidence, late-race fatigue |
| **P**erspective | First-person (internal) or third-person (external) | Internal for execution; external for form/technique |

**Evidence:** Imagery time correlates with actual performance time when timing principle is followed (e.g., canoe-slalom imagined times correlate with race times). Best results occur when imagery is **combined** with physical practice, not as a substitute.

### Imagery prescription
- Frequency: 3-5 sessions/week, 5-15 min each; daily in race week.
- Training-block content: technique cues, hard-workout success, response to discomfort.
- Race-week content: course visualization, executing pacing plan, handling specific challenges (mile-20 hill, headwind, GI issue).
- Always end imagery with successful execution.

## Self-Talk

Self-talk is the internal or vocalized dialogue an athlete has with themselves during preparation or performance. Hatzigeorgiadis et al.'s 2011 meta-analysis (32 studies, 62 effect sizes) found a moderate positive overall effect (ES = 0.48) on sport performance.

### Two functional categories

| Type | Function | Best for | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Instructional** | Direct attention to technique, strategy, focus cues | Fine motor / technical / novel tasks | "Drive arms"; "Quick feet"; "Stay tall"; "Lift through the hip" |
| **Motivational** | Increase effort, confidence, arousal regulation | Endurance, strength, gross motor / well-learned tasks | "Strong"; "I can do this"; "Push"; "One more mile" |

### Hatzigeorgiadis 2011 moderator findings

- Self-talk effects were larger on **fine motor tasks** than gross motor tasks.
- Self-talk effects were larger on **novel tasks** than well-learned tasks.
- **Instructional** self-talk outperformed motivational on fine motor tasks.
- Interventions including **explicit self-talk training** were more effective than spontaneous use.

### Application to distance running

| Race phase | Recommended self-talk type | Example cues |
|---|---|---|
| Start (settling pace) | Instructional | "Easy turnover"; "Patient"; "Breathing 3:3" |
| Mid-race steady | Instructional + form | "Tall, light, fast"; "Relax shoulders" |
| Late-race grinding | Motivational | "Strong"; "This is what you trained for"; "Stay in it" |
| Final kick | Motivational + instructional | "Drive arms, push"; "Empty the tank" |

### Decision rules
- Self-talk must be trained in workouts before relied on in races.
- Negative self-talk ("Don't slow down") is less effective than positive instructional ("Strong cadence").
- Cues should be short (1-3 words) — long sentences fail under late-race cognitive fatigue.

## Race-Day Arousal Management

Two competing models describe the arousal-performance relationship.

### Inverted-U (Yerkes-Dodson)

Performance peaks at moderate arousal; too low or too high impairs. Population-level model. Applies broadly but masks individual variance.

### IZOF — Individual Zones of Optimal Functioning (Hanin)

Each athlete has a personal optimal bandwidth of pre-competition emotion / arousal. Some perform best calm; others perform best activated. Outside this individual zone (high or low), performance degrades. Comparative studies have shown IZOF predicts performance better than inverted-U variants.

### Identifying individual optimal zone

Across 5-10 races, log: subjective arousal (1-10), anxiety (1-10), mood tag (energetic / flat / nervous), and performance (time + RPE vs. expected). The optimal zone is the arousal/anxiety range correlated with personal-best efforts.

### Over-aroused vs. under-aroused — symptoms and counter-strategies

| State | Symptoms | Counter-strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Over-aroused** | Racing thoughts, shaking hands, nausea, can't sit still, going out too fast | Slow diaphragmatic breathing (6 br/min), progressive muscle relaxation, calming self-talk ("settle"), reduce stimulation (headphones off, leave warmup area) |
| **Optimal** | Alert, focused, butterflies but not panic, mind on plan | Maintain — avoid disrupting the state |
| **Under-aroused** | Flat, sluggish, indifferent, "going through motions" | Dynamic warmup with strides, motivational self-talk, music, recall past performance highlights, light caffeine if not contraindicated |

## Pre-Race Anxiety Management

Cognitive (worry) and somatic (physical tension) anxiety often co-occur but respond to different interventions.

### Targeted interventions

| Anxiety type | Manifestation | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Cognitive (worry) | "What if I blow up?" "What if I'm not ready?" | Cognitive reframing, focusing on process not outcome, thought-stopping, reciting prepared mantras |
| Somatic (physical) | Tight muscles, shallow breathing, GI distress | Breathing protocols (below), progressive muscle relaxation, light walking |

### Reframing rules
- "I'm anxious" → "I'm activated and ready" (same physiology, different label).
- Pre-mortem: visualize a problem (cramp, missed split) and rehearse the solution. Removes novelty if it occurs.
- Limit pre-race "what-if" rumination to a fixed window (e.g., 10 min the night before), then close it.

### Logistical anxiety reducers
Kit laid out night before; course map studied; 30+ min buffer past warmup start; "if X then Y" plan for each foreseeable problem.

## During-Race Attention Strategies

### Associative vs. dissociative (Morgan & Pollock 1977 and successors)

| Strategy | Definition | Effect on performance | Effect on RPE | Injury implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Associative** | Attending inward to body signals (breathing, cadence, fatigue, effort) | Faster performance (elite-typical) | Higher perceived effort | May correlate with higher injury awareness/risk |
| **Dissociative** | Attending outward, distracting (scenery, music, conversation, math) | Lower perceived effort, possibly greater endurance at low intensities | Lower RPE | Not associated with higher injury risk |

**Morgan & Pollock 1977 finding:** Elite marathoners predominantly use associative strategies; recreational runners predominantly use dissociative. Elites monitor pace via "reading the body."

### Application by race distance / phase

| Distance / phase | Default attention | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | Predominantly associative throughout | Effort is high; body signals carry pacing info |
| 10K | Associative | Same |
| Half marathon (early) | Mixed; brief dissociative ok | Conserve mental energy |
| Half marathon (last 5K) | Associative | Drift management |
| Marathon (miles 1-16) | Mixed; dissociative ok at low-RPE phases | Avoid burning mental energy early |
| Marathon (miles 17-26) | Associative | Pacing precision matters most here |
| Ultramarathon | Cycle deliberately between the two | Long duration requires mental energy management |

**Rule:** Inexperienced runners over-dissociate and miss pacing drift. Experienced runners sometimes over-associate and amplify discomfort. Both are trainable.

## Pacing and Discipline — The Early-Mile Temptation

### Mechanism of overpacing
- Crowd energy + rested legs make goal pace feel "easy."
- Confirmation bias: an early fast mile is interpreted as "fitness improved" rather than "warning."
- Sunk-cost / anchoring: once 1 mile is run 15 sec fast, runners aim to hold rather than correct.

### Cost (typical recreational marathon)

| Early-pace error | Glycogen depletion timing | Late-race slowdown |
|---|---|---|
| On goal pace | Normal (~mile 20) | Minimal |
| 5 sec/mi too fast | Slightly accelerated | 10-20 sec/mi slowdown after mile 20 |
| 10 sec/mi too fast | ~30% earlier depletion | 60-90 sec/mi slowdown after mile 18-20 |
| 15+ sec/mi too fast | Severe early depletion | "Wall" mile 16-20; survival jog to finish |

### Discipline tools
Negative-split target (second half ≤ first half forces conservative start); first-mile speed cap (goal pace + 0 sec; never goal − 10 sec); audible split alerts at mile 1, 3 with deliberate slow-down if fast; verbal mantra ("patient," "save it") as cognitive brake; train pacing in workouts via long-run progressions and goal-pace tempos — race pacing fails when only practiced on race day.

## Pain Tolerance and Embracing Discomfort

### Marcora's psychobiological model

Endurance performance is determined primarily by the conscious balance between **perceived effort** and **potential motivation** (the maximum effort an athlete is willing to exert). Termination of exercise is a conscious decision, not a passive failure of muscle. Perception of effort arises from corollary discharge of central motor command — the brain "hears" the signal it sends to muscles.

**Implication:** Anything that lowers perceived effort or raises motivation lengthens time to exhaustion. Anything that raises perceived effort (mental fatigue, negative self-talk, depleted blood glucose) shortens it.

### Mental fatigue and endurance

Marcora's mental-fatigue research shows that prior cognitively demanding tasks reduce time to exhaustion in subsequent endurance work — through elevated perceived effort, not physiological capacity changes. **Practical**: avoid taxing cognitive work in the 2-3 hours before a hard workout or race; protect attention and decision-making capacity.

### Pain vs. discomfort — discrimination rule

| Sensation | Character | Meaning | Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Discomfort** | Diffuse, dull, builds with effort, settles when stopping | Normal endurance effort | Tolerate, reframe as effort |
| **Pain (training)** | Localized, sharp, persistent at rest | Possible injury | Investigate, consider stopping |
| **Pain (race)** | Sharp, stabbing, escalating | Acute injury | DNF threshold (see below) |

### Trainable mental skills for discomfort
Expectation calibration (rehearsing late-race pain reduces subjective suffering); reframing ("this hurts because I'm working" vs. "something is wrong"); chunking (next aid station / next mile / next minute); mindful acceptance (acknowledge sensation; do not fight it — see MAC, below).

## Post-Race Blues and Depression

### Mechanism
Drop in training-induced endorphin / endocannabinoid / dopamine signaling; loss of the structured goal that organized weeks/months of identity and time; physiological recovery debt; dispersal of race-week social structure.

### Typical timeline

| Phase | Duration | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| Acute high | 0-72 h post-race | Euphoria, soreness, social satisfaction |
| Comedown | Days 3-10 | Flat affect, low motivation, irritability, sleep disruption |
| Adjustment | Weeks 2-4 | Variable; gradual return to baseline |
| Resolution | Up to 8 weeks | Wellbeing returns to pre-race baseline |

**"Post-race blues" is not a clinical diagnosis.** It is a normative situational mood reaction.

### Management
Plan the next thing before the current race (even loosely — recovery cycle, a 5K in 6 weeks, a non-running goal); schedule active recovery (light walking, cross-training, easy running once cleared); maintain social connection independent of running; do a post-race write-up (what went well, what to change); tolerate the dip without medicating it — sleep, nutrition, time.

### Escalation criteria — see "Therapy" section
If symptoms persist past 4-6 weeks, worsen, or include hopelessness / anhedonia / suicidal ideation, refer to clinical care.

## DNF Decisions

A DNF (did not finish) is rational in some scenarios, irrational in others. Pre-rehearse the rules so the in-race decision is not a fresh judgment under duress.

### When DNF is correct

| Trigger | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Sharp, localized, escalating pain | Likely acute injury — continuing causes greater damage |
| Suspected stress fracture (deep bony pain) | Cannot self-diagnose; cost of being wrong is severe |
| Symptoms of heat illness (confusion, stopped sweating, nausea + dizziness) | Medical emergency |
| Symptoms of cardiac event (chest pain, syncope) | Emergency |
| Suspected hyponatremia (severe headache, vomiting, disorientation in late-race) | Emergency |
| Mechanical inability (severe blistering compromising gait) that risks injury | Damage compounds quickly |

### When DNF is wrong

| Pseudo-trigger | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Goal time is gone" | C-goal still available; finish honors training |
| "I feel terrible at mile 6 of a marathon" | Bad patches resolve; commit to next aid station |
| "It's hot / windy / bad day" | Conditions are equal for everyone; relative effort still trainable |
| "Bored / not feeling it" | Avoidance coping pattern — reinforces future quitting |

### Decision protocol during a race

1. Stop at next aid station, do not decide in stride.
2. Eat and drink something.
3. Walk 60-90 seconds.
4. Reset the goal: "Can I finish in any time, walking allowed?"
5. If still wanting out — and physical-emergency criteria absent — wait one more aid station before deciding.

**Research finding:** Non-finishers score higher on avoidance coping. Habitual DNF makes future DNF easier — pattern self-reinforces. A salvaged C-goal finish is more valuable to long-term mental development than a clean DNF.

### When DNS (did not start) is right
- Acute illness with fever
- Acute injury
- Confirmed lab markers of overtraining / RED-S that warrant rest
- Extreme weather where event is unsafe (cancellation territory)

## Comeback from Injury — Mental Aspects

Physical readiness ≠ psychological readiness. Roughly 60% of athletes self-report "not ready" mentally even when medically cleared; only ~63% return to pre-injury level despite ~90% regaining physical function.

### Phases and psychological tasks

| Phase | Typical duration | Psychological task | Common pitfall |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acute (post-diagnosis) | 0-2 weeks | Accept reality of injury; grief is normal | Denial, training through pain |
| Rehab early | Weeks 2-6 | Comply with protocol; set process goals (rehab milestones) | Catastrophizing, identity collapse |
| Rehab middle | Months 1-3 | Maintain fitness via cross-training; preserve runner identity loosely | Comparison to non-injured peers |
| Return to running | Variable | Tolerate slowness; trust the protocol | Doing too much too fast |
| Return to training | Variable | Rebuild confidence in the body | Re-injury anxiety, kinesiophobia |
| Return to racing | Variable | Manage expectations | Comparing to pre-injury PRs prematurely |

### Effective psychological interventions
PETTLEP imagery (mental rehearsal of running technique during rehab maintains motor patterns); cognitive restructuring ("I'll never come back" → "I am rebuilding"); mindfulness / acceptance (reduces rumination and reinjury fear); social support (training partners, coach contact, community); process-only goals during return (focus on completing prescribed sessions, not pace).

### Re-injury anxiety
Common around the original injury site. Address with graded exposure (progressively challenging surfaces/speeds), education on healed-tissue capacity, and movement screening with a trusted clinician for objective reassurance.

## Comeback from Poor Performance

A bad race is a data point, not a verdict.

### Post-race review structure

| Question | Purpose |
|---|---|
| What did I execute well? | Preserves confidence in transferable skills |
| What conditions diverged from expectation? | Separates controllable from non-controllable factors |
| What decisions would I change? | Process-level learning |
| What was the realistic fitness ceiling that day? | Recalibrates self-assessment |
| What is the next concrete training adjustment? | Moves attention forward |

### Cognitive pitfalls
Generalization ("I'm a failure"), mind-reading (others' judgments), permanence framing ("I'll never run that pace again"), all-or-nothing ("anything short of A-goal is failure").

### Decision rule
Wait 7 days minimum before retroactively rewriting the training plan. Initial post-race appraisals are emotionally biased.

## Growth vs. Fixed Mindset (Dweck)

| Mindset | Belief about ability | Response to hard workout | Response to bad race | Long-term result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed** | Talent is innate and fixed | Anxiety, panic when session goes wrong | Identity threat; "I'm not really fast" | Plateau, avoidance of challenge |
| **Growth** | Ability develops via effort and strategy | Tolerates bad sessions; treats as data | Process review; sees as feedback | Continued development |

### Cultivating growth mindset
Praise effort and strategy (self-praise included), not innate talent. Frame plateaus as normal precursors to breakthroughs. Compare to past self, not to others. Add "yet": "I haven't broken 1:30 *yet*." Dweck's research shows mindset is malleable — fixed-mindset patterns shift through deliberate framing.

## Identity and the Runner Self-Concept

### Athletic identity
Degree to which a person identifies with the athlete role. High athletic identity correlates with greater training engagement and commitment, but also with greater psychological distress when the role is disrupted (injury, retirement, performance decline).

### Identity foreclosure
Strong commitment to the athlete identity to the exclusion of other potential identities. Risks: higher depression during injury, higher burnout, greater difficulty with sport transitions, reduced coping resources.

### Diversification rule
Maintain 2-3 non-running domains contributing to self-concept (work, family, hobby, community) to buffer identity shock when running is disrupted.

### Healthy runner identity profile
"I am a person who runs," not "I am only a runner." Self-worth is partially but not solely tied to performance. Bad race or injury is painful but not destabilizing. Off-season is tolerable without identity crisis.

## Burnout Prevention (Smith Cognitive-Affective Model)

Smith (1986) describes athletic burnout as a four-stage chronic stress reaction. Distinct from acute fatigue or short-term overtraining.

### Four stages

| Stage | Component | Manifestation |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Situational demands | Training load, life stress, expectations, pressure | High mileage + work stress + family demands stacking |
| 2. Cognitive appraisal | Athlete evaluates demands vs. resources and consequences | "I cannot meet what's required, and the cost will be severe" |
| 3. Physiological & psychological responses | Anxiety, tension, insomnia, illness, mood disturbance | Rising RHR, poor sleep, frequent minor illness |
| 4. Behavioral consequences | Rigid behavior, performance decline, withdrawal | Skipping workouts, dropping out, leaving the sport |

### Burnout signature (vs. ordinary fatigue)

| Symptom dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| Physical/emotional exhaustion | Persistent, not relieved by rest day |
| Devaluation of sport | Loss of meaning; "I don't even care anymore" |
| Reduced sense of accomplishment | Workouts feel pointless even when objectively good |

### Prevention strategies
Manage situational load (deload every 3-4 weeks; reduce during high life-stress); modify cognitive appraisal (reframe demands as challenges; protect realistic expectations); maintain recovery infrastructure (sleep, fueling, social and active rest); inject variety (terrain, partners, goal types); periodic identity check-ins.

### Early-warning signs
Workouts feel harder than HR/pace data suggest they should; loss of enthusiasm for races or training partners; resentment toward training schedule; sleep + mood disruption > 10-14 days; frequent minor illness.

If 3+ persist > 2 weeks, intervene with 50%+ training reduction for 1-2 weeks before deciding next steps.

## Motivation Maintenance through Long Training Cycles

Marathon and ultramarathon training cycles span 12-24 weeks. Motivation drift is normal at predictable points.

### Predictable low points

| Cycle phase | Common motivational dip | Counter-strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Week 3-4 (initial honeymoon ends) | Novelty wears off; daily grind | Add accountability — training partner, log review |
| Mid-cycle (weeks 7-10) | Mileage peak fatigue; race feels far away | Insert tune-up race; deload week |
| 3 weeks pre-race (taper start) | Reduced volume creates self-doubt | Educate on taper physiology; trust |
| Post-race | Goal vacuum | Plan next thing in advance |

### Tools
Monthly training-log review (counters "I haven't done enough"); mixed goal types (outcome + performance + process); new route every 2 weeks; weekly group run minimum; strategic recovery when motivation drops (reduce, do not push through); reconnect to "why" — if the reason is gone, the goal may need revision rather than the runner.

## Pre-Race Rituals and Superstition

### When rituals help
Anxiety reduction (familiar sequence under uncertain conditions); self-efficacy boost (perceived control); attentional focus (narrows to task-relevant cues); arousal regulation (paces pre-race timeline). Removing established rituals can degrade performance via increased anxiety and reduced self-efficacy.

### When rituals become problematic

| Healthy ritual | Problematic compulsion |
|---|---|
| "Lays out kit night before, eats same breakfast" | "Cannot race if the lucky socks are missing" |
| Flexible — adaptable if conditions change | Rigid — disruption causes panic |
| Time-bounded (15-30 min) | Expanding sequence consuming hours |
| Adds confidence | Performance contingent on ritual completion |
| Lighthearted in framing | Anxiety-driven, fear-based |

### Decision rule
Rituals are useful when they:
1. Take less than ~30 min.
2. Survive disruption (lost item, changed schedule) without performance collapse.
3. Are framed positively ("this helps me focus") rather than fearfully ("if I skip this, I'll fail").

When a ritual fails any of these, treat as a sign to deliberately disrupt and rebuild.

## Mantras and Cue Words

### Function
Replace negative self-talk with rehearsed positive content; reduce cognitive load under late-race mental fatigue (short mantras require minimal processing); anchor attention to chosen focus (form, effort, plan); activate trained associations from prior workout use.

### Two categories

| Mantra type | Example | Best deployment |
|---|---|---|
| Process / instructional | "Light feet"; "Tall, relaxed"; "Drive"; "Quick cadence" | Form correction, focus restoration |
| Identity / affirmational | "Strong"; "I am ready"; "Trained for this" | Confidence, late-race grit |

### Construction rules
1-3 words ideal (max 5); present-tense, positive (no "don't"); personally meaningful (borrowed mantras are weaker); trained in workouts (esp. hard intervals and late long-run portions); rotate 2-3 to avoid habituation.

### Evidence
Mixed but generally positive performance effects; a marathon-training study showed an 18% increase in time-to-exhaustion in the mantra group vs. control; brain imaging shows reduced activity in planning regions during silent mantra repetition (consistent with reduced cognitive load).

## Breathing Techniques for Arousal Regulation

Slow, controlled breathing increases parasympathetic tone (rest-and-digest) and decreases sympathetic activation (fight-or-flight). Useful for downregulation pre-race and post-race.

### Common protocols

| Technique | Pattern | Duration | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Box breathing** | Inhale 4s — hold 4s — exhale 4s — hold 4s | 5-10 min | Pre-race anxiety; quick reset |
| **4-7-8** | Inhale 4s — hold 7s — exhale 8s | 4-8 cycles | Sleep onset, sharp anxiety reduction |
| **Six breaths/min (resonant breathing)** | Inhale 5s — exhale 5s | 5-15 min | Sustained downregulation |
| **Physiological sigh** | Double inhale through nose — long exhale | 1-3 cycles | Acute stress spike (race start, pre-warmup nerves) |

### Evidence
Olympic-athlete trial: 5 min pre-competition box breathing reduced competitive anxiety by ~38%. 4-7-8 reduced state anxiety in clinical RCT. All slow-breathing protocols (≤6 br/min) shift HR/BP/HRV toward parasympathetic dominance.

### Application timeline
Race week: nightly before bed (sleep aid). Race morning: 5-10 min in quiet space. Start corral: physiological sighs as needed. In-race: rhythmic breathing (3:2 or 2:2 inhale:exhale) for cadence anchoring. Post-race: sustained slow breathing as cool-down aid.

## Mindfulness for Runners — MAC Approach

Gardner & Moore's **Mindfulness-Acceptance-Commitment (MAC)** is a sport-specific application of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT). It departs from traditional psychological skills training (which seeks to control negative inner states) by emphasizing **non-judgmental acceptance** of internal experience while acting on values.

### Core components

| Component | Description | Running application |
|---|---|---|
| **Mindful awareness** | Present-moment attention to internal/external experience | Noticing fatigue without reacting; sensing pace |
| **Acceptance** | Allowing thoughts/sensations without struggle to suppress them | "This hurts and I keep running"; not fighting fatigue |
| **Values clarification** | Identifying what matters about running and life | Writing personal running values |
| **Committed action** | Behavior aligned with values, regardless of inner state | Showing up to run on low-motivation days |

### Why MAC suits endurance running
Late-race discomfort is unavoidable; suppression drains cognitive resources. Acceptance reduces secondary suffering ("the suffering about the suffering"). Values-driven action sustains commitment across long cycles where motivation fluctuates.

### Empirical findings
Improved attentional control, emotion regulation, and performance; reduced maladaptive cognitive responses under pressure.

### Practice prescription
5-15 min daily mindfulness (breath-focus or body-scan); mid-run "noticing" check-ins (scan 4-5 sensations without judgment, return to running); weekly values reflection (2-3 sentences on why training and racing matter).

## Therapy and Referral

The coach / sport psychologist scope is performance enhancement and normative adjustment. Clinical issues require licensed mental health professionals.

### Refer when these are present

| Signal | Threshold for referral |
|---|---|
| Persistent depressed mood | > 2 weeks, most days, with anhedonia or hopelessness |
| Suicidal ideation | Any — immediate referral |
| Disordered eating patterns | Restriction, bingeing, purging, secretive food behaviors, body-image distortion |
| Substance abuse | Any pattern interfering with function or sport |
| Trauma symptoms | Intrusive memories, avoidance, hyperarousal post-event |
| Severe anxiety / panic attacks | Beyond pre-race nerves; functionally impairing |
| OCD-like rigidity | Compulsive rituals causing distress or impairment |
| Persistent post-race blues | > 4-6 weeks without improvement |
| Identity crisis post-injury or retirement | Clinically significant distress, role-loss grief |

### Referral targets

| Provider | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Licensed clinical psychologist** | Diagnosis and treatment of mental disorders; psychotherapy |
| **Clinical sport psychologist** | Above, with sport-context expertise (anxiety, depression, eating disorders in athletes) |
| **Psychiatrist** | Medication management; severe presentations |
| **Eating-disorder specialist (multidisciplinary team)** | Restrictive eating, RED-S, body-image disorders — requires team of therapist + dietitian + physician |
| **Licensed counselor / social worker** | Adjustment issues, mild-to-moderate mood, life stressors |

### Handoff guidance
Refer early; provide a warm handoff (specific names if possible); continue coaching the running side in parallel; maintain confidentiality (coach does not need clinical details); re-evaluate training load — clinical issues often co-occur with overtraining.

### What sport psychology coaching is **not**
Not a substitute for therapy of clinical disorders; not appropriate for crisis (suicidal, eating-disorder medical instability); not a venue for trauma history.
