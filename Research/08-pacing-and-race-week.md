# 08 — Pacing Strategy and Race Week Protocols

*Reference doc. Generic principles for any distance road racer. Race-day execution and the final week of preparation.*

---

## 1. Glossary

- **Even / negative / positive split:** second half equal to / faster than / slower than first half.
- **Coefficient of variation (CV) of pace:** statistical measure of pace variability across segments. Lower is better; elite marathoners run CV of 1.5-3%.
- **Goal pace (GP), marathon pace (MP), half marathon pace (HMP):** target average pace, max-effort pace at the named distance.
- **Lactate threshold (LT2 / MLSS):** highest sustained effort at which lactate clearance equals production. Approximately one-hour race effort. For trained runners, 5-15 sec/mile faster than HMP.
- **The wall:** abrupt late-race pacing collapse, typically at 30-35 km of a marathon, caused by combined glycogen depletion and central nervous system fatigue.
- **RPE:** rating of perceived exertion (Borg 6-20 or CR-10).
- **Cardiovascular drift:** progressive rise in HR at constant pace, driven by plasma loss and rising core temperature. Typical rate: 3-5 bpm/hour.
- **Dew point:** temperature at which air becomes saturated; dominant variable for evaporative cooling.
- **T-2, T-1, T-0:** two days before race, one day before, race day.

## 2. Pacing Strategy: The Research Foundation

### 2.1 Even, negative, or positive — what wins

- **Elite marathoners:** even pacing dominates. World Marathon Major podium analyses (Hettinga, Diaz, Renfree and Casado) show CV of 5-km splits at 1.5-3%. World records have ranged from perfect even (Kipchoge Vienna, sub-second/mile variation) to slight negative (Bekele Berlin, second half 41 sec faster).
- **Recreational marathoners:** a 2024 systematic review found 77% of recreational marathons show positive pacing, only 18% negative. The 2025 Frontiers review concludes sub-elites benefit most from a slight negative split (or controlled even pace with conservative early miles). Mechanism: glycogen sparing — early conservation cuts anaerobic burn, delaying the 30-35 km crisis.
- **5K, 10K, half marathon:** even or slight positive splits dominate. The aerobic-anaerobic balance shifts toward "use what you have, finish empty."

### 2.2 Diaz / Hettinga 5-km segment framework

The standard analytical method in marathon pacing literature divides the 42.195 km into eight 5-km segments plus a final 2.195-km section.

Key empirical findings:

| Performance tier | 5-km CV (men) | 5-km CV (women) | Late-race pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| World class (sub-2:10 / sub-2:25) | 1.5-3% | 1.5-3% | Slight end-spurt |
| National-class | 3-5% | 3-5% | Modest fade |
| Sub-3 amateur | 5-8% | 4-7% | Fade after 30 km |
| 3:00-3:30 | 7-10% | 6-9% | Marked slowdown after 30 km |
| 3:30-4:00 | 8-12% | 7-11% | Wall around 32-37 km |
| 4:00-5:00+ | 10-15%+ | 10-14%+ | Major slowdown after 25 km |

The fourth 5-km segment (15-20 km) is the most prognostic: the runner who hits the wall typically begins decelerating here, often 1-3 km before they perceive it.

### 2.3 The Foster pacing model

Foster and deKoning frame endurance racing as anticipatory regulation: the brain throttles output based on predicted remaining work. Going out too fast triggers premature regulation (forced slowdown); too conservative leaves fitness on the table. The optimal strategy is a slightly conservative start with acceleration into the body of the race. Validated repeatedly in time trials: even or slight negative pacing beats aggressive starts in efforts longer than 2 minutes.

## 3. Distance-Specific Pacing

### 3.1 First-mile pacing by distance

The opening mile is the highest-leverage decision in any race. It is also the most distorted by adrenaline, crowd dynamics, and downhill course profiles.

| Distance | First-mile target vs. goal pace | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | -2 to +5 sec/mile (very close to GP) | Race is too short to bank time; too short to recover from fast start |
| 10K | +5 to +10 sec/mile slower | Conservative opener buys ability to push at 7-9K |
| Half marathon | +10 to +15 sec/mile slower | LT-effort race; early acidosis derails the second half |
| Marathon | +10 to +20 sec/mile slower | Glycogen-conservation imperative; first 5K should feel "annoyingly easy" |
| Hilly marathon (Boston, Big Sur) | +30 to +45 sec/mile on opening descent | Eccentric quad damage on early downhills surfaces 16+ miles later |

Useful rule: **the first mile should feel one full effort tier easier than goal effort.** For a marathon, that's "easy aerobic" not "marathon pace." For a 5K, that's "controlled hard" not "all-out."

### 3.2 The 5K

5K runs at 95-100% VO2max for trained runners. Glycogen is not the limiter; lactate accumulation, muscle acidosis, and central drive are.

```
Mile 1: hit goal pace within 1-2 sec; never more than 3 sec faster
Mile 2: hold goal pace — the mental dark zone where most fades happen
Mile 3: maintain or accelerate slightly
Final 400m: kick, empty the tank
```

Critical errors: going out >5 sec/mile too fast (collapse exceeds 15 sec/mile by mile 2); holding back hoping to "kick from behind" (deficit can't be made up in 5K); watching the watch (5K runs by feel and breathing).

### 3.3 The 10K

10K runs at approximately 90-94% of VO2max. The middle 5K is where races are won and lost.

**Pacing template:**

| Segment | Pace | Cue |
|---|---|---|
| 0-2 km | GP +5 to +10 sec/mi | Controlled |
| 2-7 km | At GP | Sustainable hard |
| 7-9 km | At GP, hold form | Very hard |
| 9-10 km | Push, kick last 400-600 m | All out |

Framework: run the 10K as "5K plus a 5K." First 5K at open 5K PR pace + 5-15 sec; second 5K at whatever's left (typically 10-30 sec slower than the first 5K for amateurs; even or faster for elites).

### 3.4 The half marathon

HMP tracks lactate threshold within 5-10 sec/mile for most trained runners. Aerobic system runs 88-92% VO2max.

| Segment | Pace vs. GP |
|---|---|
| Mile 1 | +10 to +15 sec slower |
| Miles 2-3 | +5 to +10 sec slower |
| Miles 4-10 | At goal pace |
| Miles 11-13.1 | At goal pace; push final mile |

A correctly paced half feels "comfortably hard" through 10 km, "hard" through 16 km, "very hard" in the final 5 km. If miles 1-3 feel "hard," pace is wrong.

### 3.5 The marathon

MP runs at 78-88% VO2max. Glycogen is the dominant limiter. The first half determines whether the second half is possible.

**10-10-10 method (most amateurs):**
```
Miles 1-10:  GP + 5-10 sec ("controlled")
Miles 11-20: GP ("locked in")
Miles 21-26: GP or slight push ("empty tank")
```

**Pfitzinger conservative:**
```
Miles 1-3:   GP + 5-10 sec
Miles 4-20:  GP exactly
Miles 21-26: hold; do not slow more than 5 sec/mile
```

**Canova specificity (advanced):**
```
Miles 1-2:   GP + 10 sec
Miles 3-22:  GP, with 1-2 5-km segments at GP -5 sec
Miles 23-26: hold; controlled push final 2K
```

**Key rule:** second 10K should never be slower than first 10K by more than 1-2%. A 4:00 marathon as 1:55/2:05 is botched; the same fitness as 2:00/2:00 finishes 5+ minutes faster.

## 4. Pacing in Adverse Conditions

### 4.1 Heat and humidity

Heat strain compounds with race duration. The slower the runner, the more time spent generating heat at any given distance, and the larger the slowdown.

**Dew-point pacing adjustment (approximate, marathon distance):**

| Dew point (°F) | Conditions | Pace adjustment vs. cool-weather GP |
|---|---|---|
| Below 50 | Optimal | None |
| 50-55 | Good | None or +0-5 sec/mile |
| 55-60 | Mild stress | +5-10 sec/mile |
| 60-65 | Moderate | +10-20 sec/mile |
| 65-70 | Hard | +20-35 sec/mile |
| 70-75 | Very hard | +35-60 sec/mile; consider downgrading goal |
| Above 75 | Dangerous | +60+ sec/mile; race for completion, not time |

**Combined T+DP method (temperature + dew point in °F, marathon):**

| Sum (T+DP) | Pace adjustment |
|---|---|
| <100 | None |
| 100-120 | 0-1.5% |
| 120-140 | 1.5-3% |
| 140-150 | 3-4.5% |
| 150-160 | 4.5-6% |
| 160-170 | 6-8% |
| >170 | >8%, often DNF risk |

**Heat racing rules:**
- Convert to effort-based pacing (RPE or HR with drift allowance) by mile 5.
- Pre-cool: ice slurry 30 min pre-race, ice sock or cold towel at start.
- +10-25% fluid, +200-300 mg/h sodium above baseline.
- Pour water on head, neck, forearms at every aid station.
- Slow the first half *more* than the second; heat damage compounds.
- Slower runners are penalized more: a 4:00 marathoner loses 15-25 min in high-dew-point conditions, an elite 4-6 min.

### 4.2 Cold

45-55°F is optimal. 30-45°F is neutral if dressed correctly. Below 30°F adds a 1-2% slowdown from airway cooling and warmup requirements. Cold races need longer warmup; the first 2-3 km may feel sluggish — do not panic-accelerate.

### 4.3 Optimal conditions — slight negative split

When conditions are good (45-55°F, dew point <50°F, light wind, flat course), the optimal strategy for sub-elites is **a controlled even or slight negative split**:

```
First half: GP + 5-10 sec/mile
Second half: GP exactly, with a push the final 5K
```

A 1-2% negative split (e.g., 1:30:30/1:29:30 for a 3:00 marathon) is the realistic stretch goal. A 3%+ negative split usually means the first half was too slow and time was left on the table.

### 4.4 Hilly courses — effort-based, not pace-based

On a hilly course, attempting to hold even pace forces the runner above threshold on uphills, depleting glycogen non-linearly and damaging the second half.

**Effort-based hill pacing rules:**

| Grade | Pace adjustment vs. flat GP |
|---|---|
| -3% (downhill) | -10 to -20 sec/mile (let gravity do work, but control braking) |
| -1 to -2% | -5 to -10 sec/mile |
| Flat | GP |
| +1 to +2% | +10 to +15 sec/mile |
| +3% | +20 to +30 sec/mile |
| +5% | +40 to +60 sec/mile |
| +7% | +60 to +90 sec/mile, often a hike |

Hold heart rate or RPE constant rather than pace. On a 5% uphill at marathon effort, expect 30-45 sec/km slower while HR holds.

### 4.5 Net downhill courses (Boston, Big Sur, CIM, Revel series)

Net downhill is not a free PR. Eccentric contractions during downhill braking damage quads; the damage surfaces 60-90 min later regardless of how easy the descent felt.

**Boston (Hopkinton drop → Newton Hills → descent to Boylston):**
- Miles 1-4 (downhill): run 30-45 sec/mile *slower* than flat GP. Quad damage here breaks the race at miles 22-24.
- Miles 5-15: hold GP.
- Miles 16-21 (Newton Hills): convert to effort; expect 15-30 sec/mile slower on Heartbreak.
- Miles 22-26: closing descent punishes early quad damage. Correctly run, miles 22-26 splits stay within 10 sec of GP.

**Big Sur:** rolling, not net-downhill as commonly assumed. The 2-mile Hurricane Point climb (mile 10-12) is the defining test. Miles 1-5 conservative (+15-30 sec); race miles 13-22.

**CIM, Revel, Tucson, St. George:** course-PR potential is real but only with quad-protective training (downhill long runs) and conservative early pacing. Expected gain vs. flat: 1-3% for trained downhill runners; 0% or negative for untrained. Do not chase early miles — 6:30/mile through mile 6 of CIM feels like 7:00/mile flat but eccentric load is double.

## 5. Tactical Racing

### 5.1 Sit and kick

When the runner has a stronger finish than the field, or the field starts slow. Sit 1-3 sec behind the leader's shoulder through the body of the race; kick with 600-1200 m to go on roads, 200-400 m on track. Failure mode: field surges at 2K and the kicker is dropped before the kick opens. Solution: practice mid-race surges in training.

### 5.2 Front-running / breakaway

When the runner has aerobic strength but no kick, or conditions reward fast pacing. Push the early-middle stage (e.g., hard 5-km block at 10K-15K of a half) to drop sprinters before the kick. Risk: soloing into headwind costs 5-15% extra at any pace.

### 5.3 Pack running

For most amateurs, the highest-value tactic. Drafting in a pack of 2-6 cuts metabolic cost 1-7% depending on pace and wind. In marathon racing, a stable pace pack is worth 1-2% of finish time vs. soloing.

**Rules:** find a pack within 5 sec/mile of GP in the first 2 km; if pack is faster than GP, let it go (don't "bank time"); stay in second/third row (front gets no draft); take fueling lines that don't break draft.

### 5.4 Pacing groups (official pacers)

**When pacers help:**

| Situation | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Goal pace is 5+ minutes from current PR | Mental anchor, prevents going out too fast |
| First marathon at the distance | Reduces decision fatigue |
| Crowded race start | Pacer commands clear lane through field |
| Variable course (rolling) | Pacer manages effort by feel |

**When pacers hurt:**

| Situation | Risk |
|---|---|
| Pacer goes out 5+ sec/mile fast | Runner banks lactate they can't repay |
| Pace group balloons to 30+ runners | Surging, weaving, jostling at every aid station |
| Pacer banks early time on rolling course | Cumulative early surge, late fade |
| Runner's true fitness is between two posted paces | Pacer pulls runner above ceiling |

**Rules for using pacers:** treat as a guide, not a guarantor; carry a pace band as backup; if pacer goes out fast in the first 5K, drop to GP immediately; drift to the back of the pack to avoid surging at aid stations.

## 6. Heart Rate and RPE in Racing

### 6.1 Heart-rate ceilings by distance

HR ceilings are *guides*, not laws. Cardiovascular drift adds 3-5 bpm/hour at constant effort, so fixed mid-marathon caps are unreliable. Use HR as a backstop; pace and RPE primary.

| Distance | %HRmax | %LTHR |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | 95-100% | 105-110% |
| 10K | 92-96% | 100-105% |
| Half | 88-92% | 96-100% |
| Marathon | 80-88% | 88-95% |

**Rules:** marathon HR >90% HRmax in the first half predicts a blow-up; race HR >95% HRmax sustained >10 min predicts collapse for races >10K; account for ~5 bpm drift between km 5 and km 30.

### 6.2 RPE as primary control

For races over 10K and any race in heat or hills, RPE outperforms pace as a control variable. Borg CR-10 anchors:

| Race | RPE at start | RPE at midpoint | RPE final 25% |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5K | 6-7 | 8 | 9-10 |
| 10K | 5-6 | 7-8 | 9-10 |
| Half | 5 | 6-7 | 8-9 |
| Marathon | 3-4 | 5-6 | 8-10 |

**Calibration cue:** if the marathon's first 10K is RPE 6+, the pace is wrong. The first 10K should feel "comfortable" and trigger active restraint.

## 7. Cadence and Form During Late-Race Fatigue

### 7.1 What happens

Under fatigue, cadence falls disproportionately to stride length: cadence drops 3-8 spm in the final 10K of a marathon, stride lengthens (overstriding), vertical oscillation increases, posture collapses (anterior lean, head drops), ground contact time rises. Oxygen cost rises, pace falls, perceived effort rises. The cadence drop precedes the pace drop by a few minutes.

### 7.2 Late-race form cues

A short, repeatable mental cue is worth more than a complex form correction in late-race fatigue. Effective cues:

| Cue | What it fixes |
|---|---|
| "Quick feet" | Restores cadence |
| "Tall and proud" | Restores posture, opens chest |
| "Drive the elbow" | Restores arm swing rhythm |
| "Squeeze the glute" | Restores hip extension |
| "Soft hands" | Reduces shoulder tension |
| "Light steps" | Reduces braking force |

Cycle through cues every 30-60 seconds in the last 10K. They reset focus and momentarily reorganize neuromuscular patterns.

## 8. Mental Cues, Segmenting, and the Wall

### 8.1 Race chunking (segmenting)

Breaking the race into segments reduces the cognitive load of late-race effort. Common segmentation strategies:

| Method | How it works |
|---|---|
| 5K segments (marathon) | Mentally race "another 5K," not "another 22 miles" |
| Aid station to aid station | Reset every ~2 miles: drink, fuel, reassess |
| Gel intervals | Each segment ends with the next gel |
| Mile-by-mile ("run the mile you're in") | Hardest mental discipline; refuses to think past the next mile |
| Course landmarks | Use bridges, turns, hills as natural breakpoints |
| Quartiles (first quarter / second quarter / etc.) | Particularly useful for half marathons |

Tactical layer: each segment gets a process goal. E.g., "first 10K = controlled breathing"; "miles 11-20 = stay with pack"; "miles 21-26 = one mile at a time, form cues only."

### 8.2 The wall — mechanism and prevention

**What it is:** abrupt pacing collapse, typically at 30-35 km. Pace drops 30-90 sec/mile in 1-2 km. Subjective: legs go from "tired" to "unable to respond."

**Mechanism:** combined glycogen depletion (peripheral) + central fatigue. When muscle glycogen falls below ~20% of starting capacity, fat oxidation cannot supply ATP at MP rates. The brain detects falling fuel and downregulates motor output (Noakes central governor framework).

**Who:** 40-60% of marathon finishers report some wall-like experience. 4:00+ marathoners disproportionately affected (more time at intensities above fat-oxidation capacity); sub-3:00 runners hit fewer walls but more catastrophic ones.

**Prevention checklist:**

| Lever | How |
|---|---|
| Aerobic base | Higher base mileage builds glycogen capacity; fat-oxidation training spares glycogen |
| Marathon-pace specific work | Long runs with 12+ miles at MP train the metabolic machinery |
| Pacing | Conservative first half cuts anaerobic glycogen burn by 30-50% |
| Carb loading | 8-12 g/kg/day for 24-48 h pre-race adds 50-100% to glycogen stores |
| Race-day fueling | 60-90 g/h carbs delays muscle glycogen depletion by 30-60 min |
| Caffeine | 3-6 mg/kg pre-race + late-race dose blunts central fatigue |
| Heat management | Lower core temp preserves CNS function |

### 8.3 Late-race rescue (if the wall hits)

1. **Walk through the next aid station.** 30-60 sec of walking restores HR and lets fuel hit; time loss recovered if running resumes.
2. **Take gel + caffeine + water immediately.** 30 g carbs + 100 mg caffeine.
3. **Reduce pace to what can be held without further deterioration.** A 30 sec/mile slowdown that holds beats a "try to maintain GP" that becomes 90 sec/mile.
4. **External focus.** Tether to a runner 10-20 m ahead. Internal focus on fatigue accelerates collapse.
5. **Mile-by-mile cuing.** Refuse to think past the next aid station.

## 9. The Taper: Final 2-3 Weeks

### 9.1 Taper duration by distance

| Distance | Taper length | Volume reduction (peak week) |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | 5-7 days | 25-35% |
| 10K | 7-10 days | 30-40% |
| Half marathon | 10-14 days | 30-50% |
| Marathon | 14-21 days | 40-60% |
| Ultra (50K-100M) | 14-28 days | 50-70% |

**Rules:**
- The largest cut is to easy mileage; intensity is preserved through the taper.
- Run frequency is maintained at ~80% of normal. Don't suddenly add rest days.
- Add no novel workout types. Anything new in the final 10 days creates fatigue without adaptation.

### 9.2 Marathon taper structure (3 weeks)

| Week | Volume | Quality session | Long run |
|---|---|---|---|
| -3 | 80-90% peak | Final MP-specific (14-16 mi w/ 10-12 mi at MP) | Last long (20-22 mi) |
| -2 | 60-70% peak | 6-8 mi at MP, or 4-5 mi threshold | 12-14 mi w/ MP miles late |
| -1 | 40-50% peak | 3-4 mi w/ 4-6 x 1 min at 5K pace, 4-5 days out | "Freshener" 8-10 mi |

### 9.3 Day-by-day race week templates

The templates below assume the runner is competition-ready and has a Sunday race start.

**Marathon — race week template (Sunday race):**

| Day | Workout | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Mon | Easy run | 35-50 min |
| Tue | Race-prep workout (e.g., 3 mi w/ 5 x 1 min @ 5K pace, full recovery) | 35-45 min |
| Wed | Easy + 4-6 strides | 30-40 min |
| Thu | Rest or short easy shakeout | 0-30 min |
| Fri | Easy run + 4 strides | 25-35 min |
| Sat | Shakeout: 15-20 min easy + 2-4 strides | 15-25 min |

**Half marathon — race week template (Sunday race):**

### 9.4 Taper feel

"Taper crud" / "taper madness" — fatigue, sluggish legs, irritability, sleeplessness, phantom pains — is normal. Resist the urge to test fitness. The work is done.

## 10. Race Week Nutrition

### 10.1 Carb loading — by distance

Glycogen supercompensation matters for races >90 min. Below that, normal training nutrition suffices.

| Distance | Protocol |
|---|---|
| 5K, 10K | Normal training carbs (5-7 g/kg/day) |
| Half marathon | 7-8 g/kg/day for 24-36 h |
| Marathon | 8-12 g/kg/day for 36-48 h |
| Ultra (50K+) | 8-12 g/kg/day for 48-72 h |

**Marathon example (70 kg, 3-day load):** T-3: 7-8 g/kg = 490-560 g; T-2: 9-10 g/kg = 630-700 g; T-1: 9-10 g/kg = 630-700 g.

**Food choices (high carb, low fiber, low fat):** white rice, pasta, bread, bagels, English muffins, peeled potatoes, bananas, applesauce, honey, jam, pancakes, sports drinks (Maurten 320, Skratch), pretzels, rice cakes, small portions of lean protein.

**Errors:** all-you-can-eat pasta dinner the night before (carbs store slowly, volume causes GI distress); high-fiber "healthy carbs" the day before; novel foods.

### 10.2 Fiber reduction (T-1 to T-0)

In the final 24-48 h, drop dietary fiber to <10 g/day. Goal: empty the colon before the race.

**Drop:** salad, leafy greens, raw vegetables, beans/lentils/chickpeas, whole grains (oats borderline; many tolerate), cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage), nuts, seeds, dried fruit, high-fat foods, dairy if lactose-sensitive.

### 10.3 Pre-race meal

**Composition:**
- 1-4 g/kg carbohydrate.
- <10 g fiber.
- <15 g protein.
- <10 g fat.
- Familiar foods only.

**Timing:**

| Time before race | Approximate carb dose | Example meal |
|---|---|---|
| 4 hours before | 3-4 g/kg | Bagel + peanut butter + banana + 16 oz sports drink |
| 3 hours before | 2-3 g/kg | Oatmeal + banana + honey + coffee |
| 2 hours before | 1-2 g/kg | English muffin + jam + small banana |
| 1 hour before | 0.5-1 g/kg | Banana + half bagel or 1 gel + sports drink |
| 15 min before | One gel (25-30 g) | Topping off |

**Templates:** bagel + PB + jam + banana + coffee (3 h out); oatmeal + banana + honey (3 h out); white toast + jam + applesauce (3 h out).

**Individual variation:** test the meal in training before 2-3 long runs at GP. Large breakfasts: 4 h out. Small stomachs: 2-3 h. Coffee on race morning only if a regular coffee drinker.

### 10.4 Hydration in the final 48 hours

Goals: arrive normally hydrated (not over-hydrated); sodium 3-5 g/day (above normal); urine pale yellow on race morning, not clear.

| Time | Fluid |
|---|---|
| T-2 days | Normal + extra 500-1000 ml; salt food liberally |
| T-1 day | 30-40 ml/kg fluid (sports drink or electrolyte) |
| Race morning | 400-500 ml water/electrolyte 2-3 h before; sip 150-250 ml in final 60 min |
| Final 30 min | Sip only |

**Avoid:** chugging 1 L of water race morning ("preloading"). Causes mid-race urination and dilutes sodium.

### 10.5 Race-day fueling plan

**Carbs per hour (target by intensity and distance):**

| Race distance | Target carbs/h |
|---|---|
| 5K, 10K | None (race is too short) |
| Half marathon | 30-60 g/h (1-2 gels for non-elites) |
| Marathon | 60-90 g/h |
| Ultra | 90-120 g/h (with gut training) |

**Glucose:fructose ratio:**
- 60 g/h or below: 2:1 (most single-source gels are fine).
- 60-90 g/h: 2:1 ideal; 1:0.8 acceptable.
- 90-120 g/h: 1:0.8 required (glucose alone hits absorption ceiling around 60 g/h).

**Sodium:** cool 300-500 mg/h; hot/humid 500-800 mg/h; salty sweaters up to 1000 mg/h.

**Fluid:** cool 400-500 ml/h; warm 500-800 ml/h. Drink to thirst, not ahead of thirst. Hyponatremia from over-hydration is more dangerous than mild dehydration.

**Marathon fueling template (sub-3 to 4-hour runner, 70 kg):**

| Time | Fuel |
|---|---|
| 15 min pre | 1 gel (25 g) + sip water |
| Mile 4-5 | 1 gel (25 g) + 2-3 sips water/electrolyte |
| Mile 8-9 | 1 gel (25 g) + electrolyte at aid station |
| Mile 12-13 | 1 gel (25 g) + 100 mg caffeine |
| Mile 16-17 | 1 gel (25 g) + electrolyte |
| Mile 20-21 | 1 gel (25 g) + 100 mg caffeine |
| Mile 23-24 | Optional gel or sports drink |

Total: ~150 g carbs in race + ~25 g pre-race = 175 g; ~70-80 g/h depending on finish time.

## 11. Sleep and Race Morning

### 11.1 Sleep priority: T-2 night matters most

T-2 has the largest impact on race-day performance. Pre-race anxiety commonly disrupts T-1, but a single bad night has minimal effect on physiological capacity (HR, VO2, leg strength preserved). RPE rises with poor sleep but pace can be held.

**Sleep banking (T-7 through T-2):** add 60-90 min/night for 5-7 nights pre-race. Earlier bedtimes preferred. Naps of 20-90 min can substitute partial night sleep.

**T-1 protocol:** accept poor sleep is likely (lying still delivers ~50% of sleep's recovery value); no alcohol or screens after dinner; lay out kit before bed; set two alarms.

### 11.2 Race morning timing

Working backward from gun time (call it T-0):

| Time | Action |
|---|---|
| T-3:30 to T-4:00 | Wake up |
| T-3:00 to T-3:30 | Pre-race meal |
| T-2:00 | Begin sipping water/electrolyte |
| T-1:30 | Depart for venue (allow buffer for traffic, security, gear check) |
| T-1:00 | Arrive at venue, gear check, first bathroom visit |
| T-0:45 to T-1:00 | First gel + small sip if 4 h since meal |
| T-0:30 | Begin warmup (longer for shorter races; see §12) |
| T-0:15 | Final bathroom, final gel if marathon |
| T-0:05 | Corral / start position |
| T-0 | Gun |

**Early-start races (7:00 AM gun) often force a 4:00 AM wake.** Alternative: wake 4:30, smaller breakfast (~1.5 g/kg) at 5:00, gel top-up at 6:30.

### 11.3 Bathroom strategy

- Use the bathroom 30-60 min before gun time; earlier visits often need a repeat.
- Plan 60-90 min at venue for porta-potty queues; major marathons can run 30+ min waits at peak.
- Coffee 60-90 min pre-race stimulates the gastrocolic reflex; for many, this triggers the pre-race bowel movement.
- Find bathrooms outside obvious clusters (hotels, race buses, less-publicized banks).
- Mid-race needs are common in 3:30+ marathoners and in heat; plan for one possible stop.

## 12. Warmup Protocols

The shorter the race, the longer the warmup. The faster the race intensity, the more thoroughly the body needs to be primed.

### 12.1 Warmup by distance

| Race | Total time | Protocol |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | 15-25 min | 2-3 mi easy + dynamic drills + 4-6 strides ending with 1 at 3K-mile pace (research-backed primer: ~6 sec faster 5K) |
| 10K | 15-20 min | 1.5-2.5 mi easy + drills + 4-6 strides, last 1-2 at 10K pace |
| Half | 10-15 min | 0.5-1.5 mi easy + drills + 3-4 strides at HMP (or walk 5 min + 4 strides if no space) |
| Marathon | 5-10 min, optional | Walk 5-10 min or jog 3-5 min + 2-3 strides; first 3 km of race is the warmup |

**Why the inverse relationship:** marathon runs at 80-88% VO2max; a few easy miles in cost no race fatigue. 5K runs at 95-100% from gun; without priming, the first km wastes time hitting steady state. Glycogen conservation > muscle priming as race lengthens.

### 12.2 Cold-weather adjustment

In races below 40°F, add 5 minutes to warmup. A long-sleeve top during warmup that is shed at the corral is standard.

### 12.3 Hot-weather adjustment

In races above 70°F or high dew point, shorten warmup by 30-50% and emphasize cooling (ice towel, ice slurry). Pre-race core temperature dictates the heat ceiling.

## 13. Caffeine Timing and Dose

### 13.1 Established protocol

| Variable | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Dose | 3-6 mg/kg body mass |
| Pre-race timing | 45-60 min before gun |
| Mid-race redose | 1-2 mg/kg every 60-90 min for races >2 h |
| Half-life | 4-5 hours (a pre-race dose covers a full marathon) |

For a 70 kg runner: 210-420 mg pre-race. For reference, 8 oz brewed coffee ≈ 95-150 mg; 1 espresso ≈ 65-75 mg; one Maurten 100 caf gel = 100 mg; one 5-Hour Energy = 200 mg.

### 13.2 Templates

- **5K / 10K:** single pre-race dose 3-6 mg/kg, 45-60 min before gun.
- **Half marathon:** single pre-race dose 45-60 min before gun; optional 50-100 mg gel at mile 8.
- **Marathon:** 3 mg/kg pre-race + 100 mg gel at mile 13 + 100 mg gel at mile 20.
- **Ultra:** lower per-dose (1-2 mg/kg) but more frequent (every 90-120 min) to manage habituation.

### 13.3 Cautions

Caffeine-naive runners: do not start during the race; test in training 4-6 weeks out. Caffeine increases gut motility — time the pre-race dose around bathroom strategy. Caffeine before evening races impairs post-race sleep. Regular high-dose users may need a 3-7 day taper to restore acute response.

## 14. Pace Strategy Commitment

### 14.1 Writing it down

A pre-race written plan cuts in-race decision fatigue. Components: goal time and goal pace (min:sec/mile and /km); first-mile target (5-15 sec slower than GP); halfway split target with band; late-race rules (e.g., "no push before mile 20"); fueling schedule (gels, caffeine); walk-through-aid rule if used; bail-out plan (hold form, fuel, restart at adjusted pace).

### 14.2 Watch field setup

For most runners, a watch displaying these four fields is optimal:

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| Current lap pace (most recent km or mile) | Best-of-class pacing feedback; avoid instant pace which is noisy |
| Total time | Anchors against goal time |
| Distance | Anchors against course |
| Heart rate | Backstop against over-effort |

A pace band or wrist tattoo with mile-by-mile splits is a useful backup if the watch fails or auto-laps drift from course markers.

### 14.3 Pre-committed decision rules

Effective pre-race rules are written as if-then statements. Examples:

- "If first mile is more than 10 sec faster than GP, slow to GP +5 by mile 2."
- "If HR exceeds 90% HRmax at mile 5, hold pace and reassess at mile 10."
- "If pace falls 15 sec/mile in the second half, walk 30 s through next aid, take gel, restart at adjusted pace."
- "If race conditions force pace adjustment, the goal becomes age-graded performance, not original time goal."

## 15. Mental Preparation

### 15.1 Visualization

Two-week protocol: daily 5-10 min sessions, eyes closed. Rehearse start, body of race, finish; specifically the hard miles (marathon 18-23, half 9-12, 10K 4-5, 5K mile 2). Rehearse correct response to setbacks: GI distress, missed gel, off-pace early miles, weather change.

### 15.2 Self-talk

Research supports second-person self-talk over first-person ("you've got this" outperforms "I've got this"). Phrase library:

| Phase | Self-talk |
|---|---|
| Start | "You're prepared. Stick to the plan." |
| Body of race | "Smooth and strong." |
| First sign of fatigue | "This is normal. Keep moving." |
| Mid-race doubt | "One mile at a time." |
| Wall / dark patch | "You've trained for this. Drive the elbows." |
| Final 2K | "Empty the tank." |

### 15.3 Pre-race nerves

Pre-race anxiety is normal and useful (sympathetic activation primes performance). Manage excess via box breathing (4-4-4-4) for 2-3 min pre-gun; reframing ("I'm excited" beats "I'm nervous" — same physiology, different label); routine (same warmup, shoes, playlist as long runs).

## 16. Travel and Time Zone Management

(Cross-reference Task 12: travel races and adaptation.)

### 16.1 Arrival timing

| Race location | Recommended arrival |
|---|---|
| Same city / drive | T-1 day or T-0 morning |
| Same time zone, flight | T-1 day minimum, T-2 preferred |
| 1-3 time zones | T-2 to T-3 days |
| 4-6 time zones | T-5 to T-7 days; or arrive T-1 to "punch through" jet lag (single-night strategy) |
| 7+ time zones | T-7 to T-10 days |

### 16.2 Eastward vs. westward

- **Eastward travel (US to Europe, US to Asia):** harder; advance bedtime 30-60 min/day for 3-5 days pre-flight.
- **Westward travel (US to Asia eastbound flights, Europe to US):** easier; delay bedtime 30-60 min/day pre-flight.

Rule of thumb: 1 day per time zone for full circadian adjustment.

### 16.3 Flight day rules

Direct flights when possible. Hydrate 8 oz/hour, no alcohol. Stand and walk every 60-90 min (DVT prevention; calf compression sleeves help). Pack race kit, shoes, fuel, key meds in carry-on. Set watch to destination time on takeoff.

### 16.4 First days at destination

Morning sunlight day 1 anchors circadian clock. No naps past 4 PM. Eat at destination meal times even if not hungry. Easy run day 1; standard shakeout day before race.

## 17. Race Kit Selection

### 17.1 The "nothing new on race day" rule

Every kit element should be tested in ≥2 long runs, ≥1 at goal pace or faster. Common failure modes: shoes (blisters, foam-stiffness calf strain), socks (blisters from new fabric/seams), shorts (inner-thigh chafe, waistband irritation), singlet/shirt (nipple/underarm/shoulder-seam chafe), sports bra (band/strap chafe), hat (sweat, fit), watch (auto-lap, GPS, battery), belt/pockets (bouncing, gel access).

### 17.2 Kit selection by weather

| Conditions | Top | Bottom | Accessories |
|---|---|---|---|
| 35-45°F, dry, light wind | Long sleeve or arm warmers + singlet | Shorts or capris | Gloves, hat (discardable) |
| 45-55°F (optimal) | Singlet | Shorts | Optional arm warmers (discardable) |
| 55-65°F | Singlet | Shorts | None |
| 65-75°F | Singlet | Shorts | Hat for sun, sunglasses |
| 75°F+ | Singlet (light color) | Shortest tested shorts | White hat, sunglasses; ice strategy |
| Cold + rain | Lightweight long-sleeve + singlet | Shorts (legs warm up faster than torso) | Hat, gloves; trash bag at start (discard before gun) |
| Heavy rain | As above + body glide on chafe points | Shorts | Hat with brim |
| Snow / ice | Long-sleeve base + lightweight wind layer | Tights | Gloves, hat, screw-traction shoes if icy |

### 17.3 Shoe selection

Race shoe must have ≥40 miles of training use, ideally with at least one MP long run. For marathon, race shoe should have ≤200-250 miles to preserve foam responsiveness in carbon-plated models. Backup shoe ready at gear check. Socks tested, mid-thickness, wool or synthetic blend — never cotton.

### 17.4 Pre-race kit checklist

Race kit (singlet, shorts, sports bra, socks); race shoes (worn day before, not bagged); bib + pins; charged GPS watch; pace band; anti-chafe (Body Glide, Vaseline); hydration carry if needed; fuel (gels, blocks, salt) in tested storage; caffeine source; throwaway warm layer; sunglasses, hat, sunscreen; extra toilet paper; watch charger; post-race dry clothes, recovery shoes, recovery snack.

## 18. Quick Reference Tables

### 18.1 Pacing decision tree

```
Course:     flat → pace by GP. Hilly → effort/HR. Net downhill → conservative early.
Conditions: cool/dry → GP. Warm/humid → dew-point table. Hot → effort/HR only.
Distance:   5K/10K → even or slight positive. Half/Marathon → controlled even / slight negative.
Confidence: high → execute. Low/sick → goal becomes finish, not time.
```

### 18.2 Race week errors

| Error | Cost |
|---|---|
| Novel workouts in final 10 days | Fatigue without adaptation |
| New food day before | GI distress in race |
| Over-hydrating (1+ L water) | Hyponatremia, mid-race urination |
| New shoes / kit / fuel | Blisters, GI distress, failures |
| Going out 5+ sec/mile too fast | Wall, blow-up |
| Skipping habitual caffeine | Headache, withdrawal |
| Cutting all intensity in taper | Sluggish legs |
| Excessive carb load (>12 g/kg) | GI distress, water weight |

### 18.3 In-race decision triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| Mile 1 is 10+ sec/mile too fast | Slow to GP+5 for next 2 miles |
| HR >90% HRmax in first half | Hold pace, reassess at next 5K |
| Stomach distress | Walk through next aid, sip electrolyte, restart slowly |
| Missed gel | Take asap, restart 30-min intervals |
| Pace falls 15+ sec/mile mid-race | Walk 30 s, fuel + caffeine, restart adjusted |
| Cramping | Salt + slow + shorten stride; resume gradually |
| Side stitch | Slow, deep diaphragmatic breath, exhale on opposite footstrike |
| GPS drifting from markers | Switch to course markers; GPS overestimates on tangents |
