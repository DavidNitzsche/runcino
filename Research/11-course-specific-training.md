# 11 — Course-Specific Training (Hills, Downhills, Trail, Altitude)

How to adjust a generic training plan to match the demands of a specific race course: hills (up and down), trail, altitude, surface, heat, and course recon. Generic reference; not personalized advice.

## Definitions

- **DOMS** — Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness; soreness peaking 24–72 h after eccentric loading.
- **EIMD** — Exercise-Induced Muscle Damage; structural damage to muscle fibers, measured by creatine kinase (CK), strength loss, soreness.
- **Eccentric contraction** — muscle generates force while lengthening (quad on landing, especially downhill).
- **Concentric contraction** — muscle generates force while shortening (quad on push-off uphill).
- **RBE** — Repeated Bout Effect; protective adaptation from a prior eccentric stimulus that reduces damage from a subsequent identical stimulus.
- **LHTL** — Live High, Train Low altitude protocol (Levine & Stray-Gundersen).
- **Vert / Vertical gain** — total cumulative elevation climbed in a run, typically reported in feet or meters.
- **Cadence** — steps per minute (spm).
- **Specificity principle** — adaptations are specific to the imposed demand; training should mirror race demands as the goal date approaches.

## Hill Training: Principles and Adaptations

Hills produce four trainable adaptations in one workout: leg strength, neuromuscular power, VO2max stimulus, and running economy. Uphill loading recruits high-threshold motor units at lower joint-impact than flat speed work, making it a lower-injury alternative to track intervals.

### Adaptation Targets by Workout Type

| Workout | Grade | Duration per rep | Reps | Recovery | Primary adaptation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hill strides | 6–8% | 8–12 s | 6–10 | walk/jog down | Neuromuscular, mechanics |
| Short hill repeats | 6–10% | 30–60 s | 6–12 | jog down full | Power, VO2max stimulus |
| Long hill repeats | 4–6% | 2–5 min | 4–8 | jog down | VO2max, lactate threshold |
| Hill tempo | 2–4% (rolling) | 15–40 min cont. | 1 | — | Threshold + climbing economy |
| Sustained climb sim | race-specific | 10–30 min | 1–3 | jog down | Race-specific economy |

### Mechanical Effects of Uphill Running

- Force production from quadriceps, glutes, calves, hip flexors increases relative to level running.
- Cadence increases, stride length shortens, ground contact lengthens — the gait shifts toward a more upright posture.
- Energy cost rises ~3.3% per 1% of grade up to ~10–15%.
- Running economy gains transfer to flat running because of improved stride mechanics and force-production efficiency.

### Decision Rule: Hill Training Volume

```
If race vert <500 ft per 10 mi:        1 hill workout / 2 weeks (maintenance)
If race vert 500–1500 ft per 10 mi:    1 hill workout / week
If race vert 1500–3000 ft per 10 mi:   1 hill workout + 1 hilly long run / week
If race vert >3000 ft per 10 mi:       2 hill sessions/wk + most LRs hilly
```

## Training for Hilly Road Races

Three generic hilly-marathon profiles cover most major events:

| Profile type | Description | Example archetype |
|---|---|---|
| **Front-loaded climbs** | Bulk of climbing in first 10–15 mi, flatter finish | Hilly-then-flat course |
| **Late hills** | Flat/rolling first half, sustained climbs at miles 16–22 | Boston-style (Newton Hills) |
| **Persistent rollers + bridges** | Constant up/down, several discrete bridge climbs | NYC-style |
| **Symmetric rolling** | Hills distributed throughout course | San Francisco-style |

### Front-Loaded Climbs

- Practice running goal pace on tired legs after early hills: long run with the first 30–40% on hilly terrain, finish flat at marathon pace.
- Avoid blowing the first half climbing too hard; train heart-rate-based effort on hills, not pace.

### Late-Hill Courses (Boston Profile)

A net-downhill opening with concentrated late climbs (e.g., a series of climbs at miles 16–21) demands two distinct preparations:

1. **Eccentric quad durability** for the early descent (see eccentric loading section).
2. **Late-race climbing economy** when fatigued.

Generic prep:

- Progression long runs with the last 6–10 mi over rolling terrain at marathon effort.
- "Boston simulation" workouts: 10–14 mi long run with the first 4 mi gently downhill, then sustained climbs in miles 8–12.
- Hill repeats should target total climb roughly equal to the race's late-hill climb volume.

### Persistent Rollers + Bridges (NYC Profile)

Bridges are typically 1.0–1.5 mi at 3–5% grade, often into wind. Train:

- Long sustained climbs at marathon effort (not flat-out).
- Wind exposure: avoid always running tailwind-favored loops.
- Treadmill 4% incline tempo blocks (see treadmill specificity below) when terrain isn't available.

### Symmetric Rolling Hills (SF Profile)

When climb is distributed across the course, rolling-terrain tempo runs are the most specific session: 30–60 min at threshold-to-marathon effort over course-similar terrain. Avoid flat-only training; the runner must learn pace fluctuation by effort.

### Pacing Rule for Hilly Courses

```
Effort-based pacing (HR or RPE), NOT pace-based.
On climbs:    add 10–30 s/mi to flat goal pace; keep effort steady.
On descents:  shave 5–15 s/mi BUT cap at goal pace minus 20 s/mi
              (over-running downhills wrecks quads — see next section).
```

## Eccentric Quad Loading and Late-Race Quad Failure

Downhill running is the dominant eccentric stress in a race. The quadriceps lengthen under tension at every foot strike to absorb body weight as the knee flexes. Repeated cycles produce micro-tears, reduced peak torque, and DOMS.

### Documented Damage from Downhill Bouts

| Variable | Typical change after unaccustomed downhill bout |
|---|---|
| Maximal voluntary force (quads) | 15–30% reduction, recovery 24–96 h |
| Creatine kinase (CK) | 3–10× baseline, peak 24–48 h |
| Pain/soreness | Peaks 24–72 h |
| Stride length | Shortened during bout and after |
| Running economy (flat) | Worsened acutely, recovers over days |

### Ground Reaction Forces

At a –9° (≈–16% grade) slope, normal-impact-force peaks rise ~54% and parallel braking forces ~73% relative to level running (Vernillo et al.). Loading is concentrated in the quads, patellar tendon, and tibialis anterior.

### Repeated Bout Effect (RBE)

A single prior bout of downhill running confers protection against a subsequent identical bout for weeks to months:

- Reduction in DOMS markers ~40–50%.
- Reduction in CK rise ~50%.
- Reduction in strength loss and economy decrement.
- Protection persists up to ~6 months after a single substantial bout.

Mechanism is largely neural: shift toward low-threshold motor unit recruitment, improved synchronization, and connective-tissue remodeling. Eston's work showed that even short repeated downhill exposures (e.g., 5×8 min at moderate pace) produced protection against a future bout 5 weeks later.

### Eccentric Loading Protocol for Downhill-Heavy Races

```
Lead time: start 8–10 weeks before race
Progression:
  Week 1: 1× downhill segment, 5–8 min @ –4% to –5%, easy pace
  Week 2: 1× 8–10 min @ –4 to –6%
  Weeks 3–6: progress to 2 sessions/wk OR add downhill repeats:
            6–10 × 400–800 m @ –4 to –8% at goal race pace
  Weeks 7–8: peak — 1 long downhill simulation (race pace) + maintenance
  Final 10–14 days: taper downhill volume to avoid race-day residual damage
Strength complement: slow eccentric step-downs, split squats, Bulgarian
                     split squats, Nordic hamstring curls (3–4 s lowering).
```

### Avoid the Late-Taper Trap

A heavy downhill session inside ~10 days of race day risks racing on quads still impaired by EIMD. The last race-pace downhill should be 2–3 weeks out; final downhill running in the taper is short and easy.

## Net-Downhill Races and Negative-Split Strategy

Net-downhill marathons (point-to-point with substantial elevation loss, often with rolling first miles, e.g., CIM-style or Revel-style courses) reward conservative early pacing followed by progressive effort.

### Why Negative Splits Work on These Courses

- The earliest miles often have the largest gradient (gravity tempts overstriding).
- Early overstriding amplifies eccentric quad damage; quads fail in miles 18–22.
- Late-race terrain on most net-downhill courses is flatter or shallower descent, so a faster second half is mechanically achievable when quads are intact.

### Pacing Rule for Steep-Start Net Downhill

```
First 5 mi (steep descent):   goal flat pace MINUS 5–10 s/mi MAX
                              (do not chase free time)
Miles 5–13 (rolling-down):     goal pace, maintain cadence ≥176–180
Miles 13–22 (flatter down):    goal pace; can dip 3–5 s/mi if fueled
Miles 22–26.2 (often flat):    push if quads intact; this is the negative-split window
```

### Net-Downhill Training Adjustments

- 60–80% of long-run mileage should occur on terrain with similar grade to the race's average descent.
- Practice the exact race-day cadence; cadence within ±5% of preferred minimizes caloric and impact load on descents.
- Repeat-bout sessions every 2–3 weeks during build phase.

### Boston-with-Prep Negative Split

Boston rewards a controlled first half (especially the early downhill miles 1–4 and 6–9) so quads survive Newton (≈mi 16–21). Generic guidance: open at flat goal pace, do not bank time on early descent, expect to slow through Newton, and aim for flat or modest positive split. True negative splits at Boston are uncommon and require disciplined opening.

## Trail Running Specifics

Trails fall on a spectrum from "runnable singletrack" (smooth dirt, mild grade) to "highly technical" (rocks, roots, scree, exposure).

### Technical vs. Runnable: Training Differences

| Demand | Runnable trail | Technical trail |
|---|---|---|
| Avg pace vs. road | 90–95% | 60–80% |
| Cadence | road-similar | higher, shorter |
| Foot-eye coordination | low | dominant skill |
| Vertical:horizontal ratio | low | often >100 ft/mi |
| Power-hike segments | rare | frequent above ~15% grade |
| Specific training need | volume + climbing | terrain practice + agility + ankle stability |

### Generic Training Add-Ons for Technical Terrain

- ≥1 weekly run on technical surface during specific phase (last 8–12 wk).
- Fast feet drills, ladder/agility work, single-leg balance.
- Trail-shoe rotation matched to race surface (rock plate for rocky, low-stack for technical, cushioned for runnable).
- Sight-reading practice: scan 3–5 m ahead, not at feet.

### Power Hiking

Above ~15–20% grade, hiking is more energy-economical than running. Hike-pace practice on steep grades is required for any race with sustained steep climbs.

```
Power-hike trigger thresholds:
  Grade > 15–18%: most runners hike more efficiently
  HR > LT during sustained climb: hike to recover capacity
  Race terrain mostly steep: 1–2 hike-specific sessions/wk
```

## Ultra-Specific Training Adjustments

Ultras (>marathon, often with significant vert) shift the training emphasis from pace-specific work toward duration, vert, fueling, and time-on-feet.

### Vertical Gain Targeting

- Build to 50–80% of race-day vert per **week** during peak phase.
- Average daily vert during peak: 1,500–2,000 ft for mountain ultras.
- One weekly long climbing session (3–6 h, race-similar grade) is the keystone workout.

### Other Ultra Adjustments

- Back-to-back long runs (e.g., long Saturday + medium-long Sunday) build fatigue resistance.
- Practice race fueling at race intensity; gut training is non-optional.
- Night running practice for races starting in dark or extending overnight.
- Pack/vest weight worn during long runs to acclimate shoulders/hips.
- Long efforts at goal race grade (uphill + downhill) — both directions trained.

### Avoiding Vert Overload

Continuously high vert with no recovery weeks compromises running economy and risks chronic fatigue. Plan a deload every 3–4 weeks, with vert reduced 40–60%.

## Altitude Races: Sea-Level Athletes Racing High

Altitude reduces oxygen partial pressure; aerobic performance drops at all elevations above ~500 m, with effect compounding at higher altitudes and longer events.

### Performance Penalty by Altitude (Unacclimatized)

| Elevation | 5K penalty | Marathon penalty |
|---|---|---|
| 1,000 m / 3,300 ft | ~1–2% | ~3–4% |
| 1,500 m / 5,000 ft | ~2–3% | ~5–6% |
| 2,000 m / 6,600 ft | ~3–4% | ~7–9% |
| 2,500 m / 8,200 ft | ~4–6% | ~10–12% |

Approximate rule: ~3% per 1,000 m for short events, ~5–6% per 1,000 m for marathon.

Acclimatization reduces the penalty by roughly one-third (partial, 1–3 wk) to two-thirds (fully acclimatized, 3+ wk).

### Arrival Timing for Sea-Level Athletes Racing at Altitude

There are two viable strategies; the worst window is 18 h to 4 days post-arrival.

| Strategy | Arrive | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Race-on-arrival** | <18 h before race | Plasma volume not yet contracted; less acute mountain sickness; sea-level physiology mostly intact |
| **Acute window** | (avoid 1–4 days pre-race) | Worst symptoms: sleep disruption, plasma volume loss, no acclimatization yet |
| **Partial acclimatization** | 7–14 days | Initial ventilatory + hematological adjustments |
| **Full acclimatization** | 21+ days (ideal 3–4 wk) | Maximum red cell mass and economy adaptations |

### Training Adjustments Leading In

When residing at sea level for the build:

- Most training intensity stays at sea level (intensity at altitude is compromised).
- 7–14 days of altitude exposure pre-race is a useful compromise for many runners.
- Tempo and VO2max sessions should be sub-threshold during the acclimatization window (HR-based, not pace-based).
- Iron status: maintain ferritin ≥ ~40 ng/mL before altitude exposure; iron supplementation may be needed under medical guidance.
- Pre-acclimatization options: hypoxic tent, intermittent hypoxic exposure (IHE) for 4+ wk before travel.

### Race-Day Adjustments at Altitude

- Goal pace: apply altitude-conversion calculator to set realistic targets.
- Pacing by HR or RPE rather than pace; HR may run higher at threshold even after acclimatization.
- Fueling: increased carbohydrate utilization at altitude; plan ≥60–90 g/h for marathon.
- Hydration: insensible losses higher (cool dry air, hyperventilation).

## Altitude Athletes Racing at Sea Level

Acclimatized residents of altitude carry adaptations to sea level — but the optimal race window after descent is contested.

### Generic Window

| Days post-descent | Performance state |
|---|---|
| Days 1–3 | Often a "fast" window for short events; sea-level air feels easy |
| Days 4–10 | "Re-coordination" period; neuromuscular timing recalibrates |
| Days 14–21 | Hematological benefits still present; coordination restored — typically optimal |
| Days 21–60 | Diminishing benefit; most adaptations gone by ~3 weeks but may persist to 3 mo |

Levine and Stray-Gundersen's classical guidance: race within ~14–21 days of descent for endurance events; the window is athlete-specific. For LHTL training, the protocol typically includes 4 weeks at moderate altitude (~2,500 m) with sea-level intensity work, then descent ~14 days pre-race.

### LHTL Method Summary

```
Live altitude:    2,000–2,500 m (red cell mass stimulus)
Train altitude:   ≤1,250 m for high-intensity sessions
Duration:         ≥3 weeks (4 wk classic)
Performance gain: ~1.5% sea-level endurance (range: 0–6%)
Mechanism:        ~5–8% increase in red cell mass; ~5% VO2max
Benefit duration: ≥3 weeks after descent
```

## Hot-Weather Destination Races

Heat acclimation is documented in detail in Task 6. Cross-reference summary for course planning:

### Heat Acclimation Timing

| Days exposed | Adaptation completeness |
|---|---|
| 5–7 days | ~70–80% of full adaptation |
| 10–14 days | Near-complete (gold standard) |
| Ending 2–3 days pre-race | Adaptations preserved without residual fatigue |

Daily protocol: 30–60 min with core temperature ≥38.5 °C, 5–6×/wk for 10–14 days. Methods: sauna, hot bath post-run, deliberately overdressed running, training in mid-day heat.

### Conflicts with Other Course-Specific Demands

- Heat acclimation training reduces ability to hit pace targets during build — schedule before sharpening peak workouts, not during.
- For altitude + heat (e.g., desert mountain races), prioritize altitude exposure first; heat adapts faster (10–14 d) and can be added late.

## Course Recon: When to Drive or Run the Course

Course recon converts unknowns into knowns and reduces race-day cognitive load.

### Decision Rules

| Course type | Recon recommendation |
|---|---|
| Repeat race, same course | Skip; review prior data |
| Major flat road race | Optional; map study sufficient |
| Hilly road race, drivable | Drive once; run key segments if possible |
| Local hilly race | Run full course or longest hill segment in training |
| Trail/ultra, technical | Run as much as possible; minimum critical sections |
| Ultra with navigation risk | Run the entire course or hire course-knowledge guide |

### What Recon Captures

- Climb locations, gradients, and exposure (sun, wind).
- Aid station spacing and access (ultras).
- Surface transitions (asphalt → concrete → cobble; runnable → technical).
- Mental landmarks (where to push, where to conserve).
- Weather microclimates (coastal fog, valley heat).

### Recon Timing

- Drive recon: 2–4 weeks pre-race.
- Run recon (key segments): 3–6 weeks pre-race so it does not eat taper.
- Full-course run recon (ultras): 6–12 weeks pre-race.

## Specificity Principles: How Much Course-Specific Training?

Specificity is a continuum across the build. Early phases prioritize general fitness; later phases prioritize race-specific demands.

### Generic Periodization for Course Specificity

| Phase | Weeks before race | Course specificity |
|---|---|---|
| Base | 16–24 | Low; build aerobic + strength on varied terrain |
| Build | 8–16 | Medium; introduce course-similar workouts 1×/wk |
| Specific | 4–8 | High; majority of long runs and key sessions match course |
| Taper | 1–3 | Maintain specificity at reduced volume |

### Minimum Course-Specific Dose Heuristics

- Hilly marathon: ≥6 long runs and ≥4 quality sessions on race-similar terrain.
- Net-downhill marathon: ≥4 downhill long runs; ≥2 downhill race-pace sessions; final downhill stimulus ≥10 days out.
- Mountain ultra: ≥50% of long-run vert similar to race; race-day grade practiced both directions.
- Altitude race: ≥7 days altitude exposure (ideal 14–21).
- Hot race: 10–14 days heat acclimation ending 2–3 days pre-race.

### When Specificity Is Impossible

If the runner cannot access race-like terrain:

- Treadmill incline and decline (where available; many treadmills decline to –3%).
- Stair repeats for climb stimulus.
- Parking-garage ramps for sustained downhill eccentric loading.
- Stationary eccentric strength (slow step-downs, Spanish squats).
- Travel to similar terrain for one or two key sessions if possible.

## Surface Considerations

Surface modifies impact, biomechanics, and the rate of muscle recruitment.

### Surface Comparison

| Surface | Impact (relative) | Energy cost | Specificity for road race |
|---|---|---|---|
| Track (synthetic) | Low–moderate | Lowest | High for fast pace, low for distance |
| Road (asphalt) | Moderate | Reference | Highest for road races |
| Concrete | High | Slightly higher | Lower (avoid for high volume) |
| Treadmill (cushioned) | Lowest | Lower (no air resist.) | Moderate; +1% incline approximates outdoor |
| Hard-packed trail | Moderate | Slightly higher | High for trail races |
| Soft trail / grass | Lower | Higher | High for soft-surface trail |
| Sand / loose surface | Low impact, high effort | Highest | High specificity for sand events |

### Treadmill Specificity

- Treadmill ≈ outdoor energy cost when set to +1% incline (compensates for absence of air resistance).
- Treadmill running reduces hamstring and glute activation versus outdoor; chronic-only-treadmill runners may underdevelop these muscles.
- Treadmill is acceptable for: precise incline targeting (bridge simulation, 4% sustained climbs), heat training (room kept warm), hill repeats in flat geographies.
- Treadmill is poor for: technical-trail prep, downhill eccentric loading (most consumer treadmills don't decline), variable wind/surface practice.

### Track Use

- Useful for VO2max and threshold work, neuromuscular sharpening.
- Curves load the inside leg asymmetrically; alternate direction on long sessions.
- Limited value for hilly course preparation.

## Combining Course Demands: Decision Matrix

Real races combine multiple demands. Priority ordering:

```
Priority 1: Race date is fixed; work backward.
Priority 2: The slowest-adapting demand drives the schedule.
            - Altitude (3+ wk for full adaptation)
            - Eccentric quad durability (8+ wk progression)
            - Vert/strength (12+ wk progression)
Priority 3: Faster-adapting demands fit in last 2–3 wk.
            - Heat (10–14 d)
            - Course recon
            - Tapered specificity work
Priority 4: Conflicting demands → cap volume, prioritize highest-specificity.
            (e.g., heat training + downhill repeats both increase damage —
             alternate days, never stack.)
```

### Red Flags

- Heavy downhill session inside 10 days of race.
- First altitude exposure 1–4 days pre-race (worst possible window).
- Heat acclimation ending too early (>5 days pre-race) — partial detraining.
- Skipping heat adaptation for a hot destination race (high DNF risk).
- Treadmill-only training for a trail or technical race.
