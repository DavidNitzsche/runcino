# 18 — Fueling Products and Nutrition Database

Reference for during-training and race-day fueling. Generic data on commercial products, real-food alternatives, dosing protocols, and supplement evidence quality. All product specs reflect manufacturer-published data as of 2026.

---

## 1. Carbohydrate Intake Guidelines

**Carbohydrate (CHO):** sugar/starch macronutrient providing 4 kcal/g. Primary fuel for endurance running >60 min.

### Hourly intake by exercise duration

| Exercise duration | CHO intake (g/hr) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| <45 min | 0 | Water sufficient; mouth-rinse OK |
| 45–75 min | Small amounts / mouth-rinse | Single-source CHO acceptable |
| 1–2.5 hr | 30–60 g/hr | Single transportable CHO sufficient |
| 2.5–3 hr | 60–90 g/hr | Multiple transportable CHO required (glucose:fructose) |
| >3 hr (trained gut) | 90–120 g/hr | Requires gut training; 1:0.8 glucose:fructose |

### Updated 2024–2025 research

The traditional ceiling of 90 g/hr is being revised upward. Contemporary endurance research shows trained athletes with adapted guts can tolerate and oxidize up to 120 g/hr when CHO is delivered as a 1:0.8 glucose:fructose blend. Tour de France cyclists routinely consume 120 g/hr; trained marathoners and ultra runners are increasingly adopting these targets after gut-training protocols.

### Caveats

- 60 g/hr is the threshold above which single-source glucose causes GI distress in most runners.
- 90+ g/hr without multiple transportable carbs leads to gut bloat, nausea, sloshing.
- Heat, altitude, and high-intensity efforts reduce gut tolerance — back off 10–20%.
- Cross-reference Task 0A for full hourly targets by event distance.

## 2. Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates (Jeukendrup)

### The transporter argument

The intestinal wall absorbs glucose via SGLT1 transporters (saturate around 60 g/hr) and fructose via GLUT5 transporters (independent pathway, ~30 g/hr capacity). Co-ingesting both bypasses SGLT1 saturation and roughly doubles total absorption capacity.

**Glucose-only ceiling:** ~60 g/hr exogenous oxidation
**Glucose + fructose ceiling:** ~90–105 g/hr exogenous oxidation (peaks reported at 1.75 g/min)

### Optimal ratios

| Ratio (glucose:fructose) | Maximum absorption | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| 1:0 (glucose only) | ~60 g/hr | Short events <90 min |
| 2:1 | ~78 g/hr | 60–90 g/hr targets |
| 1:0.8 | ~90–120 g/hr | High-CHO targets, trained guts |
| 1:1 | ~85 g/hr | Slightly less efficient than 1:0.8 |

The 1:0.8 ratio (sometimes written 0.8:1 fructose:glucose) is now considered optimal for high-CHO fueling. SiS Beta Fuel, Maurten Gel 100, and several others use this blend.

### Performance benefit

Co-ingesting glucose-fructose vs glucose-only at matched CHO doses (≥90 g/hr) improves time-trial performance by ~8–9% and reduces GI symptoms.

## 3. Energy Gels — Brand Profiles

### Definitions

- **Single-source CHO:** maltodextrin or glucose only.
- **Dual-source / multiple transportable:** glucose + fructose in defined ratio.
- **Hydrogel:** alginate/pectin matrix that encapsulates CHO in stomach acid and reduces sloshing/osmotic load.
- **Isotonic:** dissolved at same osmolality as blood (~290 mOsm/kg); no water co-ingestion required.

## 4. Drink Mixes — Brand Profiles

### Definitions

- **Hypotonic:** lower osmolality than blood; absorbed faster than plasma.
- **Isotonic:** matches blood osmolality (~290 mOsm/kg); good for hydration + fueling.
- **Hypertonic:** higher osmolality; slows gastric emptying, used for high-CHO fueling.

### Maurten

- **Drink Mix 160 (40 g sachet → 500 ml):** 40 g CHO, 160 mg sodium, glucose:fructose 1:0.5. Hydrogel-forming.
- **Drink Mix 320 (80 g sachet → 500 ml):** 80 g CHO, ~110 mg sodium, glucose:fructose 1:0.7. Hydrogel.
- **Drink Mix 320 Caf 100:** same + 100 mg caffeine.
- Mix with low-calcium water (<40 mg/L) to ensure hydrogel formation.

### Precision Fuel & Hydration

- **PF 30 Drink Mix:** 30 g CHO, 250 mg sodium, 2:1 glu:fru.
- **PF 60 Drink Mix:** 60 g CHO, 500 mg sodium per 500 ml, 2:1 glu:fru.
- **PF 90 Drink Mix:** 90 g CHO, 1000 mg sodium, 2:1 glu:fru. Highest single-bottle CHO option.

### SiS Beta Fuel Drink

- 80 g CHO per 500 ml serving (2 scoops), 200 mg sodium, 1:0.8 maltodextrin:fructose. Designed for 80–120 g/hr targets.

### Tailwind Endurance Fuel

- **Per scoop (27 g):** 25 g CHO, 303 mg sodium, ~36 mg potassium, magnesium + calcium. Dextrose + sucrose (delivers glucose + fructose via sucrose hydrolysis ≈ 1:1). Designed as the only fuel needed (calories + electrolytes + hydration). Mild flavors, dilutable.
- 2-scoop bottle = 50 g CHO, 600 mg sodium.

### Skratch Labs

- **Sport Hydration Mix (22 g scoop):** 19–20 g CHO (cane sugar + dextrose), 400 mg sodium, 39 mg potassium, real fruit flavoring, no artificial sweeteners. Light hydration drink — not a high-CHO race fuel.
- **Super High-Carb Sport Mix:** 100 g CHO/serving, 380 mg sodium, dextrose-heavy, less common in running.

### LMNT (electrolytes only)

- **Per stick (no calories):** 1000 mg sodium, 200 mg potassium, 60 mg magnesium. Zero CHO. Used pre-race or during low-intensity ultras as a salt-only addition.

### Liquid IV Hydration Multiplier

- **Per stick (16 g):** 11 g CHO (cane sugar + dextrose), 500 mg sodium, 370 mg potassium, B-vitamins, vitamin C. CTT (Cellular Transport Technology) — marketed for rapid hydration. Lower CHO density than dedicated sports drinks.

## 5. Electrolyte Products

**Electrolytes:** charged minerals (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Cl-) lost in sweat. Sodium is the most commonly depleted.

### Sweat sodium losses

| Sweat sodium concentration | Category | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| <500 mg/L | Low (light sweater) | 300–500 mg/hr typical |
| 500–800 mg/L | Moderate | 500–800 mg/hr |
| 800–1200 mg/L | Heavy/salty | 800–1200 mg/hr; PH 1500 |
| >1200 mg/L | Very salty | 1200+ mg/hr, individual sweat test |

## 6. Caffeine — Sources, Dosing, Timing

### Effective dose

- **3–6 mg/kg body mass** is the established ergogenic range (ISSN position stand 2021).
- **Low dose** (1–3 mg/kg) — minimal but real effect.
- **>9 mg/kg** — high side-effect risk, no additional benefit.
- **70 kg runner: 210–420 mg total race-day intake.**

### Timing

- **Capsules / coffee:** 60 min pre-race for peak plasma levels at start.
- **Caffeinated gels / chews:** 5–15 min before needed; absorption is faster.
- **Caffeinated gum:** 5 min before; bypasses gut absorption.
- **Split dosing in long events:** 100 mg pre-start + 50–100 mg every 60–90 min thereafter.

### Race-distance caffeine plans

| Distance | Total caffeine (70 kg runner) | Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| 5K | 210 mg | All 60 min pre-race |
| 10K | 210–280 mg | Pre-race (single dose) |
| Half marathon | 210–280 mg | Pre-race + optional 1 caf gel at 45 min |
| Marathon | 280–420 mg | 200 mg pre + 100 mg at mile 13 + 100 mg at mile 20 |
| Ultra (50K+) | 300–500 mg | Spread over race; concentrate later half when fatigue dominant |

### Considerations

- Habitual caffeine users: minimal acute tolerance issue; benefits remain.
- 7-day caffeine taper before key race is unnecessary per recent meta-analyses.
- Avoid first-time race-day caffeine — always trial in training.
- Slow metabolizers (CYP1A2 GG genotype, ~10% of population) may experience reduced benefit / more anxiety.

## 8. Sports Drinks: Training vs. Racing

| Context | Drink type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Easy run <60 min | Water only | No fueling stimulus needed |
| Easy run 60–90 min | Water + electrolytes | Sodium replacement; CHO optional |
| Quality workout >75 min | Sports drink (30–60 g/hr CHO) | Race rehearsal; gut training |
| Long run 2–3 hr | Race-day product (60–90 g/hr) | Specificity; gut adaptation |
| Race <90 min | Concentrated CHO + caffeine | Maximize delivery |
| Race >2 hr | Multiple transportable CHO drink | Gut tolerance + sustained fueling |

### Key principle
Race-day fueling is rehearsed, not improvised. The drink and gels used on race day should have been used in 4–6+ long runs.

## 9. Water-Only Training

Appropriate when:

- Run duration <60 min and not a quality session.
- Easy/recovery run regardless of duration up to 90 min in cool conditions.
- "Train low" sessions deliberately performed in fasted or low-CHO state to upregulate fat oxidation (advanced; cross-reference periodization research).
- Recovery shakeout the day after a hard session.

Inappropriate when:

- Workout includes intervals at marathon pace or faster.
- Long run >90 min.
- Hot/humid (electrolytes needed).
- Race week (rehearsal).

## 12. Ultra-Distance Fueling

### Phase strategy

| Phase | Duration | Primary fuel | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 0–4 hr | Gels + drink mix | Front-load engineered CHO; gut still happy |
| Phase 2 | 4–8 hr | Mix gels + real food | Sweet fatigue starts; rotate flavors |
| Phase 3 | 8–16 hr | Real food dominant | Boiled potatoes, PB&J, broth, soup, pierogies |
| Phase 4 | 16+ hr | Salty/savory, fats | Cheese, bacon, hummus wraps; sweet aversion peaks |

### Sweet fatigue

The progressive aversion to sweet flavors during ultras is well documented. Marathon des Sables and 24-hour ultra studies show runners shift palate preference toward salty/savory/fatty foods. Practical implications:

- Stock aid stations and drop bags with savory options (chips, broth, cheese cubes, pierogies, bacon, salted potatoes).
- Avoid relying on a single flavor profile for >4 hr.
- Plain water + salt tablets becomes a fallback when even drink mix becomes unpalatable.

### Real food + fat
Finishers of 100-mile races consume ~5× more fat than non-finishers — typically as cheese, nuts, nut butters, hummus, or jerky. Fat intake is minimal in shorter races but becomes meaningful in events >8 hr.

### Caloric range
- 100K: 200–300 kcal/hr
- 100-mile: 200–250 kcal/hr (drops as race progresses)
- Multi-day stage races: 250–300 kcal/hr active + 1000+ kcal in rest periods

## 13. Gut Training Protocols (Costa et al.)

The gut adapts to repeated CHO exposure during exercise. Two weeks of daily gut training increases:

- SGLT1 transporter density (glucose absorption capacity ↑ 45–54%).
- Gastric emptying rate.
- Tolerance to high-osmolality solutions.

### Costa 2-week protocol

**10 sessions over 14 days. Each session:**
- 2 hr running at 60% VO2max (easy aerobic pace).
- 30 g CHO every 20 min (= 90 g/hr) via gels or whole food.
- Reported outcomes: 44–49% reduction in GI symptoms; 5% improvement in exercise performance.

### Progressive build (runners new to high-CHO fueling)

| Week | CHO target during long run | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 30 g/hr | Single product, single ratio |
| 2 | 45 g/hr | Add fluids |
| 3 | 60 g/hr | Trial caffeinated gel |
| 4 | 75 g/hr | Multiple product types |
| 5 | 90 g/hr | Race rehearsal |
| 6+ | 90–120 g/hr (if targeting) | 1:0.8 ratio mandatory |

### Key principles

- CHO during exercise must match race-day product (not different brand for training).
- Gut training is reversible — detrains within ~2 weeks of stopping.
- Run gut-training sessions at workout pace, not just easy runs.

## 14. Stomach Issues — Product Selection

| Symptom | Likely cause | Product adjustments |
|---|---|---|
| Nausea | High osmolality, dehydration | Switch to hydrogel (Maurten) or isotonic (SiS GO); add water |
| Bloating / sloshing | Slow gastric emptying, too much fluid | Reduce volume per intake; smaller more frequent sips |
| Cramping (gut) | Too much fructose, too concentrated | Lower fructose ratio; reduce concentration |
| Side stitch | Eating too close to start | Push pre-race meal earlier; smaller portion |
| Reflux / burping | Carbonation, fatty pre-meal | Avoid carbonated drinks; lower-fat pre-race meal |
| Diarrhea | Fructose malabsorption, FODMAPs, caffeine | Reduce fructose load; cut caffeine; trial low-FODMAP gels |
| Vomiting | Heat, severe dehydration, overconsumption | Reduce intake; cool down; switch to dilute drink |

### Gentlest products by GI category

- **Hydrogel:** Maurten Gel 100, Maurten Drink Mix.
- **Real-food, low-FODMAP:** Spring CanaBerry, Huma Original (chia base).
- **Sodium-free for cramp-prone gut:** Precision PF 30 Gel.
- **Isotonic, low concentration:** SiS GO Isotonic.

## 15. Dietary Restriction Adaptations

### Vegan

- All Maurten products, SiS Beta Fuel, Spring Energy, Huma, Precision PF gels, Tailwind, Skratch — vegan-compatible.
- Most whey/casein recovery products are NOT vegan; use plant-protein blends (pea + rice, soy isolate).
- Focus on: B12 supplementation, iron monitoring, leucine intake (3 g/serving target post-exercise).

### Gluten-free

- Most engineered gels and drink mixes are gluten-free (none use wheat-derived CHO).
- Real-food caution: pretzels, bagels, regular bread, oats unless certified GF.
- Maurten Solid 160 contains oats — check certification per market.
- Honey Stinger waffles contain wheat; gels do not.

### Low FODMAP

- Avoid fructose-only products and high-fructose-load gels (>20 g fructose/serving) — fructose is a FODMAP at high doses.
- Avoid honey, agave, apple, pear, mango-based products (high in fructose/fructans).
- Maltodextrin is well-tolerated; SiS GO Isotonic (maltodextrin only) is low-FODMAP-friendly.
- Tailwind dextrose-based formula is generally tolerated.
- Avoid sorbitol-containing products (some "sugar-free" gums, dried apricots).

### Nut allergies

- Most gels are nut-free; check Spring Energy Awesome Sauce (contains MCT oil, sometimes processed in shared facility).
- Avoid PB&J in ultra fueling — substitute sunflower-seed butter.

## 16. Hydration Protocols

Cross-reference Task 19 for full sweat-rate testing and personalized hydration plans.

### Generic guidelines

| Condition | Fluid intake (ml/hr) | Sodium (mg/hr) |
|---|---|---|
| Cool, easy effort | 400–600 | 200–400 |
| Cool, race effort | 500–700 | 400–600 |
| Hot, easy effort | 600–900 | 600–900 |
| Hot, race effort | 700–1000 | 800–1200 |
| Very hot/humid | 800–1200 (max gut tolerance) | 1000–1500 |

### Pre-race hydration

- 24 hr before: drink to clear/light-yellow urine; add 500 mg sodium with last meal.
- 2–3 hr before: 500–600 ml water + electrolyte.
- 15 min before: 150–250 ml top-up.

### Hyponatremia risk

Drinking plain water in excess (>1 L/hr for hours) without sodium replacement can cause exercise-associated hyponatremia. High-risk: slow marathoners, hot-weather ultras, light sweaters who over-hydrate.

**Evidence quality scale:**
- **A**: Multiple high-quality RCTs + meta-analyses; consistent positive effect.
- **B**: Several positive RCTs; some heterogeneity.
- **C**: Limited RCTs; emerging or mixed evidence.
- **D**: Equivocal or null findings; not recommended for performance.

### Specific supplement notes

#### Creatine monohydrate (3–5 g/day)
Strong for sprint, power, repeated efforts; mixed for steady-state endurance. Indirect benefits for runners: increased glycogen storage (water bound), improved high-intensity track work, recovery from strength training. 1–2 kg water-weight gain is normal during loading.

#### Beta-alanine (4–6 g/day, split doses)
Increases muscle carnosine; buffers H+ during high-intensity efforts. Effects best seen at 1500m–5K distances. Causes paresthesia (tingling) at acute doses >800 mg — use sustained-release forms or split doses. Loading takes 4 weeks.

#### Sodium bicarbonate (0.2–0.3 g/kg, 60–90 min pre-event)
Buffers blood lactate; benefits high-intensity exercise lasting 30 sec–12 min. GI distress is the main barrier — use enteric-coated forms, take with food, split dose, or experiment with serial loading (0.2 g/kg/day × 5 days). Most relevant for 800m–5K runners.

#### Beetroot juice / inorganic nitrate (400–800 mg nitrate)
Reduces oxygen cost of submaximal running by ~1–3%. Best response in non-elite endurance athletes; elite responders less consistent. Take 2–3 hr pre-race for acute, or daily for 3+ days for chronic loading. ~15–20% genetic non-responders. Do NOT use mouthwash (kills oral nitrate-reducing bacteria) within 24 hr of dosing.

#### Caffeine
Most evidence-supported legal ergogenic for endurance running. See section 6 for protocols.

#### Iron
Routine supplementation in non-deficient runners shows no benefit and may cause harm (oxidative stress). Test ferritin annually; supplement only with documented deficiency. Female runners and amenorrheic athletes are highest risk.
