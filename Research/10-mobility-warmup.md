# 10 — Mobility, Warmup, and Cool-down Protocols

## Definitions

- **Static stretching**: holding a muscle at end-range without movement (typically 15–60 s).
- **Dynamic stretching**: active controlled movement through ROM, 8–15 reps/side.
- **Ballistic stretching**: bouncing into end-range. Not recommended; injury risk exceeds benefit.
- **PNF**: proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation; isometric contraction + passive stretch (contract-relax, CRAC).
- **SMR**: self-myofascial release via foam roller, ball, or stick ("rolling").
- **Drill**: isolated movement pattern to reinforce mechanics (A-skip, B-skip).
- **Stride**: 60–120 m controlled acceleration to ~90% effort, 15–25 s.
- **Activation**: low-load exercises pre-session to recruit target muscles.
- **Mobility**: usable, controllable joint ROM (vs. passive flexibility).
- **Closed-chain**: distal segment fixed (foot on ground). Open-chain: distal segment free.
- **Dorsiflexion**: ankle motion bringing toes toward shin.
- **Hip extension**: thigh behind trunk; propulsive phase of running.

## Why Dynamic Pre-Run, Static Post-Run

### The static-stretching-pre-exercise problem

Pre-exercise static stretching produces a measurable, dose-dependent decrement in force output, power, and explosive performance. Pooled effects from meta-analyses:

| Outcome | Mean acute decrement |
|---|---|
| Maximal strength | −5.4% |
| Power | −1.9% |
| Explosive performance (jump, sprint) | −2.0% |

Magnitude scales with hold duration:

| Static hold per muscle group | Effect on strength/power |
|---|---|
| < 30 s | Trivial; no meaningful decrement |
| 30–60 s | Small decrement (~1–3%) |
| ≥ 60 s | Clear decrement (~5–8%) |
| ≥ 90 s | Largest decrements; commonly used in studies showing impairment |

Mechanism: (1) reduced musculotendinous stiffness cuts elastic energy return; (2) transient neural inhibition reduces motor-unit recruitment. Effect dissipates within 10–20 min if followed by dynamic activity.

### Why dynamic warmup is preferred

Dynamic stretching either improves or has no negative effect on subsequent power, sprint, and jump performance. It raises muscle temperature, increases nerve-conduction velocity, primes the stretch-shortening cycle, and rehearses sport-specific patterns. McGowan's review: structured warmups improved sprint in 69%, jump in 87.5%, and agility in 83% of studies.

### Combined protocols

If pre-run static stretching is necessary, keep holds < 30 s and follow with ≥ 5 min of dynamic activity + strides. This abolishes the decrement.

## RAMP Framework

The RAMP protocol (Jeffreys, 2006) is the modern S&C standard. Phases:

| Phase | Goal | Duration | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **R**aise | Increase HR, core temp, blood flow | 3–5 min | Easy jog, brisk walk, skipping |
| **A**ctivate | Recruit key muscles | 2–4 min | Glute bridge, clamshell, monster walk |
| **M**obilise | Active joint ROM | 3–5 min | Leg swings, lunges, hip circles, ankle rocks |
| **P**otentiate | Match session intensity | 2–5 min | Strides, drills, race-pace pickups |

Total 10–20 min depending on session demand.

## Pre-Run Dynamic Warmup

### When the warmup is needed vs. optional

| Session type | Warmup needed? | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Easy/recovery run < 60 min | Optional | First mile of run = warmup |
| Long run | Optional/minimal | First 1–2 mi at very easy pace |
| Steady/tempo | Yes (10–12 min) | Need to access threshold pace clean |
| Threshold/cruise intervals | Yes (12–15 min) | Same |
| Track intervals (VO2max) | Yes (15–20 min) | High neuromuscular demand |
| Short repeats / sprint work | Yes (20–25 min) | Maximal recruitment required |
| Race < half marathon | Yes (15–25 min) | Hard from gun |
| Marathon | Minimal (5–10 min) | First miles serve as warmup |

### Standard dynamic warmup sequence

Default 8–10 min sequence before any quality session. In order; each ~30–60 s.

| # | Exercise | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Easy jog | 5–10 min |
| 2 | Walking knee hug | 8/side |
| 3 | Walking quad stretch (heel to glute) | 8/side |
| 4 | Walking figure-4 | 8/side |
| 5 | Walking spiderman lunge | 6/side |
| 6 | Walking lunge with rotation | 6/side |
| 7 | Front-to-back leg swings | 12/side |
| 8 | Side-to-side leg swings | 12/side |
| 9 | Knee-to-wall ankle rocks | 10/side |
| 10 | High-knee march in place | 20 steps |
| 11 | Butt kicks | 20 steps |
| 12 | A-skip | 20–30 m × 2 |
| 13 | Strides (quality sessions only) | 4–6 × 80–100 m |

### Activation block (after jog, before mobility)

Optional ~3 min for runners with hip stability concerns or PFP/ITBS history.

| Exercise | Dose |
|---|---|
| Glute bridge | 10 reps |
| Single-leg glute bridge | 8/side |
| Clamshell (banded if available) | 12/side |
| Monster walk (banded) | 10 steps each direction |
| Standing fire hydrant | 10/side |

## Race Warmup

Longer and more progressive than workout warmups: adrenaline shortens perceived effort and races start hard.

### 5K race warmup (25–30 min, finish 5–10 min before gun)

| Time before start | Activity | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| T-30 min | Easy jog | 10–15 min |
| T-15 min | Dynamic mobility sequence | 5–8 min |
| T-10 min | Drills (A-skip, B-skip, high knees) | 2 min |
| T-7 min | Strides at race pace | 4–6 × 100 m |
| T-3 min | Walking, breathing reset | 2–3 min |

### 10K race warmup (20–25 min)

Same template, slightly less volume: 8–12 min jog, dynamic, 4 strides at 10K pace.

### Half marathon warmup (10–15 min)

5–10 min light jog, abbreviated dynamic, 2–3 short strides at half-marathon pace.

### Marathon warmup (minimal, 5–10 min)

Light walking + brief dynamic. No strides. First 1–3 mi of race serve as warmup. Conserve glycogen.

### General rule

| Race distance | Warmup volume relative to 5K |
|---|---|
| 1500 m / mile | 110% |
| 5K | 100% (baseline) |
| 10K | 75% |
| Half marathon | 40% |
| Marathon | 15–20% |
| Ultra | 10% (walk to start) |

## Track Session Warmup

Track intervals demand the highest neuromuscular readiness. Full sequence:

| Step | Activity | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Easy jog | 15–20 min |
| 2 | Standing mobility (leg swings, hip circles, ankle rocks) | 5 min |
| 3 | Activation (glute bridge, clamshell) | 2 min |
| 4 | Walking lunge, knee hug, figure-4 | 1 lap (400 m) |
| 5 | Drills (A-skip, B-skip, high knees, butt kicks, carioca) | 20 m each, 1–2 sets |
| 6 | Strides at goal pace | 4–6 × 80–100 m, full recovery |
| 7 | Conservative first 100–200 m of opening rep | — |

Total 25–30 min. For short fast repeats (200s/400s above VO2max pace), lengthen strides toward target pace. Finish warmup ≤ 5 min before first rep.

## Long Run Warmup

Metabolic/durability session, not neuromuscular. Warmup is minimal.

| Option | Description |
|---|---|
| **First-mile (default)** | First 1–2 mi at 30–60 s slower than goal long-run pace. No drills, no strides. |
| **Pre-run mobility (if stiff)** | 2–3 min leg swings, ankle rocks, hip circles, then run. |
| **Cold-weather variant** | Add 3–5 min walk before jog. |

End-of-long-run strides (last 1–2 mi) are a useful base-day neuromuscular stimulus; not part of warmup.

## Drills

Drills are mechanics rehearsal, not warmup substitutes. Execute on flat, smooth surface (track, grass, smooth pavement); 20–30 m per rep; walk back to recover.

| Drill | Purpose / Key cue |
|---|---|
| **High-knee march** | Hip flexor activation, posture. Knee to hip height, opposite arm drives. |
| **High-knee run** | Cadence and hip flexor speed. Quick contact, knee high. |
| **Butt kicks** | Hamstring activation, heel recovery. Heel to glute; foot stays under hip. |
| **A-skip** | Cyclical leg action, dorsiflexed foot strike under COM. Knee up, ball-of-foot strike under hip. |
| **B-skip** | Active hamstring "paw-back." A-skip + leg extension and sweep down. |
| **C-skip** | Hip abduction/external rotation. Circular outward leg motion. |
| **Carioca** | Transverse-plane hip mobility. Crossover step; hips rotate freely. |
| **Skipping** | Stretch-shortening cycle, ankle stiffness. Springy contact, minimal ground time. |
| **Bounding** | Power, hip extension. Long stride, full triple extension. |
| **Straight-leg run** | Hamstring activation. Locked knees, paw down. |
| **Fast feet** | Turnover, ground-contact time. Tiny steps, very high cadence. |

### Drill prescription

| Session | Drill volume |
|---|---|
| Easy run | None (or 1 set after run) |
| Workout warmup | 1–2 sets of 3–5 drills, 20 m each |
| Track session | 2 sets of 4–6 drills, 20–30 m each |
| Race warmup | 1 set of 3–4 favorites |
| Standalone drill session | 3–4 sets, 6–8 drills, 20–40 m |

## Strides

A 60–120 m controlled acceleration: build to ~90–95% max speed over 20–30 m, hold relaxed fast running 5–10 s, decelerate 10–20 m. Total 15–25 s per stride.

### Technique

- Tall posture, relaxed shoulders.
- Arms drive forward-back, elbows ~90°.
- Foot lands under center of mass, midfoot contact.
- Cadence at or above race-pace cadence.
- "Fast but smooth," not max sprint — runner should feel they could push harder.

### Dosing table

| Use case | # | Length | Rest | Pace |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| End of easy run | 4–6 | 80–100 m | Walk-back | Smooth fast |
| Pre-quality workout | 4 | 80–100 m | Full | Goal interval pace |
| Pre-race (5K–10K) | 4–6 | 80–100 m | Full | Race pace |
| Pre-race (half/full) | 2–3 | 60–80 m | Full | Race pace |
| Standalone neuromuscular | 8–10 | 100–150 m | Full (60–90 s) | Smooth fast |

### Surface and frequency

| Surface | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Track | Best — flat, predictable |
| Smooth grass | Best for high volume — softer impact |
| Smooth pavement | Acceptable |
| Trail / uneven | Avoid — ankle injury risk |
| Treadmill | Acceptable; harder to manage acceleration |

Frequency: 2–3/week. 2/wk drives consistent adaptation; 3/wk if training volume allows.

### Placement

- After easy runs; never before hard workouts (except as final step of workout warmup).
- Inside warmup before quality sessions or races.
- Avoid day before a race (2 short ones max if any).

## Hip Flexor Mobility

Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus) and rectus femoris shorten with running volume + prolonged sitting. Chronic tightness drives anterior pelvic tilt, reduced hip extension, and compensatory low-back load.

### Tests

| Test | Checks |
|---|---|
| Modified Thomas test | Iliopsoas, rectus femoris length |
| Prone hip extension | Active hip extension ROM |

### Static hip-flexor stretches (post-run / standalone)

| Stretch | Hold |
|---|---|
| Half-kneeling hip flexor (posterior pelvic tilt, squeeze rear glute) | 45–60 s/side |
| Couch stretch (rear shin against wall) | 60–90 s/side |
| Standing quad pull with posterior pelvic tilt | 30–45 s/side |
| Lunge with overhead reach + side bend | 30–45 s/side |

Hip flexor holds ≥ 45 s for meaningful length change.

### Dynamic hip flexor work (pre-run)

| Drill | Reps |
|---|---|
| Walking knee hug | 8/side |
| Walking quad pull | 8/side |
| Spiderman lunge w/ rotation | 6/side |
| World's greatest stretch | 5/side |

## Hip Extension and Glute Activation

Glute max = prime hip extensor. Glute med = pelvic stabilizer in single-leg stance. Reduced gluteal activation correlates with patellofemoral pain and contralateral pelvic drop during running.

### Activation circuit (3 min, pre-run on quality days or as daily routine)

| Exercise | Dose |
|---|---|
| Glute bridge (2 s top squeeze) | 10 reps |
| Single-leg glute bridge | 8/side |
| Clamshell (banded) | 12/side |
| Side-lying hip abduction | 12/side |
| Monster walk (band above knees) | 10 steps each direction |
| Single-leg RDL hold | 30 s/side |

### Bridge cues (to actually hit glutes)

- Posterior pelvic tilt before lifting.
- Drive through the heel.
- Stop at hip-knee-shoulder line; no hyperextension.
- Squeeze glutes hard at top.

A bridge done in anterior pelvic tilt with lumbar extension loads hamstrings and erectors, not glutes.

## Thoracic Spine Mobility

Running posture, desk work, and cycling collapse the T-spine into flexion. Limited T-spine extension drives compensation at the lumbar spine and shoulders, restricting arm carriage and breathing.

### Tests

| Test | Target |
|---|---|
| Wall angel | T-spine extension + scapular control |
| Quadruped thoracic rotation | T-spine rotation |
| Seated rotation (arms crossed) | T-spine rotation, isolated |

### Mobility drills

| Drill | Dose |
|---|---|
| Foam roller T-spine extension (roller perpendicular at lower thoracic, hands behind head) | 5–8 reps × 2 segments |
| Quadruped thread-the-needle | 8/side |
| Quadruped T-spine rotation (hand to head, elbow to ceiling) | 8/side |
| Wall slides | 10 reps |
| Open book (side-lying) | 8/side |
| Cat-cow | 8 reps |

### Protocol for restricted T-spine

3–5 min foam roller passive mobilization, then 1–2 active drills. Daily × 4–6 weeks for measurable change.

## Ankle Mobility (Dorsiflexion)

Dorsiflexion ROM affects stride length, midfoot strike, knee tracking, and shock absorption. Limited dorsiflexion is linked to knee valgus, calf/Achilles strain, and plantar fasciopathy.

### Knee-to-wall test (weight-bearing lunge)

Standard closed-chain dorsiflexion assessment.

**Procedure**: Face wall, foot pointing at wall. Lunge knee to wall, heel flat. Move foot back to furthest distance where knee still touches wall heel-flat. Measure big toe to wall.

| Distance (cm) | Interpretation | Approximate dorsiflexion |
|---|---|---|
| < 5 | Significant restriction | < 18° |
| 5–8 | Moderate restriction; address | 18–29° |
| 8–10 | Functional minimum | 29–36° |
| 10–12 | Adequate for running | 36–43° |
| > 12 | Excellent | > 43° |

Each cm ≈ 3.6° dorsiflexion. Side asymmetry > 1.5 cm is clinically meaningful.

### Closed-chain dorsiflexion drills

| Drill | Dose |
|---|---|
| Knee-to-wall ankle rock | 10/side |
| Banded ankle mobilization (posterior tibial glide) | 10/side |
| Half-kneeling dorsiflexion rock | 10/side |
| Goblet squat hold | 30–60 s |
| Calf wall stretch — straight knee (gastroc) | 30 s/side |
| Calf wall stretch — bent knee (soleus) | 30 s/side |

## Calf and Achilles Mobility

Calf complex = gastrocnemius (crosses knee + ankle) + soleus (crosses ankle only). Both attach to Achilles. Achilles load ≈ 6–8× body weight per stride.

### Stretches

| Stretch | Hold |
|---|---|
| Wall calf — straight knee (gastroc) | 30–45 s/side |
| Wall calf — bent knee (soleus) | 30–45 s/side |
| Step-edge heel drop | 30–45 s/side |
| Downward dog with heel pedal | 30 s |

### Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol)

Standard for Achilles tendinopathy and calf-Achilles resilience.

| Variant | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Straight-knee heel drop on step | 3 × 15 | 2x/day | Gastrocnemius/Achilles |
| Bent-knee heel drop on step | 3 × 15 | 2x/day | Soleus |

12 weeks for tendinopathy rehab. For prevention: 2–3×/week.

**Insertional tendinopathy mod**: do not drop below step (avoid insertion compression against calcaneus).

## Post-Run Cool-down

### What research shows

Active cool-down has limited evidence for enhancing same- or next-day performance, does not measurably reduce injuries, and does not blunt long-term adaptation. Modest genuine benefits:

- Faster lactate clearance vs. immediate rest (matters when next effort < 20 min away).
- Gradual HR/BP return; reduces venous pooling and post-exercise dizziness.
- Psychological transition from training mode.
- Modest DOMS reduction, especially combined with foam rolling.

### Practical post-run cool-down

| Step | Activity | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Easy jog or walk | 5–10 min after hard sessions; skip after easy runs |
| 2 | Light walking | 2–3 min until HR < 120 |
| 3 | Static stretching of running-specific muscles | 5–10 min |
| 4 | Foam rolling (optional) | 5–10 min |

## Static Stretching Post-Run

### Timing and dose

- Within 10–15 min of finishing the run is the prime window.
- Hold each stretch 30–60 s.
- 1–2 sets per muscle group.
- Total 5–10 min for the running-relevant set.

### Standard post-run static set

| Stretch | Hold |
|---|---|
| Wall calf — straight knee (gastroc) | 30 s/side |
| Wall calf — bent knee (soleus) | 30 s/side |
| Standing quad pull | 30 s/side |
| Half-kneeling hip flexor | 45 s/side |
| Standing forward fold or seated hamstring | 30 s/side |
| Figure-4 | 30 s/side |
| Pigeon pose | 45 s/side |
| Butterfly | 30 s |
| Child's pose | 30 s |

Skip on easy days when the runner feels fine; benefit comes from consistency on hard-session days.

## Foam Rolling Protocols

### Evidence summary

Foam rolling acutely increases ROM with no decrement to subsequent strength or power. Post-exercise rolling reduces DOMS and may modestly improve next-day performance. No consensus on optimal duration/pressure; effects are short-lived.

### Pre-run vs. post-run rolling

| Timing | Goal | Duration per muscle |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-run (only if needed) | Increase ROM in restricted area | 30–60 s, lighter pressure |
| Post-run | Recovery, DOMS reduction | 60–120 s, moderate pressure |
| Standalone evening | General maintenance | 60–90 s per area |

### Standard post-run foam roll sequence (~8–10 min)

| Area | Duration |
|---|---|
| Calves | 60 s/side |
| Peroneals (outer lower leg) | 30 s/side |
| Quadriceps | 60 s/side |
| IT band (lateral thigh, lighter pressure) | 30 s/side |
| Adductors | 30 s/side |
| Hamstrings | 60 s/side |
| Glutes | 60 s/side |
| Thoracic spine (roller perpendicular) | 60 s |
| Lats | 30 s/side |

### Rules

- Roll in line with muscle fibers, slow pace (~1 in/s).
- On a tender point, pause 20–30 s with breath.
- Avoid: bony prominences, IT band insertion at knee, lumbar erectors, neck, joints.

## Recovery Yoga Sequences

Yoga adds what isolated stretching does not: breath-coupled, posturally integrated movement and parasympathetic activation. Prefer yin / restorative / slow flow over power yoga for recovery.

### 15–20 min runner's recovery flow (post-run or evening)

| Pose | Hold |
|---|---|
| Child's pose | 1 min |
| Cat-cow | 8 cycles |
| Downward dog (with heel pedal) | 1 min |
| Low lunge (anjaneyasana) | 60 s/side |
| Half splits | 45 s/side |
| Lizard pose | 60 s/side |
| Pigeon pose | 90 s/side |
| Seated forward fold | 60 s |
| Wide-legged seated forward fold | 60 s |
| Supine spinal twist | 60 s/side |
| Happy baby | 45 s |
| Legs up the wall | 3–5 min |
| Savasana | 2–3 min |

Hold target: 5–10 breaths minimum (~30 s); deeper stretches 60–90 s.

## Daily Mobility Routine (5–10 min)

Minimum effective dose for runners without dedicated yoga/mobility sessions. Any day, any time.

| Movement | Dose |
|---|---|
| Cat-cow | 8 cycles |
| World's greatest stretch | 4/side |
| Couch stretch or half-kneeling hip flexor | 45 s/side |
| Quadruped T-spine rotation | 6/side |
| 90/90 hip switches | 10 reps |
| Knee-to-wall ankle rock | 10/side |
| Standing forward fold | 30 s |
| Glute bridge | 10 reps |
| Bird dog | 6/side |

~7 min total. Hits hip flexors, glutes, T-spine, ankles, hamstrings, posterior chain.

## Pre-Bed Mobility for Sleep Quality

Goal: parasympathetic activation, not joint-ROM gains. Low-intensity, breath-paced, dim light.

### 8–10 min pre-bed sequence

| Pose | Hold |
|---|---|
| Child's pose with deep breathing (4 in / 6 out) | 90 s |
| Supine figure-4 | 60 s/side |
| Supine spinal twist | 60 s/side |
| Happy baby | 60 s |
| Reclined butterfly | 60 s |
| Legs up the wall (box breathing 4-4-4-4) | 5 min |
| Savasana | 1–2 min |

Avoid: aggressive end-range holds, deep foam rolling, drills (all sympathetic).

## Mobility for Specific Tightness Patterns

### Anterior chain tightness

Pattern: tight hip flexors + quads + chest/anterior shoulder. Causes: running volume + sitting/cycling. Symptoms: anterior pelvic tilt, low-back tension, reduced hip extension.

| Intervention | Dose |
|---|---|
| Couch stretch | 60–90 s/side, daily |
| Half-kneeling hip flexor with overhead reach | 45 s/side |
| Foam roll quads | 60 s/side |
| Doorway pec stretch | 30 s/side |
| Glute activation circuit | Daily |
| Posterior tilt drills (cat-cow, dead bug) | 8 reps each |

### Posterior chain tightness

Pattern: tight hamstrings + calves + lumbar erectors. Symptoms: limited forward fold, posterior knee tension, calf cramps.

| Intervention | Dose |
|---|---|
| Standing forward fold | 60 s, daily |
| Single-leg hamstring stretch (strap) | 45 s/side |
| Wall calf stretch (both variants) | 30 s/side each |
| Eccentric heel drops | 3 × 15, 2–3×/week |
| Foam roll calves, hamstrings | 60 s each |
| Downward dog | 60 s, 2 sets |

### Hip rotator restriction

Pattern: tight piriformis, deep external rotators, glute med. Symptoms: limited internal rotation, lateral hip soreness, sciatic-pattern aching.

| Intervention | Dose |
|---|---|
| Pigeon pose | 60–90 s/side |
| 90/90 stretch with rotation | 45 s/side |
| Figure-4 stretch | 45 s/side |
| Lacrosse ball glute / piriformis | 60–90 s/side |
| Clamshell, side-lying abduction | 12/side, daily |
| Supine spinal twist | 60 s/side |

## Self-Massage and Lacrosse Ball Work

Use a lacrosse ball for focal trigger points where a foam roller is too broad. Avoid direct compression of bone, joint, or major nerve.

### Target areas and protocols

| Target | Position | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Plantar fascia | Standing/seated, ball under arch | 60–90 s/side |
| Calves (deeper than roller) | Floor, ball under calf, opposite leg stacked | 60 s/side |
| Peroneals | Side-lying, ball on lateral lower leg | 30–45 s/side |
| Glute med / piriformis | Supine, ball under cheek, knee crossed | 60–90 s/side |
| TFL | Side-lying, ball just below ASIS | 30–45 s/side |
| QL | Supine, ball above iliac crest, lateral to spine | 30 s/side |
| Pectoralis | Standing, ball between chest and wall | 30–45 s/side |
| Sub-occipitals | Supine, two balls in a sock at base of skull | 60–90 s |

### Rules

- No pressure on lumbar spine, kidneys, sciatic nerve, popliteal fossa, anterior throat, abdominal aorta.
- Stop if symptom worsens or radiates.
- Breathe through tender points; if breath holds, pressure is too high.
- Max 1–2 sessions/area/day during high-load weeks.

## Quick Reference: Session-by-Session Protocol

| Session type | Pre | Post |
|---|---|---|
| Recovery / easy < 60 min | Optional 2–3 min mobility; first mile slow = warmup | Walk 2 min; skip stretching |
| Easy 60–90 min | 5 min jog + light dynamic | Walk 3 min; brief stretch (calves, hips) |
| Steady / tempo | RAMP 12 min + 2 strides | Walk 5 min; static stretch 5–8 min; foam roll calves & quads |
| Threshold intervals | RAMP 12–15 min + 4 strides | Jog cool-down 5–10 min; full static set 10 min; foam roll |
| Track / VO2 | Full warmup 25 min + drills + 4–6 strides | Jog cool-down 10 min; full static + foam roll 15 min |
| Long run | 1–2 mi at slow pace as warmup | Walk 3–5 min; targeted stretch (hips, calves); legs-up-wall 5 min |
| Short race (5K–10K) | Race warmup 25–30 min | Walk 5 min; full static post-shower 10 min; foam roll evening |
| Half marathon | Race warmup 10–15 min | Walk 5–10 min; light static; defer foam roll to next day |
| Marathon | 5–10 min walk + brief mobility | Walk only; defer all rolling/stretching ≥ 24 h |
