# 21 — Common Form Errors and Corrections (Detailed)

Reference document for diagnosing specific running form errors and prescribing targeted corrective work. Each error is structured the same way: identification cues, root causes, corrective drills with prescription, strength work, mobility work, and a 4–8 week implementation protocol. Companion to Doc 16 (conceptual overview); this is the "user has problem X, do drills Y" drilldown.

## Definitions used throughout

- **Cadence (step rate, SPM)**: number of foot-strikes per minute, both feet combined.
- **Stride length**: distance covered per step.
- **Ground contact time (GCT)**: milliseconds the foot spends on the ground per step.
- **Vertical oscillation (VO)**: vertical displacement of the center of mass per stride, in cm.
- **Center of mass (COM)**: roughly the navel; the body's overall mass center.
- **Initial contact (IC)**: the moment the foot first touches the ground.
- **Stance phase**: the period from initial contact to toe-off.
- **Mid-stance**: the moment of maximum knee flexion under load, when the COM is directly over the supporting foot.
- **Toe-off**: the moment the foot leaves the ground.
- **Swing phase**: the period when the foot is off the ground.
- **Hip extension**: the trail leg moving behind the body at toe-off; reference value at jogging pace ≈10°, at faster paces ≈20°.
- **Dorsiflexion (DF)**: ankle bending so toes move toward shin.
- **Plantarflexion**: ankle pointing away (toes down).
- **Pronation**: inward roll of the foot; supination/inversion is the outward roll.
- **Knee-to-wall (KTW) test**: weight-bearing lunge test for ankle DF. Big toe to wall, push knee forward without lifting heel; measure foot-to-wall distance. Normal ≥10 cm (≈36°).
- **Trendelenburg sign**: contralateral pelvic drop in single-leg stance, indicating hip abductor (gluteus medius) insufficiency.
- **Reactive Strength Index (RSI)**: jump height ÷ ground contact time on a depth jump; proxy for stretch-shortening cycle quality.

## How to use this document

1. Identify the error from video, wearable data, or felt cues.
2. Confirm root cause from the listed candidates (mobility vs. strength vs. motor control).
3. Run the implementation protocol: most issues need 4–8 weeks of targeted work.
4. Drills go in warm-up (fresh nervous system). Strength goes after running or on alternate days. Mobility daily, plus before/after runs as listed.

Form changes require both the capacity (strength/mobility) and the motor pattern (drill repetition with cueing). One without the other regresses within weeks.

## 1. Overstriding

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal)**: foot lands well in front of the knee at IC; tibia angled forward; knee extended.
- **Felt**: heavy "thud" landing; sense of braking; sound of slap.
- **Wearables**: cadence <165 SPM at easy pace; high vertical loading rate; long GCT (>270 ms at easy pace).
- **Numeric**: foot-to-COM distance at IC >25 cm (versus ≤10 cm in efficient runners).

### Root causes
- **Motor control**: low cadence habit; "reach for the ground" pattern.
- **Strength**: weak hip flexors/glutes (cannot quickly recover the swing leg).
- **Mobility**: limited hip extension forces a forward reach to compensate.
- **Cueing**: belief that long stride = fast.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metronome stride at +5–10% cadence | 3 × 2 min, then 4 × 3 min | 2–3×/week | "Quick light feet" |
| A-skip | 3 × 20 m | 2×/week, pre-run | Foot lands under hip with paw-back |
| Wall-supported high-knee march | 3 × 30 s | 2×/week | Down-stroke, not knee lift |
| Ankling (foot tap) | 2 × 20 m | 2×/week | Quick taps under body |
| Strides at +10% cadence | 6 × 20 s @ 5K effort | 1–2×/week | Tall, quick, relaxed |

### Strength work
- Single-leg deadlift: 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.
- Hip flexor band march (resisted): 3 × 30 s/side, 2×/week.
- Step-ups (knee drive emphasis): 3 × 10/side, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Couch stretch: 2 × 60 s/side, daily.
- Hip flexor mobilization (half-kneeling rock-back): 2 × 10/side, daily.

### Implementation protocol (6 weeks)
- **Week 1–2**: Metronome at +5% baseline cadence, 2 × 5 min in middle of easy runs. Drills 2×/week.
- **Week 3–4**: Metronome at +7–8%, 3 × 8 min. Add strides.
- **Week 5–6**: Target +10% cadence sustained on all easy runs. Heiderscheit (2011) showed this reduces knee energy absorption ~20%.

## 2. Heel striking with braking

### Identification cues
- **Video**: heel contact with a distinct vertical slap; tibia leans forward of vertical at IC; knee fully extended.
- **Felt**: jarring at heel; brake sensation each step.
- **Wearables**: high vertical instantaneous loading rate (VILR >80 BW/s); sharp impact peak in tibial accelerometer.

### Root causes
- Overstriding (see #1). Heel strike *under* the COM with bent knee is biomechanically benign; the issue is heel strike *ahead* of the COM with a stiff leg.
- Stiff knee at IC (insufficient knee flexion at landing).
- Weak posterior chain (cannot pull leg backward fast enough).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Soft landing drill (treadmill, audio feedback) | 5 × 3 min | 2×/week | "Land quietly" |
| Forefoot-strike strides (short, optional) | 4 × 15 s | 1×/week | Feel forefoot first, then heel kisses |
| Paw-back drill | 3 × 20 m | 2×/week | Pull foot down and back before contact |
| Slight forward lean from ankles | 3 × 30 s during easy run | Daily | "Fall forward, catch with quick feet" |

### Strength work
- Eccentric Nordic hamstring curl: 3 × 5, 2×/week.
- Single-leg Romanian deadlift: 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.
- Hamstring bridge with foot on bench: 3 × 12, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Ankle DF drills (see #11) if KTW <10 cm.
- Quad/hip flexor stretching to enable knee flexion at IC.

### Implementation protocol (8 weeks, gait-retraining approach per Davis)
- **Week 1–2**: Treadmill with audio cue ("land quietly") 8 sessions. Davis showed this reduces tibial acceleration ~50%, vertical loading rate ~30%.
- **Week 3–4**: Faded feedback (every other run).
- **Week 5–8**: Cue is internalized. Effects persist 1+ year per follow-up data.
- **Note**: do not chase forefoot strike for its own sake. The goal is foot-under-COM with a compliant knee, regardless of which part of the foot kisses first.

## 3. Hip drop / Trendelenburg

### Identification cues
- **Video (frontal/rear)**: contralateral pelvis drops >5° at mid-stance on the affected side.
- **Felt**: "wobbling" hip; outer-knee or IT band irritation.
- **Wearables**: pelvic rotation/drop metric on premium watches; left/right GCT imbalance.
- **Clinical test**: Trendelenburg test — single-leg stance for 30 s, contralateral hip drops or shoulder leans toward stance leg.

### Root causes
- **Strength**: gluteus medius weakness (primary). Side-lying hip abduction shows highest EMG of glute med activation.
- **Motor control**: gluteus medius slow to fire even when strong enough.
- **Stability**: weak contralateral obliques/QL.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Side plank with top-leg abduction | 3 × 10/side | 3×/week | Hip stacked, no sag |
| Hip airplane | 2 × 8/side | 2–3×/week | Pelvis level, rotate around stance hip |
| Single-leg balance, eyes closed | 3 × 30 s/side | Daily | "Pelvis level" |
| Banded lateral walks | 3 × 15 steps/direction | 3×/week | Knees out, hips still |
| Pistol squat to box (assisted) | 3 × 5/side | 2×/week | No hip drop in descent |

### Strength work
- Side-lying hip abduction with band: 3 × 15/side, 3×/week. Highest glute med EMG in research.
- Copenhagen plank: 3 × 20 s/side, 2×/week (also targets adductors).
- Single-leg squat: 3 × 8/side, 2×/week (52–82% MVIC of glute med).

### Mobility work
- Adductor stretching (frog, side-split rocking): 2 × 60 s, daily. Tight adductors antagonize abductors.

### Implementation protocol (8 weeks)
- **Phase 1 (week 1–2): Activation**. Side-lying clamshell, hip abduction, glute bridge with band — 3×/week. Drill teaches *firing*, not load.
- **Phase 2 (week 3–5): Strength**. Side plank with abduction, single-leg squats. Add load (band, dumbbell) when form holds.
- **Phase 3 (week 6–8): Functional integration**. Hip airplane, single-leg deadlift, then add running drills with mirror or video feedback. Strength must transfer to gait — without timing/control work, a "strong" glute med can still fail to fire under running load.

## 4. Cross-over gait

### Identification cues
- **Video (rear, treadmill)**: feet land on or across the body's midline; a single-line footprint pattern.
- **Felt**: knees clip each other; medial shin/calf contact during swing; IT band tightness.
- **Wearables**: narrow step width on premium gait analysis.
- **Numeric**: step width <5 cm or negative (foot crosses midline).

### Root causes
- Gluteus medius weakness (closely related to #3, often co-occurring).
- Weak hip external rotators (deep six).
- Trunk lateral lean/instability.
- Habitual narrow-line tracking (e.g., always running on a painted line).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wide-track running (run with feet on either side of a line) | 4 × 2 min | 2×/week | Step "outside the rails" |
| Lateral bound | 3 × 6/side | 2×/week | Stick the landing, no knee dive in |
| Banded knee-out march in place | 3 × 30 s | 2×/week | "Shine knees outward" |
| Monster walks (heavy band above knees) | 3 × 12 steps each direction | 3×/week | Toes forward, hips back |
| Single-leg running stance hold | 3 × 20 s/side | 2×/week | Trail knee at hip height, pelvis level |

### Strength work
- Same as Hip Drop (#3) — gluteus medius is the shared driver.
- Add deep external rotator: clamshell with banded hip abduction, 3 × 12/side, 2–3×/week.
- Side-lying leg raise with toe-pointing-down (favors glute med over TFL): 3 × 12/side, 3×/week.

### Mobility work
- Adductor and TFL release (foam roll lateral thigh): 2 min/side daily.
- Hip internal rotation mobility: figure-4 stretch and 90/90 rocking, 2 × 60 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (6–8 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Establish glute med activation (Phase 1 of #3 protocol).
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add wide-track running 2× per week, 4 min total. Cue: "land on either side of an invisible rail."
- **Weeks 5–6**: Plyometric lateral work (lateral bounds, side-shuffle).
- **Weeks 7–8**: Step-width carries over to all easy runs. Re-film at week 8.

## 5. Excessive vertical oscillation

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal)**: head bobs >10 cm per stride.
- **Felt**: bouncy stride; "running uphill" sense on flat ground.
- **Wearables**: VO >10 cm at easy pace; ratio (VO ÷ stride length) >7%.

### Reference values (typical)
| Pace | Acceptable VO | Concerning VO |
|---|---|---|
| Easy run | 6–9 cm | >10 cm |
| Tempo | 7–10 cm | >11 cm |
| 5K | 8–11 cm | >12 cm |

### Root causes
- Low cadence (each step has more time aloft).
- Vertical push at toe-off rather than horizontal.
- Restricted hip extension forces vertical compensation.
- Calf tightness limiting smooth knee/ankle excursion.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cadence increase (+5–10%) | See #1 | 2–3×/week | "Quick light steps" |
| Forward-lean fall start | 3 × 6 falls | 2×/week | Lean from ankle, catch into run |
| Fast feet (low-amplitude) | 3 × 10 s | 2×/week | "Hot coals" |
| Wicket / hurdle runs (low hurdles) | 4 × 10 hurdles | 1×/week | Skim the top, don't pop over |
| Strides on flat ground with focus on horizontal projection | 6 × 20 s | 1×/week | "Forward, not up" |

### Strength work
- Posterior-chain push-off: hip thrust 3 × 10, 2×/week.
- Calf raises (straight + bent knee): 3 × 15 each, 2–3×/week.
- Plyometric drops to broad jump: 3 × 5, 1×/week.

### Mobility work
- Hip flexor / couch stretch (see #10): 2 × 60 s/side daily.
- Calf stretching (gastroc + soleus): 2 × 45 s each, daily.

### Implementation protocol (6 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Cadence retraining (Heiderscheit protocol — +5% then +10%).
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add forward-lean and horizontal-projection cues.
- **Weeks 5–6**: Plyometric work building horizontal force; re-test VO. Expect 1–3 cm reduction.

## 6. Long ground contact time

### Identification cues
- **Wearables**: GCT >270 ms easy, >250 ms tempo, >220 ms 5K. Elite ranges: 180–230 ms.
- **Video**: foot visibly "dwells" on ground; stance leg collapses excessively at mid-stance.
- **Felt**: heavy, plodding stride; lack of pop.
- **Symptom**: GCT rises >10% across a long run as fatigue accumulates.

### Root causes
- Low leg-spring stiffness (poor stretch-shortening cycle).
- Weak posterior chain / push-off.
- Overstriding (foot has to wait for body to arrive).
- Low calf/Achilles stiffness.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pogo hops (in place) | 3 × 20 | 2×/week | Stiff ankles, ground is hot |
| Single-leg pogo | 3 × 10/side | 2×/week | Bounce, don't squat |
| Ankling | 3 × 20 m | 2×/week | Quick toe-off |
| Bounding (long, powerful) | 3 × 6 bounds | 1×/week | Cover ground, low contact |
| Hill strides (4–6% grade) | 6 × 15 s | 1×/week | Quick turnover up the hill |

### Strength work
- Heavy calf raises (eccentric emphasis): 4 × 6, 2×/week.
- Trap-bar deadlift or split squat heavy: 4 × 5, 2×/week.
- Reactive Strength Index work: depth drops to broad jump, 3 × 5, 1×/week.

### Mobility work
- Ankle DF (see #11) — restricted DF prolongs contact.
- Big-toe extension mobility: 2 × 30 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (8 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Calf strength base. Pogo hops daily 1 × 30. Build connective-tissue tolerance.
- **Weeks 3–5**: Add bounding and single-leg pogo. Heavy calf 2×/week.
- **Weeks 6–8**: True plyometrics (depth drops). Re-test GCT and RSI.

## 7. Forward head posture

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal)**: ear is anterior to shoulder by >2 cm; chin juts forward.
- **Felt**: neck tightness mid-run; tension in upper traps; headache post-run.

### Root causes
- Underactive deep neck flexors, mid-back (lower trap, rhomboid).
- Overactive upper trap, levator scapulae, SCM, scalenes.
- Daily desk/phone posture carrying into running.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chin tucks (supine) | 2 × 10 holds × 5 s | Daily | Make a double chin |
| Wall-supported chin tuck with arm raise | 2 × 10 | Daily | Head on wall, ribs down |
| Running cue: "crown of head string" | Continuous on easy runs | Every run | Tall, not strained |
| Scapular squeeze (Y-T-W on bench) | 2 × 8 each letter | 2×/week | Squeeze shoulder blades back/down |

### Strength work
- Prone Y-T-W with light dumbbells (2–4 lb): 2 × 8 each, 2×/week.
- Face pull with band: 3 × 12, 2×/week.
- Deep neck flexor endurance: chin-tuck head-lift 30 s holds.

### Mobility work
- Pec stretch in doorway: 2 × 45 s/side daily.
- Thoracic extension over foam roller: 10 segments daily.
- SCM/scalene self-release: 30 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (4–6 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Mobility + chin tucks daily. Set ergonomic posture habits at desk/phone.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add scapular strengthening and Y-T-W work.
- **Weeks 5–6**: Internalize running cue ("tall crown") on every run.

## 8. Anterior pelvic tilt

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal, standing or running)**: pronounced lumbar lordosis; ASIS visibly lower than PSIS; "duck-butt" appearance.
- **Felt**: low-back tightness during/after running; hip flexor tightness.
- **Clinical test**: Thomas test — supine, hold one knee to chest. Positive if contralateral thigh rises off the table (hip flexor tightness) or knee extends (rectus femoris tightness).
- **Numeric**: ASIS-to-PSIS angle >15°.

### Root causes
- Tight hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris) — primary.
- Weak glutes and abdominals.
- Tight thoracolumbar erectors.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Posterior-tilt activation (supine, knees bent) | 3 × 10 × 5 s | Daily | Press low back into floor |
| Dead bug (with breath) | 3 × 8/side | 3×/week | Ribs down, low back flat |
| Hip-flexor stretch + glute squeeze (couch stretch) | 2 × 60 s/side | Daily | Squeeze glute to deepen stretch |
| Bird dog | 3 × 8/side | 3×/week | No pelvis tilt during arm/leg lift |

### Strength work
- Glute bridge: 3 × 12, 2–3×/week. Squeeze hard at top.
- Hip thrust with bar: 3 × 8, 2×/week. Range stops at neutral hip — don't hyperextend.
- Reverse crunch: 3 × 10, 2×/week.
- Plank (RKC-style, posterior tilt): 3 × 30 s, daily.

### Mobility work
- Couch stretch: 2 × 60 s/side daily.
- Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch with glute squeeze: 2 × 45 s/side daily.
- Quad stretch (rectus femoris bias): 2 × 45 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (8–12 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–4**: Daily hip flexor mobility + posterior tilt activation. Postural habit work (don't sit collapsed). 8–12 weeks is realistic for visible change.
- **Weeks 5–8**: Add loaded glute work (hip thrust, bridge with band). Anti-extension core (dead bug, plank with posterior tilt).
- **Weeks 9–12**: Integrate cue into running ("tail tuck slightly," "ribs over hips").

## 9. Posterior pelvic tilt

### Identification cues
- **Video**: flat lumbar spine ("flat back"); tucked tailbone; running posture looks slumped.
- **Felt**: lower-back tightness from compression; reduced hip extension.
- **Visual sign**: when squatting, "butt wink" — pelvis tucks under at depth.

### Root causes
- Tight hamstrings pulling pelvis under.
- Tight glutes (chronic clench).
- Weak hip flexors and lumbar extensors.
- Habitual slumped sitting.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anterior tilt activation (supine, knees bent) | 3 × 10 × 5 s | Daily | Arch low back gently |
| Cobra / sphinx pose | 2 × 60 s | Daily | Lengthen front body |
| Standing hip-flexor march (high knee, neutral pelvis) | 3 × 10/side | 2×/week | Knee up, no rounding |
| Bird dog with full hip extension | 3 × 8/side | 3×/week | Reach long, don't tuck |

### Strength work
- Hip flexor strengthening (psoas march, banded knee raise): 3 × 12/side, 2×/week.
- Lumbar extensor: prone Superman holds, 3 × 20 s, 2×/week.
- Hamstring stretching paired with quad strengthening (split squat): 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Hamstring stretching (PNF-style): 3 × 30 s/side daily.
- Glute (figure-4, pigeon) release: 2 × 60 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (6–8 weeks)
- Note: avoid forcing into anterior tilt — full extension under load can stress discs.
- **Weeks 1–3**: Mobility (hamstrings, glutes) + gentle anterior tilt activations.
- **Weeks 4–6**: Add hip flexor strengthening. Cue: "tall, ribs forward of hips slightly."
- **Weeks 7–8**: Integrate into running. Especially helpful on uphills and at faster paces.

## 10. Limited hip extension

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal, running, mid-pace)**: at toe-off, trail femur is ≤10° behind vertical (should be 15–25°).
- **Felt**: short stride at faster paces; reliance on knee drive instead of hip drive; chronic hip flexor tightness.
- **Clinical test**: modified Thomas test — measured hip extension <0° (i.e., thigh above horizontal at rest).

### Root causes
- Tight hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris, TFL).
- Anterior pelvic tilt (#8) — often comorbid.
- Glute amnesia / underactivation.
- Sitting >6 hours/day chronically.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| B-skip | 3 × 20 m | 2×/week | Reach forward, paw down/back |
| Walking lunges with reach-up | 3 × 10/side | 2×/week | Long stride, glute squeeze on stand-up |
| Hip flexor stretch with active glute | 2 × 60 s/side | Daily | Squeeze glute to drive stretch |
| Banded hip extension (cable kick-back) | 3 × 12/side | 2–3×/week | Knee straight at top |
| Reverse lunge to high-knee | 3 × 8/side | 2×/week | Drive trail leg back behind body |

### Strength work
- Glute bridge / hip thrust: 3 × 10, 2×/week.
- Romanian deadlift (single-leg): 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.
- Reverse plank: 3 × 30 s, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Couch stretch: 2 × 60 s/side daily. Pair with glute bridge to use reciprocal inhibition.
- Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch (hands overhead, side bend away): 2 × 45 s/side daily.
- 90/90 hip rotations: 2 × 8/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (8 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Daily couch stretch + half-kneeling hip flexor stretches. Bridges 3×/week.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add B-skip drills, walking lunges. Paired stretch + activation (couch stretch immediately into glute bridge).
- **Weeks 5–6**: Add loaded RDL, banded hip extension.
- **Weeks 7–8**: Integrate to running. Re-film hip extension angle.

## 11. Limited ankle dorsiflexion

### Identification cues
- **Clinical**: knee-to-wall test <10 cm (≈36°). Asymmetry >1.5 cm side-to-side.
- **Video**: heel comes off ground early in stance; mid-stance compensation (foot collapse, knee valgus).
- **Felt**: calf tightness; Achilles soreness; difficulty squatting flat-footed.
- **Wearables**: shorter stride on the restricted side.

### Root causes
- Soft tissue: tight gastrocnemius and/or soleus, posterior capsule of ankle.
- Joint: anterior talocrural impingement; restricted talar posterior glide.
- Old ankle sprain residual stiffness.
- Habitual elevated-heel footwear.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knee-to-wall mobilization | 3 × 10/side | Daily | Knee directly over toes, heel down |
| Banded talar posterior glide + lunge | 3 × 10/side | Daily | Band pulls front of ankle backward |
| Weighted DF stretch (toes elevated, lean forward) | 2 × 45 s/side | Daily | Feel calf, not just ankle |
| Ankle ABCs and pumps | 1 × 26 letters | Daily | Slow, full ROM |

### Strength work
- Tibialis anterior raises (standing, toes pulled up): 3 × 15, 2×/week.
- Calf raises through full ROM (heel below step): 3 × 12, 2–3×/week.
- Soleus raise (seated calf raise): 3 × 15, 2×/week — bent knee biases soleus.

### Mobility work
- Gastrocnemius stretch (knee straight): 3 × 30 s/side daily.
- Soleus stretch (knee bent): 3 × 30 s/side daily.
- Plantar fascia and calf release with ball/roller: 2 min/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (4–8 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Daily KTW mobilization + banded glide. Re-test KTW weekly.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add loaded DF (weighted stretch, deep lunges).
- **Weeks 5–8**: Carry over to running with cadence emphasis (high cadence reduces DF demand and lets the recovering joint ease in). Goal: KTW ≥10 cm bilateral, asymmetry <1 cm.

## 12. Foot collapse (excessive pronation under load)

### Identification cues
- **Video (rear)**: heel rolls inward sharply at IC; arch flattens visibly; medial knee dives in.
- **Wear pattern**: medial heel and forefoot wear on shoes.
- **Felt**: arch fatigue; medial shin / posterior tib pain.
- **Clinical test**: navicular drop >10 mm.

### Root causes
- Weak intrinsic foot muscles and tibialis posterior.
- Weak hip external rotators / glute med (allow knee to dive in).
- Genuine structural pes planus.
- Shoe choice (overly cushioned/soft midsole).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Short-foot exercise (arch dome) | 3 × 10 × 5 s holds | Daily | Pull ball of foot toward heel, no toe curl |
| Single-leg balance with arch active | 3 × 30 s/side | Daily | "Tripod foot": big toe, pinkie, heel pressed |
| Towel scrunches | 2 × 30 s/side | Daily | Toes grip, then release |
| Toe yoga (big toe up, others down — and reverse) | 2 × 10/side | Daily | Isolate toes |
| Single-leg calf raise with controlled pronation/supination | 3 × 10/side | 2×/week | Drive through big toe at top |

### Strength work
- Tibialis posterior: heel raise with ball squeezed between heels: 3 × 15, 2×/week.
- Hip external rotator: clamshell, 3 × 15/side, 3×/week.
- Single-leg deadlift: 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Plantar fascia release with ball: 2 min/foot daily.
- Big toe extension stretch: 2 × 30 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (9 weeks per RCT)
- **Weeks 1–3**: Foot intrinsic activation (short foot, toe yoga, towel scrunches) daily. RCT showed FPI improves ~1.66 points in 9 weeks with intrinsic + extrinsic + core program.
- **Weeks 4–6**: Add hip ER and glute med strength. Single-leg balance progressions.
- **Weeks 7–9**: Functional integration — single-leg loaded work, jumping landings on tripod foot. Re-evaluate gait video.
- **Footwear**: stability shoes can be a band-aid; rebuild capacity rather than substitute for it.

## 13. Supinated foot strike

### Identification cues
- **Video (rear)**: foot lands on lateral edge; minimal inward roll through stance.
- **Wear pattern**: lateral heel and lateral forefoot wear.
- **Felt**: lateral ankle/knee pain; sense of "rolling outward."
- **Population**: high-arched runners, often with rigid forefoot.

### Root causes
- Genuine high arch (cavus foot) — structural.
- Tight peroneals, lateral ankle stiffness.
- Weak intrinsics and inability to load medial column.
- Old lateral ankle sprain residuals.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Loaded big-toe press (banded) | 3 × 12/side | Daily | Drive big toe down hard |
| Single-leg balance on uneven surface | 3 × 30 s/side | Daily | Tripod foot |
| Lateral lunge with controlled descent | 3 × 8/side | 2×/week | Knee tracks toes; load medial foot |
| Inversion/eversion isometrics with band | 2 × 10/direction | Daily | Slow, controlled |

### Strength work
- Peroneal strength (eversion against band): 3 × 15/side, 3×/week.
- Calf raises emphasizing big-toe drive at top: 3 × 12, 2×/week.
- Glute med (hip airplane, side plank abduction): see #3.

### Mobility work
- Lateral ankle and peroneal release: 2 min/side daily.
- Big-toe mobilization (extension and flexion): 2 × 30 s/side daily.
- Plantar fascia release: 2 min/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (6–8 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Daily peroneal and intrinsic mobility. Single-leg balance.
- **Weeks 3–5**: Strengthen peroneals + tibialis posterior + intrinsics. Train medial column loading.
- **Weeks 6–8**: Functional carries (lateral lunges, plyometric landings on tripod foot). Cushioned neutral shoe is appropriate; structural cavus does not "correct" but symptomatic load distribution does improve.

## 14. Arms crossing midline

### Identification cues
- **Video (front)**: hands cross the body's midline at the front of swing; shoulders rotate excessively.
- **Felt**: neck/upper-back tension; sense of fighting balance.
- **Coupled with**: cross-over gait (#4) — arms often follow legs.

### Root causes
- Tight pectorals, lats, anterior deltoid.
- Weak rhomboid, mid/lower trap.
- Compensation for hip rotation instability (arms cross to balance pelvis rotation).
- Habitual posture (shoulders rolled forward).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seated arm swing | 2 × 30 s | Daily | Front-to-back, no rotation |
| Standing arm swing with cadence | 3 × 30 s @ 90 cycles/min | 2×/week | Hands skim hips, not midline |
| Running cue: "hand-to-pocket" | Continuous on easy runs | Every run | Hands track straight forward, not across |
| Wall slide (Y-T-A) | 2 × 8 | 3×/week | Arms stay on wall |

### Strength work
- Mid/lower trap: prone Y-T-W, 2 × 8 each, 2×/week.
- Rhomboid: face pull with band, 3 × 12, 2×/week.
- Anti-rotation core: Pallof press, 3 × 10/side, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Pec stretch in doorway: 2 × 45 s/side daily.
- Lat stretch (kneeling overhead reach): 2 × 30 s/side daily.
- Thoracic rotation (open book): 2 × 8/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (4–6 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Pec/lat mobility + scapular strengthening daily. Seated arm swing.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Add anti-rotation core. Running cue ("hand-to-pocket").
- **Weeks 5–6**: Re-film. Often improves dramatically once underlying cross-over gait (#4) is resolved.

## 15. Shoulder elevation / tension

### Identification cues
- **Video**: shoulders ride high near ears, especially after the first mile or under fatigue.
- **Felt**: upper trap tightness mid-run; clenched fists; jaw tension.
- **Coupled with**: forward head posture (#7).

### Root causes
- Habitual stress-pattern breathing (shallow, upper-chest).
- Underactive lower trap and serratus anterior.
- Tight upper trap, levator scapulae.
- Low arousal-management awareness (gripping).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diaphragmatic breathing | 2 × 2 min | Daily, pre-run | Belly out on inhale, soft shoulders |
| Shoulder rolls (active down/back) | 2 × 10 | Pre-run | Slide shoulder blades into back pockets |
| Running tension scan (every 5 min) | Ongoing | Every run | "Soft shoulders, soft hands, soft jaw" |
| Loose-fist arm swing | 3 × 30 s | Daily | Imagine holding a potato chip uncrushed |

### Strength work
- Lower trap activation: prone Y, 2 × 10 hold 3 s, 2×/week.
- Serratus anterior: scapular push-up, 3 × 8, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Upper trap and levator stretch: 2 × 30 s/side daily.
- Pec stretch: 2 × 45 s/side daily.
- Cervical rotations and side-bends: 1 × 8/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (4 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Establish breathing pattern off the run. Pre-run mobility ritual.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Tension scan during runs (cue at every km marker). Often resolves in 4 weeks because it is largely arousal/awareness, not capacity.

## 16. Lower back arching (excess lumbar extension during run)

### Identification cues
- **Video (sagittal, mid-run)**: lumbar lordosis deepens visibly; ribs flare; pelvis tips into anterior tilt under fatigue.
- **Felt**: low-back fatigue late in long runs; "tight" feel in QL.
- **Often pairs with**: limited hip extension (#10) — back compensates by extending where hip cannot.

### Root causes
- Limited hip extension forces lumbar substitution.
- Weak deep core (transverse abdominis, internal obliques).
- Anterior pelvic tilt baseline (#8).
- Tight thoracic spine (lumbar over-extends to make up for thoracic stiffness).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dead bug | 3 × 8/side | 3×/week | Ribs to floor entire rep |
| Plank with posterior tilt | 3 × 30 s | Daily | Tail under, ribs down |
| Bird dog | 3 × 8/side | 3×/week | Long line head-to-foot, no rotation |
| Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch with overhead reach | 2 × 45 s/side | Daily | Side-bend away to lengthen QL |

### Strength work
- Anti-extension core progression: dead bug → hollow hold → ab-wheel rollout, 3×/week.
- Glute strength to take over hip extension load: glute bridge, hip thrust (see #10).
- Thoracic mobility to reduce lumbar compensation: foam roll t-spine extensions, daily.

### Mobility work
- Couch stretch (#10): 2 × 60 s/side daily.
- Cat-cow: 2 × 8 daily.
- QL stretch (side reach): 2 × 30 s/side daily.

### Implementation protocol (6 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Resolve underlying hip extension restriction (#10). Dead bug + plank with tilt daily.
- **Weeks 3–4**: Build core anti-extension capacity. Add bird dog, hollow hold.
- **Weeks 5–6**: Long-run cue — "ribs over hips, don't lead with the back" — especially in the last third when fatigue tempts compensation.

## 17. Asymmetric gait (left vs. right)

### Identification cues
- **Wearables**: GCT balance >52/48 split (or >5% asymmetry).
- **Video**: visible difference in stride length, knee drive, or hip drop side-to-side.
- **Felt**: chronic one-sided soreness or injury history; "lopsided" feel.
- **Wear**: shoe wear differs noticeably between left and right shoes.

### Root causes (rule out highest-impact first)
- True leg-length discrepancy (LLD): >5–10 mm structural; effect amplified ~3× in running vs. walking.
- Old injury residual (post-ankle sprain, post-stress fracture stiffness).
- Strength asymmetry (one glute med weaker; one hamstring tight).
- Habitual side-dominance (camber bias on roads, always-same-direction track running).

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-leg work, weak side first | All single-leg lifts | 2–3×/week | Match weak side, then mirror |
| Carioca / lateral shuffles both directions | 3 × 30 s each direction | 2×/week | Don't favor dominant side |
| Reverse-direction track sessions | Alternate weekly | Ongoing | Counter the always-counterclockwise bias |
| Mirror running (treadmill with mirror) | 5 min in middle of run | 1×/week | Self-monitor side-to-side |

### Strength work
- Single-leg progressions, weak side dictates volume — match strong side to weak side capacity, not the reverse.
- Single-leg deadlift, single-leg squat, single-leg calf raise, all 3 × 8/side, 2×/week.
- Address specific findings (e.g., weaker glute med — use #3 protocol on that side at higher volume).

### Mobility work
- Asymmetric mobility findings (e.g., one tighter hip flexor, one stiffer ankle) get 2× the mobility dose on the restricted side until matched.

### Implementation protocol (8–12 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–2**: Comprehensive screen — KTW each side, Thomas test each side, single-leg squat each side, hop test for distance and quality each side.
- **Weeks 3–6**: Address specific identified asymmetries (mobility for restriction, strength for weakness). Reassess every 2 weeks.
- **Weeks 7–12**: Functional re-integration. Re-test wearable GCT balance. If LLD is structural and >10 mm, refer for orthotic heel lift evaluation; correct ~50% initially, add 25% after 4–6 week tolerance window.

## 18. Cadence too low at all paces

### Identification cues
- **Wearables**: cadence consistently <165 SPM at easy pace; <170 SPM at tempo; <175 SPM at 5K.
- **Video**: long stride reach; long aerial phase.
- **Felt**: heavy landings; "I run in slow motion."

### Reference values
| Pace | Recreational typical | Concerning | Elite |
|---|---|---|---|
| Easy | 165–175 SPM | <160 | 170–185 |
| Tempo | 170–180 | <165 | 180–190 |
| 5K | 175–185 | <170 | 185–200+ |

Note: 180 is not a magic number — it came from observation of elite athletes at Olympic-marathon pace. Recreational targets are 5–10% above the runner's current baseline, not 180 universally.

### Root causes
- Habituation to long stride.
- Weak hip flexors / poor swing leg recovery.
- Shoes too cushioned/soft (encourage longer aerial phase).
- Belief that high cadence = inefficient.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metronome run (Heiderscheit protocol) | +5% baseline × 5 min, build to +10% × 30 min | 2–3×/week × 2 weeks | Match feet to beep |
| Music at target BPM | Continuous | Easy runs | Strong downbeat = footstrike |
| Fast-feet drill | 3 × 10 s | 2×/week | "Hot coals" |
| Hill strides (forces high cadence) | 6 × 15 s up 5–8% grade | 1×/week | Don't reach |

### Strength work
- Hip flexor strengthening (banded knee raise, psoas march): 3 × 12/side, 2×/week.
- Plyometric pogo hops: 3 × 20, 2×/week.

### Mobility work
- Hip flexor mobility (couch stretch): 2 × 60 s/side daily — paradoxically, tight hip flexors can slow swing recovery.

### Implementation protocol (Heiderscheit / IJSPT 2-week metronome protocol, then 4-week consolidation)
- **Day 1 (instructional)**: measure baseline. Set metronome at +7.5% (midpoint of 5–10%).
- **Days 2–14**: 4–6 sessions, 30 min at metronome cadence at easy effort.
- **Weeks 3–6**: drop the metronome but use rhythmic music. Cadence carries over and is sustained at 1-month follow-up in research.
- **62% reduction in injury risk** in novice runners with 12-month cadence-modification programs.

## 19. Stride pattern doesn't change with pace

### Identification cues
- **Wearables**: cadence is identical (within 2 SPM) at easy, tempo, and 5K paces; only stride length scales — or vice versa.
- **Felt**: at faster paces, runner reaches farther rather than turning over faster.
- **Symptom**: hits a "speed wall" because they cannot scale stride length further without overstriding.

### Root causes
- Locked-in motor pattern (always same cadence).
- Weak posterior chain (cannot increase push-off force, so stride length plateaus).
- Limited hip extension (#10) caps stride at the back.
- Lack of speed work — never trained the high-cadence pattern.

### Reference: how pace should scale
- Healthy runners scale BOTH cadence and stride length with pace.
- Cadence rises ~5–10% from easy to 5K pace; remainder is stride length.
- Elite runners (e.g., Bekele) sometimes scale almost entirely via cadence (190 → 216 SPM) at constant stride length when stride length is already at structural maximum.

### Corrective drills

| Drill | Sets × Reps | Frequency | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cadence ladder (3 paces, 3 cadences) | 3 × 1 min @ easy/tempo/5K-feel | 1×/week | Feel the cadence shift up with pace |
| Strides (full speed scaling) | 6 × 20 s @ near-mile pace | 1–2×/week | High cadence + powerful push |
| Hill sprints (10–15 s) | 6–8 × 10 s | 1×/week | Trains stride length under high force |
| Flying 30s | 4 × 30 m at sub-mile pace | 1×/week (advanced) | Top-end mechanics |

### Strength work
- Heavy single-leg work (split squat, RDL): 3 × 5, 2×/week — builds force capacity for stride length.
- Plyometrics (bounds, depth jumps): 3 × 5, 1×/week — trains rapid force at high cadence.
- Hip thrust: 3 × 8, 2×/week — push-off power.

### Mobility work
- Hip extension (couch stretch, see #10): 2 × 60 s/side daily.
- Thoracic rotation: 2 × 8/side daily — counter-rotation enables longer stride.

### Implementation protocol (8–12 weeks)
- **Weeks 1–4**: Strength foundation (heavy single-leg, hip thrust). Cadence work at top-end (strides, hill sprints).
- **Weeks 5–8**: Cadence ladder once weekly. Build a deliberate "shift gears" feel — at faster paces, cadence rises first, then stride length.
- **Weeks 9–12**: Add plyometric and flying-30s work. Re-test cadence at three paces; should now show a clear gradient (e.g., 168 / 174 / 180).

## Cross-cutting principles

1. **Capacity before pattern**. If the runner lacks ankle DF or hip extension, no amount of running drill rewires the gait — the body just compensates. Mobility/strength precedes motor-control work.
2. **Drill before run, not after**. Drills go in the warm-up so the nervous system is fresh. Drilling fatigued reinforces tired patterns.
3. **Cue in the run, not just in the gym**. A drill that never crosses over to actual running was wasted. Carry one cue per run.
4. **One change at a time**. Two simultaneous changes confuse the nervous system. Resolve one error, then move to the next. 4–8 weeks per error is realistic.
5. **Re-test, don't guess**. KTW, Thomas, single-leg squat, gait video — measure at the start, at week 4, at week 8.
6. **Cadence is the highest-leverage single intervention**. Heiderscheit's 5–10% bump alone improves overstriding, vertical oscillation, GCT, knee/hip energy absorption, and braking force. If only one thing can change, change cadence.
7. **Beware the band-aid**. Stability shoes, orthotics, and tape can mask symptoms but don't build capacity. Use them to keep training while rebuilding, not as the endpoint.
