# 14 — Age-Group Considerations (Youth, Masters, Senior)

Reference doc on training adaptations across the lifespan. Definitions, lookup tables, decision rules. Companion to Task 13 (sex-specific physiology, including menopause).

## Definitions

- **Youth**: pre-pubertal and early adolescent, generally under 14.
- **Adolescent**: pubertal, roughly 13–18, encompassing the high-school years.
- **Collegiate**: 18–22, post-pubertal, peak trainability window.
- **Open / Peak years**: 20s and early 30s; biological prime for endurance performance.
- **Masters**: World Athletics defines masters status as 35+; the colloquial "masters runner" usage is 40+. This doc follows World Athletics for definitions and uses 5-year age bands (M40, M45, ... M85+) for normative data.
- **Senior**: 60+ in this doc (matches typical age-grading inflection points).
- **Sarcopenia**: age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass and strength, accelerating after age 50.
- **VO2max**: maximal oxygen uptake (mL/kg/min); ceiling on aerobic power.
- **LT (lactate threshold)**: the running speed at which blood lactate begins to accumulate above resting baseline (typical operational cut-offs: 2 mM or ~4 mM).
- **Running economy (RE)**: oxygen cost per unit distance at a given submaximal velocity (mL/kg/km).
- **Apophysis**: secondary growth-plate ossification center where tendon attaches to bone (e.g., tibial tuberosity at the knee, calcaneal apophysis at the heel). Common site of youth overuse injury.
- **Tanner stage**: 1–5 sexual maturity rating; Tanner 5 = full pubertal development.

## Youth Running Guidelines (Under 14)

### Core principles (AAP, RRCA, World Athletics)

- Running for children should be self-driven, varied, and play-based; structured high-volume training is inappropriate before puberty.
- Healthy, asymptomatic children may race up to **5K**. Distances beyond 5K require individualized clearance.
- **Full marathon is contraindicated below Tanner stage 5.** Half marathons are not recommended pre-puberty.
- After full pubertal development (Tanner 5), adult guidelines apply.
- Mileage should not increase more than **10% per week**. Build at least one easy/recovery week per 3–4 weeks of progression.
- Children should take **2–3 months/year off structured running** with cross-training or unstructured activity, especially after a competitive season.
- Pediatric physiology favors aerobic activity but limits anaerobic capacity (lower glycolytic enzyme activity, reduced thermoregulation efficiency in heat).

### Volume and racing limits by age (RRCA / Bowerman / pediatric sports medicine consensus)

| Age | Max sessions/wk | Max long run | Max weekly mileage | Max race distance |
|-----|-----------------|--------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| <8  | Unstructured play; 1–2 fun runs/wk | 1–2 mi | <6 mi | 1 mi (fun run) |
| 8–10 | 3–4 | 6 mi | 15 mi | 5K |
| 10–12 | 4 | 8 mi | 20 mi | 5K (10K only with clearance) |
| 12–14 | 5 | 10 mi | 30 mi | 10K (half-marathon strongly discouraged) |

### Growth-plate (apophyseal) considerations

- Apophyses are the weakest link in the pre-pubertal musculoskeletal chain — weaker than ligament and tendon. Force that would tear adult tendon avulses bone in children.
- Common youth running apophysitides:
  - **Sever's disease** (calcaneal apophysitis) — heel pain, ages 8–12.
  - **Osgood-Schlatter** (tibial tubercle) — anterior knee pain, ages 10–14.
  - **Sinding-Larsen-Johansson** (inferior patellar pole) — ages 10–13.
  - **Iselin's** (5th metatarsal base) — lateral foot pain, ages 9–13.
- Red flags warranting referral: night pain, asymmetric pain, pain that does not warm up, antalgic gait, point tenderness over a growth plate after a single event.
- Manage apophysitis with relative rest, eccentric loading of the involved muscle group, ice after activity, and gradual return when pain-free during running.

## Adolescent Training (High School, ~14–18)

Post-Tanner-5 athletes can train as adults but periodization and progression must respect bone maturation (skeletal maturity reached 16–20 in females, 18–22 in males).

### Mileage progression by training year (consensus high-school coaching)

| Training year | Summer base peak (mpw) | In-season (mpw) | Long run cap |
|---------------|------------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Pre-9th (rising freshman) | 25–30 | 20–25 | 8 mi |
| Pre-10th | 30–40 | 30–35 | 10 mi |
| Pre-11th | 40–50 | 35–45 | 12 mi |
| Pre-12th | 50–60 | 45–55 | 14 mi |

Reduce upper bounds ~15–20% for athletes with shorter training history or smaller frame; girls progress somewhat more conservatively in absolute mileage.

### Periodization rules

- Year-round base + 2–3 peak racing seasons (XC, indoor, outdoor) with ≥2 weeks of unstructured activity between seasons.
- Hard sessions: 1–2 per week in-season; 1 in base; never 2 hard days back-to-back.
- Strength training (bodyweight, plyometrics, basic barbell): 2x/week year-round.
- Race longest in season: 5K cross-country; track distance up to 3,200m / 2-mile.
- Avoid year-round single-sport specialization with no off period — strongest predictor of overuse injury and burnout in pediatric sports medicine literature.

## Collegiate Training (18–22)

Post-skeletal maturity. Highest sustainable volume window of the lifespan.

| Tier | Weekly mileage (base) | Weekly mileage (peak race season) |
|------|-----------------------|------------------------------------|
| Walk-on / D3 distance | 50–70 | 40–55 |
| D1 mid-distance (800–1500m) | 55–75 | 45–60 |
| D1 distance (5K–10K) | 70–100 | 60–80 |
| D1 elite distance / marathon-track aspirants | 90–120 | 75–95 |

- Progression from high-school peak should be ~10–15% increase per training year, not a single-summer jump.
- Two-a-days appear in distance programs typically 2–4 days/wk during base, with the second run easy and short.
- Strength training: 2–3x/wk; emphasize hip/posterior chain, single-leg work, plyometric power maintenance.
- Female collegiate distance runners: monitor energy availability and menstrual function (REDs / Female Athlete Triad — see Task 13).

## Peak Performance Ages (20s and Early 30s)

- **Marathon physiological peak: 25–35 (mean ~29).** ~73% of elite marathon PRs occur 25–34.
- **Track 5K–10K peak: 24–30.**
- **Mile / 1500m peak: 23–28.**
- VO2max, HRmax, and stroke volume peak in early 20s; sustained training delays measurable decline until ~35.
- Running economy improves with cumulative training years and continues improving into the early 30s in marathoners — partly explaining why marathon peaks later than shorter events.
- Trainability is highest in this band: response to a given stimulus is largest, recovery between sessions fastest. Periodization can include the highest weekly mileage tolerated across the lifespan.

---

## 30s Training (Subtle Changes; Marathon Sweet Spot)

- VO2max begins to decline measurably by ~35 in trained athletes (~5% per decade with continued training; see Tanaka/Seals).
- HRmax declines about 0.7 bpm/yr (Tanaka formula: HRmax = 208 − 0.7 × age).
- Running economy and lactate threshold are largely preserved if training continues; marathon performance at 30–35 can match or exceed 25–30 because RE and aerobic durability accumulate.
- Lepers' analysis of single-age world records and elite marathon fields shows the **U-shaped age–marathon time relationship**: nadir at 28–30, near-flat plateau out to ~35, then progressive decline.

### Adjustments in the 30s

- Add 1–2 strides sessions per week to defend neuromuscular power.
- Begin (if not already established) 2x/wk strength work.
- Recovery requirements increase modestly — typically one extra day between hardest sessions versus 20s.
- Sleep and nutrition margin narrows: same training stress with worse recovery practices yields more injury than in the 20s.

---

## Masters Athletes (35+)

World Athletics: masters = 35+. Traditional usage (race age groups, USATF masters): 40+. Age groups in 5-year bands.

### Universal masters principles

- VO2max declines ~5–7% per decade in trained athletes vs ~10–12% per decade in sedentary peers. Continued training cuts the decline rate roughly in half.
- Lactate threshold velocity declines slower than VO2max — masters runners race at a higher %VO2max than younger runners, partially compensating.
- Running economy is largely preserved if training is continuous.
- Strength loss outpaces VO2 loss in those who don't lift; **power loss outpaces strength loss** (Type II fiber atrophy is greater than Type I).
- Recovery time between hard sessions increases; muscle damage is similar in magnitude to younger runners but resolves more slowly.

---

## 40s Training Adjustments

- Hard sessions: typically 2/wk (one quality + one long with quality) instead of 3/wk; or 3/wk with one shorter and easier.
- Strength training: 2x/wk minimum, emphasizing posterior chain, single-leg, calf/Achilles complex, and explosive (jump-based) work to preserve power.
- Add a dedicated mobility/hip routine 3–5x/wk (10–15 min).
- Begin scheduling a true easy week (-30% volume) every 3–4 weeks rather than 4–5.
- Achilles, hamstring, and hip-flexor tendinopathy risk rises — load tendons gradually, especially with calf raises and eccentric work.
- Continue VO2max-style intervals (3–5 min reps) at least once every 10–14 days; this is the trainable but most-rapidly-eroding system.

---

## 50s Training Adjustments

- Default to 2 hard sessions per week with at least 72 hours between them.
- Reduce single-day max volume; consider splitting long runs in some weeks (long + medium-long).
- Strength training becomes non-negotiable. 2–3x/wk; include heavy compound lifts (squat, deadlift variants, calf raises, single-leg) at 70–85% 1RM. Power training (jump squats, pogos, hill sprints) 1–2x/wk.
- Bone-loading: maintain weight-bearing impact in training; this is a key window for hip BMD preservation. Avoid full removal of running in favor of cycling/swimming alone.
- Perform regular mobility work; tendon stiffness increases meaningfully in this decade.
- Consider a coach-supervised cardiac evaluation prior to any return to high-intensity training (see screening section).
- Typical performance decline from age 40 baseline: ~6% per decade; expect ~8–10 min slower marathon, ~3–5 min slower half-marathon.

---

## 60s Training Adjustments

- 5–6 running days max; replace one run with cross-training (cycling, elliptical, aqua-jog) for a similar aerobic stimulus with reduced impact.
- Hard sessions: 2/wk only when recovery is solid; otherwise 1 quality + 1 fast finish long run.
- Shift some intensity from VO2max to threshold work (better preservation; threshold remains highly trainable into 70s).
- Strength training 2–3x/wk; include power work — falls risk and stride power both depend on rate of force development.
- Recovery: an extra easy day after long runs; sleep and protein intake have larger relative effects than in younger runners.
- Performance reference: a typical recreational 60-year-old loses ~6 min off a 10K, ~15 min off a half, and ~60 min off a marathon vs. their age-40 self.

---

## 70+ Training

- Daily running becomes optional; alternate-day running is a defensible default for many.
- Maintain 2 strength sessions per week. Emphasis shifts further toward power and balance (single-leg balance, step-up power, calf jumps).
- Continue impact loading (running, hopping) in modest doses unless contraindicated by orthopedic pathology — this is bone-loading currency.
- Reduce single-session intensity; favor frequent, lower-stress aerobic exposure.
- Marathon decline becomes nonlinear: world records show roughly linear decline to ~75–80, then exponential decline thereafter.
- Even at 75, world-class endurance masters maintain VO2max ~46–50 mL/kg/min and can sustain 91–94% of VO2max for marathon distance.

---

## The VO2max Decline Curve

### Rates (Tanaka & Seals)

| Population | VO2max decline |
|------------|----------------|
| Sedentary adults | ~10–12% per decade after 30 |
| Continuously trained masters | ~5–7% per decade |
| Elite continuously trained | ~5% per decade until 60s, steeper after |
| Reduced-training masters (longitudinal) | −5% to −46% per decade, scales with training-volume reduction |

### Mechanism

- **Primary driver: HRmax decline** (~0.7 bpm/yr; independent of training).
- Stroke volume and a-vO2 difference fall ~10% in trained adults across decades — much less than the change in maximal HR.
- VO2max per kg falls faster than absolute VO2 if body composition shifts (sarcopenia + fat gain).

### Countermeasures

- Preserve weekly intensity dose: 1 VO2-style session every 7–14 days (3–5 min reps at hard but controllable effort).
- Maintain training volume; volume reduction is the single largest modifiable contributor to accelerated decline.
- Strength + plyometrics defend stroke-volume-relevant lean mass and stride power.

---

## Lactate Threshold Preservation

- LT (as a fraction of VO2max) is preserved or even increases with age in trained masters runners.
- Absolute LT velocity declines ~1.0–1.4% per year in masters who maintain mostly easy running; ~0.5–0.8% per year in those doing structured threshold work.
- Practical implication: threshold pace is the most defendable performance variable. Programming 1 threshold session per 7–10 days in masters is high-yield.

---

## Running Economy Preservation

- Cross-sectional and longitudinal studies show **no decline in running economy with age in continuously trained masters** through age 70+.
- RE may improve with training years even as VO2max falls — runners can race at higher %VO2max to compensate.
- Detraining causes RE to regress quickly (months); re-training restores it slowly (months to years).
- Strides, hill sprints, and plyometric work appear to be the most reliable RE-preserving stimuli for masters.

## Recovery Requirements by Decade

Approximate recommended hours between hard sessions for the same physiological system, assuming a healthy, consistent runner.

| Decade | Hard-to-hard interval | Easy weeks per cycle | Annual full down weeks |
|--------|-----------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| 20s    | 36–48 h               | every 4 weeks        | 1–2 weeks              |
| 30s    | 48–60 h               | every 3–4 weeks      | 2 weeks                |
| 40s    | 60–72 h               | every 3 weeks        | 2–3 weeks              |
| 50s    | 72–96 h               | every 3 weeks        | 3 weeks                |
| 60s    | 72–96 h, 2 hard/wk max| every 3 weeks        | 3–4 weeks              |
| 70+    | 96+ h, 1–2 hard/wk    | every 2–3 weeks      | 4+ weeks               |

Drivers of slower recovery with age: reduced anabolic hormone activity (testosterone, GH, IGF-1), lower NAD+ availability, slower satellite-cell-mediated muscle repair, slower glycogen resynthesis at equivalent intake, and less restorative sleep.

## Strength Training: Increased Importance With Age

### Why it matters more

- Sarcopenia: ~3–8% loss of muscle mass per decade after 30, accelerating after 60.
- Type II (fast-twitch) fiber atrophy is preferential — power declines faster than strength.
- Bone density depends on strength training stimulus, especially axial loading.
- Strength-trained masters approximate younger non-trained adults in maximal force, contraction speed, and cross-sectional area.

### Programming defaults by decade

| Decade | Sessions/wk | Strength focus | Power focus |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| 20s–30s | 1–2 | Optional supplement | Strides, plyometrics 1x |
| 40s | 2 | Compound lifts 70–85% 1RM | Plyometrics 1x, hill sprints 1x |
| 50s | 2–3 | Heavy compound + single-leg | Plyometrics 1–2x, hill sprints |
| 60s | 2–3 | Compound + balance | Power emphasis (jump squat, pogo) |
| 70+ | 2 | Strength + balance + sit-to-stand | Step-up power, ankle hops |

Minimum effective: deadlift or hip-hinge variant, squat or split-squat, calf raise, single-leg hop or pogo. Two sets of 6–10 reps for strength; lower-rep, explosive sets for power.

---

## Mobility: Increased Importance With Age

- Tendon stiffness and connective tissue cross-linking increase with age, reducing range of motion at hip, ankle, and thoracic spine.
- Loss of ankle dorsiflexion and hip extension are the two most performance-relevant mobility losses in masters runners.
- Daily 5–10 min routine + 2x/wk longer (15–20 min) session is the typical effective dose.
- Dynamic mobility before runs; static stretching (>30 s holds) post-run only — pre-run static stretching transiently impairs RE and power.
- Hip-flexor / thoracic-rotation / ankle-mobility triad covers the largest performance- and injury-risk leverage.

---

## Injury Risk Shifts by Age

Different injuries become more common in different decades. Approximate relative risk shifts in distance runners:

| Injury / pathology | Highest-risk band | Notes |
|--------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Apophysitis (Sever's, Osgood-Schlatter) | <14 | Pre-skeletal-maturity overuse |
| Tibial / metatarsal stress fracture | 15–25 (esp. female), again 50+ | Bone density, energy availability, mileage spikes |
| Patellofemoral pain | 15–35 | Quad weakness, biomechanics |
| ITB syndrome | 20–35 | Volume + biomechanics |
| Plantar fasciitis | 35–55 | Calf/Achilles complex stiffness |
| Achilles tendinopathy | 35–60 | Tendon collagen turnover slows; calf weakness |
| Hamstring tendinopathy (proximal) | 40–60 | Eccentric weakness |
| Hip osteoarthritis-related pain | 55+ | Joint changes; not caused by recreational running |
| Meniscal degeneration | 50+ | Often incidental imaging finding |
| Sarcopenia-related falls / acute strain | 65+ | Rate-of-force-development decline |

Soft-tissue tendinopathies dominate masters injuries (vs. bone/joint dominance in youth and stress fractures dominating young female runners with low energy availability).

## Hormone Changes (Cross-Reference)

- **Male endurance runners**: chronic high-volume endurance training is associated with lower resting testosterone (~30% reduction) plateauing at ~5 years of training — the "exercise-hypogonadal male condition." Acute cortisol rise + testosterone fall after marathons normalizes within a week. Age-related decline in total testosterone (~1%/yr after 30) is layered on top of training effects. Symptomatic low T (libido, fatigue, recovery, mood) warrants medical evaluation.
- **Female endurance runners**: covered in Task 13 (sex-specific physiology). Briefly: perimenopause (typical onset 40s) brings hormone fluctuation, sleep disruption, and slower recovery; menopause (mean age 51) reduces estrogen-mediated bone, tendon, and muscle adaptations. Training implications: more strength training, more recovery time, more attention to sleep and protein intake. Hormone therapy decisions are individual and medical.
- See Task 13 for full treatment.

## The U-Shaped Age–Performance Relationship in Elite Marathoners (Lepers)

- Across multiple Lepers et al. analyses of elite marathon fields and single-age world records (5–93 years):
  - Performance improves rapidly through the teens.
  - **Plateau / nadir** at 25–35.
  - Linear decline 35 → 75–80.
  - Exponential decline thereafter.
- Magnitude of decline is greater in females than males in absolute terms, similar in percentage terms.
- Sub-3-hour marathoners across five consecutive decades show smaller relative age-decline than less-trained peers — performance level retained scales with training continuity.
- Implication for coaching: most adult amateurs racing first marathons in their 30s–40s have substantial PR upside despite chronological aging, because their training age and economy are the binding constraints, not VO2max.

## Returning to Running at Older Ages

For new runners or returners 40+:

- **Medical clearance** advisable before re-initiating, especially if sedentary >5 years or any cardiovascular risk factor present.
- **Walk-run progression** (e.g., 1 min run / 2 min walk, building toward continuous) for 6–10 weeks.
- Increase weekly minutes (not miles) by ≤5–10% per week.
- Strength training **before or alongside** running, not after — establishes tissue tolerance and balance reserve first.
- Plan 16–20 weeks for adaptations that took 6–8 weeks at age 30.
- Cross-training (cycling, swimming, elliptical) substitutes for running days early in the build to reduce impact-related overuse.
- Flat surfaces, supportive shoes, and a return-to-impact progression for tendons are higher-leverage than they were at 25.
- Returners with running history: tissue memory accelerates the cardiovascular comeback; tendons and ligaments lag behind muscle and aerobic fitness, so injuries cluster in weeks 4–12 of return.

## Bone Density and Impact Loading

- Running, especially with vertical impact (running, hops, jumps), is osteogenic at the **hip and femoral neck**. Impact loading downregulates sclerostin, increasing bone formation and decreasing resorption.
- Running is **not** strongly osteogenic at the lumbar spine — strength training (squat, deadlift) covers spinal BMD that running misses.
- Runners with osteoporosis, prior compression fracture, or prior low-trauma fracture should not perform jumping or high-impact running until cleared; loading still helps but must be progressed under guidance.
- Female masters runners with menstrual irregularity history (current or past), low energy availability, or RED-S history are higher-risk; DXA scan is reasonable.
- Male masters runners with chronic low testosterone or low energy availability have parallel risk.
- Calcium (1,000–1,200 mg/d) and vitamin D (800–2,000 IU/d, individualized to serum 25(OH)D) support bone-loading benefits.

## Cardiovascular Screening Recommendations by Age

### Youth and adolescent (AHA 14-element preparticipation evaluation; AAP)

- All competitive youth: history + physical including AHA 14-point screen at sport entry and at least every 2 years.
- Routine ECG screening of all youth athletes is **not** universally recommended in the US; selective ECG when history/physical or family history raises suspicion.
- Red flags warranting cardiology referral: exertional syncope, exertional chest pain, family history of sudden cardiac death <50, known inheritable cardiac disease, exertional dyspnea out of proportion to fitness.

### Adults entering or returning to vigorous training (ACSM, current algorithm)

ACSM 2014+ algorithm screens on three axes:

1. Current activity level (≥30 min, 3 d/wk moderate-or-vigorous for ≥3 months = "active").
2. Signs/symptoms or known cardiovascular, metabolic, or renal disease.
3. Desired exercise intensity (moderate vs. vigorous).

| Profile | Medical clearance prior to vigorous exercise? |
|---------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Active, asymptomatic, no known disease | Not required |
| Active, asymptomatic, known disease | Recommended if not recently cleared |
| Active, symptomatic | Required |
| Inactive, asymptomatic, no known disease | Not required for moderate; recommended before vigorous |
| Inactive, asymptomatic, known disease | Required |
| Inactive, symptomatic | Required |

ACSM intentionally removed risk-factor counting from the algorithm — population CVD risk-factor prevalence is high while exercise-related sudden cardiac events remain rare, and risk-factor profiling poorly predicts those events.

### Masters athletes (AHA Masters statement; 35+)

- Underlying coronary artery disease is the dominant cause of exercise-related sudden cardiac death in masters athletes.
- Recommend AHA Preparticipation Screening Questionnaire or PAR-Q+ at entry; comprehensive cardiovascular history and physical.
- Stress test (graded exercise test) reasonable for: men ≥45 / women ≥55 with multiple risk factors initiating vigorous training; any masters athlete with concerning symptoms; any masters athlete with known disease.
- Annual self-screen of symptoms and a periodic clinician review are reasonable defaults for asymptomatic masters runners.

### Symptom-driven escalation (any age)

Stop training and seek evaluation for:

- Exertional chest pain, pressure, or tightness
- Exertional syncope or near-syncope
- New irregular heartbeat or palpitations during/after exercise
- Disproportionate exertional dyspnea
- New-onset exertional fatigue or performance decline beyond expected detraining
